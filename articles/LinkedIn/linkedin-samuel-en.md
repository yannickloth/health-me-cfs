# Samuel did not die from "Covid management." He died from fifty years of indifference toward Myalgic Encephalomyelitis.

On January 30, 2026, Samuel, a 22-year-old Austrian, chose assisted suicide.

Reddit link: https://www.reddit.com/r/Austria/comments/1qg8oit/ich_bin_samuel_21_leide_an_mecfs_und_in_12_tagen/

A few days later, the RTBF (Belgian public broadcaster) headlined: "The assisted suicide of a 22-year-old Austrian calls into question the management of long Covid." (article by Julien Beauvois)

RTBF article: https://web.archive.org/web/20260223072251/https://www.rtbf.be/article/parler-est-totalement-impossible-le-suicide-assiste-d-un-autrichien-de-22-ans-remet-en-question-la-gestion-du-covid-long-11682355

This article commits a diagnostic media error whose consequences extend far beyond Samuel's case. Samuel was not suffering from some vague "Covid aftereffect." He was suffering from Myalgic Encephalomyelitis (ME), also known as Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) — a devastating multisystemic neuroimmune disease, classified by the WHO as a neurological condition since 1969 (ICD code G93.3).

The RTBF article never states this diagnosis. Not once. And this omission is not trivial. It has concrete, measurable, and tragic consequences for millions of patients and their families worldwide.
I. WHAT ME/CFS ACTUALLY IS: BEYOND THE WORD "FATIGUE"

Let us start by undoing a fundamental misunderstanding. The name "Chronic Fatigue Syndrome" is unanimously recognized by researchers and patients as one of the worst names ever given to a disease. It is like calling Alzheimer's disease "Chronic Forgetfulness Syndrome" or lung cancer "Persistent Cough Syndrome." The word "fatigue" trivializes a condition that destroys lives.

What it actually is: a multisystemic neuroimmune disease that affects the central nervous system, the immune system, cellular energy metabolism, and the autonomic cardiovascular system. It affects between 0.2% and 1% of the world's population, meaning between 15 and 80 million people. Up to 75% of patients are unable to work. A quarter of them are homebound or bedbound.
The cardinal symptom: Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM)

PEM is what fundamentally distinguishes ME/CFS from fatigue, depression, burnout, and most forms of long Covid. It is not about being "tired after exertion." It is an abnormal biological response where the slightest stimulus — physical, cognitive, or sensory — triggers a systemic collapse of the body that can last days, weeks, or even cause permanent deterioration.

In concrete terms: taking a shower can trigger a 48-hour crash. Reading an article can render the patient unable to speak for a day. Going grocery shopping can lead to a week of total bed rest.

This is not a metaphor. This is the daily reality of millions of people.
II. WHAT SCIENCE HAS DEFINITIVELY PROVEN: THE NIH STUDY (FEBRUARY 2024)

The RTBF article presents Samuel's case as though medicine were groping in the dark. Yet on February 21, 2024, the National Institutes of Health (NIH) of the United States published in Nature Communications the results of the most important intramural clinical study ever conducted on ME/CFS: "Deep Phenotyping of Post-Infectious Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome" (Walitt et al., 2024).

This study, conducted by 75 authors from 15 of the NIH's 27 institutes, and described by Dr. Avindra Nath as "the most complex project he had ever led," demonstrated in ME/CFS patients:

    Brain activity abnormalities: dysfunction of integrative brain regions, with a direct impact on motor control and decision-making. Dr. Nancy Klimas's conclusion: "This is a disease that comes from the brain."

    Central catecholaminergic system dysregulation: the brain's chemical command system governing effort, motivation, and autonomic regulation is fundamentally impaired.

    Chronic immune activation: increased naive B lymphocytes and decreased switched memory B lymphocytes, suggesting persistent antigenic stimulation — the immune system is in a permanent state of alert.

    Metabolic abnormalities: failure of cellular energy production.

    Autonomic nervous system consequences: cardiac dysregulation, dysautonomia, orthostatic intolerance.

This is not "in the head." It is not "psychosomatic." It is not a "lack of willpower" or a "post-viral fatigue that will pass." It is a biologically documented multisystemic organic failure identified by the world's largest biomedical research institution.

At this point, given that the mechanisms of the disease are known, it should no longer be called a syndrome: it is indeed a disease.

And Covid-19 was merely the trigger. ME/CFS has been caused by multiple infectious agents for decades: mononucleosis (Epstein-Barr virus), influenza, enteroviruses, bacteria. Samuel fell ill after Covid. Others fall ill after a common sore throat. The disease is the same. Not naming it erases this reality.
III. THE SPECTRUM OF THE DISEASE: FROM "HALF A LIFE" TO ABSOLUTE HELL

The RTBF article focuses on Samuel's tragic end. But it says nothing about what millions of patients experience at different stages. Understanding these stages is essential, because this is where prevention comes into play: a moderate patient, poorly advised, can tip into severe. And this tipping point is often irreversible.
Mild stage (approximately 50% of patients)

The patient can still work, sometimes part-time, but lives in permanent energy deficit. Every day is a rationing calculation: "Do I take a shower or do I cook tonight? Do I attend the meeting or do I save enough energy to pick up my child from school?" Friends do not understand why they decline invitations. Colleagues think they are lazy. The doctor tells them to "exercise more." Their physical appearance is normal. Their suffering is completely invisible.
Moderate stage (approximately 25-30% of patients)

This is the stage where social and professional life collapses. The patient can no longer work. They spend most of the day lying down or semi-reclined. They can still get up for basic tasks — eating, going to the bathroom — but every activity is paid for with hours or days of "crash": a brutal worsening of symptoms that confines them to bed in the dark.

At this stage, the patient lives in a state of permanent flu: diffuse muscle and joint pain, swollen lymph nodes, recurring sore throats, cognitive fog so dense they can no longer follow a conversation, read a book, or look at a screen for more than a few minutes. Working memory is devastated. Many describe the sensation of having "a brain wrapped in cotton" or "thinking through molasses."

Orthostatic intolerance (often in the form of POTS — Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome) makes simply standing upright exhausting: the heart races, blood pressure drops, the patient must lie down or risk fainting. Standing at the sink doing dishes for ten minutes can trigger a collapse.

This is the stage where the misunderstanding is most destructive. The patient "looks fine" during a brief visit. They may sometimes smile, hold a five-minute conversation. The people around them conclude that they are "getting better." The doctor suggests "exercise rehabilitation." But this brief appearance of normality is a biological facade that will be paid for in the hours that follow by a collapse that nobody sees, because the patient will be alone, in the dark, unable to move.
Severe and very severe stage (10-25% of patients)

This was Samuel's stage. The patient is bedridden 24/7 in complete darkness and total silence. Light causes unbearable neurological pain. Sound — even a whispered voice — is a physical assault. The patient often wears double hearing protection (earplugs + noise-canceling headphones) at all times.

Speech disappears. Not by choice, but because the neurological effort of formulating words and articulating them exceeds the body's energy capacity. Samuel wrote on Reddit, in a final act of torn courage: "I have to stay in bed 24 hours a day and cannot move much. The darkness must be complete because I cannot tolerate light. I wear double hearing protection because I cannot tolerate any noise. I can no longer even talk to my mother, who takes care of me, because listening is too exhausting, and speaking has become totally impossible."

Some patients are tube-fed. Many cannot tolerate physical contact. The slightest sensory stimulus — a door closing, a ray of light under the door, a cooking smell — can trigger a neurological pain crisis lasting hours.

It must be stated clearly: these patients are prisoners of their own bodies, in a sensory cell from which they cannot escape. This is not depression. This is not anxiety. This is a neurological disease that has walled them in alive.
IV. A QUALITY OF LIFE WORSE THAN CANCER: THE FIGURES THE ARTICLE OMITS

The landmark study by Hvidberg et al. (2015), published in PLOS ONE, compared the health-related quality of life (EQ-5D score) of ME/CFS patients with that of 20 other chronic conditions. The results are unequivocal:

ME/CFS patients have a quality of life significantly lower than that of patients with:

    Multiple sclerosis

    Lung cancer

    Breast cancer (after surgery)

    Congestive heart failure

    Type 2 diabetes

    Stroke

    Rheumatoid arthritis

These figures are not contested. They are published in peer-reviewed journals and are reproducible.

Yet, while each of these diseases benefits from structured care pathways, validated treatment protocols, reference centers, and massive research funding, ME/CFS remains the poor relation of medicine. NIH research funding for ME/CFS has historically been lower than that for hay fever — for a disease that disables millions of people.

When the RTBF article says that "Samuel's doctor admitted his powerlessness," this is not a medical mystery. It is the result of fifty years of systemic underfunding of a disease that medicine chose to ignore.
V. AN EARTHQUAKE THAT SHATTERS FAMILIES

ME/CFS does not strike an individual. It devastates an entire family unit. This is a dimension that the RTBF article totally ignores, and yet it may be the most devastating.
The partner: caregiver without training, without relief, without recognition

In moderate forms, the partner becomes the pillar bearing everything: the household income (often alone), domestic tasks, child-rearing, administrative procedures, medical appointments — all while becoming, de facto, an unqualified caregiver for a disease that nobody understands.
The Norwegian study by Kielland et al. (2025), published in Discover Public Health, demonstrated using national registry data that partners of people with ME/CFS experience a significant deterioration in their own health, a decline in their work capacity, and an increase in their use of social transfers. In other words: the disease makes caregivers sick in turn.

The lack of medical recognition poisons the relationship. When the doctor says "there's nothing wrong with him" or "it's in his head," the partner is placed in an impossible position: should they believe the patient or the doctor? This doubt, instilled by the medical institution itself, is an acid that corrodes couples from the inside. The separation rate is massive.
Children: growing up in the shadow of an invisible disease

A child growing up with a parent who has ME/CFS lives an experience for which no word exists in everyday language.

Their parent is there, physically present in the house, but absent from their life. The parent cannot attend their school play. Cannot play with them in the garden. Sometimes cannot even tolerate the sound of their laughter or their crying without their condition worsening. The child learns, very early, to walk on tiptoe in their own home. To whisper. To play in silence. Not to slam doors. Not to run in the hallway.

For a child, this situation is both incomprehensible and guilt-inducing. "Why can't Mum come to my show?" "Is it my fault that Dad is in bed?" "Why does the doctor say it's nothing, when Mum cries every day?"

The study by Vyas et al. (2022), published in BMJ Open, documented the catastrophic impact of ME/CFS not only on patients but also on their partners and families, revealing levels of psychological distress and quality-of-life degradation among loved ones comparable to those observed in caregivers of patients with neurodegenerative diseases.

These children frequently develop anxiety, depression, and a chronic sense of emotional insecurity. They grow up in a climate of permanent unpredictability: the parent may be "doing well" for an hour and collapse the next. No family outing can be planned with certainty. No promise can be kept.
Isolation: the social death of the patient AND their family

Friends disappear. Not out of cruelty, but out of incomprehension. "He always cancels." "She's probably exaggerating." "He looked fine the last time I saw him." Without a clearly named and socially understood diagnosis, the people around them end up judging, then drifting away.

Isolation strikes the patient, but also the entire family. The partner no longer has time or energy for their own social relationships. The children do not dare invite friends to the house ("we have to stay quiet, Mum is sick"). The entire family finds itself trapped by a disease that nobody on the outside understands in either its severity or its permanence.
VI. THE DEADLY DANGER OF NOT NAMING THE DISEASE: EXERCISE THERAPY

This is where the omission of the diagnosis becomes potentially lethal.

For decades, the recommended treatment for ME/CFS in many countries was "graded exercise therapy" (GET) and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), based on the theory that patients were "deconditioned" and that the disease was "maintained by exercise avoidance."

In October 2021, NICE (the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence) in the UK made a historic 180-degree turn by withdrawing graded exercise therapy from its recommendations, declaring it not only ineffective but potentially harmful. NICE explicitly concluded:

> "Any programme using fixed incremental increases in physical activity or exercise (for example, graded exercise therapy), or physical activity or exercise programmes that are based on deconditioning and exercise avoidance theories, should not be offered to people with ME/CFS."

Patient surveys revealed that GET was the treatment judged most harmful: thousands of patients testified to a permanent worsening of their condition after following these programs.

Here is the direct link to Samuel and the RTBF article: if ME/CFS is not named, if only "long Covid" is mentioned, then exercise rehabilitation protocols — which are indeed recommended for certain mild forms of long Covid (lung damage, cardio-respiratory deconditioning) — continue to be applied to patients who actually meet the criteria for ME/CFS.

For these patients, exercise is not a remedy. It is a poison.

Samuel, in his very severe form, is the ultimate illustration of what happens when the body has exhausted all its energy capacity. The RTBF article quotes Tomaso Antonacci: "We continue to force people to resume their activity, while studies demonstrate the harmful effects." But without naming ME/CFS and PEM, this warning remains abstract. With a named diagnosis, it becomes a concrete prevention protocol that could prevent hundreds of thousands of patients from tipping into severe forms.
VII. THE INVISIBILIZATION OF A HISTORIC DISEASE

ME/CFS was not born with the 2020 pandemic. The first documented outbreaks date back to the 1930s (Los Angeles, 1934). The disease was named "Myalgic Encephalomyelitis" after the Royal Free Hospital epidemic in London in 1955. The WHO classified it as a neurological disease in 1969.

By absorbing Samuel's case under the generic label of "long Covid," the media participate in erasing this history. For patients who have been fighting for decades — long before 2020 — to have their condition taken seriously, seeing their diagnosis replaced by a more "current" term is a form of betrayal.

"Long Covid" is an umbrella term that groups together very different clinical realities: lung damage, cardiac damage, loss of taste, transient cognitive fog, postural tachycardia syndrome, and — in a significant proportion of patients — a genuine ME/CFS meeting strict diagnostic criteria (Canadian Consensus Criteria or Institute of Medicine Criteria).

Mixing everything under one term creates a patient group so heterogeneous that no study can produce coherent results, no targeted treatment can be developed, and no public health policy can be effective.
VIII. THE BUDGETARY "STOWAWAY"

Since 2020, billions have been allocated for long Covid research. By not naming ME/CFS as a distinct condition within this spectrum, a blatant health inequity is created:

- A patient who develops ME/CFS after Covid has access (in some countries) to "post-Covid" clinics and dedicated funding.

- A patient who develops the exact same disease after mononucleosis in 2018 remains in a total medical desert. No specialized clinic. No funding. No recognition.

The disease is biologically identical. Only the trigger differs. But one has access to a care pathway, the other has access to nothing.

It is as if fracture research were funded only for fractures caused by car accidents, while ignoring those caused by falls. The broken bone is the same. The treatment should be the same. The absurdity should be obvious.
IX. THE DIAGNOSTIC DELAY FOR FUTURE PATIENTS

By speaking only of "long Covid," future patients are deprived of the words to understand what is happening to them.

A young person who develops ME/CFS after mononucleosis will not identify with "long Covid." Their doctor, who only hears about Covid in the media, will not make the connection. The patient will then begin a medical odyssey that lasts on average 4 to 8 years before obtaining a diagnosis — if they ever obtain one.

During these years of wandering, the patient will be bounced from specialist to specialist, undergo dozens of tests that will come back "normal" (because standard tests do not detect ME/CFS), and inevitably be told: "Everything is normal, it might be in your head." They will be referred to psychiatry. They will be prescribed antidepressants that will have no effect on their disease. They will be encouraged to "gradually take up exercise again" — the worst advice one can give an ME/CFS patient.

Naming the disease in the media means planting a marker in the collective consciousness. It means enabling the next young person who falls ill after an infection to type the right words into a search engine. It means enabling the next doctor to make the right connection. It means shortening by several years a diagnostic ordeal that destroys lives.
X. MEDIA RESPONSIBILITY AND JOURNALISTIC ETHICS

By headlining the "management of Covid," the RTBF article shifts the debate toward public health policy: masks, vaccines, lockdowns, government responsibility. It is an angle that generates clicks, but betrays Samuel's clinical reality.

The debate should not be about politics. It should be about:

    The absence of biomedical research on ME/CFS

    The absence of approved treatments for a disease recognized by the WHO for over half a century

    The absence of care pathways for patients

    The absence of medical training: the majority of medical schools do not teach ME/CFS

    The historic contempt of part of the medical establishment, which for decades labeled this disease "psychosomatic," a "yuppie flu," or "imaginary fatigue"

By turning a specific medical tragedy into a generalist opinion piece about the pandemic, citizens are prevented from understanding why a 22-year-old reaches such a point. An act of despair in the face of a neurological disease with no treatment is turned into a "Covid crisis victim." Responsibility is shifted to "crisis management," when it rests on decades of neglect toward a specific condition.
XI. THE IMPACT ON THE RIGHT TO EUTHANASIA AND UNDERSTANDING SAMUEL'S ACT

If the public thinks that Samuel took his own life because of "long Covid" — perceived by many as a fluctuating and temporary condition — his act appears incomprehensible, even disproportionate. "At 22, he could have recovered." "It's sad, but it was just fatigue."

If severe ME/CFS is named, if it is explained that it is a neurological disease with a quality of life lower than that of terminal cancers, if the total sensory entombment in which Samuel lived is described — then his choice, without ceasing to be tragic, becomes understandable. And above all, it becomes a cry of alarm that society can no longer ignore.

Timo Strotmann, who wrote Samuel's last testimony at his request, summarizes: "Severe ME/CFS had turned his life into continuous torment, leaving him no respite, no truce, and no future he could physically endure."

Not naming the disease distorts the severity of his ordeal and strips his act of its meaning: a call to action for research and recognition of a disease that walls hundreds of thousands of people in alive.
XII. THE PARADOX OF THE "IMAGINARY DISEASE" MADE REAL BY COVID

For fifty years, ME/CFS was labeled "psychosomatic" by an influential segment of the medical profession. Patients were sent home with advice about "positive thinking" or referred to psychiatry.

Long Covid, because it affected millions of people simultaneously and visibly, gained legitimacy more quickly. It is a cruel irony: the same disease, when it affects an isolated individual after mononucleosis, is "imaginary." When it affects millions of people after a widely publicized virus, it becomes "real."

By refusing to name ME/CFS in Samuel's case, this fracture is inadvertently reinforced. It validates the idea that suffering is only legitimate if the virus that caused it makes the headlines. And it deprives the "historic" ME/CFS patients — those from before 2020 — of the scientific validation that Samuel's case could have brought to their cause.
CONCLUSION: NAMING SO AS TO NO LONGER BETRAY

Samuel knew what he suffered from. His Reddit post names it explicitly: ME/CFS. His loved ones knew. The medical team that accompanied him toward assisted suicide knew. The text that Timo Strotmann wrote on his behalf says it clearly: "Severe ME/CFS had turned his life into continuous torment."

Only the RTBF article does not say it.

By not naming ME/CFS, we:

    Invisibilize a neurological disease recognized since 1969

    Prevent prevention by concealing the danger of Post-Exertional Malaise

    Sterilize research by drowning a specific condition in an umbrella term

    Condemn future patients to years of diagnostic wandering

    Abandon families in the face of a nameless monster

    Maintain a health inequity between post-Covid patients and patients whose trigger is not newsworthy

    Distort Samuel's act by reducing it to a consequence of "crisis management"

ME/CFS is one of the last major biological diseases to be treated as though it did not exist. Every article that refuses to name it adds a brick to the wall of silence behind which millions of people suffer, invisible, inaudible, forgotten.

To the author and the RTBF editorial team: Covid-19 was the spark, but ME/CFS was the pyre. Samuel knew this. His last wish was that his story be told and carried, "in the hope that people living with ME/CFS are no longer forced into invisibility, but that their suffering is finally seen, recognized, and met with concrete action."

Let us honor that wish. Let us name the disease. It is the first step toward dignity, toward research, and perhaps — finally — toward a treatment.
