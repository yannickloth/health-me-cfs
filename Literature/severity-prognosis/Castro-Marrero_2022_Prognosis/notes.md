# Factors Influencing the Prognosis of Patients with ME/CFS

**Authors:** Castro-Marrero J, Sáez-Francàs N, Segundo MJ, Calvo N, Faro M, Aliste L, Fernández de Sevilla T, Alegre J

**Journal:** Healthcare (Basel), 2022, 10(10):1958

**DOI:** 10.3390/healthcare10101958

**PMID:** 36292229

**PMCID:** PMC9600584

## Key Points

- **Diagnostic delay is the most significant modifiable prognostic factor**: Patients who recovered/improved had median diagnostic delay of 23 months vs 55 months for non-recovered patients (p=0.0004)

- **Recovery rates are low but improvement is possible**: 8.3% recovery rate, 4.8% improvement rate over median 55-month follow-up

- **Older age at onset paradoxically associated with better outcomes**: OR 1.06 (p=0.028), possibly because older patients better implement pacing strategies

- **Early intervention matters**: Multivariate analysis confirmed diagnostic delay inversely associated with recovery/improvement (OR 0.98, p=0.036)

## Methodology

- **Study Design:** Retrospective longitudinal cohort study
- **Sample Size:** n=168 adult ME/CFS patients
- **Study Period:** 2011-2020
- **Follow-up:** Median 55 months
- **Diagnostic Criteria:** Fukuda 1994 criteria
- **Assessment Tools:** Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS), Fatigue Scale (FS), Modified Fatigue Impact Scale (MFIS)
- **Treatment:** All patients received standardized pacing strategy instruction

## Acronym Definitions

- ME/CFS: Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome
- FSS: Fatigue Severity Scale
- FS: Fatigue Scale
- MFIS: Modified Fatigue Impact Scale
- PEM: Post-Exertional Malaise
- IBS: Irritable Bowel Syndrome
- OR: Odds Ratio
- R/I: Recovery/Improvement

## Certainty Assessment

- **Study Quality:** MEDIUM-HIGH
  - Standardized assessments with validated scales
  - Clear operational definitions of recovery/improvement
  - Multivariate analysis controlling for confounders
  - Limitations: Retrospective design, single-center study

- **Sample Size:** n=168 (adequate for multivariate analysis)

- **Replication:** Confirms literature on diagnostic delay impact; novel finding on age at onset

- **Conflicts of Interest:** No conflicts declared

## Clinical Implications for Acute Onset Protocol

1. **Early diagnosis is critical**: Every month of diagnostic delay reduces recovery odds
2. **First 6 months may be crucial window**: Median 23-month delay in recovered patients suggests intervention before 2 years is important
3. **Pacing from onset is essential**: All patients received pacing instruction; those who recovered/improved had earlier access to this guidance
4. **Hope for all ages**: Older onset age associated with improvement, suggesting neuroplasticity and adaptation possible at any age
