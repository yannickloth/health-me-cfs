# Key Findings: Diagnostic Delay Predicts ME/CFS Prognosis

## Main Findings

1. **Diagnostic delay is inversely correlated with recovery**:
   - Recovered/improved patients: 23 months median diagnostic delay
   - Non-recovered patients: 55 months median diagnostic delay
   - Statistical significance: p=0.0004
   - Multivariate OR: 0.98 (p=0.036)

2. **Low but measurable recovery rates**:
   - 8.3% achieved recovery (>50% FSS reduction)
   - 4.8% achieved improvement (30-50% FSS reduction)
   - Combined R/I: 13.1% over median 55-month follow-up

3. **Age at onset paradox**:
   - Older age at disease onset associated with better outcomes (OR 1.06, p=0.028)
   - Hypothesis: Better implementation of pacing strategies in older adults

4. **No strong predictors from baseline characteristics**:
   - Fatigue severity at baseline: Not significantly associated with outcomes
   - PEM severity: Not predictive
   - Psychiatric comorbidity: Not predictive
   - Fibromyalgia comorbidity: Not predictive (differs from some literature)
   - IBS comorbidity: Trend toward worse outcomes (p=0.090)

## Clinical Implications

- **Early diagnosis saves recovery potential**: Every month of delay matters
- **Therapeutic window exists**: Evidence supports aggressive early intervention
- **Pacing is foundational**: All patients received pacing instruction; earlier access = better outcomes
- **Focus on modifiable factors**: Can't change age or onset pattern, but CAN reduce diagnostic delay

## Limitations

- **Retrospective design**: Cannot establish causation definitively
- **Single center**: May not generalize to all populations
- **Self-reported improvement**: Some subjectivity in outcomes
- **Fukuda criteria**: Study predates IOM 2015 criteria
- **Low overall recovery rates**: Even with early intervention, most patients did not achieve full recovery
