## Key Points to Convey

1. **Objective measurement:** Not just subjective complaints - measured with controlled cognitive task
2. **Modality-specific:** Auditory impaired, visual intact (differential pattern)
3. **Diagnostic differentiation:** Different pattern from MS (which shows global impairment)
4. **Clinical relevance:** Explains patient difficulties in noisy environments, following conversations

## Certainty Assessment for Integration

- **Quality:** Medium-High (peer-reviewed, well-controlled design with appropriate comparison groups)
- **Sample:** n=20 per group (adequate for initial study, but limited)
- **Replication:** Limited - this is an early finding from 1996, needs modern replication
- **Limitations:** Small sample, single task type, older study methodology

**Certainty recommendation:** Medium-High
- Strong design and differential findings warrant achievement environment
- But note limitations: small sample, needs replication with modern methods

## Additional Notes

**Why this matters:**
- Provides objective evidence for commonly dismissed symptoms
- Suggests specific neural substrates (phonological loop/auditory cortex)
- Differential from MS supports ME/CFS as distinct neurological entity
- Clinical implications: environmental accommodations, assessment protocols

**Suggested compound citation (for comprehensive picture):**
```latex
ME/CFS patients exhibit objective auditory processing deficits~\cite{Johnson1996}
and significantly elevated tinnitus prevalence~\cite{Schubert2021}, consistent
with dysfunction in brainstem auditory pathways~\cite{Barnden2021} or regional
cerebral hypoperfusion affecting auditory cortex~\cite{Biswal2011}.
```

**Phrasing suggestions:**
- "modality-specific cognitive deficit"
- "selective auditory processing impairment"
- "phonological loop dysfunction"
- "differential neurological pattern distinguishing ME/CFS from MS"

**Avoid overstating:**
- Small sample (n=20) - don't claim "definitively established"
- Single study from 1996 - needs modern replication
- Don't extrapolate beyond auditory processing to all cognitive domains
