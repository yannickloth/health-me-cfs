# Selective impairment of auditory processing in chronic fatigue syndrome

**Authors:** S K Johnson, J DeLuca, B J Diamond, B H Natelson

**Journal:** Perception & Motor Skills, 1996, Volume 83(1), Pages 51-62

**DOI:** 10.2466/pms.1996.83.1.51

**PubMed:** 8873173

## Key Points

- Compared information processing across sensory modalities in CFS (n=20), MS (n=20), and healthy controls (n=20)
- CFS patients showed **modality-specific deficit**: impaired on auditory tasks but normal on visual tasks
- MS patients showed equal impairment on both auditory and visual tasks
- This differential pattern suggests distinct underlying mechanisms in CFS vs. MS
- Findings interpreted through Baddeley's working memory model (phonological loop vs. visuospatial sketchpad)

## Methodology

- **Study Design:** Cross-sectional comparison across three groups
- **Sample Size:** 20 CFS patients, 20 MS patients, 20 healthy controls
- **Task:** Serial addition test (PASAT-like) in either auditory or visual modality
- **Measures:** Accuracy and speed of information processing
- **Framework:** Baddeley's working memory model

## Results Summary

**CFS Group:**
- Significantly impaired on auditory version of task
- Normal performance on visual version
- Selective auditory deficit

**MS Group:**
- Impaired on both auditory AND visual versions
- Non-selective, general processing deficit

**Interpretation:**
- CFS involves modality-specific cognitive dysfunction (auditory phonological loop)
- MS involves generalized cognitive slowing
- Different neurological substrates for the two conditions

## Relevance to ME/CFS Documentation

This is a landmark early study demonstrating **objective, measurable auditory processing deficits** in ME/CFS. Key implications:

1. **Not just subjective:** Auditory difficulties can be objectively measured
2. **Modality-specific:** Suggests focal neurological dysfunction, not general slowing
3. **Diagnostic potential:** Could differentiate ME/CFS from other neurological conditions
4. **Mechanistic insight:** Implicates specific brain regions (auditory cortex, phonological processing areas)

The selective auditory impairment aligns with patient reports of:
- Difficulty following conversations in noisy environments
- Sound sensitivity (hyperacusis)
- Tinnitus
- Difficulty processing spoken instructions

## Certainty Assessment

- **Study Quality:** Medium-High (peer-reviewed, well-controlled design with appropriate comparisons)
- **Sample Size:** n=20 per group (adequate for initial study, but relatively small)
- **Replication:** Limited replication in literature (this is an early finding)
- **Conflicts of Interest:** Not mentioned in abstract
- **Limitations:** Small sample, single cognitive task, 1996 study (needs replication with modern methods)

## Research Questions Raised

1. What specific brain regions underlie this auditory processing deficit?
   - Auditory cortex dysfunction?
   - Brainstem auditory pathways?
   - Phonological processing areas (left temporal lobe)?

2. Does the deficit correlate with:
   - Tinnitus severity?
   - Disease duration or severity?
   - Other cognitive symptoms (brain fog)?

3. Is the deficit:
   - Permanent or fluctuating?
   - Related to post-exertional malaise?
   - Responsive to treatment?

4. Would modern neuroimaging (fMRI during auditory tasks) reveal specific abnormalities?
