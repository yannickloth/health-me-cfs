Title: Homocysteine and Mitochondria in Cardiovascular and Cerebrovascular Systems
Authors: Peter Kaplan, Zuzana Tatarkova, Monika Kmetova Sivonova, Peter Racay, Jan Lehotsky
Journal: International Journal of Molecular Sciences
Year: 2020
Volume: 21(20): 7698
DOI: 10.3390/ijms21207698
PMID: 33080955

ABSTRACT (fetched from PMC):
Comprehensive review examining the relationship between elevated homocysteine (hyperhomocysteinemia, HHcy) and mitochondrial dysfunction in cardiovascular and cerebrovascular systems.

Key mechanisms documented:
1. HHcy deteriorates mitochondrial energy metabolism by decreasing activities and protein
   contents of ETC components (complexes I, II, III, IV, V).
2. Increased ROS generation via auto-oxidation of homocysteine and altered ETC function.
3. Imbalance in antioxidant enzymes: decreased mitochondrial SOD and catalase.
4. N-homocysteinylation of proteins (via homocysteine thiolactone), notably affecting
   cytochrome c and electron transport functionality.
5. Altered mitochondrial dynamics: dysregulated fusion/fission proteins (DRP1, Mfn2, Opa1),
   increased mitophagy.
6. ER stress crosstalk: PERK activation and upregulation of pro-apoptotic CHOP.
7. Concentration-dependent apoptotic activation: upregulation of Bax/Bad, downregulation of Bcl-2.

Brain-specific effects:
- Complex I inhibition in cortex (persistent long-term)
- Decreased complex IV in hippocampus, amygdala, cerebral cortex
- Decreased complex V in hippocampus and cortex

Disease connections: Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, stroke, neurodegeneration, glaucoma.

Therapeutic insight: hydrogen sulfide (H2S) supplementation restores mitochondrial function
and activates Nrf2 antioxidant pathways.
