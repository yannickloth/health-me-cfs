# Key Findings: Kaplan et al. 2020 - Homocysteine and Mitochondria

## Main Findings

1. **ETC Complex Inhibition:** HHcy decreases activities of mitochondrial complexes I-V, with complex I inhibition particularly persistent and pronounced in brain tissue (cortex, hippocampus).
2. **Dual ROS Generation Routes:** Auto-oxidation of homocysteine itself generates ROS; impaired ETC function creates a feed-forward loop of further ROS production.
3. **Protein N-Homocysteinylation:** Homocysteine thiolactone (a reactive metabolite) covalently modifies proteins including cytochrome c, directly disrupting electron transfer.
4. **Antioxidant Depletion:** Mitochondrial SOD and catalase activities are decreased, worsening the oxidative burden.
5. **Mitochondrial Fragmentation:** Altered DRP1/Mfn2/Opa1 balance leads to mitochondrial fragmentation and increased mitophagy.
6. **Apoptotic Cascade Priming:** HHcy upregulates Bax and Bad while downregulating Bcl-2, priming cells for apoptosis; cytochrome c release further activates caspases.
7. **H2S as Potential Therapeutic:** Exogenous H2S restores mitochondrial respiratory rate, membrane potential, and activates Nrf2-mediated antioxidant defense.

## Clinical Implications for ME/CFS

- If ME/CFS patients have elevated homocysteine (even in CSF while serum is normal, per Regland 1997), the ETC complex inhibition mechanism provides a biochemical bridge to documented ME/CFS mitochondrial dysfunction
- The NAD+/Sirt1 pathway disruption (Ndufa1 suppression by homocysteine, per 2025 Cell Death Disease study) overlaps with documented ME/CFS findings of impaired NAD+ metabolism
- The ER stress - mitochondria crosstalk identified here aligns with the WASF3-ER stress pathway in ME/CFS (Syed et al. 2025)

## Limitations

- No ME/CFS patient data (all cardiovascular/neurological models)
- Mostly animal and in vitro; human HHcy data limited
- Quantitative dose-response data not consistently reported
- Does not address post-exertional malaise specifically
