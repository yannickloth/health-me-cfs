## Published Literature Found

### 1. ME/CFS-Specific TSPO PET Studies

#### Raijmakers et al. (2021) - Negative Finding
**Citation:** Raijmakers R et al. (2021) Neurol Neuroimmunol Neuroinflamm 9(1):e1113
**PMID:** 34815320
**PMCID:** PMC8611501
**Certainty:** Medium (peer-reviewed, small sample)
**Sample:** n=9 CFS patients, n=10 Q fever fatigue syndrome, n=9 healthy controls (all women)
**Tracer:** [11C]-PK11195
**Findings:**
- No statistically significant differences in TSPO binding potential (BPND) between CFS patients and healthy controls
- No statistically significant differences in TSPO binding between Q fever fatigue syndrome and healthy controls
- **Negative correlation** between TSPO binding and symptom severity in CFS patients
- Positive correlation between TSPO binding and symptom severity in Q fever fatigue syndrome
- Conclusion: "Unable to find signs of neuroinflammation in patients with CFS and QFS"

**Quality assessment:**
- Small sample (n=9 per group)
- Single study, not replicated
- First-generation TSPO tracer (PK11195) - lower signal-to-noise than newer tracers
- Only brain imaging, not whole-body
- Peer-reviewed (Neurology journal)

**Significance:** This is the **only published TSPO PET study specifically in ME/CFS**. It found NO neuroinflammation signal, contradicting the hypothesis that TSPO elevation in brain is a biomarker for ME/CFS.

#### VanElzakker et al. (2019) - Review
**Citation:** VanElzakker MB et al. (2019) Front Neurol 9:1033
**PMID:** 30687207
**PMCID:** PMC6335565
**Type:** Critical review
**Findings:**
- Reviews three methods for studying neuroinflammation in ME/CFS:
  1. TSPO PET imaging
  2. Magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS)
  3. Cytokine assays (blood/CSF)
- Argues that "vast majority of ME/CFS neuroimaging has failed to use optimal techniques for studying brainstem"
- Notes methodological difficulties in TSPO PET for ME/CFS
- Critical of cytokine biomarker approaches (replication issues, biological complexity)
- TSPO PET described as "promising" but with technical challenges

**Quality assessment:**
- Review article (not primary data)
- Published in Frontiers (open-access, but not top-tier)
- Provides balanced assessment of limitations

#### Alshelh et al. (2020) - Gulf War Illness (Related Condition)
**Citation:** Alshelh Z et al. (2020) Brain Behav Immun 87:498-507
**PMID:** 32027960
**PMCID:** PMC7864588
**Sample:** n=15 Gulf War Illness veterans, n=33 healthy controls
**Tracer:** [11C]PBR28 (second-generation TSPO tracer)
**Findings:**
- **Widespread cortical elevations** in TSPO PET signal in GWI veterans
- Elevated signal in precuneus, prefrontal, primary motor, and somatosensory cortices
- No significant group differences in plasma inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β)
- No correlation between TSPO PET signal and clinical variables or cytokines
- Conclusion: Provides "first direct evidence of brain upregulation of neuroinflammatory marker TSPO in veterans with GWI"

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Gulf War Illness shares symptom overlap with ME/CFS (fatigue, cognitive problems, musculoskeletal pain)
- Demonstrates that TSPO PET CAN detect neuroinflammation in fatigue-related conditions
- Positive finding here contrasts with negative finding in Raijmakers ME/CFS study
- Different tracer (PBR28 vs PK11195) may matter (better signal-to-noise)

**Quality assessment:**
- Moderate sample (n=15 patients)
- High-impact journal (Brain, Behavior, and Immunity)
- First evidence of TSPO elevation in a fatigue syndrome
- Not replicated yet

### 2. TSPO Tracer Properties and Mechanisms

#### Khaing et al. (2026) - [18F]LW223 Whole-Body Biodistribution
**Citation:** Khaing PH et al. (2026) Eur J Nucl Med Mol Imaging 53(6):3951-3959
**PMID:** 41518399
**PMCID:** PMC13121222
**Type:** First-in-human biodistribution and dosimetry
**Tracer:** [18F]LW223 (third-generation TSPO tracer, rs6971 polymorphism-independent)
**Findings:**
- First whole-body human biodistribution analysis of [18F]LW223
- Biodistribution recapitulated known TSPO expression in various tissues
- Main elimination route: hepatobiliary (not renal)
- Critical organ: intestine
- Effective dose: 11.8-12.5 µSv/MBq (within regulatory limits)
- No bone uptake (no in vivo defluorination)
- Concluded "[18F]LW223 is suitable for translation into human clinical studies"

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates feasibility of whole-body TSPO PET imaging with modern tracers
- Shows TSPO is expressed in peripheral tissues (consistent with whole-body imaging approach)
- No data on ME/CFS patients

**Quality assessment:**
- Healthy volunteers only (no patient data)
- Third-generation tracer (best available technology)
- Peer-reviewed (European Journal of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging)

#### Barzon et al. (2026) - BBB Transport Limitation
**Citation:** Barzon L et al. (2026) Brain Behav Immun 136:106779 (online ahead of print)
**PMID:** 42034206
**Sample:** 358 dynamic TSPO PET scans from healthy controls, depression, schizophrenia
**Tracers:** [11C]-PK11195, [18F]-DPA714, [11C]-PBR28
**Findings:**
- **Negative correlation** between peripheral inflammation (CRP) and blood-to-brain influx rate constant (K1) of TSPO tracers
- Brain-wide K1 reduced by peripheral inflammation across three different tracers
- Association not influenced by diagnostic labels (depression, schizophrenia, or control)
- Body weight/BMI and sex also affect K1
- Imaging transcriptomics: effect of CRP on K1 associated with transport and homeostasis genes
- Conclusion: "Increased peripheral inflammation is associated with reduced blood-to-brain transport of TSPO tracers, suggesting a role for blood-brain barrier permeability"

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- **Critical mechanistic insight:** If ME/CFS involves peripheral inflammation, TSPO PET may UNDER-estimate brain TSPO due to BBB transport issues
- Explains potential discrepancy: ME/CFS may have neuroinflammation that is invisible to TSPO PET because tracer can't cross BBB efficiently
- Consistent across three tracers (robust finding)
- Supports emerging model of peripheral-to-central immune interactions via brain barriers

**Quality assessment:**
- Large sample (358 scans)
- Multi-tracer validation (PK11195, DPA714, PBR28)
- High-impact journal (Brain, Behavior, and Immunity)
- Peer-reviewed
- No ME/CFS patients, but findings generalizable

#### Yasuno et al. (2025) - DPA-713 in Alzheimer's Disease
**Citation:** Yasuno F et al. (2025) Neuroimmunomodulation (online ahead of print)
**PMID:** 40820409
**Sample:** n=23 AD or prodromal AD patients
**Tracer:** [11C]-DPA-713
**Findings:**
- Plasma IL-6 emerged as only significant predictor of behavioral symptoms (DBD scale)
- CSF IL-6, CSF IL-1β, CSF TNF-α, CSF CRP: no association with behavioral symptoms
- **TSPO-PET binding (DPA-713): no association with behavioral symptoms**
- No correlation between plasma IL-6 and CSF IL-6
- No correlation between plasma IL-6 and TSPO-PET measures
- Conclusion: "Peripheral IL-6 is significantly associated with BPSD severity in AD, independently of central inflammatory markers"

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates DPA-713 tracer in human clinical use
- Shows disconnect between peripheral inflammation (plasma IL-6) and central TSPO signal
- Suggests peripheral inflammation may have neuropsychiatric effects independent of neuroinflammation
- Supports hypothesis that peripheral mechanisms may drive ME/CFS symptoms even if brain TSPO is normal

**Quality assessment:**
- Small sample (n=23)
- Peer-reviewed (Neuroimmunomodulation journal)
- Use of DPA-713 tracer (relevant to search query)

### 3. TSPO in Related Contexts

#### James et al. (2023) - TREM1 vs TSPO in Multiple Sclerosis
**Citation:** Chaney AM et al. (James ML, senior author) (2023) Sci Transl Med 15(702):eabm6267
**PMID:** 37379371
**Type:** Mouse model of multiple sclerosis (EAE)
**Tracers:** 64Cu-TREM1 mAb, [18F]-GE-180 (TSPO tracer)
**Findings:**
- TREM1-PET detected active disease with 14-17-fold higher sensitivity than TSPO-PET
- TREM1 identified CNS-infiltrating peripheral myeloid cells (proinflammatory)
- TSPO-PET less specific (detects both microglia and peripheral myeloid cells)
- TREM1-PET detected response to siponimod (FDA-approved MS therapy)
- TREM1+ cells found in clinical MS brain biopsy samples (not in healthy controls)

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates that TSPO-PET has limitations in specificity
- TSPO is expressed in both central microglia AND peripheral myeloid cells
- Newer targets (TREM1) may be more specific for peripheral immune cell infiltration
- Michelle James is senior author, but this is NOT an ME/CFS study

**Quality assessment:**
- Preclinical (mouse model only, no human data)
- High-impact journal (Science Translational Medicine)
- Stanford group (relevant to search query)

#### James et al. (2022) - TREM1 vs TSPO in Parkinson's Disease
**Citation:** Lucot KL et al. (James ML, senior author) (2022) J Nucl Med 63(10):1570-1578
**PMID:** 35177426
**Type:** Mouse model of Parkinson's disease (6-OHDA)
**Tracers:** 18F-GE-180 (TSPO), 64Cu-TREM1 mAb
**Findings:**
- Both tracers showed elevated signal in striatum of 6-OHDA mice
- **18F-GE-180 detected inflammation in brain AND peripheral tissues** (blood, spleen)
- 64Cu-TREM1 mAb more specific for neuroinflammation (higher signal-to-background in brain)
- TREM1 specific to infiltrating myeloid cells
- TSPO detects both resident microglia and peripheral myeloid cells

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Confirms TSPO tracers detect peripheral inflammation, not just brain
- Supports whole-body imaging approach (TSPO signal in blood, spleen)
- Demonstrates TSPO's dual role in central and peripheral immunity
- Michelle James is senior author, but this is NOT an ME/CFS study

**Quality assessment:**
- Preclinical (mouse model only, no human data)
- Peer-reviewed (Journal of Nuclear Medicine)
- Stanford group (relevant to search query)

## Critical Gaps in Literature

### 1. ME/CFS-Specific Evidence
- **Single published TSPO PET study in ME/CFS** (Raijmakers 2021) - negative finding
- **No whole-body TSPO PET study in ME/CFS** in PubMed
- **No [11C]DPA-713 study in ME/CFS** in PubMed
- **No Michelle James ME/CFS study** in PubMed (contradicts briefing assumption)

### 2. Tracer Limitations
- First-generation tracers (PK11195) have poor signal-to-noise (used in Raijmakers study)
- Second-generation tracers (PBR28, DPA-714, GE-180) have genetic polymorphism issues (rs6971)
- Third-generation tracers (LW223) address polymorphism but are new and expensive
- **BBB transport limitation:** Peripheral inflammation reduces tracer brain influx (Barzon 2026)

### 3. Interpretation Challenges
- TSPO is expressed in both central microglia AND peripheral myeloid cells (James 2022, 2023)
- Whole-body imaging cannot distinguish peripheral vs central signal without careful analysis
- **Peripheral inflammation may mask brain neuroinflammation** by reducing tracer BBB transport
- TSPO elevation could indicate:
  - Neuroinflammation (microglial activation)
  - Peripheral immune cell infiltration
  - Mitochondrial dysfunction (TSPO also involved in mitochondrial physiology)
  - All three simultaneously

### 4. Replication and Sample Size
- **Raijmakers 2021:** n=9 CFS patients, not replicated
- **Alshelh 2020 (GWI):** n=15 patients, not replicated
- **All other studies:** Preclinical (mouse models) or healthy volunteers
- No large-scale (n>100) TSPO PET studies in fatigue syndromes

## Mechanistic Context

### TSPO Biology
- 18 kDa translocator protein (formerly PBR - peripheral benzodiazepine receptor)
- Located on outer mitochondrial membrane
- Upregulated in activated microglia and peripheral immune cells
- Involved in mitochondrial cholesterol transport, steroidogenesis, apoptosis
- **Dual role:** Inflammation marker + mitochondrial function regulator
- Genetic polymorphism (rs6971) affects binding affinity for second-generation tracers

### Blood-Brain Barrier Implications
- **Key finding (Barzon 2026):** Peripheral inflammation reduces TSPO tracer brain influx
- Suggests BBB dysfunction may be a confounder in TSPO PET studies
- If ME/CFS involves systemic inflammation, brain TSPO may be under-detected
- Consistent with hypothesis that peripheral mechanisms drive ME/CFS symptoms

### Peripheral vs Central Signal
- TSPO expressed in: microglia (brain), monocytes/macrophages (blood), astrocytes (brain), endothelial cells (BBB)
- Whole-body TSPO PET detects: brain, bone marrow, spleen, heart, lungs, liver, kidneys
- Cannot distinguish source of signal without additional data (e.g., plasma cytokines, flow cytometry)

## Recommendations for ME/CFS Research

### Immediate Actions
1. **Locate Michelle James ME/CFS study:** Check Stanford website, conference proceedings (Berlin conference), preprint servers
2. **Replicate Raijmakers 2021 with modern tracer:** Use PBR28, DPA-714, or LW223 in ME/CFS
3. **Measure BBB permeability:** Use K1 influx rate (Barzon 2026 methodology) to control for BBB effects
4. **Correlate with peripheral biomarkers:** Plasma cytokines, flow cytometry of peripheral immune cells
5. **Consider whole-body imaging:** Include peripheral organs (bone marrow, spleen) to assess systemic inflammation

### Future Directions
1. **TREM1-PET in ME/CFS:** James et al. 2023, 2022 show TREM1 more specific for infiltrating myeloid cells
2. **Multi-tracer approach:** Compare TSPO, TREM1, and mitochondrial tracers (e.g., FDG-PET for metabolism)
3. **Longitudinal studies:** Track TSPO signal before and after PEM triggers
4. **Sample size:** Aim for n>30 per group to detect moderate effect sizes
5. **Genetic stratification:** Test rs6971 polymorphism for second-generation tracer studies

## Conclusion

**The literature on TSPO PET imaging in ME/CFS is extremely limited.**

- Only one published study (Raijmakers 2021) found NO neuroinflammation in ME/CFS
- No whole-body TSPO PET studies in ME/CFS
- No DPA-713 studies in ME/CFS
- The Michelle James Stanford study mentioned in the briefing does not appear in PubMed

**Key mechanistic insight:** Peripheral inflammation may reduce TSPO tracer brain influx (Barzon 2026), potentially masking neuroinflammation in ME/CFS. This complicates interpretation of negative findings.

**Alternative hypothesis:** ME/CFS may involve peripheral inflammation and mitochondrial dysfunction without significant central microglial activation, or central activation that is invisible to TSPO PET due to BBB transport limitations.

**Urgent need:** Replication with modern tracers, whole-body imaging, and BBB permeability assessment.
