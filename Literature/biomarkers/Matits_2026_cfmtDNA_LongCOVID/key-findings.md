# Key Findings: Matits et al. 2026 — cf-mtDNA in Long COVID

## Main Findings

1. **Reduced cf-mtDNA in PASC:** Long COVID patients showed lower relative circulating cell-free mitochondrial DNA compared to recovered controls. Statistical significance was marginal after covariate adjustment (p=0.089 with full covariates; p=0.038 excluding high-CRP outliers). Effect sizes were very small (partial η²=0.01-0.02).

2. **Cognition-cf-mtDNA correlation:** Lower cf-mtDNA associated with worse general cognition scores. This was described as the "strongest relationship" in the study. Mechanism unclear.

3. **Inflammation-cf-mtDNA inverse relationship:** Higher CRP negatively correlated with cf-mtDNA. cf-mtDNA may act as a mediator between inflammation and cognitive impairment.

4. **No generalised cell death signal:** Total cell-free DNA was not elevated, indicating the mitochondrial-specific reduction is not due to generalised necrosis.

5. **25% low-grade inflammation:** One-quarter of PASC patients showed elevated CRP, consistent with ongoing inflammatory state.

## Clinical Implications

- Reduced cf-mtDNA suggests dysfunctional mitophagy (clearance of damaged mitochondria may be impaired) rather than increased mitochondrial release
- This could mean damaged mitochondria accumulate intracellularly, contributing to chronic cellular stress and energy deficits
- The exercise-activity confound is important: PASC patients are typically less active, and cf-mtDNA rises with exercise

## Limitations

1. Very small effect sizes (partial η²=0.01-0.02)
2. Marginal significance lost after covariate adjustment
3. PASC cohort heterogeneous — does not specifically enrich for ME/CFS-like phenotype
4. Activity level differences between PASC and controls not fully controlled
5. Cannot determine causality (cf-mtDNA levels could be a consequence rather than driver of Long COVID)
6. Small sample precedent — only second paper on cf-mtDNA in PASC

## ME/CFS vs Long COVID: cf-mtDNA Comparison

**Current evidence picture:**

| Condition | cf-mtDNA finding | Compartment | Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| Acute COVID-19 | Elevated | Plasma total | Massive cell death, predicts mortality |
| Long COVID (Matits 2026) | Reduced | Plasma relative | At rest, population-based |
| Long COVID (Szögi 2024) | Reduced | Nasal mucosal tissue | n=5, highly selected |
| ME/CFS (Tsilioni 2022) | Elevated post-exercise | Serum exosome-associated | Only after exercise challenge |
| ME/CFS (Myhill 2012) | Total cfDNA elevated | Plasma (total, not mtDNA-specific) | Clinical audit, methodological concerns |

**Critical interpretation:** The ME/CFS literature does NOT have a direct equivalent study measuring circulating cf-mtDNA at rest in a properly matched cohort. The Tsilioni 2022 finding (elevated exosome-associated mtDNA post-exercise) is in a different compartment (exosomes vs. free plasma), measures a different thing (exosome-associated vs. free cf-mtDNA), and is only seen post-exercise. Therefore, the claim that "Long COVID shows low cf-mtDNA while ME/CFS shows high cf-mtDNA" is plausible but NOT confirmed by direct head-to-head comparison. It remains a hypothesis.

**Research gap:** No study has directly measured free circulating cf-mtDNA (not exosome-associated) in ME/CFS patients at rest using the same qPCR method used in the Matits or Szögi studies.
