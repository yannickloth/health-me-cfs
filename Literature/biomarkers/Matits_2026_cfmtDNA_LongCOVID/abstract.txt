Circulating mitochondrial and cellular damage markers in long COVID: Links to cognitive function, psychological distress, and inflammation

Lynn Matits et al.; EPILOC Phase 2 Study Group

Molecular Psychiatry. 2026. Online ahead of print: 7 February 2026.
DOI: 10.1038/s41380-026-03471-0
PMID: 41654652

ABSTRACT (reconstructed from PubMed record and Science for ME forum thread):

Persistent mitochondrial inflexibility and mitochondrial damage have been postulated to contribute to long COVID (post-acute sequelae of COVID-19; PASC). In this nested case-control study from the population-based EPILOC (Epidemiology of Long COVID) Phase 2 study in Baden-Württemberg, Germany, we investigated circulating cell-free mitochondrial DNA (ccf-mtDNA) and other cellular damage markers in relation to cognitive function, psychological distress, and inflammation.

In 228 adults (PASC n=128; recovered controls n=100), we measured relative ccf-mtDNA by quantitative real-time PCR, total ccf-DNA by UV-Vis spectroscopy, C-reactive protein (CRP), and systemic immune-inflammation index (SII). Cognitive function and psychological distress were assessed by neuropsychological tests analysed via principal component analysis yielding three components (general cognition, verbal memory, psychological distress).

PASC patients demonstrated significantly lower cognitive function and higher psychological distress than recovered controls, and also had elevated CRP levels and lower relative ccf-mtDNA, with 25% showing low-grade inflammation. Across all participants, general cognition correlated positively with relative ccf-mtDNA, while CRP correlated negatively with relative ccf-mtDNA. Mediation analysis suggested relative ccf-mtDNA as a potential mediator of CRP differences between groups, though this association did not persist after adjusting for confounders (age, sex, education, smoking, BMI, psychiatric medication): F(1,192)=2.92; p=0.089; partial eta-squared=0.01. The difference remained significant when excluding participants with high CRP (>10 mg/L): F(1,181)=4.36; p=0.038; partial eta-squared=0.02.

Lower relative ccf-mtDNA in PASC patients might indicate altered mitochondrial quality control (mitophagy dysregulation), potentially leading to mitochondrial dysfunction, accumulation of damaged mitochondria, and increased inflammation — rather than reflecting generalised cell death.
