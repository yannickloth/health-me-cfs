# Increased CSF Homocysteine in Fibromyalgia and CFS

**Authors:** Regland B, Andersson M, Abrahamsson L, Bagby J, Dyrehag LE, Gottfries CG
**Journal:** Scandinavian Journal of Rheumatology, 1997, 26(4):301-307
**DOI:** 10.3109/03009749709105320
**PMID:** 9310111

## Key Points

- All 12 FM/CFS patients had elevated CSF homocysteine
- Fatiguability (CPRS subscale) positively and significantly correlated with CSF-HCY
- Mental fatigue severity correlated with both CSF-HCY and CSF-B12
- Serum/blood homocysteine was NOT elevated — CNS-specific compartment effect
- Authors conclude: CNS-localized B12 deficiency impairs homocysteine remethylation

## Methodology

Clinical study: 12 female outpatients with FM/CFS diagnosis. CSF lumbar puncture plus CPRS
psychopathological rating. Biochemical analysis of CSF and blood for homocysteine and B12.

## Acronym Definitions

- CSF: Cerebrospinal Fluid
- HCY: Homocysteine
- FM: Fibromyalgia
- CFS: Chronic Fatigue Syndrome
- CPRS: Comprehensive Psychopathological Rating Scale

## Certainty Assessment

- **Study Quality:** Low-Medium (small n, single-group, no healthy control comparison reported,
  combined FM+CFS cohort complicates ME/CFS-specific interpretation)
- **Sample Size:** n=12 (very small)
- **Replication:** Not independently replicated in a large CFS-specific cohort; the 2015 Regland
  paper from the same group provides supportive but not independent replication
- **Conflicts of Interest:** Not noted; Swedish academic institution
- **Limitations:** All female; FM+CFS comorbid (not pure ME/CFS); no control group with normal
  CSF values explicitly compared; sample size severely limits statistical power; 1997 diagnostic
  criteria may not correspond to current ME/CFS case definitions
- **Historical note:** This is a foundational study often cited but predates modern ME/CFS
  research methods
