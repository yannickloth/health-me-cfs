## Key Findings Summary

### 1. Deload Principles (Bell et al. 2023)
- Expert consensus: Every 4-6 weeks, ~7 days duration
- Volume reduction 40-60% through fewer sets/reps/sessions
- Can be pre-planned or autoregulatory (data-driven)
- **ME/CFS adaptation:** Much more conservative—50% reduction, every 4 weeks, 7-14 days

### 2. Overtraining Syndrome Parallels (Meeusen et al. 2013)
- OTS shares features with ME/CFS: fatigue, autonomic dysfunction, sleep/mood disturbance
- Both respond to structured rest, not "push through"
- **Critical difference:** OTS recovery weeks-months; ME/CFS often months-years
- OTS baseline = athletic; ME/CFS baseline = 10-20% of healthy

### 3. HRV-Guided Recovery (Javaloyes et al. 2024)
- HRV within "smallest worthwhile change" (SWC) = training readiness
- HRV below baseline = need for reduced activity
- Use rolling 7-day averages, individualized thresholds (±0.5-1 SD)
- **ME/CFS caveat:** Some patients have chronically suppressed HRV; requires personalization

### 4. Resting Heart Rate Monitoring
- 5+ bpm elevation = overtraining red flag (some use 7+ bpm)
- Simple, zero-cost alternative to HRV
- Daily morning measurement, 7-day trend analysis
- **ME/CFS application:** Less sensitive than HRV but highly accessible

### 5. Subjective Recovery Scales (Hooper et al. 1995; updated 2024)
- Subjective measures often outperform objective for overtraining detection
- Tools: POMS, RESTQ-Sport, DALDA, ARSS (2024 validation)
- Simple 0-10 daily ratings: recovery, sleep, energy, pain, stress
- **ME/CFS application:** Low burden, high sensitivity to trends

### 6. Structured Periodization Prevents Cumulative Fatigue
- Planned rest-activity cycles more effective than constant limits
- Athletic: 3 weeks loading + 1 week deload
- **ME/CFS adaptation:** 3 weeks baseline + 1 week deload; NO progressive loading unless extremely stable

## Clinical Implications

### Potential Benefits (Mild-Moderate ME/CFS)
- More structured, proactive approach than reactive pacing
- Objective metrics improve recovery awareness
- Planned deloads prevent cumulative fatigue
- Familiar framework for athletic patients
- Quantifiable progression pathway (if stable)

### Risks and Limitations
- No ME/CFS-specific validation (extrapolation from sports)
- Risk of misunderstanding as "progressive overload = push harder"
- Monitoring burden (cognitive, financial for wearables)
- Psychological stress from metrics for some
- Much lower baseline and slower progression than athletics

### Absolute Contraindications
- Severe or very severe ME/CFS
- Actively deteriorating or unstable
- Unable to establish stable baseline
- Recent major crash (<3 months)

### Relative Contraindications
- Psychological distress from tracking
- Cognitive impairment preventing monitoring
- Tendency toward overachievement
- Lack of understanding of conservative approach

## Comparison: Standard Pacing vs. Sports-Adapted

| Feature | Standard Pacing | Sports Medicine-Adapted |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------------|
| **Structure** | Subjective, flexible | Quantified, structured |
| **Activity limits** | Daily constant | Periodized variation |
| **Recovery** | Reactive (after crash) | Proactive (planned deload) |
| **Monitoring** | Subjective symptoms | Objective + subjective |
| **Progression** | Gradual, opportunistic | Structured, very conservative |
| **Deload** | As needed | Pre-planned every 4 weeks |
| **Suitable for** | All severity levels | Mild-moderate only |
| **Evidence** | Clinical experience | Extrapolation from sports |

## Distinction from Graded Exercise Therapy (GET)

### Critical Differences

| GET (Inappropriate) | Sports-Adapted Pacing |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| Progressive increase indefinitely | Mandatory regular deloads |
| Treats PEM as psychological | Treats PEM as biological stop |
| Fixed schedule regardless of symptoms | Autoregulatory based on metrics |
| Aims to "decondition" avoidance | Respects energy envelope reality |
| One-size-fits-all | Highly individualized |
| Progression is primary goal | Stability is primary goal |
| Ignores autonomic dysfunction | Incorporates HRV/RHR monitoring |
| Based on deconditioning hypothesis | Based on metabolic/immune recovery |

**Bottom Line:** GET has been shown harmful in ME/CFS and is no longer recommended by CDC/NIH. Sports-adapted pacing is explicitly designed around energy envelope theory with structured recovery phases. However, it remains experimental without ME/CFS validation.
