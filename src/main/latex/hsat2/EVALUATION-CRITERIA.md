# Evaluation Criteria — Hypothesis/Perspective Papers for Professional Audiences

Scope: papers that propose a mechanistic hypothesis (not report original data), targeted at **physicians and clinicians** — not biologists, geneticists, or basic-science researchers. The defining constraint is that the audience has a medical degree but likely no training in molecular biology, epigenetics, or genomics beyond what they recall from medical school.

Checklist of what "doctors, not scientists" implies:
- No chromosomal biology background assumed: centromeres, heterochromatin, histones, CpG methylation are NOT in a practising physician's daily vocabulary
- No molecular biology lab techniques assumed: PCR, sequencing, ChIP, FISH are opaque to most clinicians
- Immune system knowledge is at medical-school level (T-cells, B-cells, NK, cytokines) — deeper immunology (MDSC, myeloid lineage markers, exhaustion pathways) is NOT
- Reading time available: ~15 minutes between patients. A paper exceeding this length in plain text may be skimmed or skipped
- Primary concern: "Can I use this information for diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment?" — mechanism is interesting only if it connects to clinical action
- The word "epigenetic" may be unfamiliar to many; terms like "methylation" may be only vaguely recalled

Evaluation results (scorecards, verdicts, residual issues) go in datetime-prefixed files, never here.

## 1. Clarity of the central hypothesis
- A non-specialist reader can state the hypothesis in one sentence after reading the abstract
- The hypothesis is falsifiable in principle, not just a framework or analogy
- Scope is bounded: the paper says what it claims and what it does not

## 2. Accessibility to target audience
- Every technical term defined in plain language at first use
- Acronyms expanded at first use
- No assumed background beyond a medical degree
- Reading level appropriate for a 15-minute read by a clinician

## 3. Narrative arc
- Structure answers: What is it? → What does it do elsewhere? → Why might it apply here? → How do we find out?
- Each section answers the question the previous one raises
- No digressions into side-mechanisms not required for the central claim

## 4. Evidence calibration
- Claims proportional to evidence strength
- Established facts cited; extrapolations flagged as hypotheses
- No "demonstrated/shows/proves" language for speculative steps
- Cross-context transfers (e.g., cancer → infection) explicitly marked as analogy

## 5. Falsifiability
- Predictions are specific, measurable, and testable with named methods
- Both confirmatory and refuting outcomes stated
- Required sample types and stratification variables identified

## 6. Mechanistic coherence
- Each causal step physically/biologically plausible
- No gaps in the chain that require "and then a miracle happens"
- Trigger, amplification, propagation, and endpoint are each linked

## 7. Reference discipline
- Every factual claim has a citation
- Preprints explicitly identified
- No invented or mis-attributed references
- Author's own prior work cited where load-bearing

## 8. Clinical relevance
- Why should a clinician care? Diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment implication
- Subgroup identification strategy, not just mechanism
- No therapeutic recommendations beyond evidence

## 9. Honesty about limitations
- States what is NOT known
- Alternative explanations acknowledged
- Single-study dependencies flagged

## 10. Actionability
- A funded lab could start testing this week
- Existing datasets identified for zero-cost re-analysis
- Existing validated assays named

## 11. Fidelity to source material
- All claims traceable to cited literature, email correspondence, or referenced PDFs in the project repository
- No invented evidence, no arguments from authority, no pattern-completion ("this usually appears with X so we include it")

## 12. Appropriate collaboration disclosure
- AI involvement transparently stated (model name, role, scope)
- Preprints identified as such
- No false peer-review status claimed

## 13. Author integrity
- All named authors contributed substantively; no ghost authorship
- Corresponding author clearly identified with institutional affiliation

## 14. Scope discipline (abstract–body alignment)
- Body does not introduce claims, mechanisms, or evidence that the abstract does not commit to
- Every section in the body serves a claim in the abstract
- No tangential mechanisms, no digressions into side-pathways not required for the central argument
