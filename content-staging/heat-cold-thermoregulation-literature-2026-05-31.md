# Heat and Cold Thermoregulation Literature in ME/CFS
**Date:** 2026-05-31
**Topic:** Thermoregulatory dysfunction, heat/cold intolerance, thermal therapies in ME/CFS

---

## Summary

**Total papers found:** 4 NEW papers identified
**Papers added to bib:** 4
**Key findings:**
- Whole-body hyperthermia shows cellular benefits (autophagy reduction, mitochondrial respiration increase) but with methodological limitations
- Heat stroke mechanisms share multiple pathways with ME/CFS (gut permeability, endotoxemia, systemic inflammation)
- Skin temperature circadian rhythms disrupted in ME/CFS with endothelin-1 involvement

## Papers Identified and Evaluated

### 1. Hochecker et al. 2025 - Whole-Body Hyperthermia Treatment
**Citation:** Hochecker B, Matt K, Scherer M, Meßmer A, von Ardenne A, Bergemann J. Heat vs. Fatigue: Hyperthermia as a Possible Treatment Option for Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS). Int J Mol Sci. 2025 Jun 1;26(11):5339.
**PMID:** 40508148
**PMCID:** PMC12154023
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijms26115339

**Study Type:** Pilot study, within-subject pre-post design
**Sample Size:** n=9 ME/CFS patients (38±10 years; 7F, 2M); n=6-9 healthy controls for comparison
**Intervention:** Whole-body hyperthermia (WBH) using IRATHERM®1000; 1-hour session to Tc max 39°C

**Methods:**
- Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) collected before and after WBH
- Assays: LC3-II autophagy marker (flow cytometry), mitochondrial respiration (Seahorse), mRNA expression (qPCR)

**Key Findings:**
- Autophagy: LC3-II levels decreased by 17.84% after WBH (p=0.0065); ME/CFS basal levels 20.01% higher than healthy controls
- Mitochondrial respiration increased significantly: basal +66.60% (p=0.0040), ATP production +61.41% (p=0.0023), maximal +97.88% (p=0.0071), spare capacity +112.35% (p=0.0086)
- mRNA: HSPA5 (HSP70) increased 48.33%; IL-10 increased 37.33%
- Hypothesis: WBH restores oxygen supply via improved blood flow, reducing hypoxia-induced autophagy and enhancing mitochondrial function

**Quality Assessment:**
- Strength: Within-subject design, all 9 participants showed response, multi-parameter analysis
- Limitations: Very small sample (n=9), no standardized symptom measures, treatment conditions not fully standardized (some patients couldn't tolerate full protocol), only immediate effects measured (no follow-up)
- Journal: Int J Mol Sci (IF ~6.0, reputable)
- Certainty: 0.55 (pilot study, small n, mechanistic but not clinically validated)

**Relevance to ME/CFS Heat/Cold Effects:**
- Demonstrates thermal therapy may improve cellular function without triggering PEM-like responses
- Supports heat intolerance hypothesis (patients couldn't tolerate full protocol)
- Links autophagy and mitochondrial function to vascular oxygenation

### 2. Stanculescu et al. 2021 - Heat Stroke Mechanisms Comparison
**Citation:** Stanculescu D, Sepúlveda N, Lim CL, Bergquist J. Lessons From Heat Stroke for Understanding Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome. Front Neurol. 2021 Dec 13;12:789784.
**PMID:** 34966354
**PMCID:** PMC8710546
**DOI:** 10.3389/fneur.2021.789784

**Study Type:** Narrative review
**Scope:** Comprehensive comparison of pathophysiological mechanisms between heat stroke and ME/CFS

**Key Mechanisms Compared:**

| Mechanism | Heat Stroke | ME/CFS | Evidence Quality |
|-----------|------------|--------|------------------|
| Splanchnic vasoconstriction/hypoxia | ✓ | ✓ (subset) | Moderate |
| Gut permeability/leaky gut | ✓ | ✓ (40-67% elevated LPS antibodies) | Good |
| Endotoxemia | ✓ | ✓ | Good |
| Systemic inflammation (IL-6, TNF-α, NLRP3) | ✓ | ✓ | Good |
| CNS dysfunction/neuroinflammation | ✓ | ✓ (brain fog, imaging evidence) | Good |
| Coagulation disorders | ✓ | Mixed findings | Poor |
| Endothelial-cell injury | ✓ | ✓ (impaired dilation) | Good |
| Mitochondrial dysfunction | ✓ | ✓ (fragmentation, hypometabolic state) | Good |
| Heat shock protein deficiency | ✓ | ✓ | Moderate |

**Transcriptomic Overlaps:**
- Heat stress: Downregulation of respiratory chain genes, switch to glycolysis, reduced ATP production, increased oxidative stress
- ME/CFS: Similar patterns in PBMCs (mitochondrial, immune function, oxidative stress genes)

**Predisposing Factor Overlaps:**
- Pre-existing inflammation/infection
- Viral reactivation (EBV, CMV)
- Immune suppression
- Cumulative stress
- Microbiome disturbances
- Female predominance
- HSP deficiency

**Quality Assessment:**
- Strength: Comprehensive mechanistic review, bridges disparate literatures, identifies therapeutic opportunities from heat stroke research
- Limitations: Narrative review (no systematic methodology), no new primary data, speculative connections
- Journal: Frontiers in Neurology (IF ~4.0, open access)
- Certainty: 0.65 (good synthesis, but review-level evidence)

**Relevance to ME/CFS Heat/Cold Effects:**
- Provides framework for understanding heat intolerance in ME/CFS
- Suggests shared pathways (endothelial dysfunction, inflammation, hypoxia)
- Identifies potential therapies from heat stroke research (gut barrier repair, anti-inflammatories, anticoagulation, HSP induction)
- Explains why environmental heat may exacerbate ME/CFS symptoms

### 3. Cambras et al. 2023 - Skin Temperature Circadian Rhythms and Endothelin-1
**Citation:** Cambras T, Castro-Marrero J, Zaragoza MC, Díez-Noguera A, Alegre J. Skin Temperature Circadian Rhythms and Dysautonomia in Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: The Role of Endothelin-1 in the Vascular Tone Dysregulation. Int J Mol Sci. 2023;24(5):4835.
**PMID:** 36902264
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijms24054835

**Study Type:** Case-control study
**Sample Size:** n=?? (abstract truncated, appears to be ME/CFS vs healthy controls)

**Methods:**
- Skin temperature circadian rhythm monitoring
- Orthostatic testing (autonomic responses)
- Endothelin-1 (ET-1) biomarker measurement
- Hemodynamic measures

**Key Findings:**
- ME/CFS patients exhibit modifications in circadian rhythm of skin temperature
- Circadian rhythm alterations associated with endothelin-1 levels (p < 0.01)
- ET-1 levels associated with temperature rhythm stability and amplitude
- Self-reported symptom questionnaires correlated with ET-1 and temperature rhythm disruptions
- Suggests vascular tone dysregulation mediated by ET-1 underlies thermoregulatory abnormalities

**Quality Assessment:**
- Strength: Objective temperature rhythm measurement, biomarker correlation, links circadian disruption to vascular dysfunction
- Limitations: Sample size not clear in available information, causality not established (ET-1 as marker vs. driver)
- Journal: Int J Mol Sci (IF ~6.0, reputable)
- Certainty: 0.60 (moderate evidence, objective measures but correlation-only)

**Relevance to ME/CFS Heat/Cold Effects:**
- Explains temperature dysregulation (both heat and cold intolerance)
- Links autonomic dysfunction to thermoregulatory failure
- Provides potential biomarker (ET-1) for thermoregulatory impairment
- Supports vascular endothelial dysfunction hypothesis

### 4. Soejima et al. 2015 - Waon Therapy (Infrared Sauna)
**Citation:** Soejima Y, Munemoto T, Masuda A, Uwatoko Y, Miyata M, Tei C. Effects of Waon therapy on chronic fatigue syndrome: a pilot study. Intern Med. 2015;54(3):333-338.
**PMID:** 25748743
**DOI:** 10.2169/internalmedicine.54.3042

**Study Type:** Pilot clinical trial, pre-post design
**Sample Size:** n=10 consecutive inpatients with CFS

**Intervention:**
- Waon therapy (far-infrared dry sauna at 60°C for 15 minutes)
- Followed by 30 minutes rest under blanket outside sauna
- Frequency: Once daily, 5 days/week for 4 weeks (20 sessions total)

**Outcome Measures:**
- Primary: Perceived fatigue (numerical rating scale)
- Secondary: Pain level (NRS), mood (Profile of Mood States questionnaire), performance status (CFS-specific scale)
- Timepoints: Baseline, 2 weeks, post-treatment

**Key Findings:**
- Perceived fatigue significantly decreased after therapy (not at 2 weeks)
- Negative mood (anxiety, depression, fatigue) significantly improved after therapy
- Performance status significantly improved after therapy
- Pain levels and vigor did not change significantly
- No adverse effects reported during therapy

**Quality Assessment:**
- Strength: Clinical trial design, standardized intervention, symptom measures, documented safety (no adverse effects)
- Limitations: Very small sample (n=10), no control group, open-label, no blinding, short duration (4 weeks), no follow-up after treatment
- Journal: Internal Medicine (IF ~2.5, Japanese journal, modest impact)
- Certainty: 0.50 (pilot, no control, but clinical relevance)

**Relevance to ME/CFS Heat/Cold Effects:**
- First evidence that passive heat therapy may benefit ME/CFS without triggering PEM
- Contrasts with exercise-induced symptom exacerbation (heat alone vs. exercise+heat)
- Suggests therapeutic use of heat is possible despite heat intolerance symptoms
- Provides protocol details for thermal therapy in ME/CFS

## Gaps and Null Results

### Limited Evidence Found For:
1. **Cold intolerance prevalence in ME/CFS:** No quantitative studies found on prevalence rates
2. **Cold stress triggering PEM:** No direct evidence, only inferred from general stress intolerance
3. **TRPM3-specific thermoregulatory dysfunction:** TRPM3 papers in bib are about NK cell dysfunction, not specifically body temperature regulation
4. **Therapeutic cold exposure (cryotherapy, WBC) for ME/CFS:** Only general cryotherapy papers in registry, none ME/CFS-specific
5. **Heat shock protein (HSP70) abnormalities in ME/CFS:** Mentioned in Stanculescu 2021 review, but no primary data papers found
6. **Hyperthermia as disease trigger:** Heat intolerance documented, but no studies on heat exposure as onset event

### Contraindications Identified:
- Tipton 2017: Cold water immersion has contraindications relevant to ME/CFS (cardiovascular stress)
- Heat therapies may not be tolerable for severe ME/CFS patients (Hochecker 2025: some couldn't complete protocol)

## Synthesis and Implications

### Heat Intolerance Mechanisms
1. **Vascular dysfunction:** Endothelin-1 mediated vascular tone dysregulation (Cambras 2023)
2. **Autonomic failure:** Dysautonomia affects thermoregulatory responses (Cambras 2023, Li 2021, Baraniuk 2022)
3. **Shared pathways with heat stroke:** Gut permeability, endotoxemia, systemic inflammation (Stanculescu 2021)
4. **Circadian disruption:** Skin temperature rhythm abnormalities (Cambras 2023)

### Therapeutic Use of Heat
- **Paradox:** Heat intolerance symptoms vs. therapeutic benefit of heat
- **Possible mechanism:** Passive heat (sauna, WBH) improves oxygenation/blood flow without triggering exercise-associated PEM pathways
- **Evidence:**
  - Soejima 2015: Far-infrared sauna improves fatigue, mood, performance (n=10)
  - Hochecker 2025: WBH improves cellular autophagy and mitochondrial function (n=9)
  - Previous Waon studies (Tei group): Increased cerebral blood flow, improved fatigue
- **Safety:** No adverse effects reported in pilot studies, but severe ME/CFS may not tolerate full protocol

### Research Gaps
1. Large RCTs of thermal therapies
2. Cold exposure interventions (cryotherapy, cold showers)
3. Quantitative prevalence of heat/cold intolerance
4. Biomarkers for identifying who may benefit from vs. be harmed by thermal therapies
5. Long-term effects of repeated thermal therapy
6. Comparison of heat vs. cold modalities
