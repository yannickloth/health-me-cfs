## 1. Not All Mechanisms Are Equal

**Source:** Ch 16, Sections 1–4 (three-tier framework, trigger-capable criteria, classification table)

**Core argument:** ME/CFS research catalogues dozens of broken mechanisms. The critical question isn't *which ones are broken* but *which ones matter*. Four strict criteria distinguish root causes from amplifiers from consequences — and only root causes can initiate the disease from scratch.

**Key content:**
- The four criteria for trigger-capable root causes (precipitant sufficiency, PEM explanation, chronicity, multi-system reach)
- The three-tier classification: 4 root causes, 6 amplifiers, 5 consequences
- Why this matters: treating a consequence cannot cure the disease; treating an amplifier reduces severity without enabling recovery

**Audience hook:** Anyone who has wondered "but if X is broken, why doesn't fixing X cure it?"

## 2. Four Doors, One Disease

**Source:** Ch 16, Section 5 (entry points, final common pathway)

**Core argument:** A patient whose ME/CFS started after mono, another after a car accident, a third after extreme stress — all end up with the same symptoms. How? Because different triggers enter through different "doors" but converge on the same final common pathway: CNS energy failure + broken restoration machinery.

**Key content:**
- The four entry doors mapped to specific precipitants (viral → any door; stress → safe mode; surgery → multiple doors simultaneously; vaccination → autoimmune)
- Why clinical homogeneity despite diverse triggers
- Why within-disease heterogeneity (entry "signatures" overlay shared core)
- Why treatment responses vary (different active mechanisms per entry path)

**Audience hook:** "Why does everyone with ME/CFS look so similar despite such different onset stories?"

## 3. Load-Bearing Walls vs. Interior Walls

**Source:** Ch 16, Section 6 (load-bearing vs. secondary locks)

**Core argument:** Not all disease-maintaining mechanisms are equal. Some are load-bearing — remove them and the disease structure can collapse. Others are secondary — removing them makes patients feel better without enabling recovery. The therapeutic dilemma: the most symptomatic locks (what hurts now) may not be the load-bearing ones (what matters for recovery).

**Key content:**
- Building analogy: load-bearing walls vs. partition walls
- Predicted load-bearing locks: epigenetic consolidation, autoimmune persistence
- Predicted secondary locks: oxidative stress, sleep fragmentation, viral reactivation
- The therapeutic dilemma and why both must be addressed
- Daratumumab response pattern as evidence

**Audience hook:** "What if the treatments that make you feel better aren't the ones that will make you well?"

## 4. Root Cause ≠ Treatment Priority

**Source:** Ch 16, Section 7 (treatment implications, priority table)

**Core argument:** The most upstream mechanism isn't necessarily the best treatment target. Treatment priority = causal importance × therapeutic tractability. A highly causal mechanism you can't reach has zero treatment priority. A moderately causal mechanism you *can* treat may be the best bet.

**Key content:**
- Three reasons root cause ≠ treatment priority (trigger may be gone; most upstream = least accessible; multi-lock model predicts single-target failure)
- The 10-mechanism treatment priority ranking with rationale
- Why GPCR autoantibodies rank #1 (root cause + moderate-high tractability) while CNS energy crisis ranks #7 (highest causal importance but lowest tractability)
- Treatment sequencing strategy: high-tractability first → root-cause as available → experimental as field advances

**Audience hook:** Challenges the intuition that "find the cause, fix the cause" is always the right strategy

## 5. Why Single-Target Treatments Keep Failing in ME/CFS

**Source:** Ch 16, Sections 3–4 + 7 (multi-lock trap, treatment implications)

**Core argument:** ME/CFS is maintained by multiple self-reinforcing mechanisms ("locks") that compensate for each other. Removing one lock — even an important one — leaves the others in place to sustain the disease. This is why every promising single-target treatment has disappointed: the disease architecture is inherently combinatorial.

**Key content:**
- The multi-lock trap concept
- Why rituxamab showed initial promise but failed in RCT (works for ~20% neurovascular subtype, diluted in unselected population)
- Why NAD+ helps but doesn't cure (amplifier, not root cause)
- The combinatorial treatment imperative
- Why disease duration predicts prognosis (epigenetic consolidation deepens the trap)

**Audience hook:** Directly addresses the frustration of "this treatment was supposed to be the one"

## 6. Can Math Tell Us What Causes ME/CFS?

**Source:** Ch 33, Sections 1–5 (methodology, trigger sufficiency analysis)

**Core argument:** Verbal reasoning about disease mechanisms can tell us what's plausible, but not what's *sufficient*. Formal mathematical models — 67 coupled differential equations — can test whether a single mechanism, perturbed alone, can push the body from health into disease.

**Key content:**
- The three formal tools: trigger sufficiency, sensitivity analysis, lock removal
- Results: Complex I reduction, safe mode activation, and GPCR autoantibodies are each independently trigger-sufficient; TRPM3 is conditional (predisposing but not sufficient alone)
- The cascade sequences: what breaks first, second, third after each trigger
- Specific thresholds (Complex I must drop below ~65% of normal)

**Audience hook:** "What if we could use math to tell root causes from red herrings?"

## 7. The Multi-Hit Model: Why Only Some People Get ME/CFS After Infection

**Source:** Ch 33, Section 5.6 (multi-hit scenarios, trigger interactions)

**Core argument:** Only a fraction of people who get COVID, mono, or flu develop ME/CFS. Why? The mathematical model shows that sub-threshold vulnerabilities (genetic, prior autoimmunity, TRPM3 variants) can bring someone close to the disease threshold, so that a routine infection provides the final push. ME/CFS onset is often a two-hit (or three-hit) event.

**Key content:**
- The multi-hit equation: how sub-threshold perturbations combine
- All pairwise interactions are synergistic (positive γ values)
- Strongest synergy: Complex I + autoantibodies (both bottleneck oxygen-dependent ATP)
- Why TRPM3 dysfunction is the paradigmatic predisposing factor (lowers barrier by ~23%)
- Epidemiological prediction: fraction developing ME/CFS = fraction with pre-existing sub-threshold perturbations

**Audience hook:** "Why did I get ME/CFS from COVID when my coworker who had the same virus was fine in a week?"

## 8. Why Severe ME/CFS Resists Treatment (and What the Math Says We Need)

**Source:** Ch 33, Section 8 (minimum intervention sets)

**Core argument:** The mathematical model identifies a "severe/locked" disease state where no single treatment — no matter how effective in milder subtypes — can achieve recovery. The minimum requirement: at least two simultaneous interventions, one targeting energy production and one targeting epigenetic consolidation. This predicts that severe ME/CFS needs combination therapy, and that epigenetic reversal is the rate-limiting step.

**Key content:**
- No single-parameter restoration rescues the severe attractor (Table)
- Only two successful pairs, both include epigenetic reversal
- Why epigenetic reversal is rate-limiting (the "brake" that prevents other treatments from taking full effect)
- Clinical prediction: even aggressive mitochondrial or immune therapy will fail in severe patients unless accompanied by epigenetic intervention
- The early intervention imperative: treat before epigenetic consolidation deepens

**Audience hook:** Speaks directly to severe patients and their carers who have tried everything

## 9. An Early Treatment Window for ME/CFS? The Epigenetic Clock

**Source:** Ch 33, Section 9.3 (prediction 3: early intervention window)

**Core argument:** The model predicts a critical treatment window of approximately 18–24 months after onset. Before this threshold, single-target therapies can work. After it, epigenetic consolidation has deepened the disease trap so much that response rates drop 2–3×. This isn't metaphor — it's a quantifiable sigmoidal decline in treatment response probability.

**Key content:**
- The epigenetic consolidation curve (exponential approach to locked state)
- The formal response decay equation
- Predicted 2–3× response rate difference before vs. after the window
- Why illness duration is the strongest predictor of prognosis (not initial severity)
- The clinical urgency: early diagnosis and treatment aren't just "nice to have" — they may be the difference between recovery and chronicity

**Audience hook:** "Is there a clock ticking on your chance of recovery?"

## 10. Critical Slowing Down: Could Wearables Predict ME/CFS Relapses?

**Source:** Ch 33, Section 9.4 (prediction 4: critical slowing down)

**Core argument:** Bifurcation theory predicts that before major transitions — whether improvement or worsening — the body's physiological signals should show specific mathematical signatures: rising variance and rising autocorrelation. These "critical slowing down" signals could potentially be detected by wearables (HRV monitoring) weeks before a crash or a recovery breakthrough.

**Key content:**
- The bifurcation theory behind critical slowing down
- The specific prediction: >2σ increase in 7-day rolling HRV variance + autocorrelation >0.7 precedes transitions by 2–4 weeks
- The concept of "recovery distance" — measuring how close a patient is to the tipping point
- Required: continuous HRV monitoring in ≥50 patients over ≥12 months
- The vision: an early warning system for crashes

**Audience hook:** The wearable health tech angle makes this accessible and forward-looking

## 11. ME/CFS Is Not One Disease: A Mathematical Case for Subtyping

**Source:** Ch 33, Sections 7–8 (lock necessity matrix, treatment priority tables)

**Core argument:** The mathematical model identifies four distinct disease "attractors" (subtypes), each maintained by a different necessary lock and requiring a different primary treatment. Immune-dominant responds to immune modulation. Metabolic-dominant responds to mitochondrial support. Neurovascular-dominant responds to autoantibody clearance. Severe/locked responds to nothing alone. Treating ME/CFS as one disease guarantees that most patients in any trial will receive the wrong treatment.

**Key content:**
- The four subtypes and their defining locks
- Lock necessity matrix: each subtype has a specific key
- Treatment priority tables per subtype (different #1 target for each)
- Why rituximab RCT failed (effective for ~20%, null result in unselected population)
- Why LDN shows ~30–50% response (matches predicted immune-dominant prevalence)
- The case for subtype-stratified trials

**Audience hook:** "What if the reason your treatment didn't work is that you have a different version of ME/CFS than the person it helped?"
