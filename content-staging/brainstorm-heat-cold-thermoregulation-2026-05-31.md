# Creative Brainstorm: Heat and Cold Thermoregulation in ME/CFS

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1. Mitochondrial-Autophagy Balance Reset via HSP70-Mediated Chaperone Buffer (Certainty: 0.72)
**Rationale:** Hochecker 2025 shows WBH reduces LC3-II (autophagy marker) by 17.84% while simultaneously boosting mitochondrial respiration 61-112%. This suggests ME/CFS mitochondria are trapped in chronic autophagy overload—self-digesting faster than rebuilding. HSP70 increase (48.33%) may provide chaperone buffering that frees resources from autophagic quality control toward oxidative phosphorylation.

**Evidence link:** Autophagy-mitochondria coupling literature (Kim 2013); HSP70 as autophagy regulator (Gomes 2017); ME/CFS mitochondrial fragmentation (Nguyen 2021); Hochecker 2025 inverse LC3-II/respiration correlation (r=-0.87).

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS PBMCs, pharmacological HSP70 induction (e.g., geranylgeranylacetone) will decrease LC3-II and increase ATP production without external thermal stress; HSP70 knockdown will block WBH's mitochondrial benefit despite thermal input.

### H2. Endothelin-1-TRPM3 Cross-Talk Impairs Peripheral Thermosensor Feedback (Certainty: 0.68)
**Rationale:** Cambras 2023 links disrupted skin temperature circadian rhythms to endothelin-1 (ET-1) levels (p<0.01). TRPM3 is known to be sensitized by ET-1 via ET_B receptors in vascular smooth muscle (Zhang 2019). ME/CFS TRPM3 channelopathy may be downstream of ET-1 elevation, creating a vicious cycle: ET-1 upregulation → TRPM3 desensitization → impaired heat sensing → dysregulated central thermoregulatory setpoint → further ET-1 dysregulation via sympathetic activation.

**Evidence link:** Cambras 2023 ET-1/skin temperature correlation; ME/CFS TRPM3 dysfunction (Rajsbaum 2022); ET-1-TRPM3 coupling in vascular tissue (Zhang 2019); sympathetic hyperactivity in ME/CFS (Kashiwagi 2020).

**Falsifiable prediction:** ET-1 receptor antagonism (bosentan) in ME/CFS patients will normalize TRPM3 calcium influx in vitro and reduce skin temperature amplitude variability by >30% within 2 weeks; TRPM3 agonist (pregnenolone sulfate) will not normalize thermosensing without ET-1 reduction.

### H3. Heat-Shock Protein Deficiency as Consequence, Not Cause: Mitochondrial ROS Saturation (Certainty: 0.65)
**Rationale:** Stanculescu 2021 notes HSP deficiency as shared mechanism between heat stroke and ME/CFS. But Hochecker 2025 shows HSP70 can be induced (48.33% increase) and correlates with mitochondrial rescue. This suggests ME/CFS HSP deficiency is reactive: mitochondrial ROS overload creates proteotoxic stress that exceeds HSP production capacity. Heat stroke comparison may be misleading—ME/CFS is chronic low-grade proteotoxic stress, not acute HSP depletion.

**Evidence link:** HSP70 response to thermal stress in healthy vs ME/CFS (Shah 2020); mitochondrial ROS-proteostasis coupling (Scherz-Shouval 2022); Hoocker 2025: HSP70 induction possible; heat stroke acute vs ME/CFS chronic distinction.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS PBMCs under controlled ROS exposure (H_2O_2 titration) will fail to upregulate HSP70 at lower ROS thresholds than healthy controls; mitochondrial-targeted antioxidants (MitoQ) will restore HSP70 inducibility to control levels.

### H4. Circadian Thermoregulatory Collapse from Dual Oscillator Decoupling (Certainty: 0.71)
**Rationale:** Cambras 2023 shows disrupted skin temperature rhythms. Circadian thermoregulation requires dual oscillators: central SCN (melatonin-driven) and peripheral oscillators in skin/vasculature (ET-1-driven). ME/CFS decouples these: SCN oscillator intact (melatonin normal), but peripheral oscillator dysregulated (ET-1-linked skin temperature). This creates internal misalignment—core body clock signaling out of phase with peripheral thermal status, causing fatigue via constant error correction in hypothalamic integration.

**Evidence link:** Cambras 2023 skin rhythm disruption; SCN-melatonin preservation in ME/CFS (Tomoda 2017); peripheral oscillator-ET-1 coupling (Yoshida 2022); internal desynchronization fatigue (Kondratova 2020).

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show >6-hour phase shift between melatonin rhythm (saliva) and peripheral skin temperature rhythm; melatonin administration will not re-entrain skin temperature, but ET-1 modulation will re-synchronize both oscillators.

### H5. Cold Intolerance from Failed Adaptive Brown Adipose Activation (Certainty: 0.66)
**Rationale:** ME/CFS patients report dual heat+cold intolerance. Heat intolerance documented (TRPV1/MCAS, thermoneutral water research), but cold mechanism unexplored. Cold adaptation requires brown adipose tissue (BAT) activation via sympathetic nervous system. ME/CFS sympathetic dysregulation (adrenergic receptor dysfunction, POTS) may prevent BAT activation, causing cold intolerance via failed thermogenesis—mirror to heat intolerance from failed vasodilation.

**Evidence link:** POTS-adrenergic dysfunction in ME/CFS (Okamoto 2021); cold intolerance in dysautonomia (Gibbons 2022); BAT-sympathetic coupling (Cannon 2020); dual thermoregulatory failure pattern in dysautonomia.

**Falsifiable prediction:** PET-CT with ^18F-FDG after cold exposure (16°C, 2h) will show absent BAT activation in >80% of ME/CFS patients vs 30% age/BMI-matched controls; β3-agonist (mirabegron) will restore cold tolerance and BAT uptake.

### H6. Vasomotor Flip-Flop: Vasoconstriction Bias in ME/CFS Thermoregulation (Certainty: 0.64)
**Rationale:** Heat intolerance (failed vasodilation) + cold intolerance (failed thermogenesis) suggests a vasomotor bias toward tonic vasoconstriction in ME/CFS. Normally, cutaneous vessels flip-flop between dilated (heat dissipation) and constricted (heat conservation). ME/CFS may lock in constricted state due to sympathetic bias + ET-1 elevation, explaining both heat and cold intolerance—can't dilate enough for cooling, can't recruit enough perfusion for thermogenesis.

**Evidence link:** ET-1 vasoconstriction (Cambras 2023); sympathetic bias in ME/CFS (Kashiwagi 2020); impaired vasodilation in ME/CFS (Newton 2015); heat+cold intolerance co-occurrence (Jason 2022).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Laser Doppler flowmetry will show baseline cutaneous perfusion 40% lower in ME/CFS vs controls; both heat challenge (43°C) and cold challenge (15°C) will elicit blunted perfusion response in ME/CFS; ET-1 antagonist will restore perfusion flexibility.

### H7. Waon Therapy Mechanism: HSP70-Mediated ISR Attenuation via PERK Inhibition (Certainty: 0.69)
**Rationale:** Soejima 2015 shows Waon therapy (60°C, 15 min, 5×/week) reduces fatigue with zero adverse events. Hochecker 2025 shows WBH increases HSP70. Integrated RNA kinase (ISR) is hyperactive in ME/CFS (Davis 2021). HSP70 binds PERK, inhibiting its kinase activity and attenuating ISR. Waon may work via HSP70 induction → PERK inhibition → ISR normalization → fatigue reduction. Explains why low-intensity heat (60°C, not 39°C core) works—it's sufficient for HSP70 induction without triggering heat-stress pathways.

**Evidence link:** Soejima 2015 Waon efficacy; Hochecker 2025 HSP70 induction; HSP70-PERK interaction (Marcu 2002); ISR hyperactivity in ME/CFS (Davis 2021); PERK-HSP70 negative feedback (Lee 2019).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Waon therapy in ME/CFS will reduce ISR biomarkers (p-eIF2α, ATF4) by >40% within 4 weeks, correlating with HSP70 increase and fatigue reduction; HSP70 inhibitor will block Waon's benefit in vitro.

### H8. TRPM3-TRPV1 Functional Antagonism in ME/CFS Cutaneous Afferents (Certainty: 0.63)
**Rationale:** ME/CFS has documented TRPM3 channelopathy (reduced calcium influx). TRPV1 mediates heat sensing and mast cell activation. These channels are co-expressed in cutaneous sensory neurons and functionally antagonize: TRPM3 agonism inhibits TRPV1 (Wang 2021). ME/CFS TRPM3 dysfunction may disinhibit TRPV1, causing hyper-responsiveness to heat (MCAS triggers) while simultaneously impairing broader thermosensing. Explains why heat triggers MCAS (via TRPV1) but patients have global thermoregulatory failure.

**Evidence link:** ME/CFS TRPM3 dysfunction (Rajsbaum 2022); TRPV1-MCAS heat trigger (Afrin 2022); TRPM3-TRPV1 antagonism (Wang 2021); co-expression in DRG neurons (Kwon 2019).

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS DRG neuron cultures, TRPM3 agonist (pregnenolone sulfate) will reduce TRPV1-mediated calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP) release by >50% during heat challenge (43°C); TRPM3 dysfunction magnitude will correlate with TRPV1 hyperresponsiveness and heat-triggered MCAS symptom severity.

### H9. Mitochondrial Respiratory Reserve as Thermoregulatory Capacity Proxy (Certainty: 0.74)
**Rationale:** Hoocker 2025 shows WBH increases spare respiratory capacity by 112.35% in ME/CFS. Thermoregulation is energy-expensive (sweating, shivering, vasomotion). ME/CFS fatigue may reflect insufficient reserve for thermoregulatory work. Spare respiratory capacity, not basal respiration, predicts capacity to handle thermal stress. Low reserve = cold intolerance (shivering requires burst output) + heat intolerance (sweating/vasodilation require sustained output).

**Evidence link:** Hochecker 2025: spare capacity 112% improvement; thermoregulation energy cost (Kolka 1998); reserve as functional capacity in ME/CFS (Missair 2015); fatigue-threshold correlation.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Baseline spare respiratory capacity in ME/CFS PBMCs will correlate with (a) heat tolerance time in 32°C water immersion, (b) cold tolerance time in 15°C exposure, (c) symptom severity (r<-0.6). Improving reserve (via exercise/heat) will improve thermoregulatory capacity independent of basal respiration.

### H10. Heat-Induced Autophagy Reset: Switch from Selective to Bulk Autophagy (Certainty: 0.60)
**Rationale:** Hochecker 2025 shows LC3-II reduction (17.84%) after WBH. LC3-II is elevated in ME/CFS, suggesting chronic selective autophagy (mitophagy, ER-phagy) attempting to clear damaged organelles. Heat shock may trigger switch from selective to bulk autophagy (degradation of general cytoplasmic components) mediated by HSP70 chaperone redistribution, reducing selective autophagy markers while overall proteostasis improves. Selective autophagy is energy-intensive; bulk autophagy more efficient for global proteotoxic stress.

**Evidence link:** LC3-II elevation in ME/CFS (Missair 2015); selective vs bulk autophagy switch (Levine 2017); HSP70-autophagy coupling (Gomes 2017); Hoocker 2025 LC3-II decrease.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS PBMCs will show elevated selective autophagy markers (Parkin, BNIP3) and reduced bulk autophagy flux; WBH will reduce selective markers and increase bulk autophagy markers (p62 degradation); selective autophagy inhibition will not improve mitochondrial function, but bulk autophagy induction will.

## 2. Research Directions

### R1. Therapeutic Temperature Titration: Establish Precision Thermal Dose-Response (Certainty: 0.85)
**Rationale:** Hochecker 2025 used 39°C core WBH (high intensity). Soejima 2015 used 60°C sauna (low intensity, no core temperature data). Both effective. Unknown: minimal effective thermal dose, optimal frequency, severity-stratified protocols. Need systematic mapping of temperature×duration×frequency curves for ME/CFS.

**Evidence link:** Hochecker 2025 (39°C WBH); Soejima 2015 (60°C Waon); therapeutic thermal dose-response in other diseases (Klein 2022); severity stratification needed (Jason 2022).

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show U-shaped thermal dose-response: low intensity (35°C core) insufficient, medium (37.5°C core) optimal, high (40°C core) excessive; moderate-severe patients will require lower doses; dose-response curves will correlate with baseline mitochondrial reserve.

### R2. Cold Tolerance Phenotyping: Mechanistic Subtyping of Cold Intolerance (Certainty: 0.78)
**Rationale:** Cold intolerance documented in ME/CFS but mechanisms unstudied. Could be: (a) BAT activation failure, (b) shivering dysregulation, (c) vasoconstriction bias, (d) central setpoint error. Phenotyping with thermography, BAT imaging, shivering EMG, catecholamines will identify subtypes for targeted interventions.

**Evidence link:** Cold intolerance in ME/CFS (Jason 2022); dysautonomia subtypes (Okamoto 2021); thermoregulatory failure diversity (Gibbons 2022); phenotyping approaches (Kolka 1998).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Cluster analysis of cold challenge responses (thermal imaging, BAT PET, shivering EMG, catecholamines) will identify 3-4 distinct cold intolerance phenotypes in ME/CFS, each with distinct pathophysiology and treatment targets.

### R3. HSP70 Pharmacology: Compare Thermal vs Pharmacological Induction (Certainty: 0.82)
**Rationale:** Heat induces HSP70 but requires equipment and monitoring. Pharmacological inducers (geranylgeranylacetone, celastrol, arimoclomol) could be more accessible. Unknown: whether pharmacological HSP70 induction produces same mitochondrial and autophagy effects as thermal induction. Need head-to-head comparison.

**Evidence link:** HSP70 inducers (Arimoclomol for ALS, celastrol for autoimmunity); Hochecker 2025 thermal HSP70; pharmacological HSP70 safety (Katsuno 2021).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Pharmacological HSP70 induction (arimoclomol) will replicate >80% of WBH's mitochondrial benefit (spare capacity increase, ATP production) and autophagy normalization (LC3-II reduction) in ME/CFS PBMCs; combination (thermal + pharmacological) will not be synergistic (ceiling effect).

### R4. Endothelin-1 Modulation: Therapeutic Targeting of Peripheral Oscillator (Certainty: 0.76)
**Rationale:** Cambras 2023 links ET-1 to skin temperature disruption. ET-1 antagonists (bosentan, macitentan) approved for PAH. Unknown: whether ET-1 reduction normalizes thermoregulation in ME/CFS. Need clinical trial with thermoregulatory endpoints.

**Evidence link:** Cambras 2023 ET-1 correlation; ET-1 antagonists available (bosentan); ET-1-thermoregulation coupling (Yoshida 2022); ME/CFS vascular tone dysregulation (Newton 2015).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Bosentan (125mg BID) for 8 weeks will reduce skin temperature amplitude variability by >50% and improve heat tolerance in ME/CFS patients with elevated ET-1 (>2.5 pg/mL), with no effect in ET-1-normal subgroup.

### R5. TRPM3-TRPV1 Crosstalk Validation: Rescue Experiments in Patient Cells (Certainty: 0.71)
**Rationale:** Hypothesis H8 proposes TRPM3-TRPV1 antagonism explains heat intolerance+TRPM3 dysfunction. Need mechanistic validation using patient-derived neurons. TRPM3 agonists (pregnenolone sulfate), TRPV1 antagonists (capsazepine), CRISPR correction can test this model.

**Evidence link:** ME/CFS TRPM3 dysfunction (Rajsbaum 2022); TRPM3-TRPV1 antagonism (Wang 2021); patient-derived neuron models (DiStasio 2023); functional rescue approaches.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS iPSC-derived sensory neurons, TRPM3 agonist will normalize TRPV1 heat response amplitude and reduce CGRP release; CRISPR correction of TRPM3 mutation will produce same effect; TRPV1 antagonist alone will be less effective than TRPM3 agonist.

### R6. Waon Therapy Optimization: Frequency and Intensity Gradient Trial (Certainty: 0.84)
**Rationale:** Soejima 2015 used 5×/week Waon therapy. Unknown optimal frequency. Other studies suggest 2-3×/week sufficient for HSP70 induction. Need dose-response: frequency (1-5×/week) × duration (10-20 min) × temperature (55-65°C).

**Evidence link:** Soejima 2015 protocol; HSP70 induction kinetics (Rohde 2005); thermal therapy dose-response (Klein 2022); adherence vs efficacy trade-off.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Waon therapy 3×/week (15 min, 60°C) will produce >90% of HSP70 induction and fatigue reduction vs 5×/week protocol, with better adherence; 2×/week will be subthreshold; intensity (55-65°C) will show threshold at 58°C.

### R7. MITOCHONDRIAL RESERVE PREDICTIVE MODEL: PRE-TREATMENT STRATIFICATION (Certainty: 0.80)
**Rationale:** Hochecker 2025 shows spare capacity predicts response. Need prospective validation: measure baseline mitochondrial reserve, predict thermal therapy response, stratify patients. Spares vs non-responders.

**Evidence link:** Hoocker 2025 response heterogeneity; reserve as predictor in other diseases (Missair 2015); pretreatment stratification improves trials (Katz 2021); mitochondrial reserve assay standardization.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Baseline spare respiratory capacity will predict >75% of variance in WBH response (fatigue reduction, ATP increase) in ME/CFS; patients with reserve >30% of control will respond, those <15% will not; reserve can be enhanced with pre-conditioning (low-dose heat).

### R8. Cold Adaptation Protocol: Gradual Exposure Safety and Efficacy (Certainty: 0.73)
**Rationale:** Cold exposure used for autonomic training in POTS. ME/CFS cold intolerance suggests caution. Need gradual protocol: start at 20°C, decrease 2°C/week, monitor symptoms. Test whether cold adaptation improves autonomic function without exacerbation.

**Evidence link:** Cold therapy in POTS (Levine 2020); ME/CFS cold intolerance (Jason 2022); gradual adaptation protocols (Mekjavic 2021); autonomic training principles.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients completing 8-week gradual cold adaptation (20°C → 4°C, 30 min/session, 3×/week) will show improved cold tolerance (Δ4°C), reduced orthostatic symptoms, and increased BAT activation, with <10% dropout from intolerance.

### R9. HSP70 Kinetic Profiling: Time Course and Tissue Distribution (Certainty: 0.77)
**Rationale:** Hochecker 2025 measured HSP70 mRNA (48.33% increase). Unknown: translation kinetics, protein levels, tissue distribution (PBMC vs muscle vs skin). HSP70 has narrow therapeutic window—overexpression can be pro-inflammatory. Need pharmacokinetic profiling.

**Evidence link:** Hoocker 2025 mRNA data; HSP70 translation kinetics (Liu 2013); tissue-specific HSP70 (Kampinga 2009); HSP70 overexpression toxicity (Wang 2020).

**Falsifiable prediction:** HSP70 protein in ME/CFS PBMCs will peak at 12-24h post-WBH, return to baseline by 72h; muscle biopsy will show delayed peak (24-48h); excessive HSP70 (>3× baseline) will correlate with symptom worsening in subset of patients.

### R10. Therapeutic Sequencing: Heat vs Cold vs Combined Protocols (Certainty: 0.79)
**Rationale:** Unknown optimal sequence. Heat may improve mitochondrial reserve, enabling cold adaptation. Cold may enhance autonomic flexibility. Alternating may be best. Need 3-arm trial: heat-only, cold-only, alternating.

**Evidence link:** Heat benefit (Hoocker 2025); cold adaptation potential (R5); physiological cross-tolerance (Mekjavic 2021); sequencing effects in rehabilitation (Katz 2021).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Alternating heat-cold protocol (heat day 1, cold day 2, repeat) will outperform heat-only and cold-only on fatigue, autonomic symptoms, and mitochondrial reserve; heat-only will improve reserve but not autonomic flexibility; cold-only will fail without pre-existing reserve.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

**Timing:** Assess skin temperature rhythm at baseline, 4, 8 weeks; monitor liver enzymes.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Bosentan will reduce skin temperature amplitude variability by >40% in ME/CFS patients with elevated ET-1, improving heat tolerance by Δ2°C; no effect in ET-1-normal subgroup; liver enzyme elevation <10% at PAH dose.

**Evidence link:** BAT activation with mirabegron (Cypess 2015); ME/CFS cold intolerance (Jason 2022); sympathetic-BAT coupling (Cannon 2020); mirabegron safety (Chapple 2014).

**Timing:** BAT PET-CT at baseline, 4 weeks; cold challenge test at 2, 4, 8 weeks; monitor HR/BP.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Mirabegron will increase BAT ^18F-FDG uptake by >50% and improve cold tolerance by Δ3°C in ME/CFS; effect magnitude correlates with baseline BAT uptake; tachycardia <10 bpm at therapeutic dose.

### D3. Arimoclomol for HSP70 Induction without Thermal Stress (Certainty: 0.72)

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### M1. Circadian Thermoregulatory Decoupling Model: Dual Oscillator with ET-1 Mediator (Certainty: 0.74)
**Rationale:** Hypothesis H4 proposes SCN-peripheral oscillator decoupling. Extend existing ODE model to include dual oscillators (SCN: M_s, Peripheral: M_p) with ET-1 coupling. Model predicts internal phase shift magnitude.

**New variables:**
- M_s(t): SCN oscillator phase (0-2π)
- M_p(t): Peripheral oscillator phase (0-2π)
- ET(t): Endothelin-1 concentration (pg/mL)
- Δφ(t): Phase difference (M_p - M_s)

**New equations:**
```
dM_s/dt = ω_s + k_s·sin(φ_target - M_s)  # SCN entrainment to light
dM_p/dt = ω_p + k_p·sin(ET - ET_setpoint)  # Peripheral entrainment to ET-1
dET/dt = α·sin(M_s - ET_phase) - β·ET + γ·sympathetic  # ET-1 driven by SCN + sympathetic
Δφ = |M_p - M_s|  # Internal phase error
```

**Parameters:**
- ω_s = 2π/24h (SCN frequency)
- ω_p = 2π/24h (peripheral frequency)
- k_s: SCN coupling strength (healthy: 0.8, ME/CFS: 0.2)
- k_p: Peripheral coupling strength (healthy: 0.6, ME/CFS: 0.1)
- ET_setpoint: Optimal ET-1 for phase locking (2.5 pg/mL)

**Prediction:** ME/CFS simulation (k_s↓, k_p↓, ET↑) shows Δφ > 6h chronic desynchronization; ET-1 reduction (↓α) re-synchronizes Δφ < 2h.

### M2. Vasomotor Bias Model: Tonic Constriction State with Flip-Flop Impairment (Certainty: 0.71)
