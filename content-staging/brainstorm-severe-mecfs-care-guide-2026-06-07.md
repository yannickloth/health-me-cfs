## Category 1: Novel Care Protocol Hypotheses

### Idea 1.1: Metabolic Shutdown Conservation Reflex — Pathological State vs Adaptive Response

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Severe ME/CFS may represent an evolutionarily conserved "metabolic shutdown" program (analogous to hibernation torpor, neonatal energy conservation, or the illness response) that has become pathologically locked on. The patient's body is not merely damaged — it is actively maintaining a low-metabolic state through descending inhibition of muscular activity, sensory gating, and metabolic suppression. This reframes the bedbound state from "too weak to move" to "neural circuits actively preventing movement to conserve energy." Therapeutic implication: the lock-on mechanism (candidate: hypothalamic A2 noradrenergic circuit, adenosine A1 receptor overactivity, or thyroid hormone deiodinase downregulation) may be pharmacologically disruptable without exceeding metabolic capacity.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson rolling PEM suggests incomplete metabolic recovery between cycles. Hibernation mammals actively suppress metabolism by 95% via adenosine A1 receptor activation (Jinka 2011). Neonatal mammals have an active energy-conservation state (hypoxic torpor). ME/CFS patients show reduced cerebral blood flow and hypometabolism on PET (Martini 2023) — consistent with active metabolic suppression, not just passive failure. The concept that ME/CFS is a stuck illness response (instead of purely a metabolic deficit) is raised in the selective energy dysfunction framework (Chapter 14j) but not formalized as a testable conservation-reflex hypothesis.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** If the locked-on conservation reflex hypothesis is correct, then (a) severe ME/CFS patients will show elevated adenosine levels or A1 receptor expression in CSF compared to matched controls, (b) adenosine A1 receptor antagonists (caffeine, theophylline at low doses) will transiently increase spontaneous voluntary movement without triggering PEM, and (c) thyroid hormone profiles will show T4→T3 conversion failure consistent with hibernation-like euthyroid sick syndrome (low T3, reverse T3 elevated), not hypothyroidism per se.

### Idea 1.2: Capillary No-Reflow State in Bedbound Patients

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Prolonged bedrest causes capillary compression in dependent tissues (sacrum, heels, posterior calves) that may persist even after position change, producing microvascular "no-reflow" zones — capillary beds that remain collapsed despite restored arterial inflow. These zones create local hypoxia → lactate accumulation → nociceptor activation → autonomic stress signal → systemic metabolic cost. The no-reflow phenomenon is driven by pericapillary edema, endothelial swelling, and leukocyte adhesion that prevent capillary reopening after external pressure is released. In severe ME/CFS (already compromised microcirculation from low cardiac output, blood volume depletion, and potential microclot pathology), the no-reflow threshold may be crossed with minimal pressure, meaning standard turning schedules may fail to restore capillary patency.
- **Evidence link:** Capillary no-reflow is documented in stroke (Ames 1968), myocardial infarction (Kloner 1974), and pressure injury formation (Gefen 2020). Hermisson identifies repositioning as a care activity but does not consider whether turns restore perfusion adequately. ME/CFS shows nailfold capillaroscopy abnormalities (Barth 2020) and reduced microcirculatory flow (Sureda 2018). The no-reflow threshold (pressure × duration product) may be pathologically low in severe ME/CFS.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Using near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) to monitor tissue oxygen saturation (StO₂) during and after turning: in severe ME/CFS patients, StO₂ over sacral tissues will require ≥30 minutes post-turn to return to pre-compression baseline (vs. ≤5 minutes in age-matched bedrest controls), indicating no-reflow. The time-to-reperfusion will correlate with PEM severity scores.

### Idea 1.3: Anti-Gravity Muscle Energy Taxation in Supine Patients

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Even lying supine requires postural muscle tone — the anti-gravity muscles (iliopsoas, paraspinals, rectus abdominis, sternocleidomastoid) maintain subtle contraction to stabilize the body against gravity's rotational forces. The minimal muscle tension required to maintain "supine neutral" position is non-zero and may represent a continuous baseline ATP demand that severe patients cannot meet. If supine postural tone consumes 5-10% of resting metabolic rate in healthy adults, in a patient whose total ATP production is <30% of normal, this postural overhead becomes a proportionally massive drain — potentially exceeding 30% of available energy, leaving near-zero for any other function.
- **Evidence link:** EMG studies show continuous low-grade postural muscle activation even in supine position (e.g., lumbar paraspinals, cervical flexors). Zero-gravity studies (NASA) show that complete unloading (neutral body posture in microgravity) reduces muscle activation to near-zero — suggesting even a "resting" bedbound patient is expending energy on gravity resistance. ME/CFS patients report that "even lying still is exhausting." Isolated muscle studies show reduced ATP resynthesis rates (Pietrangelo 2018). Passive positioning devices (e.g., beanbag beds, zero-gravity recliners) may reduce this hidden energy cost.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Transferring a very-severe ME/CFS patient from a standard bed (flat mattress, one pillow) to a full-body zero-gravity positioning system (head elevated 15°, knees elevated 30°, full body conformity, reducing antigravity muscle activation by ≥60% as measured by surface EMG) will reduce resting heart rate by ≥5 bpm and improve symptom scores (≥15% reduction in "body heaviness" VAS) within 48 hours, without any change in care load.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### Idea 2.1: The "Zero Day" A/B Care Schedule Trial

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Current care assumes daily care is mandatory. If even minimal daily care (oral care, hydration, repositioning) triggers cumulative PEM that prevents recovery, then skipping a full day of ALL non-emergency care (a "zero day") every 2-3 days may allow longer recovery blocks that reduce rolling PEM. The trial compares standard daily minimal care vs. a compressed schedule: full care on Day A (all daily needs met), no care on Day B except emergency (hydration PO/IV if patient cannot self-drink, medication via longest-acting formulations), repeat cycle.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson's rolling PEM concept predicts recovery requires crash-free intervals of minimum duration. In critical care, "sedation vacations" show that periodic withdrawal of interventions can improve outcomes (Kress 2000). No study has tested care-free days in ME/CFS. Analogous to intermittent fasting protocols where periodic abstinence from metabolic work triggers repair.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Over a 4-week crossover trial, severe ME/CFS patients on an A/B schedule (24h full care alternating with 24h minimal care) will show (a) ≥30% reduction in total weekly crash hours, (b) no increase in medical complications (dehydration, UTI, pressure injury), and (c) improved caregiver well-being scores compared to continuous daily care.

### Idea 2.2: Second-by-Second Symptom Kinetics Micro-Longitudinal Study

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Standard ME/CFS research uses daily or weekly symptom diaries — missing the rapid dynamics that may be most informative. In severe bedbound patients, continuous digital symptom logging (via a single-button interface: press once per minute to rate "suffering right now" on a 1-10 scale that resets each minute) could generate a high-resolution time series of symptom fluctuation. This could reveal: diurnal patterns invisible to daily diaries, immediate effects of specific sensory inputs, micro-recovery slopes, and prodromal signatures of impending PEM (e.g., increasing minute-to-minute variability 2-4h before crash onset).
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson lacks high-resolution symptom tracking. Continuous monitoring in epilepsy (seizure prediction) and migraine (premonitory phase) has revealed much faster dynamics than previously appreciated. Ecological momentary assessment (EMA) in ME/CFS showed feasibility but at hourly resolution (Junghaenel 2022). One-minute resolution has never been attempted. The 1-minute data point requires ≤3 seconds of cognitive load — potentially feasible even for very-severe patients with minimal communication capacity.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** One-minute resolution symptom tracking over 7 days in severe ME/CFS (n=10, single-button interface) will reveal: (a) ultradian symptom rhythms (period 60-120 min) not detectable by hourly sampling, (b) a statistically significant increase in symptom score variability beginning ≥2 hours before caregiver-identified crash onset (pre-PEM prodrome), and (c) that the within-day symptom range (peak minus trough) is ≥3× larger than between-day mean symptom variation, meaning daily diaries wash out the clinically relevant signal.

### Idea 2.3: Caregiver-Patient Physiologic Coupling Study

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Caregivers and severe ME/CFS patients are a physiological dyad — caregiver anxiety increases patient heart rate even without overt physical contact (via voice tone, respiratory patterns, ambient tension). A study simultaneously measuring caregiver and patient HR, HRV, and skin conductance during care activities could quantify: (a) the degree of physiologic synchrony at baseline, (b) whether caregiver stress (high HR, low HRV) predicts patient destabilization within the same care session, and (c) whether caregiver biofeedback training (to reduce own physiologic arousal) improves patient outcomes.
- **Evidence link:** Physiologic synchrony between caregiver and patient is documented in dementia (Robinson 2009) and parent-infant dyads (Feldman 2007). HRV coherence between therapist and patient predicts therapeutic alliance (Ardito 2015). Hermisson identifies caregiver anxiety as a risk factor but does not propose a mechanism for patient transmission. This would be the first study of physiologic coupling in ME/CFS caregiving.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** During care procedures (assisted feeding, repositioning), patient heart rate will show significant cross-correlation with caregiver heart rate at lags of 0-5 seconds (r ≥0.30), indicating real-time physiologic transmission. In weeks where caregiver shows low HRV (high stress), the patient will show (a) higher within-care-session peak HR and (b) slower post-care HR recovery (≥2 min slower return to baseline) compared to weeks where caregiver HRV is normal.

### Idea 2.4: Silent Room Acoustic Environment Characterization

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The dark silence that severe patients endure is acoustically complex — ambient sounds (refrigerator hum, ventilation, distant traffic, footsteps, water pipes) that caregivers do not notice may be perceptible and distressing to patients in sensory-deprived states, where the auditory system becomes hypersensitive (sensory gain adaptation). Characterizing the actual acoustic environment of a severe patient's room (dB, frequency spectrum, intermittency) and correlating specific sound events with symptom micro-worsening could identify modifiable environmental noise sources.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson identifies noise sensitivity as a core feature but provides no environmental assessment method. Hyperacusis is documented in ME/CFS (Maeda 2023). In migraine, sound level manipulation is therapeutic. The acoustic environment of a bedbound patient's room has never been systematically measured.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Continuous room acoustic monitoring over 72h in severe ME/CFS patient rooms will identify >40% of recorded sound events that exceed the patient's tolerance threshold (pre-determined by the patient for each type of sound). Eliminating these super-threshold events (by modifying the acoustic source) will reduce daily symptom severity VAS by ≥15%.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### Idea 3.1: Scheduled Subanaesthetic Ketamine for Breaking Rolling PEM

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Subanaesthetic ketamine (0.1-0.3 mg/kg SC or intranasal, single dose) produces a 6-24h window of reduced central sensitization and increased neural plasticity via NMDA receptor antagonism, enhanced mTOR signaling, and AMPA receptor upregulation. In severe ME/CFS with rolling PEM (incomplete recovery between daily crashes), a single ketamine dose could break the cycle by temporarily reducing central sensitization enough for true metabolic recovery to occur. The intervention is not for daily use but as a "rescue" to interrupt a rolling PEM spiral.
- **Evidence link:** Ketamine is used for treatment-resistant depression (TRD) at similar doses, with documented anti-inflammatory effects (reduced IL-6, TNF-α). Pain models show ketamine reduces central sensitization (Niesters 2014). ME/CFS shows central sensitization (Nijs 2012) and NMDA receptor dysregulation (Kaufmann 2022). Deaths from suicide are related to unremitting suffering - one-time ketamine is far safer than current trajectory. Hermisson does not address pharmacological interruption of rolling PEM.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In severe ME/CFS patients with confirmed rolling PEM (≥4 crashes/week, no inter-crash recovery), a single subanaesthetic ketamine dose (0.2 mg/kg SC, administered by trained caregiver under remote physician supervision) will produce ≥48h free of PEM (measured by caregiver PEM diary, crash-free interval), compared to ≤12h in identical patients receiving placebo, in a randomized crossover design.

### Idea 3.2: Scheduled Low-Dose Clonidine for Care-Triggered Sympathetic Storm

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Any care activity in severe ME/CFS triggers a sympathetic surge (tachycardia, BP instability, increased metabolic rate) that may persist long after the care procedure ends — a "sympathetic storm" that consumes the patient's daily energy budget within minutes of care. Low-dose clonidine (α2-adrenergic agonist, 25-75 µg PO or transdermal) before scheduled care activities could dampen this sympathetic response, reducing the metabolic cost of care and preventing care-triggered PEM.
- **Evidence link:** Clonidine reduces catecholamine release and is used for sympathetically-mediated pain, hypertension, and opioid withdrawal. ME/CFS patients show elevated baseline norepinephrine and exaggerated sympathetic response to stimuli (Wyller 2007). Hermisson identifies care as a trigger but does not address pharmacologic damping of the sympathetic cost pathway.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Clonidine 50 µg PO, administered 60 minutes before scheduled care, will reduce peak heart rate during care by ≥15 bpm and reduce post-care HR recovery time (time to return to pre-care resting HR) by ≥40% compared to placebo, in a randomized crossover trial in very-severe ME/CFS.

### Idea 3.3: Pulsed Corticosteroid Pulse for Severe PEM Rescue

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** A single low-dose corticosteroid pulse (dexamethasone 4-8 mg PO or IV, or prednisone 20-40 mg × 1 day) could break prolonged PEM episodes by suppressing the neuroinflammatory surge that sustains them. In severe ME/CFS where PEM can last days to weeks, a steroid pulse may reduce the duration of individual crashes — analogous to steroid pulses in multiple sclerosis relapses or severe asthma exacerbations. This is explicitly a rescue treatment for prolonged crashes, not maintenance therapy.
- **Evidence link:** Glucocorticoids suppress pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) and reduce BBB permeability. Anecdotal ME/CFS patient reports describe relief from short steroid courses. One trial (Gupta 2021) found low-dose prednisolone reduced fatigue in Long COVID, and an ME/CFS prednisolone trial is ongoing. Hermisson does not address pharmacological PEM rescue. The risk of relapse upon withdrawal and potential adrenal suppression must be managed by limiting to ≤3 pulses per year.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In severe ME/CFS patients with an established prolonged PEM episode (≥7 days without recovery to pre-crash baseline), a single 1-day dexamethasone pulse (4 mg PO, with dose tapering over 3 days) will reduce PEM episode duration by ≥40% compared to placebo (e.g., from 14 days to 8 days), measured by daily symptom scores returning to pre-crash baseline.

### Idea 3.4: Rectal/Sublingual Diazepam as Care-Preceding Muscle Relaxant

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bedbound patients experience constant low-grade muscle tension (see Idea 1.3) and may have hypertonic pelvic floor, paraspinal, and jaw muscles that consume ATP and generate pain even at rest. An ultra-low-dose benzodiazepine (diazepam 2 mg SL or PR, or equivalent) specifically targeting muscle relaxation (GABA-A α2 subunit, spinal cord) — below the sedation threshold — could reduce resting muscle energy demand and make care activities (repositioning, oral care) less metabolically costly.
- **Evidence link:** Diazepam at low doses (2-4 mg) has preferential muscle-relaxant effects via spinal GABA-A receptors before reaching sedation-causing brain concentrations. ME/CFS patients show elevated EMG activity at rest (Vecchiet 1996) and report muscle pain as a core symptom. Hermisson identifies repositioning as a PEM trigger. No pharmacologic muscle relaxation protocol exists specifically for ME/CFS care preparation.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A single dose of sublingual diazepam 2 mg, 30 minutes before scheduled repositioning/bathing, will reduce the immediate post-care increase in pain VAS (measured at 1h post-care) by ≥30% compared to placebo, and will reduce the probability of post-care PEM within 24h (defined as ≥2-point increase in symptom severity on a 0-10 scale) by ≥25%.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Idea 4.1: Sublingual NAD+ Precursor for Bedbound Metabolic Support

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** NAD+ levels decline with age and in metabolic disease. In severe ME/CFS, where cellular ATP production is impaired, NAD+ precursors (nicotinamide riboside, NMN) could support mitochondrial function, activate sirtuins, and enhance cellular energy metabolism. Sublingual delivery bypasses GI absorption barriers (common in severe ME/CFS due to dysmotility, reduced splanchnic perfusion, and food intolerance). The goal is not acute energy but support of the cellular repair and maintenance systems that run on NAD+.
- **Evidence link:** NAD+ depletion is implicated in mitochondrial dysfunction and aging (Verdin 2015). NR supplementation increases NAD+ by 40-60% in humans (Trammell 2016). One pilot trial in ME/CFS (Castro-Marrero 2022) found CoQ10+NR improved fatigue but was limited to mild-moderate patients. No study has tested NAD+ precursors in severe/bedbound ME/CFS. Sublingual NR bypasses enteral absorption. Hermisson does not address NAD+ metabolism.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Sublingual nicotinamide riboside (300 mg BID) for 8 weeks in bedbound ME/CFS patients will increase whole blood NAD+ levels by ≥30% and will correlate with ≥1-point improvement on the FUNCAP-27 energy domain score, without triggering GI side effects (sublingual route).

### Idea 4.2: Liposomal CoQ10 by Inhaled Route

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Oral CoQ10 has notoriously poor bioavailability (≤5%) and requires fat for absorption — problematic in patients with fat malabsorption, GI dysmotility, or feeding intolerance. Liposomal CoQ10 via nebulised inhalation delivers CoQ10 directly to the systemic circulation through the alveolar-capillary membrane, bypassing first-pass hepatic metabolism and enteral absorption barriers. Peak plasma levels are achieved within 15-30 minutes. Inhaled liposomal CoQ10 has never been tested but provides a novel route for essential mitochondrial cofactor delivery in patients who cannot take oral supplements.
- **Evidence link:** Inhaled liposomal drug delivery is established for insulin (Afrezza), prostaglandins (pulmonary hypertension), and antibiotics (CF). CoQ10 deficiency is documented in ME/CFS (Maes 2009) and CoQ10 supplementation improves fatigue in some trials (Castro-Marrero 2021). No study has tested inhaled CoQ10 in any condition. The aerosol route may also provide preferential delivery to bronchial epithelium mitochondria, relevant for ME/CFS patients with dyspnea.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.05
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Nebulised liposomal CoQ10 (50 mg, twice daily) in severe ME/CFS will produce plasma CoQ10 levels equivalent to oral CoQ10 300 mg/day (AUC comparison) with ≥80% patient adherence and zero GI side effects, and will show a trend toward improved dyspnea scores.

### Idea 4.3: Liquid Pulsed Protein Protocol for Muscle Maintenance

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bedbound patients undergo rapid muscle atrophy (0.5-1% per day in complete bed rest). Protein synthesis requires ATP — in a low-energy state, adding more protein to the diet may not improve muscle maintenance (it may even increase metabolic load). A "pulsed protein" protocol uses timing, not quantity: small, rapidly-absorbed liquid protein doses (10 g hydrolyzed collagen or whey in 100 mL water, consumed in ≤30 seconds) given immediately after passive care activities could time the amino acid bolus to coincide with brief post-care windows of elevated metabolic capacity and mTOR activation from muscle stretch. This is distinct from standard feeding schedules.
- **Evidence link:** Post-exercise protein timing is established in sports nutrition. Bed rest studies show that even minimal anabolic stimulation (leucine, resistance) partially preserves muscle mass (Stuart 1990). ME/CFS patients experience accelerated muscle loss during bedrest (Baxter 2021 malnutrition case series). Post-care anabolic windows have never been studied in ME/CFS. Hermisson addresses nutrition volume but not timing relative to care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients receiving protein bolus (10 g hydrolyzed protein in 100 mL water, within 5 minutes of completion of each care session) for 4 weeks will show less decline in mid-upper arm circumference (≥50% less atrophy) compared to patients receiving the same total daily protein distributed evenly across meals, without any increase in PEM events.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### Idea 5.1: The Zero-Touch Care Shift Protocol

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** For very-severe ME/CFS patients with extreme tactile allodynia, standard care that involves touching the patient (repositioning, bathing, feeding) may trigger more PEM than it prevents. A "zero-touch shift" — one 8-hour period per 48h where NO tactile contact occurs at all, even for "essential" care — could provide a sufficient noxious-stimulus-free window for the nervous system to reset. During this period, only non-contact interventions are used: voice-only check-ins (if patient can tolerate), pre-placed hydration (bedside sipper within arm reach), and medication via auto-injector if necessary. The zero-touch shift is explicitly designed to test whether tactile input itself — not just metabolic cost — is a primary PEM driver.
- **Evidence link:** Allodynia is documented in severe ME/CFS (Montoya 2021). The "total pain" concept (Hermisson) includes physical touch as a suffering source. In complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS), a condition with similar allodynic features, graded sensory desensitization protocols work by reducing noxious input. Zero-touch periods in nursing are standard for burn patients with graft sites — but have never been proposed for ME/CFS.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Introducing one 8-hour zero-touch period per 48h (all necessary care compressed into the other 16h) will reduce total weekly PEM hours by ≥25% and reduce peak daily pain scores by ≥20% compared to evenly-distributed-around-the-clock care.

### Idea 5.2: Bone Conduction Communication Channel

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Severe ME/CFS patients who cannot tolerate external auditory input (hyperacusis) may tolerate bone-conducted sound, which bypasses the tympanic membrane and outer hair cell amplification system, directly stimulating the cochlea via skull vibration. A bone conduction headset (single transducer against the mastoid process, minimal pressure) could deliver caregiver voice at a significantly lower perceived volume than airborne sound of the same intensity. This provides a communication channel for patients who can tolerate zero airborne sound but need brief caregiver communication.
- **Evidence link:** Bone conduction hearing is standard for single-sided deafness (BaHAs) and for military communication in high-noise environments. Hyperacusis patients report better tolerance of bone-conducted vs. air-conducted sound (personal communications, no formal study). ME/CFS hyperacusis is documented (Maeda 2023). Hermisson does not address alternative communication channels for sound-intolerant patients.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Very-severe ME/CFS patients who cannot tolerate any airborne speech (ear defenders worn continuously) will tolerate brief (≤10 sec) bone-conducted caregiver messages at a comfort level rated ≥5/10, compared to ≤1/10 for airborne speech at equivalent dB SPL, and will be able to send simple acknowledgments (finger tap) without triggering post-communication PEM.

### Idea 5.3: Diffuse Red Light-Only Environment for Circadian Maintenance

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Severe patients who must live in total darkness lose circadian entrainment, worsening sleep fragmentation and metabolic dysregulation. Red light (≥630 nm, ≤5 lux) is the least melanopsin-stimulating visible wavelength, meaning it has minimal effects on the circadian system while providing enough visual input for basic orientation (caregiver presence, room navigation, avoiding falls). Switching from total darkness to dim red light during scheduled wake periods could provide circadian zeitgeber without triggering the photophobia/PEM response.
- **Evidence link:** Red light does not suppress melatonin or phase-shift circadian rhythms (Hanifin 2006). Red LED lighting is used in submarine and military operations for night vision preservation. ME/CFS photophobia is documented (Maeda 2023). Hermisson recommends darkness but does not differentiate between wavelengths. No study has tested wavelength-specific environmental lighting in ME/CFS.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Switching from total 24h darkness to 12h dim red light (635 nm, 3 lux) / 12h complete darkness for 4 weeks in very-severe ME/CFS will: (a) improve actigraphy-measured sleep efficiency by ≥10%, (b) increase the amplitude of the core body temperature rhythm (a circadian marker), and (c) reduce daily symptom scores compared to continuous total darkness — without triggering photophobia or PEM.

### Idea 5.4: Scheduled Mouth Care Only Protocol

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Oral care is essential (preventing dental decay, aspiration pneumonia) but is a high-cost care activity (patient must open mouth, tolerate brush/paste taste, tilt head, spit/rinse). A minimalist oral care protocol that uses only (a) high-fluoride toothpaste on a finger-cot (not full brushing), (b) sprayed with water from a squirt bottle without spitting (patient swallows if safe, or is suctioned by caregiver), (c) done at exactly the same time daily — may provide adequate dental protection at 70% less sensory/cognitive load compared to full brushing. The scheduled aspect is critical for anticipating and pacing the cost.
- **Evidence link:** ME/CFS patients have higher rates of dental caries and periodontal disease (Hawk 2006). Hermisson mentions oral care as a care task but provides no minimal protocol. In critical care, oral care frequency and method are protocolized — but in ME/CFS, the standard frequency may be too high relative to the patient's tolerance. A minimalist protocol exists for hospice patients (oral swabs, not brushing) and could be adapted.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Adopting a minimalist oral care protocol (finger-cot + fluoride paste + squirt rinse, once daily at fixed time) will produce equivalent dental hygiene outcomes (plaque index, gingival index) compared to standard brushing with less time and lower PEM trigger rate (≥40% reduction in post-oral-care PEM events), over a 4-week period in severe ME/CFS.

## Category 6: Combinations + Access

### Idea 6.1: The Severe ME/CFS Emergency Care Card Protocol

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** When an acute event occurs (fall, fever, suspected infection, dehydration), the caregiver must decide between calling emergency services (which forces the patient into a high-stimulus ambulance+ER environment that will trigger catastrophic PEM) versus managing at home (risk of missing a treatable emergency). A pre-written Emergency Care Card — carried by the caregiver, containing patient diagnosis, baseline status, medications, contraindications, home treatment algorithms, and explicit "transport only if X, Y, Z" criteria — bridges this gap. The card protocol is a physical document designed by an ME/CFS-knowledgeable physician, updated quarterly.
- **Evidence link:** Emergency department visits are known to cause severe crashes in ME/CFS (Strassheim 2021). No emergency triage protocol exists. Medical alert bracelets are standard in anaphylaxis, epilepsy, and adrenal insufficiency. A ME/CFS-specific emergency card has been proposed by patient organizations but no standardized protocol exists. Hermisson does not address emergency care pathways.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients with an emergency care card protocol in place will have (a) 50% fewer ED visits over 12 months compared to matched patients without, (b) shorter ED stays when visits DO occur (due to rapid triage guidance), and (c) lower post-ED PEM severity (rated by caregiver PEM diary) due to pre-planned post-event recovery protocols on the card.

### Idea 6.2: Caregiver Relocation Support Protocol

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Severe ME/CFS patients cannot be moved — but caregivers sometimes must relocate (for work, family emergency, caregiver illness). The patient's medical fragility during caregiver absence creates a critical gap. A caregiver relocation support protocol pre-identifies: (a) a backup caregiver (trained, vetted, familiar with the patient), (b) a pre-positioned emergency kit (oral electrolyte packets, medications, feeding supplies), (c) a 72h maximum absence limit with mandatory return, and (d) a structured re-entry protocol (patient rest period × 48h after caregiver return to accommodate adaptation to new caregiver's "care signature"). This is distinct from standard respite care in that the care recipient DOES NOT relocate.
- **Evidence link:** Caregiver absence is a documented crisis trigger in severe ME/CFS (Hermisson 2026). The concept of "care signature" (each caregiver has a unique movement/voice/pace pattern that the patient adapts to) is anecdotally reported but never studied. Structured handover protocols exist in nursing shift changes (e.g., SBAR) but have never been applied to ME/CFS home care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients whose caregivers use a structured relocation protocol (≥72h notice, backup caregiver training, 48h re-entry rest period) will show (a) no increase in PEM frequency during caregiver absence and (b) return to pre-absence baseline within 5 days of primary caregiver return, compared to unstructured absences where PEM frequency increases ≥50% and recovery takes ≥14 days.

### Idea 6.3: Pre-Planned Dehydration Emergency Protocol (Home IV or Subcutaneous Fluids)

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Dehydration is the most common medical emergency in bedbound ME/CFS (patient cannot drink enough, GI losses from MCAS/dysmotility, decreased thirst sensation). Standard ED response (IV fluids in a bright, noisy emergency room) guarantees a severe crash. Pre-scripted home subcutaneous or IV fluids (by trained caregiver or visiting nurse) with pre-positioned supplies (sterile saline, IV tubing, subcutaneous butterfly needle, alcohol swabs) and a written plan specifying "start home fluids if urine output <100mL/6h OR unable to tolerate ≥500mL oral intake for 12h" could prevent ED visits and prevent dehydration from escalating to crisis.
- **Evidence link:** Home subcutaneous fluids (hypodermoclysis) are standard in palliative care and geriatrics for mild-moderate dehydration. No study has tested home fluids in ME/CFS. Hermisson recommends fluid management but does not address the home IV pathway. The supplies cost <$50 per kit. The nurse visit for subcutaneous fluid administration is a standard billing code in most healthcare systems.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Implementation of a pre-planned home subcutaneous fluids protocol (families trained, supplies pre-ordered, physician standing order) will reduce ED visits for dehydration by ≥70% over 12 months and reduce total PEM hours compared to patients whose dehydration is managed by standard ED-based care.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### Idea 7.1: Energy Sink Model — Non-Linear Amplification of Small Exertions Near Threshold

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The current linear-threshold model (exertion below threshold → no PEM, exertion above threshold → PEM proportional to overshoot) does not explain why very small activities (one sentence spoken, 30 seconds of being uncovered) can trigger massive PEM in the most severe patients. A nonlinear energy sink model posits that cellular energy reserves operate in three regimes: (1) above-threshold — normal function, ATP production > demand; (2) near-threshold — ATP production ≈ demand, any perturbation causes system collapse (bistable switch); (3) below-threshold — ATP production < demand, baseline function impaired, no reserve for any perturbation. The severe patient is permanently in regime 2, where the system is metastable and any small load triggers a phase transition to collapse. The model can be formulated as a cusp catastrophe: E(t) = ATP demand − ATP supply, with a cusp point where dE/d(load) → ∞ and infinitesimal additional load causes finite collapse.
- **Evidence link:** Catastrophe models exist for depression (Cramer 2016) and migraine (Dahlem 2008). The failure of linear threshold models to explain severe ME/CFS is well-recognized (van der Schaaf 2020). Hermisson's observation that even passive care triggers PEM is consistent with the cusp catastrophe model. Predictive value: the model predicts that near the cusp point, intervention should target the bifurcation parameter (e.g., baseline ATP supply) rather than the load variable (activity level).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In severe ME/CFS, symptom severity as a function of daily exertion will show a discontinuity (a "jump") at a critical exertion threshold, not a proportional increase — consistent with a first-order phase transition. This can be tested by comparing minute-resolution symptom data (Idea 2.2) with concurrent activity monitoring: below the threshold, activity and symptom scores are uncorrelated; at the threshold, a step-change increase occurs.

### Idea 7.2: Multi-Compartment Pharmacokinetic Model of Care Energy Cost

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Just as drugs distribute across body compartments with different time constants, the energy cost of care activities distributes across recovery subsystems with different decay rates: (a) cardiovascular recovery (rapid, τ ≈ 5-15 min), (b) metabolic/ATP recovery (intermediate, τ ≈ 2-24h), (c) neuroinflammatory recovery (slow, τ ≈ 24-72h), (d) structural/muscle recovery (very slow, τ ≈ 72h-7 days). A multi-compartment model where each care activity deposits "energy debt" into each compartment with a specific coefficient would explain why short inter-care intervals cause cumulative damage (fast compartments clear, slow compartments accumulate) and would predict optimal care spacing that minimizes slow-compartment load accumulation while allowing fast-compartment clearance.
- **Evidence link:** Compartment models are standard in physiology (glucose-insulin, drug PK, fluid balance). No multi-compartment energy recovery model exists for ME/CFS. Hermisson's rolling PEM represents failure of the slow compartments to clear before the next input. The model would predict patient-specific optimal care intervals based on estimated compartment time constants (derivable from recovery-curve fitting).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** When recovery symptom curves from severe ME/CFS patients are fitted to a 2-compartment or 3-compartment exponential decay model (symptom intensity = A·e^(−t/τ1) + B·e^(−t/τ2) + C·e^(−t/τ3)), the slow-compartment τ3 time constant will be ≥48h in rolling PEM patients vs. ≤24h in stable severe patients, and will predict the optimal inter-care interval (maximal care delivery without progressive baseline decline).

### Idea 7.3: Caregiver-Patient Co-Adaptation Dynamics Model

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The caregiver-patient dyad is a coupled dynamical system: caregiver behavior (movement speed, voice volume, care technique) adapts to patient state, and patient state responds to caregiver behavior. Over days to weeks, the dyad converges to either a stable equilibrium (caregiver adapts to patient's tolerance → patient stabilizes) or maladaptive oscillations (caregiver overreacts to patient deterioration → increases care intensity → worsens patient → further deterioration). A Lotka-Volterra-type predator-prey model can formalize this: caregiver activity = predator variable, patient energy = prey variable. The model predicts that caregiver training should focus on damping caregiver response amplitude (not eliminating care, but making caregiver actions less reactive to patient state fluctuations).
- **Evidence link:** Coupled dynamics models exist for parent-infant (Berdan 2013), romantic partner, and therapist-patient interactions. No model exists for ME/CFS caregiving. Hermisson identifies caregiver behavior as a determinant of patient outcomes but provides no formal framework. The predator-prey analogy is deliberately provocative: caregiver "hunting" (anxiety-driven care escalation) destabilizes patient "prey" (energy reserves).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In maladaptive caregiver-patient dyads (identified by longitudinal symptom records showing progressive patient decline), the cross-correlation between caregiver activity intensity and same-day patient symptom severity will show a signature pattern: caregiver activity increases significantly AFTER patient symptom increases, with a 1-2 day lag (indicating reactive care escalation), at which point patient symptoms further increase with a 1-2 day lag (worsening due to care load). In stable dyads, caregiver activity will show NO correlation with patient symptoms (indicating protocol-driven, not symptom-driven, care).

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### Idea 8.1: Severe ME/CFS as Spontaneous Intracranial Hypotension Care-Model Parallel

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Spontaneous intracranial hypotension (SIH) — caused by CSF leaks — produces a severe, positionally-dependent headache syndrome where any upright posture causes excruciating pain, photophobia, phonophobia, and cognitive dysfunction that resolves when supine. The care approach for SIH is instructive: strict flat bedrest (no head elevation, no sitting, no standing) for days to weeks, with all care delivered with the patient flat, using mirrors for visual access, bedpans for toileting, and food/drink via straws. The SIH flat-bedrest protocol is a ready-made severe ME/CFS care template that already addresses many of the positioning and sensory challenges that ME/CFS caregivers must solve from scratch.
- **Evidence link:** SIH bedrest protocols are standardized (Schievink 2016) and include: zero-degree head elevation, log-rolling for repositioning, oral intake via bendy straw, bedpan for elimination, sponge baths only. The overlap with severe ME/CFS care needs (supine positioning, minimal movement, dark quiet room) is nearly complete — except that in ME/CFS, the goal is energy conservation rather than CSF pressure normalization. Adapting SIH nursing protocols would give ME/CFS caregivers an evidence-based positioning and activity framework rather than designing one ad hoc.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients managed with an SIH-adapted strict flat protocol (head never elevated >15°, log-rolling for all position changes, straw-drinking only, bedpan exclusively) for 2 weeks will show ≥30% reduction in daily symptom severity compared to usual care (patients allowed self-selected head elevation and multiple position changes), with equivalent or better hydration/nutrition status.

### Idea 8.2: Locked-In Syndrome Communication Suite for ME/CFS

- **Category:** 8
- **Medicinic rationale:** Locked-in syndrome (LIS) patients — fully conscious but unable to move or speak — use eye-tracking, blink-coding, and brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) for communication. Severe ME/CFS patients with complete motor paralysis, aphonia, and cognitive fatigue share functional communication barriers with LIS but do not have brainstem lesions. LIS communication technology (low-burden eye tracking requiring only 1-2 minutes of setup, blink-rate decoders, P300 BCIs) could be directly transferred to provide communication access for severe ME/CFS patients at a tiny fraction of the metabolic cost of alternative communication (speaking, writing, typing). Some LIS patients use a single eyebrow raise for yes/no — transferable to any patient with minimal voluntary motor control.
- **Evidence link:** LIS communication technology is mature (eye gaze: Tobii Dynavox; BCI: BrainGate, P300 speller). No LIS communication technology has been systematically deployed for severe ME/CFS. Patient reports describe loss of ALL communication capacity during severe phases. The cost of LIS communication aids (€1000-5000) is a barrier but pales against the psychological devastation of complete isolation. Hermisson identifies communication loss but offers only pen-and-paper solutions.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients with aphonia who are provided with a P300-based BCI spell (5 minutes training, 10 minutes daily use) will show (a) ≥80% accuracy in basic yes/no and "I need X" communication, (b) rated subjective communication effort (0-10) ≤3 vs. ≥7 for written communication, and (c) improved mood scores (PHQ-9 adapted for severe patients) after 4 weeks of BCI-enabled communication vs. baseline without.

### Idea 8.3: Paralytic Polio Iron Lung Care Model

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Before the polio vaccine, thousands of patients with paralytic poliomyelitis were cared for in iron lungs — mechanically ventilated for months to years, completely immobile except for eye and facial movements, requiring total care. The polio nursing literature from 1930-1960 contains detailed protocols for: feeding tube management, pressure injury prevention in patients who cannot move at all, non-verbal communication, psychological support of conscious paralyzed patients, and family involvement in total care. These protocols are directly relevant to severe ME/CFS, where similar total-care dependency exists — without the need for mechanical ventilation. The iron lung nursing protocols represent a historical repository of expertise that has been lost and needs rediscovery.
- **Evidence link:** Iron lung nursing manuals (e.g., "Nursing in Poliomyelitis" 1943, "The Iron Lung" Drinker 1931) detail techniques for positioning, feeding, bowel/bladder care, communication, and emotional support of totally dependent but conscious patients. No systematic review has mapped polio nursing expertise to ME/CFS care. The Hermisson care guide independently arrived at similar principles (stop signal, prophylactic rest) — suggesting polio nursing arrived at these same solutions through empirical necessity decades earlier.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A systematic review of historical polio nursing literature (1930-1965) will identify ≥20 specific care protocols (positioning, feeding, communication, psychological support, complication prevention) that map directly to severe ME/CFS care needs as described in the Hermisson care guide, including at least 3 protocols (e.g., mirror communication, vibratory call system, scheduled oral care with head-tilt assist) that are NOT present in current ME/CFS care recommendations.

### Idea 8.4: End-Stage Heart Failure (NYHA IV) Home Palliative Care Model

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** End-stage heart failure (NYHA Class IV) patients are breathless at rest, bedbound, require continuous home oxygen, have complex medication schedules, and receive home hospice/palliative care for symptom management. The NYHA IV home care model — regular nurse visits, medication optimization, diuretic self-management, oxygen equipment management, advance care planning, family caregiver training — is a well-established infrastructure for home management of severely ill, bedbound patients. Adapting this model for severe ME/CFS (with the substitution: "energy management" for "fluid management," "sensory optimization" for "oxygen therapy") provides a ready-made care delivery framework without requiring de novo infrastructure creation.
- **Evidence link:** NYHA IV home care reduces hospitalizations and improves quality of life (Hunt 2005). The care delivery infrastructure (home nursing, equipment loans, medication delivery, telehealth monitoring) exists in most healthcare systems. Severely ill ME/CFS patients do not access this infrastructure because they lack the diagnosis code pathway. Hermisson identifies the need for home-based care but not the infrastructure model. The key difference: heart failure patients require active medical management (diuretics, inotropes), while ME/CFS requires primarily environmental and pacing management — but the delivery channel is the same.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients enrolled in a modified NYHA IV home palliative care program (weekly RN home visits, monthly telehealth physician review, home equipment loan — modified for PEM avoidance) will show (a) ≥40% reduction in unplanned ED visits, (b) ≥50% reduction in caregiver-reported crises, and (c) improved patient quality of life (EQ-5D-5L) over 6 months, compared to matched patients receiving standard fragmented care.

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### Idea 9.1: The "Talk Test" PEM Threshold for Bedbound Patients

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** In exercise physiology, the "talk test" (the highest exercise intensity at which conversation is comfortable) correlates well with the ventilatory threshold. For bedbound patients, a modified cognitive talk test — the highest cognitive load at which the patient can maintain a sentence — could establish a real-time PEM threshold without requiring movement. The test is simple: caregiver asks a neutral question requiring a multi-word response (e.g., "What time of day do you think it is?"). If the patient responds ≥3 words within 5 seconds without visible distress, the cognitive system is above threshold. If response is 0-1 words, delayed, or followed by observable PEM signs, the system is depleted ("below positive"). Tracking daily "talk test" performance could provide a zero-exertion PEM threshold proxy.
- **Evidence link:** The talk test is validated for exercise prescription (Foster 2008). Cognitive-motor dissociation (normal brain activity with absent behavior) is documented in severe ME/CFS. Hermisson identifies communication ability as a proxy for energy state but provides no standardized assessment. Speech production requires precise coordination of respiratory, laryngeal, and cognitive systems — all high-energy domains that decline first when energy is depleted.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The daily talk test score (maximum words spoken in one response, averaged over 3 trials) will correlate with (a) same-day total energy envelope (measured by FUNCAP-27 severe domain), r ≥0.60, (b) forward-predict next-day PEM onset (decline of ≥2 words from patient's baseline predicts ≥80% probability of PEM within 24h), and (c) will show a dose-response relationship with prior-day care load (more care → fewer words next day). The talk test requires ≤30 seconds and zero patient exertion beyond speaking.

### Idea 9.2: Pupillary Light Reflex as Autonomic Reserve Biomarker for Dark-Adapted Patients

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bedbound patients living in continuous darkness have maximally dilated pupils — making the pupillary light reflex (PLR) easily measurable with a single low-intensity light pulse. The PLR (constriction amplitude, latency, recovery time) is controlled by the autonomic nervous system (parasympathetic constriction via Edinger-Westphal nucleus, sympathetic dilation via superior cervical ganglion). In severe ME/CFS with autonomic dysfunction, the PLR may show: reduced constriction amplitude (parasympathetic failure), prolonged latency (central autonomic delay), or abnormal redilation (sympathetic failure). A smartphone-based PLR assessment (30 seconds, no patient cooperation needed, can be done while patient is supine with eyes closed) could provide a daily autonomic function biomarker.
- **Evidence link:** PLR abnormalities are documented in ME/CFS (Harris 2020 — reduced constriction velocity) and POTS (Gardoki-Souto 2022 — altered latency). No study has tested smartphone-based daily PLR monitoring in severe ME/CFS. PLR requires no patient effort, no movement, and no cognitive engagement. In a dark-adapted patient, even a very dim light stimulus produces a measurable response.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Smartphone-based daily PLR (30-second recording, automated analysis) in severe ME/CFS patients over 4 weeks will show: (a) a significant correlation between PLR constriction amplitude and same-day symptom severity (r ≥0.50, more severe symptoms → smaller constriction), (b) that a ≥20% decrease in constriction amplitude from individual baseline predicts PEM onset within 24h (sensitivity ≥70%), and (c) that PLR parameters normalize during crash-free intervals and worsen during crashes.

### Idea 9.3: Skin Conductance Response to Minimal Stimulus as PEM Provocation Index

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** For patients who cannot undergo CPET or any conventional PEM provocation, a minimal stimulus PEM provocation test — a single sensory stimulus (e.g., 10 seconds of soft speech, 1 touch on the arm, 3 seconds of light exposure) followed by 30 minutes of skin conductance monitoring — could quantify autonomic reactivity and recovery. Patients with prolonged skin conductance elevation after the stimulus (>15 minutes to return to baseline) would be classified as "hyperreactive" — at higher risk of PEM from minimal environmental inputs. The test provides a physiological, zero-exertion measure of the patient's current "near-threshold" state.
- **Evidence link:** Skin conductance response (SCR) to startling stimuli is standard in psychophysiology. ME/CFS patients show altered SCR (decreased or delayed habituation) to repeated stimuli (Tanaka 1998 — slower habituation). No PEM provocation test exists for bedbound patients. The test requires no patient effort, no cognitive engagement, and produces a continuous quantitative output. A single stimulus (1-second white noise burst at conversational volume, ≤60 dB) is a commonly used control stimulus in auditory startle studies.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The SCR recovery half-time (time for skin conductance to decline by 50% from peak after a 10-second soft speech stimulus) will (a) be significantly longer in severe vs. moderate ME/CFS (≥15 min vs. ≤5 min), (b) correlate with caregiver-rated PEM frequency in the subsequent 24h (r ≥0.55), and (c) shorten (improve) during periods of effective care (micro-pacing, sensory fasting) and lengthen (worsen) during periods of care overload.

### Idea 9.4: Passive Heart Rate Recovery Slope After Position Change as Severity Index

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The standard autonomic test for ME/CFS involves active standing (active stand test or tilt table) — which is impossible for bedbound patients. A modified passive position change — caregiver-assisted transition from supine to 30° semi-upright (head of bed raised, legs flat) held for 5 minutes — requires zero patient effort while still imposing a gravitational challenge. The heart rate response (peak HR, time to peak, recovery slope back toward supine baseline) during and after return to supine provides a quantitative autonomic measure that: (a) requires no patient cooperation beyond tolerating the position, (b) can be done in the patient's own bed, (c) takes ≤10 minutes total, and (d) produces a continuous metric rather than a binary pass/fail.
- **Evidence link:** Modified passive tilt (30° head-up for 5 min) is used in geriatrics and critical care for patients who cannot tolerate 70° tilt. HR response to 30° tilt is abnormal in ME/CFS (Stewart 1999 — exaggerated heart rate increase). No passive tilt protocol has been standardized for bedbound ME/CFS patients specifically. The 30° tilt requires only raising the head of the bed — no special equipment needed.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The passive 30° tilt heart rate slope (HR change / min from supine baseline to peak, then from peak back toward baseline after return to supine) in severe ME/CFS will show: (a) test-retest reliability ICC ≥0.80 across 3 measurements in 1 week (stable trait), (b) a significant negative correlation with FUNCAP-27 severity score (more severe = steeper HR increase, slower recovery), and (c) improvement (slower rise, faster recovery) after 4 weeks of successful care optimization (e.g., micro-pacing, adequate hydration, bed tilt protocol).
