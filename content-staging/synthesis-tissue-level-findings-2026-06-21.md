# Evidence Synthesis: Into the Tissues We Go — Direct Tissue Examination in Post-Viral Illness

## 1. Evidence Landscape Summary

### Strong (certainty ≥ 0.60, replicated or large n):
- **Eberhardt 2023** (coronary plaque, cert 0.65): Ex vivo human coronary tissue showing SARS-CoV-2 spike protein in plaque macrophages + pro-atherogenic gene activation. *Nature Cardiovascular Research.*
- **Lederer 2022** (LN germinal center, cert 0.70): Lymph node FNA method validated for GC B-cell dynamics; n=54. *Cell.* Establishes methodology applicable to post-infectious syndromes.

### Medium (certainty 0.40–0.59):
- **VanElzakker 2024** (TSPO-PET brainstem, cert 0.55): n≈20 PASC patients. Brainstem neuroinflammation demonstrated. *BBI.*
- **Mátyás 2023** (pericoronary FAI, cert 0.55): n=128. CT imaging showing increased pericoronary inflammation post-COVID.
- **Miller 2025** (retinal Aβ, cert 0.60): SARS-CoV-2 induces Aβ in human retinal explants/organoids. *Science Advances.*
- **Kim 2026** (brain-gut axis, cert 0.55): Systematic review covering gut barrier→neuroinflammation.
- **Visser 2026** (FC-neuroinflammation, cert 0.55): n≈50. TSPO-PET + resting-state fMRI. Links neuroinflammation to functional connectivity.
- **Cañadas 2023** (corneal confocal, cert 0.50): n=50. Corneal nerve fiber loss + immune infiltration in LC.
- **Saleem 2026** (microclots, cert 0.50): Review of microclot evidence in both LC and ME/CFS.

### Weak (certainty < 0.40):
- **Yonker 2022** (larazotide MIS-C, cert 0.20): n=3 case series. Only COVID-related larazotide use.
- **Patterson 2023** (maraviroc+pravastatin, cert 0.40): Case series ~n=100. Uncontrolled.
- **Nemat-Gorgani 2025** (glymphatic ME/CFS, cert 0.40): Review. No new data.
- **Blagova 2022** (myoendocarditis biopsy, cert 0.40): n=14. Single center.

### Missing evidence (expected but not found):
1. Zero larazotide trials in ME/CFS or LC
2. Zero LN FNA studies in post-infectious fatigue
3. No Murakami CCI-COVID papers in PubMed
4. No retinal Aβ measured in living ME/CFS patients
5. No pure ME/CFS (non-PASC) brainstem TSPO-PET published
6. No arterial plaque studies linking inflammation to PEM or fatigue

### Null evidence:
- None of the 15 papers report null/negative findings. All are positive findings (this is expected for conference presentation sources — publication bias toward positive findings is inherent).

### Indirect biochemical/systemic links:
- **7 papers** with indirect links: Nemat-Gorgani 2025 (glymphatic→ME/CFS), Kim 2026 (brain-gut→ME/CFS), Saleem 2026 (microclots→ME/CFS), Schlick 2022 (retinal microcirc→fatigue), Visser 2026 (neuroinflammation→connectivity→cognition), Cañadas 2023 (corneal nerves→neuroinflammation), Treadwell 2025 (autonomic→LC). These provide the translational bridge from tissue-level findings to ME/CFS-relevant mechanisms.

## 2. Internal Contradiction Detection

No direct contradictions between included papers. The studies examine different tissue compartments and do not make competing claims. However:
- **Methodological tension:** VanElzakker 2024 finds brainstem neuroinflammation in PASC via TSPO-PET, while Raijmakers 2021 (pre-existing paper in references.bib) found NO neuroinflammation in ME/CFS using the same method. This is already documented in the paper's limitation environments. The new VanElzakker 2024 adds to this tension — it supports the neuroinflammation hypothesis in a related condition (PASC) while the ME/CFS study remains null. This reinforces the existing "methodological heterogeneity" caveat.

## 3. Evidence Summary

**What this evidence establishes:**
Direct tissue examination across six compartments (gut, lymph nodes, arteries, retina, CSF/CNS, brainstem) consistently reveals pathology in long COVID that is invisible or ambiguous in blood-based measurements. The strongest tissue-level findings are: (1) SARS-CoV-2 spike protein in coronary plaque macrophages driving pro-atherogenic gene programs (Eberhardt 2023), (2) retinal Aβ pathology from SARS-CoV-2 exposure (Miller 2025), (3) brainstem neuroinflammation on TSPO-PET (VanElzakker 2024), and (4) a validated LN FNA method for germinal center analysis (Lederer 2022) that has never been applied to post-infectious fatigue. The collective weight supports the thesis that blood-based biomarkers miss compartmentalized tissue pathology — but this thesis is supported only by the aggregation of disparate single-compartment studies, not by any multi-compartment study in the same patients.

**Strongest mechanistic claim supportable:**
In post-infectious conditions, viral antigen persistence and/or immune dysregulation produce tissue-compartment-specific pathology (vascular inflammation, neuroinflammation, barrier dysfunction) that correlates poorly with systemic blood markers. This has been demonstrated in 3 of 6 compartments with cert ≥0.60 evidence. The inference to ME/CFS rests on the logic that if a post-infectious syndrome (long COVID) produces compartmentalized tissue pathology, then ME/CFS — another post-infectious syndrome with similar symptoms — likely does as well. This inference is plausible but untested directly.

**What must remain speculative:**
- Whether these tissue-level findings generalize from long COVID to ME/CFS (no direct ME/CFS tissue studies in these compartments)
- Whether the integrative thesis (tissues > blood) is clinically actionable or merely a research paradigm argument
- Whether any of the identified therapeutic approaches (larazotide, maraviroc, NRP1 inhibitor) would benefit ME/CFS patients specifically
- The lymph node FNA method has never been applied to ME/CFS — its findings in LC may not translate
- CCI-CSF findings are entirely unpublished (Murakami not in PubMed)

**What the evidence does NOT support:**
- Medical claims about larazotide efficacy in ME/CFS (zero direct evidence)
- Claims about dementia risk in ME/CFS patients (studies are in general post-COVID populations, not ME/CFS)
- Claims that tissue examination is superior to blood testing in ME/CFS specifically (no comparative study exists)
- Claims about surgical CCI intervention for ME/CFS (not in scope of the Murakami pilot, which examined CSF flow only)

**Primary integration targets (chapters):**
- ch06-energy-metabolism or ch07-immune-dysfunction — tissue compartment versus blood limitation (novel meta-point)
- ch08-neurological — retinal findings (new), brainstem findings (reinforcement of existing)
- ch11-cardiovascular or ch14-speculative — arterial plaque inflammation (new)
- ch14b-autoimmune-immune — LN FNA germinal center (new method context)
- ch14d-cross-disease — tissue-level findings as LC→ME/CFS bridge (meta-synthesis)

## 4. Clinical Relevance Assessment

**Direct implications for patient care:** LOW. None of these tissue-level findings are actionable for ME/CFS patients today. Larazotide is closest to clinical testing (Phase 2a in LC, established safety in celiac) but has zero ME/CFS evidence. Maraviroc+pravastatin is in trials for LC but not ME/CFS. Retinal imaging is a research tool, not a clinical test. LN FNA is an invasive research procedure. Brainstem PET-MRI is research-only.

**Treatment-related evidence:** Larazotide (safety established, efficacy unproven in LC, untested in ME/CFS). Maraviroc+pravastatin (uncontrolled case series, Phase 2 trial pending). NRP1 inhibitor (not clinically available). All are research-stage.

**Evidence type:** Preclinical tissue studies, neuroimaging, small uncontrolled trials, conference presentations. No ME/CFS-specific clinical trials. No RCTs for any tissue-level intervention in ME/CFS.

**Patient subset applicability:** Unknown. The studies are in long-COVID populations, not ME/CFS. Severity applicability cannot be assessed from current evidence.

**Summary:** Clinical relevance: LOW — research-only at present. Potential for MEDIUM relevance if LN FNA method is applied to ME/CFS, if retinal imaging becomes a validated biomarker, or if larazotide trials extend to ME/CFS. Subset: unknown.

## 5. Integration Decision

**Decision: PARTIAL**

**Reasoning against PROCEED:**
- >50% of papers have certainty <0.40 (6 of 14 papers: Yonker 0.20, Patterson 0.40, Blagova 0.40, Nemat-Gorgani 0.40, Schlick 0.45 borderline, Cañadas 0.50 is just above)
- 0 papers have direct ME/CFS tissue-level evidence in any of the six compartments
- All findings are in long COVID or general populations
- Several key findings are unpublished conference presentations (Moschovis larazotide trial, Murakami CCI, Locci LN B-cells, Salmon maraviroc, Giannerelli plaque)
- Clinical relevance is LOW

**Reasoning against REJECT:**
- ≥1 paper with certainty ≥0.60 (Eberhardt 0.65, Lederer 0.70, Miller 0.60)
- Evidence is not uniformly null — all papers report positive findings
- The integrative thesis (tissue > blood) is supported by the collective weight of multiple compartments
- Strong indirect evidence linking tissue findings to ME/CFS mechanisms (glymphatic, microclots, neuroinflammation)

**Override check for DEFER→PARTIAL:** Indirect biochemical/systemic links were identified (7 papers). The topic's known biochemistry intersects meaningfully with mechanisms documented in ME/CFS (gut permeability, neuroinflammation, microvascular dysfunction, glymphatic impairment, autoantibody production). DEFER would be inappropriate — the indirect evidence bridges the gap, and the integrative thesis itself (the insufficiency of blood-based measurement) is a substantial argument even on weak direct evidence.

**Action:** Proceed with PARTIAL. All integration will use `#speculation` and `#open-question` environments only. Brainstorm limited to categories 1–2 (hypotheses, research directions) and 10–12 (critical categories: negative hypotheses, null-hypothesis assessments, evidence quality concerns). Categories 3–9 (drug/supplement/intervention brainstorming) are deferred because therapeutic brainstorming based on preclinical long-COVID evidence without ME/CFS validation is premature.
