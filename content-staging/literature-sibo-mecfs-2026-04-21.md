# SIBO in ME/CFS — Literature Survey 2026-04-21

## Overview

Eight papers identified covering: prevalence in ME/CFS cohorts, MMC/vagal pathophysiology, H2S-mitochondrial mechanism, rifaximin evidence base (IBS proxy), breath-test validation, prokinetic prevention, nutrient malabsorption, and a critical negative appraisal. Three citation keys already in references.bib (SIBOKarhu2023, HerbalSIBO2014, ElementalDiet2025) and are NOT duplicated here.

## Paper 1: SIBO Prevalence in ME/CFS (Karhu 2023)

**Status:** Already in references.bib as `SIBOKarhu2023` — conference abstract, ACG 2023.
**Key facts:** 479 referred ME/CFS patients; 48% SIBO+ in conclusive results (152/316); IBS comorbidity and older age predicted positive breath test. Retrospective, referral bias.
**Certainty:** Medium (0.35) — conference abstract only, not peer-reviewed article.

## Paper 2: Pimentel et al. 2000 — SIBO Eradication Decreases CFS Symptoms (RCT)

**Citation:** Pimentel M, Hallegua D, Chow EJ, Wallace D, Bonorris G, Lin HC. Eradication of small intestinal bacterial overgrowth decreases symptoms in chronic fatigue syndrome: a double blind, randomized study. *Gastroenterology*. 2000;118(4):A414.
**DOI:** 10.1016/S0016-5085(00)83765-8 (conference abstract DOI)
**PMID:** Not indexed in PubMed — conference abstract published in Gastroenterology supplement.
**Citation key:** `Pimentel2000SIBOcfs`

**Key findings:**
- n=31 CFS patients; 77% had SIBO by lactulose hydrogen breath test
- Double-blind RCT of neomycin vs placebo
- Breath test normalization correlated with symptom improvement
- No difference between arms overall (small n, underpowered)
- First controlled study linking SIBO eradication to CFS symptom relief

**Certainty:** Low (0.25) — conference abstract, small n, arms showed no significant difference, 25 years old, not peer-reviewed as full paper.

## Paper 3: Pimentel et al. 2011 — Rifaximin for IBS-D (TARGET 1 & 2 RCTs)

**Citation:** Pimentel M, Lembo A, Chey WD, et al.; TARGET Study Group. Rifaximin therapy for patients with irritable bowel syndrome without constipation. *N Engl J Med*. 2011;364(1):22–32.
**DOI:** 10.1056/NEJMoa1004409
**PMID:** 21208106
**Citation key:** `Pimentel2011TARGET`

**Key findings:**
- Two identically designed phase 3 RCTs (TARGET 1 + 2), rifaximin 550 mg TID × 14 days vs placebo
- Adequate relief of global IBS symptoms: 40.7% vs 31.7% (combined; p<0.001)
- Significant relief of bloating, abdominal pain, loose stools
- Effect maintained up to 10 weeks post-treatment
- Funded by Salix Pharmaceuticals (commercial COI)

**Certainty:** High (0.75) — large phase-3 RCT, NEJM publication, independently replicated. Note: IBS-D, not ME/CFS-specific. Commercial funding is disclosed COI.

## Paper 4: Pimentel et al. 2009 — Prokinetics Delay SIBO/IBS Recurrence

**Citation:** Pimentel M, Morales W, Lezcano S, Sun-Chuan D, Low K, Yang J. Low-dose nocturnal tegaserod or erythromycin delays symptom recurrence after treatment of irritable bowel syndrome based on presumed bacterial overgrowth. *Gastroenterol Hepatol (N Y)*. 2009;5(6):435–42.
**DOI:** Not available (journal predates CrossRef coverage for this title)
**PMID:** 20574504
**Citation key:** `Pimentel2009prokinetics`

**Key findings:**
- 64 IBS-SIBO patients with clinical and breath test resolution followed post-treatment
- No prevention: 59.7 symptom-free days before relapse
- Low-dose erythromycin (nocturnal): 138.5 symptom-free days
- Low-dose tegaserod (nocturnal): 241.6 symptom-free days (tegaserod > erythromycin, p<0.05)
- Mechanism: prokinetics stimulate MMC, reducing bacterial re-accumulation
- Tegaserod withdrawn from US market due to cardiac risk; prucalopride now used instead

**Certainty:** Low-Medium (0.35) — retrospective chart review, small subgroups (n=16 tegaserod), IBS-SIBO not ME/CFS-specific.

## Paper 5: Deloose et al. 2012 — MMC Control Mechanisms and Disease

**Citation:** Deloose E, Janssen P, Depoortere I, Tack J. The migrating motor complex: control mechanisms and its role in health and disease. *Nat Rev Gastroenterol Hepatol*. 2012;9(5):271–85.
**DOI:** 10.1038/nrgastro.2012.57
**PMID:** 22450306
**Citation key:** `Deloose2012MMCreview`

**Key findings:**
- Definitive review of MMC physiology: cyclic fasting motility pattern, 90–120 min cycle, four phases (I–IV)
- MMC absence associated with gastroparesis, intestinal pseudo-obstruction, and SIBO
- Vagus nerve modulates gastric phase III but NOT small bowel periodicity — enteric nervous system drives small bowel MMC
- Motilin, ghrelin, erythromycin trigger MMC phase III; serotonin, somatostatin active via duodenum
- Key implication: autonomic vagal dysfunction alone does not fully explain MMC failure — enteric neuropathy or motilin signaling disruption is additional mechanism

**Certainty:** High (0.80) — Nature Reviews Gastroenterology, comprehensive mechanistic review, widely cited.

## Paper 6: Szabo et al. 2014 — H2S Regulation of Mitochondrial Bioenergetics (Part I)

**Citation:** Szabo C, Ransy C, Módis K, Andriamihaja M, Murghes B, Coletta C, Olah G, Yanagi K, Bouillaud F. Regulation of mitochondrial bioenergetic function by hydrogen sulfide. Part I. Biochemical and physiological mechanisms. *Br J Pharmacol*. 2014;171(8):2099–122.
**DOI:** 10.1111/bph.12369
**PMID:** 23991830
**Citation key:** `Szabo2014H2Smito`

**Key findings:**
- H2S has biphasic (bell-shaped) mitochondrial effects: low concentrations donate electrons via SQR → stimulate ETC; high concentrations competitively inhibit Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) at cytochrome a3 prosthetic group
- Inhibition of Complex IV blocks O2 binding → electron backup → inner membrane potential dissipates → aerobic ATP generation stops
- At toxic/excess concentrations, H2S is functionally identical to cyanide at Complex IV
- Colonocytes use H2S as metabolic fuel via SQR, limiting systemic exposure; when this barrier is overwhelmed, systemic H2S reaches mitochondria

**Certainty:** High (0.80) — British Journal of Pharmacology, biochemical mechanism review with experimental evidence.

## Paper 7: Rezaie et al. 2017 — North American Consensus on Breath Testing

**Citation:** Rezaie A, Buresi M, Lembo A, Lin H, McCallum R, Rao S, Schmulson M, Valdovinos M, Zakko S, Pimentel M. Hydrogen and methane-based breath testing in gastrointestinal disorders: the North American Consensus. *Am J Gastroenterol*. 2017;112(5):775–84.
**DOI:** 10.1038/ajg.2017.46
**PMID:** 28323273
**Citation key:** `Rezaie2017breathConsensus`

**Key findings:**
- 26 consensus statements across five domains: indications, preparation, performance, interpretation, knowledge gaps
- SIBO positive: hydrogen rise ≥20 ppm by 90 min on glucose or lactulose breath test
- Methane positive: ≥10 ppm at any point
- Substrate doses: lactulose 10g, glucose 75g, fructose 25g, lactose 25g
- H2S not measurable by standard two-gas devices — identified as a gap; preceded trio-smart development
- Useful for carbohydrate maldigestion, methane-constipation, bloating; NOT recommended for oro-cecal transit assessment

**Certainty:** High (0.75) — multi-centre expert consensus, American Journal of Gastroenterology, widely adopted as standard.

## Paper 8: Pimentel et al. 2020 — ACG Clinical Guideline: SIBO

**Citation:** Pimentel M, Saad RJ, Long MD, Rao SSC. ACG clinical guideline: small intestinal bacterial overgrowth. *Am J Gastroenterol*. 2020;115(2):165–78.
**DOI:** 10.14309/ajg.0000000000000501
**PMID:** 32023228
**Citation key:** `Pimentel2020ACGguideline`

**Key findings:**
- GRADE-based guideline for diagnosis and treatment of SIBO
- Suggests breath testing (glucose H2 or lactulose H2) for: IBS patients, symptomatic motility disorder patients, post-abdominal-surgery patients
- Recommends antibiotics (rifaximin preferred) for symptomatic SIBO eradication
- Addresses nutrient malabsorption: B12 deficiency (bacterial consumption), fat-soluble vitamins A/D/E/K (bile salt deconjugation), iron (mucosal inflammation, competitive uptake)
- Rifaximin 550 mg TID × 14 days for H2-dominant; rifaximin + neomycin for CH4-dominant (IMO)
- Notes prokinetics as prevention option post-eradication

**Certainty:** High (0.80) — ACG official guideline with GRADE methodology.

## Paper 9: Wielgosz-Grochowska et al. 2024 — SIBO Subtypes and Nutritional Status

**Citation:** Wielgosz-Grochowska JP, Domanski N, Drywien ME. Identification of SIBO subtypes along with nutritional status and diet as key elements of SIBO therapy. *Int J Mol Sci*. 2024;25(13):7341.
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijms25137341
**PMID:** 39000446
**Citation key:** `Wielgosz2024SIBOnutrition`

**Key findings:**
- n=67 newly diagnosed SIBO patients: H+ (18%), M+ (31%), H+/M+ mixed (51%)
- H+/M+ group: lowest serum vitamin D, lowest serum ferritin, highest dietary fat intake
- M+ group: elevated folate (bacterial production), reduced fiber intake
- H+ group: primarily reduced lactose tolerance
- 87–97% of all subtypes below vitamin D AI (adequate intake)
- >70% of M+ and H+/M+ groups consumed excessive fat
- SIBO therapy must be subtype-specific and include nutritional assessment

**Certainty:** Medium (0.45) — small n (67), single-centre observational, Polish cohort, not ME/CFS-specific.

## Paper 10: Kashyap et al. 2024 — Critical Appraisal of SIBO Hypothesis (ESNM/ANMS)

**Citation:** Kashyap P, Moayyedi P, Quigley EMM, Simren M, Vanner S. Critical appraisal of the SIBO hypothesis and breath testing: a clinical practice update endorsed by the European Society of Neurogastroenterology and Motility (ESNM) and the American Neurogastroenterology and Motility Society (ANMS). *Neurogastroenterol Motil*. 2024;36(6):e14817.
**DOI:** 10.1111/nmo.14817
**PMID:** 38798120
**Citation key:** `Kashyap2024SIBOcritique`

**Key findings (negative/critical):**
- "After two decades, [the SIBO-IBS] hypothesis remains unproven"
- Core methodological flaw: lactulose H2 breath test (LHBT) measures oro-cecal transit, not bacterial overgrowth; normal transit range 25–200 min creates widespread false positives
- Three independent teams found no time point discriminates IBS from healthy controls
- Diagnostic criteria have shifted repeatedly (20 ppm at 90 min → 180 min → added gases) without validation
- Breath test normalization does not reliably predict clinical improvement
- Antibiotic use driven by unvalidated testing raises antimicrobial stewardship concerns

**Certainty context:** High-quality critical review endorsed by two major societies. Does NOT negate the existence of SIBO or its pathogenic potential; challenges whether breath testing reliably identifies it in IBS. ME/CFS-specific SIBO research (Karhu 2023) has separate referral-bias limitations.
