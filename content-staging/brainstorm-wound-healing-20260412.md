## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 The Tissue Repair Deficit Hypothesis: NK Cells as Systemic Repair Coordinators

**Mechanistic rationale:** Sobecki 2021 demonstrates that NK cells are not passive bystanders in wound healing but upstream regulators -- their HIF-1alpha/IFN-gamma/GM-CSF output directly controls healing rate. Baraniuk 2024 meta-analysis shows ME/CFS NK cytotoxicity is halved (Hedges' g = 0.96). If NK cells are required for tissue repair generally (not just skin wounds), then ME/CFS patients should show impaired repair across ALL NK-dependent tissue maintenance processes -- not just skin wounds.

**What this predicts beyond wound healing:**
- Impaired mucosal barrier repair (gut, respiratory) -- consistent with documented gut permeability in ME/CFS (Maes et al.)
- Slower muscle damage repair after exertion -- could contribute to PEM duration
- Impaired vascular endothelial repair -- consistent with documented endothelial dysfunction (ch10)
- Slower recovery from minor viral damage to epithelial tissues

**Key insight:** The document already covers gut barrier dysfunction (ch11), endothelial dysfunction (ch10), and prolonged PEM (ch06). These are currently treated as separate phenomena with separate explanations. The tissue repair deficit hypothesis unifies them under a single upstream cause: halved NK cell function impairs the repair arm of immunity systemically.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, gut mucosal repair rate (measurable via serial lactulose-mannitol permeability tests after standardised challenge) should correlate with NK cell cytotoxicity (r > 0.4). Patients with lower NK function should show slower barrier restoration.

**Certainty:** 0.40 (strong mechanistic chain but multiple inferential steps; NK role in non-skin tissue repair is less established than in skin)

### 1.2 The Repair-Defence Trade-off Trap

**Mechanistic rationale:** Sobecki 2021 reveals a fundamental biological trade-off: under hypoxia, NK cells must choose between wound healing (via GM-CSF) and antibacterial defence (via IFN-gamma), with HIF-1alpha as the switch. In ME/CFS, tissue hypoxia is documented (elevated WASF3/HIF-1alpha in ch10) AND NK cells are dysfunctional. This creates a "trap": the hypoxic ME/CFS environment activates HIF-1alpha, which should shift NK cells toward antibacterial mode -- but ME/CFS NK cells cannot execute either programme effectively because they lack the metabolic capacity (TRPM3 calcium signalling disrupted, mitochondrial dysfunction in NK cells documented in ch07).

**The trap:** Healthy tissue under hypoxic stress can choose defence OR repair. ME/CFS tissue under chronic hypoxic stress gets neither -- NK cells receive the signal to act but cannot execute. The result is simultaneously impaired infection clearance AND impaired tissue repair.

**This explains:** Why ME/CFS patients seem to get both more infections AND slower recovery -- the trade-off mechanism that normally ensures at least one arm works is broken because the effector cells themselves are compromised.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS NK cells stimulated ex vivo under hypoxic conditions (1-3% O2) should show blunted HIF-1alpha-mediated upregulation of BOTH IFN-gamma AND GM-CSF compared to healthy controls, whereas in healthy controls, one pathway should increase at the expense of the other.

**Certainty:** 0.35 (speculative; requires ME/CFS NK cells to be studied under hypoxic conditions, which has never been done)

### 1.3 The TGF-beta Wound Healing Lock: Chronic Inflammatory Resolution Without Completion

**Mechanistic rationale:** Petri 2017 shows that TGF-beta + IL-6 from mesenchymal stromal cells (MSCs) pushes NK cells into a regulatory, non-cytotoxic phenotype. This is normally time-limited -- early phase: MSCs enhance NK killing; late phase: TGF-beta terminates inflammation for wound resolution. But in ME/CFS, TGF-beta is chronically elevated (ch07, the "TGF-beta Rosetta Stone" hypothesis). This means the wound healing programme may be constitutively stuck in the "resolution" phase without having completed the "clearance" phase.

**The paradox:** ME/CFS may produce wounds that close (apparent healing) but with poor quality repair -- premature fibrotic resolution without adequate immune clearance of damaged tissue. This would explain why patients don't report dramatically delayed wound closure but might have increased scarring, keloid formation, or subclinical tissue fragility.

**Connection to existing content:** This directly extends the TGF-beta Rosetta Stone hypothesis (hyp:tgf-beta-rosetta) and the TGF-beta epigenetic lock-in speculation (spec:tgf-beta-lock) in ch07. The wound healing literature provides a concrete functional consequence of the TGF-beta-mediated NK suppression beyond just "reduced cytotoxicity."

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients should show (1) apparently normal wound closure times but (2) histologically abnormal scar tissue -- more fibrotic, less organized collagen, reduced tensile strength compared to controls. Suction blister biopsy with histological analysis at 14 and 28 days would test this.

**Certainty:** 0.35 (speculative; requires linking chronic TGF-beta to wound quality rather than speed, which is untested)

### 1.4 The Autonomic Wound Healing Phenotype: Sympathetic Overactivation as Anti-Repair Signal

**Mechanistic rationale:** Xue 2018 identifies two macrophage populations with opposing wound healing effects: CD64+CCR2+ (beta2-adrenergic, SNS-responsive, pro-inflammatory, anti-repair) and CD64+CCR2- (alpha7-nAChR, PNS-responsive, anti-inflammatory, pro-repair). ME/CFS is characterised by sympathetic overactivation and parasympathetic withdrawal (POTS, reduced HRV). This ANS imbalance would constitutively favour the pro-inflammatory, anti-repair macrophage phenotype in tissues.

**Extension beyond wound healing:** If sympathetic tone biases macrophage polarisation toward inflammatory/anti-repair in wounds, the same mechanism should operate in ALL tissues with macrophage-mediated repair:
- Brain: microglial polarisation toward M1 (neuroinflammatory) rather than M2 (neuroprotective) -- already documented in ME/CFS
- Gut: intestinal macrophage polarisation toward barrier-disrupting rather than barrier-restoring
- Muscle: satellite cell activation and muscle repair after exercise

**This creates a unifying mechanism:** Autonomic dysfunction -> macrophage polarisation bias -> impaired tissue repair across organs. The document already describes sympathetic overactivation (ch10) and microglial activation (ch08) as separate phenomena. This hypothesis suggests they may be two manifestations of a single ANS-driven macrophage polarisation defect.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with higher sympathetic tone (lower HRV, higher resting HR, more severe POTS) should show (1) slower wound healing, (2) more neuroinflammation (PET ligand binding), and (3) worse gut permeability, all correlating with the same ANS measure.

**Certainty:** 0.45 (multiple independent lines support ANS-macrophage link; ME/CFS-specific data lacking)

### 1.5 The Immune Exhaustion Cascade: Wound Healing as Canary in the Coal Mine

**Mechanistic rationale:** Stanojcic 2016 shows that the immune exhaustion phenotype (blunted early response, hyperinflammatory late response) directly predicts wound healing impairment. Barrenas 2019 shows the inverse -- species that maintain wound healing avoid immune exhaustion. This suggests wound healing capacity and immune exhaustion are bidirectionally linked: immune exhaustion impairs wound healing, AND failure to maintain wound healing accelerates immune exhaustion.

**The vicious cycle:** In ME/CFS:
1. Initial infection triggers immune activation
2. Failure to clear infection -> immune exhaustion (documented)
3. Immune exhaustion -> impaired tissue repair (Stanojcic)
4. Impaired tissue repair -> ongoing tissue damage -> more immune activation
5. More immune activation -> deeper exhaustion (loop closes)

**Connection to disease progression:** This model predicts that ME/CFS should worsen over time (progressive immune exhaustion) unless the tissue repair arm is restored. It also explains why some patients stabilise (those who achieve sufficient rest to allow partial tissue repair) while others progressively deteriorate (those with ongoing tissue damage from activity, infection, or stress).

**Connection to Barrenas 2019:** African green monkeys that naturally control SIV do so by MAINTAINING wound healing and tissue repair. The analogous strategy in ME/CFS would be to restore tissue repair capacity (support wound healing) as a way to BREAK immune exhaustion -- the reverse of the usual therapeutic direction (targeting immunity to help tissue).

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a longitudinal ME/CFS cohort, wound healing rate (standardised blister assay) at baseline should predict disease trajectory at 12 months -- patients with better wound healing at baseline should be less likely to worsen, controlling for baseline severity.

**Certainty:** 0.35 (bidirectional link is speculative; Barrenas data is from SIV/primates, not human ME/CFS)

### 1.6 Post-Infectious Epigenetic Repair Deficit

**Mechanistic rationale:** Abhimanyu 2021 demonstrates that post-infectious epigenetic scars impair BOTH cytotoxic AND wound repair arms of immunity. The AMPK-mTOR, NFAT, and NR4A pathways are epigenetically silenced. In ME/CFS, post-infectious onset is the most common pattern (>70% of cases), and epigenetic changes in immune cells are documented (ch07: T cell exhaustion epigenetics, trained immunity gone wrong).

**What's new:** The document currently treats epigenetic immune changes primarily as explaining chronicity of IMMUNE dysfunction. Abhimanyu 2021 shows that the SAME epigenetic changes also impair TISSUE REPAIR. This means ME/CFS epigenetic changes may produce TWO concurrent deficits from a single cause:
- Deficit 1: Impaired immune surveillance (already well-covered in ch07)
- Deficit 2: Impaired tissue repair capacity (NOT covered anywhere)

**Therapeutic implication:** Epigenetic modifiers (HDAC inhibitors, DNA hypomethylating agents) could potentially reverse BOTH deficits simultaneously.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with documented post-infectious onset should show greater wound healing impairment than those with gradual onset, AND this difference should correlate with the degree of epigenetic modification at NFAT/NR4A loci in circulating monocytes.

**Certainty:** 0.40 (strong mechanistic foundation from Abhimanyu; ME/CFS-specific epigenetic data exists but not linked to repair capacity)

### 1.7 Demand-Responsiveness Dimension for the Selective Dysfunction Model

**Mechanistic rationale:** The selective dysfunction model (ch14j) uses S(P) = alpha * delta_CNS + beta * rho_tilde + gamma * delta_CNS * rho_tilde. Wound healing was initially classified as preserved (delta_CNS=0.2, rho=0.2, S=0.24), but this was incorrect -- wound healing is demand-responsive (rho should be much higher: it can scale 10x+ under injury) and has moderate CNS dependency (autonomic regulation of macrophage polarisation, sympathetic effects on vascular tone at wound site).

**Proposed reclassification:**
- delta_CNS: 0.4 (autonomic regulation of macrophage polarisation, sympathetic vascular control, HPA cortisol effects on inflammation -- not fully CNS-dependent but significantly CNS-modulated)
- rho: 3.0 -> rho_tilde = 0.75 (wound healing output can increase dramatically from zero at baseline to massive tissue mobilisation after injury; this is one of the most demand-responsive processes in the body)
- Predicted S = 0.6*0.4 + 0.5*0.75 + 0.4*0.4*0.75 = 0.24 + 0.375 + 0.12 = 0.735

**Implication:** With corrected parameters, wound healing moves from the "preserved" category (S=0.24) to "moderate dysfunction" (S=0.735), which is consistent with the mechanistic prediction of impairment.

## 2. Research Directions

### 2.1 The ME/CFS Wound Healing Study (Priority: HIGH)

**Design:** Prospective case-control, n=40 ME/CFS + 40 age/sex-matched controls
**Primary outcome:** Suction blister reepithelialization time (standardised 8mm blister, negative pressure method)
**Secondary outcomes:**
- Histological scar quality at day 14 and day 28 (collagen organisation, inflammatory infiltrate, vascularity)
- Blister fluid cytokine panel (IFN-gamma, GM-CSF, TGF-beta, IL-6, IL-10)
- Correlate with NK cell cytotoxicity, HRV (sympathetic/parasympathetic balance), disease severity (Bell score)
**Stratify by:** Disease severity (mild/moderate/severe), disease duration (<3yr, 3-7yr, >7yr), POTS comorbidity

**Why this study is uniquely informative:** It simultaneously tests:
1. Whether wound healing IS actually impaired (resolving the open question oq:wound-healing-mecfs)
2. Which mechanism dominates (NK vs. ANS vs. immune exhaustion -- blister fluid cytokines distinguish these)
3. Whether the selective dysfunction model's predictions are correct
4. Whether wound healing rate correlates with disease severity (biomarker potential)

**Practical advantage:** Suction blister assay is established in dermatology, low-risk, requires no specialist equipment beyond a suction chamber, and can be done at bedside (critical for severe patients). Costs approximately 500 EUR per patient including histology.

**Certainty that this study would be informative:** 0.85

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### 3.1 Low-Dose Dexamethasone for Wound Quality (NOT Speed)

**Rationale:** If TGF-beta-mediated premature fibrotic resolution (Hypothesis 1.3) is real, the problem is not slow healing but BAD healing (excessive fibrosis, poor collagen organisation). Low-dose glucocorticoids reduce TGF-beta signalling and fibrosis in wound models. However, glucocorticoids are generally considered immunosuppressive and are not recommended in ME/CFS.

**Reframe:** The hypothesis predicts that ME/CFS wounds heal at normal speed but with poor quality. If confirmed, topical (not systemic) application of low-dose dexamethasone to wounds could improve scar quality without systemic effects.

**Risk:** Low (topical only); moderate if systemic
**Certainty:** 0.25 (speculative; wound quality in ME/CFS not yet measured)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS wound scars treated with topical dexamethasone should show improved collagen organisation vs. untreated ME/CFS scars at 28 days

---

### 3.2 Propranolol as Wound Healing Support (Via Beta2-Adrenergic Blockade)

**Rationale:** Xue 2018 shows that the pro-inflammatory, anti-repair macrophage subset (CD64+CCR2+) is beta2-adrenergic receptor responsive. Beta-blockers (propranolol) are already used in some ME/CFS patients for tachycardia management. If sympathetic overactivation drives macrophage polarisation toward the anti-repair phenotype, propranolol may have a secondary benefit: improving tissue repair by reducing beta2-adrenergic drive to wound macrophages.

**Already in clinical use:** Propranolol is prescribed for POTS/tachycardia in ME/CFS. The wound-healing benefit would be a bonus, not a new intervention.

**Known:** Propranolol improves healing of burns and chronic wounds in non-ME/CFS contexts (multiple studies). The mechanism (reduced catecholamine-driven inflammation) aligns with the Xue 2018 findings.

**Risk:** Already prescribed; no additional risk
**Certainty:** 0.50 (propranolol-wound healing link is established in burns literature; ME/CFS-specific benefit is extrapolated)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients on propranolol should show faster wound healing than ME/CFS patients not on propranolol (retrospective chart review feasible)

---

### 3.3 Pentoxifylline for Tissue Repair Support

**Rationale:** Pentoxifylline is a methylxanthine derivative that (1) improves microcirculation (relevant to ME/CFS tissue hypoxia), (2) reduces TNF-alpha and fibrosis (relevant to TGF-beta-driven premature resolution), (3) enhances wound healing in diabetic and venous ulcer contexts, (4) modulates macrophage polarisation toward M2/repair phenotype. It crosses the blood-brain barrier and has neuroprotective properties.

**ME/CFS relevance:** Addresses multiple wound-healing-relevant pathways simultaneously:
- Microcirculation (tissue hypoxia documented in ch10)
- TNF-alpha/TGF-beta balance (immune dysfunction in ch07)
- Macrophage polarisation (autonomic-immune axis)
- Rheological properties (improved RBC deformability -- relevant if microclotting is present)

**Risk:** Low; well-tolerated drug with decades of safety data; common side effects are GI (nausea). Caution with bleeding risk.
**Certainty:** 0.35 (mechanistically sound across multiple pathways; no ME/CFS-specific data)
**Falsifiable prediction:** Pentoxifylline 400mg TID for 8 weeks should improve wound healing rate, reduce circulating TGF-beta, and improve microvascular function (nailfold capillaroscopy) in ME/CFS patients

---

### 3.4 Pirfenidone as Anti-Fibrotic Repair Normaliser

**Rationale:** If ME/CFS wound healing produces excessive fibrosis due to chronic TGF-beta (Hypothesis 1.3), pirfenidone (approved for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis) could normalise repair quality. It directly inhibits TGF-beta signalling and has anti-fibrotic effects in multiple organ systems.

**Risk:** Moderate (photosensitivity, GI effects, liver monitoring required)
**Certainty:** 0.20 (highly speculative; wound quality defect not yet demonstrated in ME/CFS)
**Status:** Research-stage only; would require wound quality data first

---

### 3.5 HDAC Inhibitors for Epigenetic Immune Repair Restoration

**Rationale:** Abhimanyu 2021 shows that HDAC inhibitors can reverse post-infectious epigenetic immune suppression, specifically restoring both cytotoxic AND repair arms of immunity. If ME/CFS immune dysfunction involves epigenetic lock-in (supported by ch07 T cell exhaustion data), HDAC inhibitors could potentially unlock both immune surveillance and tissue repair capacity.

**Drugs:** Vorinostat (SAHA), romidepsin, panobinostat -- all FDA-approved for cancer; significant toxicity limits use
**Low-dose approach:** Low-dose vorinostat has been explored in HIV latency reversal with acceptable safety. Could a similar low-dose approach modulate epigenetic immune suppression in ME/CFS?

**Risk:** HIGH at oncology doses; moderate-to-low at sub-therapeutic doses (untested)
**Certainty:** 0.25 (mechanism is compelling; clinical feasibility in ME/CFS is unknown)
**Status:** Research-stage only; needs ex vivo proof of concept first (Study 2.5)

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### 4.1 Zinc + Vitamin C for Wound-Competent Immunity

**Rationale:** Zinc is required for NK cell cytotoxic function (zinc-dependent granule release) and collagen synthesis (zinc-dependent matrix metalloproteinases). Vitamin C is essential for collagen hydroxylation and neutrophil function. Both are commonly deficient or suboptimal in chronic illness. The document already mentions zinc in ch16 as "energy-neutral" (Category B).

**ME/CFS context:** If wound healing is impaired via NK cell dysfunction, ensuring zinc sufficiency removes one potential rate-limiting cofactor. Won't fix the fundamental NK exhaustion, but removes a compounding deficiency.

**Dose:** Zinc 15-30mg/day (as zinc picolinate or bisglycinate), Vitamin C 500-1000mg/day
**Risk:** Very low at recommended doses
**Certainty:** 0.50 (zinc/C for wound healing is well-established; ME/CFS-specific benefit is moderate extrapolation)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with low zinc status who supplement should show greater wound healing improvement than those with adequate zinc status

### 4.2 AHCC (Active Hexose Correlated Compound) for NK Cell Repair Function

**Rationale:** AHCC is a mushroom-derived compound with clinical evidence for enhancing NK cell activity (increased NK cell number and cytotoxicity in cancer patients, post-surgical patients). If NK cells are required for wound healing (Sobecki 2021), restoring NK function via AHCC could improve both immune surveillance AND tissue repair.

**ME/CFS context:** Already discussed in patient communities; some anecdotal reports of benefit. The wound healing connection provides a new mechanistic rationale beyond just "boosting immunity."

**Dose:** 1-3g/day
**Risk:** Low; good safety profile
**Certainty:** 0.35 (AHCC-NK link is established; whether it can overcome the depth of ME/CFS NK dysfunction is uncertain; wound healing benefit is extrapolated)
**Falsifiable prediction:** AHCC 3g/day for 12 weeks should increase NK cytotoxicity AND improve wound healing rate in ME/CFS patients, with correlation between the two improvements

### 4.3 Omega-3 Fatty Acids for Resolution Without Premature Fibrosis

**Rationale:** Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) are precursors to specialised pro-resolving mediators (SPMs: resolvins, protectins, maresins). SPMs promote CONTROLLED inflammation resolution -- they terminate inflammation without premature fibrosis. This is distinct from anti-inflammatory drugs that suppress inflammation (which could worsen the "resolution without completion" problem).

**ME/CFS context:** If TGF-beta drives premature fibrotic resolution (Hypothesis 1.3), SPMs from omega-3 could provide an alternative resolution pathway that completes repair properly. The difference: TGF-beta-driven resolution = fibrotic, incomplete; SPM-driven resolution = organised, complete.

**Dose:** EPA+DHA 2-4g/day
**Risk:** Very low
**Certainty:** 0.40 (SPM biology is well-established; application to ME/CFS wound repair is novel extrapolation)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients supplementing omega-3 at 3g/day for 8 weeks should show improved wound scar quality (less fibrosis, better collagen organisation) without change in healing speed

### 4.4 Beta-Glucan for Trained Immunity Restoration

**Rationale:** Abhimanyu 2021 describes beta-glucan as capable of inducing trained immunity -- the beneficial form of innate immune memory. If ME/CFS involves "trained immunity gone wrong" (ch07, ch08), beta-glucan could potentially re-train innate immune cells toward a healthier baseline, restoring both cytotoxic and repair functions.

**Caution:** In ME/CFS, where immune activation is already problematic, stimulating innate immunity could worsen symptoms. Beta-glucan's effects are complex and context-dependent.

**Dose:** 250-500mg/day (yeast-derived beta-1,3/1,6-glucan)
**Risk:** Moderate (potential for immune activation flares in ME/CFS)
**Certainty:** 0.25 (mechanism is sound; risk of worsening is real; ME/CFS-specific data absent)
**Falsifiable prediction:** Low-dose beta-glucan (250mg/day) should shift monocyte epigenetic profiles toward repair-competent phenotype without worsening symptom scores over 8 weeks

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### 5.1 Transcutaneous Auricular Vagus Nerve Stimulation (taVNS) for Repair-Permissive Autonomic Shift

**Rationale:** Xue 2018 shows parasympathetic activation promotes wound healing via alpha7-nAChR-expressing repair macrophages. ME/CFS involves parasympathetic withdrawal. taVNS increases parasympathetic tone. Therefore, taVNS should shift macrophage polarisation toward the repair phenotype.

**Already in document:** taVNS is discussed in ch18 as an emerging therapy for ME/CFS, but the mechanism is framed as "cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway" and "ANS reset." The wound healing literature adds a specific mechanism: parasympathetic activation directly promotes tissue repair via macrophage polarisation, not just reduces inflammation.

**Reframe for treatment chapter:** taVNS may have benefits beyond symptom management -- it may actively promote tissue repair by shifting macrophage polarisation. This could explain why some patients report gradual improvement over weeks/months of taVNS use (tissue repair takes time).

**Risk:** Very low
**Certainty:** 0.45 (taVNS-parasympathetic link established; parasympathetic-macrophage-repair link established in Xue 2018; connection to ME/CFS is logical but untested)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients using taVNS daily for 8 weeks should show improved suction blister healing rate AND increased HF-HRV, with correlation between the two

### 5.2 Strategic Rest Intervals as "Repair Windows"

**Rationale:** If tissue repair is impaired in ME/CFS and worsened by sympathetic activation (stress, activity), then strategic periods of deep rest -- beyond pacing -- could create "repair windows" where parasympathetic tone is sufficient for tissue repair macrophage activation. This is analogous to the Barrenas 2019 finding: natural SIV hosts that control disease maintain tissue repair mechanisms, which requires the right immune state.

**Practical implication:** Current pacing advice focuses on not exceeding energy envelopes. The wound healing literature suggests an additional principle: ensure SUFFICIENT rest to enable tissue repair, not just avoid crashes. The minimum effective rest may be more than "not crashing."

**Connection to PEM:** PEM involves tissue-level damage (exercise-induced inflammation, muscle damage, neuroinflammation). Recovery from PEM therefore requires tissue repair. If repair is impaired, PEM recovery takes longer -- consistent with the 24-72h PEM duration in ME/CFS. Optimising conditions for repair (deep rest, parasympathetic dominance) may shorten PEM duration.

**Risk:** None (rest is already recommended)
**Certainty:** 0.40 (logical extension of existing pacing principles; wound healing evidence provides mechanistic support)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients who add a structured 2-hour "deep rest" period daily (dark, quiet, supine, no screens) should show shorter PEM duration and improved wound healing rate over 3 months compared to patients with the same total rest time distributed throughout the day

### 5.3 Grounding/Earthing: A More Specific Mechanistic Hypothesis

**Rationale:** The document already cites Oschman 2015 on grounding and inflammation. The wound healing literature suggests a more specific mechanism: if sympathetic overactivation impairs wound healing via beta2-adrenergic macrophage polarisation (Xue 2018), and grounding has been shown to reduce sympathovagal ratio in some studies, then grounding could improve tissue repair via the same autonomic-macrophage pathway as taVNS.

**Caution:** Grounding evidence is weak and methodologically limited. However, the wound healing literature provides a testable mechanistic pathway (ANS -> macrophage polarisation -> repair) that could be measured independently of subjective symptom reports.

**Risk:** None
**Certainty:** 0.20 (grounding evidence is weak; mechanism is plausible but grounding may not produce meaningful ANS shifts)

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 Reclassification of Wound Healing in Table @tab:process-classification

**Current (incorrect):** delta_CNS=0.2, rho_tilde=0.2, S=0.24, Observed="Preserved", Certainty=0.6

**Rationale:** Wound healing has moderate CNS dependency (autonomic control of vascular tone, macrophage polarisation via ANS, HPA axis cortisol effects) and high demand-responsiveness (dormant at baseline, massive mobilisation on injury). The literature integration demonstrates this.

### 7.2 Should the Model Add a Third Dimension?

**The question:** The current model uses two dimensions: CNS-dependency (delta_CNS) and demand-responsiveness (rho_tilde). Wound healing's vulnerability comes partly from a third factor: multi-system coordination dependency. Wound healing requires NK cells, macrophages, fibroblasts, endothelial cells, platelets, and extracellular matrix components acting in temporal sequence. Even if no single step is CNS-dependent, the coordination across cell types introduces vulnerability.

**Proposed third dimension: Coordination complexity index kappa(P) in [0,1]**

$kappa(P) = 1 - 1/N_{"cell types"}$

where N_cell_types is the number of distinct cell types required for process completion.

- Hair growth: kappa = 1 - 1/2 = 0.5 (keratinocytes + dermal papilla)
- Wound healing: kappa = 1 - 1/6 = 0.83 (platelets, neutrophils, macrophages, NK cells, fibroblasts, endothelial cells)
- Exercise: kappa = 1 - 1/4 = 0.75 (motor neurons, muscle fibres, cardiovascular, respiratory)

**Extended model:** S(P) = alpha * delta_CNS + beta * rho_tilde + gamma * delta_CNS * rho_tilde + epsilon * kappa

**Problem:** This may be overparameterising. With 4 parameters and ~10 observable processes, overfitting is likely. Additionally, kappa may correlate strongly with delta_CNS (CNS-coordinated processes tend to involve more cell types). A principal components analysis on independently estimated delta_CNS, rho_tilde, and kappa values (from healthy physiology literature) would determine whether kappa adds explanatory power.

**Recommendation:** Do NOT add kappa to the formal model yet. Instead, note it as a potential extension pending the wound healing empirical data. If wound healing turns out to be more impaired than the 2-dimensional model predicts, kappa becomes a candidate explanation.

**Certainty:** 0.30 (the concept is sound but adding dimensions to an unfitted model is premature)

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### 8.1 Fibromyalgia: Shared ANS-Macrophage-Repair Axis

**Evidence:** Fibromyalgia patients show documented impaired wound healing, sympathetic overactivation, and altered macrophage function. The Xue 2018 mechanism (ANS -> macrophage polarisation -> repair) could operate identically in fibromyalgia and ME/CFS.

**Implication:** If both conditions share the ANS-macrophage-repair mechanism, wound healing rate should correlate with pain severity in BOTH conditions -- and interventions that improve wound healing (beta-blockers, taVNS) should improve pain.

**Shared vulnerability:** FM and ME/CFS may share a tissue-repair deficit that manifests differently because of the primary tissue affected (FM: muscle/fascia; ME/CFS: CNS/immune system).

**Certainty:** 0.40

---

### 8.2 hEDS/POTS Triad: Connective Tissue Repair as Common Denominator

**Evidence:** The hEDS-POTS-MCAS triad co-occurs frequently with ME/CFS (documented in references). hEDS involves inherently fragile connective tissue with slow wound healing. POTS involves autonomic dysfunction. MCAS involves immune dysregulation. The wound healing literature suggests these are not independent comorbidities but share a common vulnerability: impaired tissue repair.

**Hypothesis:** In hEDS+ME/CFS overlap, the connective tissue fragility (hEDS) compounds the repair deficit (ME/CFS), producing more severe clinical phenotype than either alone. The hEDS collagen defect means more tissue damage per unit of mechanical stress; the ME/CFS repair deficit means slower recovery from that damage.

**Prediction:** hEDS+ME/CFS patients should show worse wound healing than either condition alone (multiplicative, not additive, effect).

**Treatment implication:** hEDS+ME/CFS patients may particularly benefit from the "tissue repair stack" (Section 6.1), as they have both increased demand for and decreased capacity of tissue repair.

**Certainty:** 0.40

---

### 8.3 Long COVID: The Repair Deficit as Post-Infectious Signature

**Evidence:** Long COVID shares immune exhaustion, NK dysfunction, autonomic dysregulation, and post-infectious epigenetic changes with ME/CFS. If these produce tissue repair deficits (as the wound healing literature suggests), Long COVID patients should ALSO show impaired wound healing.

**Implication:** Wound healing assay could serve as a simple clinical test to identify Long COVID patients with ME/CFS-like pathophysiology (as opposed to other Long COVID phenotypes). This could help stratify the heterogeneous Long COVID population.

**Certainty:** 0.45

## 9. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### 9.1 Wound Healing Rate as ME/CFS Severity Biomarker

**Concept:** Suction blister healing time as an objective, functional biomarker for ME/CFS severity.

**Advantages:**
- Objective (no patient reporting bias)
- Functional (tests actual physiological capacity, not just a blood marker)
- Integrative (reflects NK function, ANS balance, immune exhaustion, and tissue repair simultaneously)
- Simple (suction blister method is standardised, low-cost, bedside-feasible)
- Reproducible (well-established dermatological technique)

**What it would measure:** Because wound healing requires NK cells, autonomic balance, macrophage polarisation, and metabolic capacity, a single wound healing rate measurement integrates information about ALL these systems. It is effectively a "systems biology readout" disguised as a simple clinical test.

**Prediction:** Wound healing time should correlate with:
- Bell disability score (r > 0.4)
- NK cell cytotoxicity (r > 0.3)
- HRV (HF-HRV) (r > 0.3)
- PEM duration (r > 0.3)

If these correlations hold, wound healing time could be a single composite biomarker that captures the multi-system dysfunction of ME/CFS.

**Practical:** Could be added to existing research protocols with minimal additional burden. Particularly valuable for severe/bedbound patients where exercise testing (CPET) is impossible.

**Certainty:** 0.40 (no data yet; conceptually strong; requires empirical validation)

---

### 9.2 Blister Fluid Cytokine Panel as Mechanistic Subtyping Tool

**Concept:** The suction blister method produces not just a wound but recoverable blister fluid containing local cytokines. Analysing this fluid could reveal which mechanism dominates in each patient:

- High IFN-gamma, low GM-CSF: NK cells shifted to defence mode (HIF-1alpha pathway active, Sobecki 2021)
- Low IFN-gamma, low GM-CSF: NK cells globally non-functional (general exhaustion)
- High TGF-beta in fluid: MSC-NK regulatory circuit activated (Petri 2017 mechanism)
- High catecholamines in fluid: Sympathetic drive to wound macrophages (Xue 2018 mechanism)

**Subtyping potential:** Different cytokine patterns would suggest different dominant mechanisms and therefore different treatment approaches:
- Pattern A (NK exhaustion dominant): AHCC, zinc, rest
- Pattern B (autonomic dominant): taVNS, propranolol
- Pattern C (TGF-beta dominant): omega-3, pentoxifylline
- Pattern D (epigenetic dominant): consider research-stage epigenetic modifiers

**Certainty:** 0.30 (logical but requires validation that blister fluid cytokines actually distinguish these patterns)

---

### 9.3 Serial Wound Healing as Treatment Response Monitor

**Concept:** If wound healing rate reflects multi-system function, serial suction blister assays (e.g., every 3 months) could track treatment response objectively. Improvement in wound healing rate would indicate that the intervention is improving the underlying pathophysiology, not just masking symptoms.

**Advantage over current endpoints:** ME/CFS clinical trials typically use subjective fatigue scales (Chalder, MFI) which have poor reliability. An objective functional measure would strengthen trial designs.

**Certainty:** 0.35 (requires first establishing that wound healing IS impaired and variable in ME/CFS)

## Caveats and Limitations

1. **No direct data:** No study has measured wound healing in ME/CFS patients. All hypotheses here are mechanistic extrapolations.
2. **Mouse models:** The Sobecki 2021 and Xue 2018 findings are from mouse models. Human translation is indirect.
3. **Inference chains:** Most ideas involve 2-3 inferential steps (ME/CFS has X -> X affects Y -> Y should produce Z). Each step introduces uncertainty that compounds multiplicatively.
4. **Publication bias risk:** The wound healing literature was specifically sought to challenge the "preserved function" claim. Negative results (studies showing preserved wound healing in immune-exhausted or sympathetically-activated states) may exist but were not found because they are less likely to be published or searched for.
5. **The clinical silence problem:** ME/CFS patients and clinicians have NOT flagged wound healing as a major complaint. This could mean (a) it is genuinely preserved through unknown compensatory mechanisms, (b) impairment is subclinical, or (c) patients have low wound burden (sedentary/homebound = few injuries to heal).
6. **All treatment ideas require validation.** None should be implemented based on mechanistic reasoning alone.
