## Tier 1 — High Relevance, Well-Supported (certainty >= 0.30)

### Postpartum Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (PRIS) as ME/CFS Trigger
**Category:** Novel hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Mechanistic rationale:** Pregnancy induces a Th2-shifted, tolerogenic immune state (elevated regulatory T cells, decreased NK cytotoxicity, IDO induction in placenta). Postpartum, this tolerance collapses rapidly within 3-6 months as estrogen/progesterone fall. In susceptible women (latent viral reservoirs, prior immune priming), this rebound may resemble HIV-IRIS — pathological reactivation against persistent antigens (EBV, HHV-6, parvovirus B19) producing chronic inflammation that crystallizes into ME/CFS. The Slack et al. observation of 3-6 month postpartum relapse aligns precisely with the timescale of immune reconstitution.
**Evidence link:** Slack et al. 2023 (3-6 mo postpartum relapse); Schacterle & Komaroff 2004 (29% worsened); Thomas et al. 2022 (HPA-HPG axis); known IRIS literature in HIV
**Falsifiable prediction:** Women developing ME/CFS within 12 months postpartum will show (a) higher EBV/HHV-6 viral loads in 1st-trimester banked samples than postpartum-healthy controls and (b) steeper Treg decline between 3rd trimester and 3 months postpartum.
**Integration target:** ch07 (immune dysfunction) or ch12 (triggers/onset); ch25b (sex-specific)

### Pregnancy as a Natural Experiment for ME/CFS Heterogeneity
**Category:** Research direction / novel hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Mechanistic rationale:** The 30% improve / 29% worsen / 41% unchanged tripartite split in Schacterle & Komaroff is too clean to be noise — it suggests three distinct pathophysiological subtypes whose dominant driver responds differently to the gestational state. Estrogen-responsive (improvers: autoimmune-dominant), cortisol/volume-responsive (improvers: dysautonomia/POTS-dominant), or metabolically-strained (worsen: mitochondrial/energy-dominant where the metabolic load of pregnancy overwhelms reserves).
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff 2004 tripartite split; Thomas et al. 2022 hormonal modulation; established estrogen immunomodulation literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-pregnancy stratification by (a) autoantibody panel (β2-AR, M3-AChR), (b) tilt-table response, (c) CPET workload threshold will predict pregnancy trajectory with concordance >0.6. Specifically: autoantibody-positive women will improve; CPET-low women will worsen.
**Integration target:** ch12 (subtypes); ch23 (epidemiology/outcomes); ch25b

### Endometriosis-ME/CFS Shared Mast Cell / Neuroinflammation Axis
**Category:** Novel hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Mechanistic rationale:** OR 2.79 for endometriosis is too high to be coincidence or referral bias alone. Both conditions feature: (1) mast cell hyperactivation with elevated tryptase, (2) peripheral nerve sensitization, (3) estrogen-driven inflammation, (4) microbiome dysbiosis. Endometriosis lesions produce IL-1β, TNF-α, NGF — chronic low-grade systemic inflammation that may seed central sensitization. Conversely, ME/CFS-associated immune dysregulation may permit retrograde menstruation implants to escape clearance.
**Evidence link:** Compton et al. 2025 (OR 2.79); Boneva 2011/2015 (gynecological history); MCAS-ME/CFS overlap literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with comorbid endometriosis will show higher serum tryptase, IL-1β, and NGF than ME/CFS-only patients matched for severity. Surgical excision of endometriotic lesions will reduce ME/CFS symptom severity by >20% on a validated scale at 6 months in >40% of dual-diagnosis patients.
**Integration target:** ch08 (comorbidities); ch07 (immune); ch25b

### Early Menopause as Marker of Accelerated Ovarian Aging in ME/CFS
**Category:** Diagnostic/biomarker
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Mechanistic rationale:** Boneva 2015 found mean menopause at 37.6 vs. 48.6 years — an 11-year acceleration is extraordinary and parallels findings in autoimmune diseases (lupus, RA). Possible mechanisms: (1) chronic inflammation accelerating follicular atresia, (2) anti-ovarian autoantibodies, (3) mitochondrial dysfunction in oocytes, (4) HPG axis suppression from chronic HPA dysregulation. AMH (anti-Müllerian hormone) would be a tractable biomarker.
**Evidence link:** Boneva 2011/2015; analogy to autoimmune POI (premature ovarian insufficiency)
**Falsifiable prediction:** Age-matched ME/CFS women aged 25-40 will have AMH levels >1 SD below healthy controls; AMH will correlate negatively with disease duration (r < -0.3).
**Integration target:** ch11 (biomarkers); ch25b; ch08 (comorbidities)

## Tier 2 — Medium Relevance, Partially Supported (certainty 0.15-0.29)

### Progesterone Allopregnanolone Withdrawal as Postpartum Crash Driver
**Category:** Novel hypothesis / drug idea
**Certainty:** 0.25
**Mechanistic rationale:** Allopregnanolone (progesterone metabolite, GABA-A positive allosteric modulator) is 10-100x elevated in pregnancy and crashes precipitously postpartum. In women with subclinical autonomic/sleep instability, this withdrawal could unmask or amplify ME/CFS-like dysregulation, paralleling postpartum depression mechanisms. Brexanolone (allopregnanolone analog) is FDA-approved for PPD.
**Evidence link:** Slack et al. 2023 (postpartum relapse); Thomas et al. 2022 (neurosteroids); brexanolone PPD literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** Postpartum women developing ME/CFS will show steeper allopregnanolone decline trajectory (3rd trimester to 6 weeks postpartum) than healthy postpartum controls. A taper-down protocol of micronized progesterone over 4-8 weeks postpartum will reduce ME/CFS incidence in high-risk women (prior CFS-like episodes) compared to abrupt cessation.
**Integration target:** ch14d (treatments); ch25b; ch12

### Placental sFlt-1 / sEng as ME/CFS Worsening Predictor in Pregnancy
**Category:** Diagnostic/biomarker
**Certainty:** 0.20
**Mechanistic rationale:** sFlt-1 (soluble fms-like tyrosine kinase) and soluble endoglin drive preeclampsia by causing endothelial dysfunction. ME/CFS features endothelial dysfunction (impaired flow-mediated dilation, microvascular abnormalities). The 30% spontaneous abortion rate in Schacterle/Komaroff may reflect pre-existing endothelial fragility unmasked by placentation demands. sFlt-1/PlGF ratio is already clinically used.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff 2004 (high SAB rate); ME/CFS endothelial dysfunction literature (Scherbakov, Wirth/Scheibenbogen)
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS pregnancies will show elevated sFlt-1/PlGF ratio at 20 weeks compared to matched healthy controls, and the elevation will correlate with subsequent pregnancy worsening of CFS symptoms (r > 0.3).
**Integration target:** ch11 (biomarkers); ch25b

### Microchimerism Persistence and Long-Term ME/CFS Risk
**Category:** Cross-disease bridge / novel hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.18
**Mechanistic rationale:** Fetal microchimeric cells persist in maternal tissues for decades and have been implicated in autoimmune diseases (scleroderma, Hashimoto's). If ME/CFS has an autoimmune component, fetal microchimerism — particularly mismatched HLA — could provide an antigenic stimulus that, combined with postpartum immune reconstitution, initiates pathology. This would predict parity-related risk gradients.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff 2004 onset patterns; established microchimerism-autoimmunity literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** Parity will positively correlate with adult-onset (>30y) ME/CFS incidence in women, with effect size larger than in nulliparous controls (HR > 1.3 per pregnancy). Y-chromosome microchimerism will be detectable at higher frequency in ME/CFS women with male offspring than in matched controls.
**Integration target:** ch07; ch12; ch23

### Iron-Hepcidin Axis Disruption Linking Heavy Menses, Pregnancy, and ME/CFS
**Category:** Novel hypothesis / supplement
**Certainty:** 0.22
**Mechanistic rationale:** Boneva noted high rates of menorrhagia and hysterectomy. Chronic iron deficiency impairs mitochondrial function (cytochrome c oxidase, aconitase) and oxygen delivery. Pregnancy doubles iron demand. Inflammation elevates hepcidin, blocking iron absorption — creating functional iron deficiency despite normal stores (anemia of chronic disease pattern). This may explain why some patients respond to IV iron despite normal ferritin.
**Evidence link:** Boneva 2011/2015 (menorrhagia, hysterectomy); ME/CFS mitochondrial literature; hepcidin-inflammation literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS women will show elevated hepcidin/ferritin ratios compared to healthy menstruating controls. IV ferric carboxymaltose will improve fatigue scores in ME/CFS women with elevated hepcidin (>5 ng/mL) more than in those with normal hepcidin (effect size difference >0.4 SD).
**Integration target:** ch14d (treatments); ch11 (biomarkers); ch25b

### Oxytocin Dysregulation in ME/CFS Postpartum Trajectory
**Category:** Novel hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.18
**Mechanistic rationale:** Oxytocin has anti-inflammatory, vagal-tone-enhancing, and HPA-dampening effects. Breastfeeding releases oxytocin pulses; cessation removes this anti-inflammatory stimulus. Women who breastfeed longer may have delayed postpartum ME/CFS onset. Conversely, oxytocin dysfunction (autonomic) may impair lactation, creating a feedback loop.
**Evidence link:** Slack et al. 2023 (postpartum patterns); oxytocin-vagal literature; ME/CFS dysautonomia
**Falsifiable prediction:** Among postpartum women with ME/CFS onset, time-to-onset will positively correlate with breastfeeding duration. Intranasal oxytocin (24 IU/day) will reduce PEM frequency in postpartum ME/CFS women in a 4-week crossover trial.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch25b; ch09 (autonomic)

## Tier 3 — Speculative / Cross-Disease (certainty < 0.15)

### Multiple Sclerosis Pregnancy-Protection Parallel Predicts Estriol Benefit
**Category:** Cross-disease bridge / drug idea
**Certainty:** 0.12
**Mechanistic rationale:** MS shows 70-80% relapse reduction in 3rd trimester, with rebound postpartum — almost mirroring the Schacterle & Komaroff pattern. In MS this is attributed to estriol (pregnancy-specific estrogen) and Th2 shift. Estriol has been trialed in MS (Voskuhl et al.) with significant benefit. If ME/CFS shares neuroinflammatory mechanisms, estriol could be tested.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff (30% improve); MS pregnancy literature; Voskuhl estriol trials
**Falsifiable prediction:** Oral estriol 8 mg/day for 12 weeks in non-pregnant ME/CFS women will reduce fatigue scores (FSS, FACIT-F) by >0.4 SD vs. placebo. Improvers in pregnancy from prior history will be enriched among responders.
**Integration target:** ch14d (treatments); ch25b

### Narcolepsy-Like Pregnancy Modulation Suggests Hypocretin/Orexin Role
**Category:** Cross-disease bridge
**Certainty:** 0.08
**Mechanistic rationale:** Narcolepsy symptoms (cataplexy especially) often shift during pregnancy. Orexin/hypocretin regulates wakefulness, autonomic tone, energy homeostasis — all dysregulated in ME/CFS. Orexin neurons are sensitive to estradiol. Pregnancy-related orexin shifts could underlie a subset of ME/CFS sleep/fatigue dynamics.
**Evidence link:** Narcolepsy pregnancy literature; orexin-energy-autonomic literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** CSF orexin-A levels in ME/CFS will be lower than healthy controls (>0.5 SD), with greater reduction in patients reporting marked diurnal hypersomnolence vs. those with insomnia phenotype.
**Integration target:** ch10 (sleep); ch25b

### Lupus Flare Pattern Suggests Type I Interferon Signature in Reproductive Trajectory
**Category:** Cross-disease bridge / biomarker
**Certainty:** 0.13
**Mechanistic rationale:** Lupus often flares postpartum and is exacerbated by estrogen — opposite to MS. The Schacterle & Komaroff "29% worse" subgroup may share this pattern. Type I IFN signature is elevated in lupus and has been reported in some ME/CFS subsets (Hornig). Estrogen upregulates IFN-α via TLR7.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff worsening subgroup; SLE pregnancy literature; ME/CFS IFN signature reports
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS women whose symptoms worsened during prior pregnancies will show elevated type I IFN signature (ISG15, MX1, IFI44 transcripts) vs. those who improved (>1 SD difference).
**Integration target:** ch07; ch11; ch25b

### Placental Mitochondrial Donation Hypothesis for Offspring Risk
**Category:** Speculative novel hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.10
**Mechanistic rationale:** If ME/CFS has a maternal mitochondrial component (heteroplasmy, oocyte mtDNA dysfunction), the 21% offspring developmental delay could partially reflect maternally inherited mitochondrial inefficiency rather than (or in addition to) gestational environment. This is testable via mtDNA copy number and heteroplasmy in offspring buccal swabs.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff 2004 offspring data; ME/CFS mitochondrial literature; mtDNA inheritance
**Falsifiable prediction:** Offspring of ME/CFS mothers will show elevated mtDNA heteroplasmy variant load and reduced mtDNA copy number in buccal cells compared to offspring of healthy mothers, with effect size >0.3 SD.
**Integration target:** ch06 (genetics); ch23

### Gestational Lactate Tolerance Test as ME/CFS Stratifier
**Category:** Diagnostic/biomarker
**Certainty:** 0.10
**Mechanistic rationale:** Pregnancy normally suppresses anaerobic metabolism via mitochondrial biogenesis upregulation (PGC-1α, estrogen-driven). ME/CFS women who worsen in pregnancy may fail this adaptation. A standardized submaximal exercise test with serial lactate could identify metabolically vulnerable phenotypes pre-conception.
**Evidence link:** ME/CFS lactate/anaerobic threshold literature; pregnancy mitochondrial physiology
**Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-conception submaximal cycle test (50W x 6 min) lactate slope will predict pregnancy worsening with AUC >0.7.
**Integration target:** ch11; ch04 (PEM/CPET)

## Research Directions

### International ME/CFS Pregnancy Registry
**Category:** Research direction
**Mechanistic rationale:** The literature gap is staggering — only 16 studies in Slack et al.'s systematic review. A prospective, harmonized international registry with standardized intake (CCC/IOM diagnosis, severity scale, autoantibody panel, baseline CPET if feasible), trimester-by-trimester symptom tracking, biobanking (serum, PBMCs, urine, placenta if available), and 5-year offspring follow-up could resolve in 5-7 years questions that descriptive retrospectives cannot.
**Feasibility:** High — leverage existing networks (US ME/CFS Clinician Coalition, UK CFS/ME Research Collaborative, EUROMENE). Estimated cost ~€3-5M over 5 years for n=500 pregnancies.
**Integration target:** ch23 (research priorities)

### Hormone Replacement Trial in Postpartum ME/CFS Onset Window
**Category:** Research direction
**Feasibility:** Medium — requires careful safety design. RCT comparing (a) micronized progesterone taper, (b) low-dose transdermal estradiol+progesterone, (c) placebo for 12 weeks postpartum in women with prior CFS-like illness or ME/CFS in remission. Primary outcome: ME/CFS recurrence/onset at 6 months. n~150 per arm.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch25b

### Single-Cell Immune Atlas of ME/CFS Pregnancy
**Category:** Research direction
**Feasibility:** Medium-high — CITE-seq + TCR-seq on PBMCs from ME/CFS pregnant women (n~30) sampled at 1st trimester, 3rd trimester, 6 weeks postpartum, 6 months postpartum. Compare to matched healthy pregnancies. Goal: map Treg/Th17 dynamics, exhausted T-cell populations, NK reconstitution kinetics.
**Integration target:** ch07; ch23

### Endometriosis Excision as ME/CFS Intervention RCT
**Category:** Research direction
**Feasibility:** Medium — collaboration with gynecology required. Pragmatic RCT in women with confirmed dual diagnosis: laparoscopic excision vs. medical management. Primary endpoint: ME/CFS symptom severity at 12 months. Pre-specified subgroup: high baseline tryptase/IL-1β.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch08

### Mendelian Randomization for AMH and ME/CFS
**Category:** Research direction / methodological advance
**Feasibility:** High — leverage UK Biobank and existing GWAS. Use AMH-associated SNPs as instrumental variables to test causal relationship between ovarian reserve and ME/CFS risk. Replicate in DecodeME cohort once available.
**Integration target:** ch06; ch23

## Clinical / Systems Ideas

### ME/CFS-Specific Antenatal Care Pathway
**Description:** Develop a structured care pathway with: (1) pre-conception counseling including realistic disclosure of relapse/worsening probabilities, (2) energy envelope-aware antenatal visit scheduling (telehealth options, no early-morning slots), (3) trimester-specific symptom monitoring tools, (4) anesthesia/delivery planning accounting for orthostatic intolerance and MCAS, (5) postpartum 3-6 month enhanced surveillance window with proactive supports.
**Integration target:** ch25b; ch16 (clinical management)

### Midwife/Obstetric Education Module
**Description:** Most obstetric care providers receive no training in ME/CFS. A 2-3 hour CME module covering: PEM physiology, orthostatic intolerance management during labor, recognition of postpartum relapse vs. normal recovery, MCAS-aware drug choices, breastfeeding considerations. Co-developed with patient advocacy groups.
**Integration target:** ch16; ch25b

### Postpartum Pacing-Plus-Support Protocol
**Description:** Structured postpartum support (extended home nursing, partner leave, household help) for ME/CFS women, framed as medical intervention. Belgium-specific: leverage INAMI/RIZIV maternity support frameworks; advocate for ME/CFS as recognized condition meriting extended postpartum care.
**Integration target:** ch16; ch17 (lifestyle); ch25b

### Patient-Reported Cycle/Pregnancy Symptom Diary App
**Description:** Lightweight tool integrating menstrual cycle phase with PEM episodes, OI events, sleep, cognitive scores. Aggregated data could feed registry. Uses validated instruments (DSQ-PEM, COMPASS-31). Low burden — designed for severe patients.
**Integration target:** ch11; ch16; ch23

### Reproductive Health Screening at ME/CFS Diagnosis
**Description:** All female ME/CFS patients at diagnosis receive: AMH, FSH/LH, prolactin, TSH/fT4, vitamin D, ferritin/hepcidin, screening questions for endometriosis (dysmenorrhea severity, dyspareunia), gynecologic referral if positive. Catches treatable comorbidities and stratifies for research.
**Integration target:** ch11; ch16; ch25b

## Mathematical Model Extensions

### Hormonal Cycling as Forcing Function in ME/CFS ODE Models
**Category:** Mathematical model extension
**Certainty:** N/A (modeling proposal)
**Description:** Existing ME/CFS ODE models (e.g., Wirth-Scheibenbogen autoantibody/vasoconstriction; Craddock-Broderick HPA-immune-gonadal) treat sex hormones as constants. Extend with:
- E(t), P(t) as 28-day periodic forcing functions for menstruating women
- Pregnancy state: E and P ramp up over 40 weeks with characteristic profiles, sharp drop postpartum
- Coupling: E modulates Th1/Th2 balance parameter; P modulates GABA-mediated autonomic dampening; both modulate endothelial nitric oxide
**Falsifiable prediction:** Model predicts symptom oscillations with cycle, with phase relationship dependent on patient subtype (autoimmune-dominant: luteal worsening; metabolic-dominant: menstrual worsening). Tested against patient diary data.
**Integration target:** ch20 or formalization volume; ch25b

### Postpartum Bifurcation Model
**Category:** Mathematical model extension
**Certainty:** N/A
**Description:** Treat postpartum transition as a fast parameter sweep through a bifurcation landscape. Healthy state and ME/CFS state are alternative attractors; pregnancy parameters favor healthy attractor; postpartum parameter trajectory may cross separatrix in vulnerable phenotypes. Uses tools from dynamical systems (slow-fast analysis, basin stability).
**Falsifiable prediction:** Patients near bifurcation (high sensitivity to perturbation) can be identified pre-pregnancy via response to a controlled stressor (orthostatic challenge variability, HRV recovery dynamics). These show higher rates of postpartum onset.
**Integration target:** ch20; formalization volume

## Supplement / Nutraceutical Ideas

### DHEA Supplementation Trial in ME/CFS Women
**Category:** Supplement
**Certainty:** 0.20
**Mechanistic rationale:** DHEA declines steeply in adrenal insufficiency and chronic stress; HPA dysregulation in ME/CFS may produce relative DHEA deficiency. DHEA is a precursor to estrogens and androgens, supports immune function, neurosteroid effects.
**Evidence link:** Thomas et al. 2022 HPA-HPG; small ME/CFS HPA studies
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS women with low baseline DHEA-S (<100 µg/dL) given 25 mg DHEA daily x 12 weeks will show fatigue improvement >0.3 SD vs. placebo.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch25b

### Choline + DHA in ME/CFS Pregnancy
**Category:** Supplement
**Certainty:** 0.18
**Mechanistic rationale:** Both critical for fetal brain development and depleted by pregnancy. Maternal choline status correlates with offspring cognitive outcomes. Given 21% offspring delay rate and possible mitochondrial/methylation contributions, optimizing one-carbon metabolism is plausible mitigation.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff offspring data; established choline-DHA prenatal literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS pregnancies with high-dose choline (550-930 mg/day) + DHA (>500 mg/day) will show offspring developmental delay rate <15% vs. registry baseline of ~21%.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch25b; ch17

### Magnesium + B6 Cycle-Phase Protocol
**Category:** Supplement / non-pharmacological
**Certainty:** 0.18
**Mechanistic rationale:** Both depleted in luteal phase, both support GABA/serotonin synthesis and mitochondrial function. Premenstrual ME/CFS exacerbation may partially reflect cofactor depletion compounding existing mitochondrial dysfunction.
**Evidence link:** PMS literature; ME/CFS mitochondrial cofactor literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** Cycle-phase-targeted magnesium glycinate (400 mg) + P5P (50 mg) in luteal phase will reduce premenstrual PEM episodes by >25% vs. continuous dosing in n=40 crossover trial.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch17; ch25b

## Drug / Medication Ideas

### Low-Dose Naltrexone Cycle-Phase Adjustment
**Category:** Drug repurposing
**Certainty:** 0.15
**Mechanistic rationale:** LDN modulates microglia and may interact with sex hormones (estrogen modulates μ-opioid receptor density). Some patients report cycle-phase efficacy variation. Targeted dose adjustment by cycle phase is testable.
**Evidence link:** LDN ME/CFS literature; estrogen-opioid receptor literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** LDN dose increase (3 mg → 4.5 mg) in luteal phase will improve symptom scores vs. constant dose in cycle-aware crossover.
**Integration target:** ch14d

### Hydroxychloroquine in Endometriosis-ME/CFS Subset
**Category:** Drug repurposing
**Certainty:** 0.12
**Mechanistic rationale:** HCQ has anti-inflammatory effects, reduces type I IFN signaling, and is safe in pregnancy. If endometriosis-comorbid ME/CFS has shared autoimmune-leaning pathology, HCQ might benefit both. Small ME/CFS trials have shown mixed results; subgroup targeting may improve signal.
**Evidence link:** Compton et al. 2025; SLE pregnancy use of HCQ
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12-week HCQ 200 mg BID in endometriosis-ME/CFS subgroup will improve fatigue scores by >0.4 SD vs. placebo, with no improvement in non-endometriosis ME/CFS comparison.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch08

### Sildenafil for Endothelial Dysfunction in Pregnancy-Worsening Subset
**Category:** Drug repurposing
**Certainty:** 0.10
**Mechanistic rationale:** PDE5 inhibition enhances NO signaling; placental and ME/CFS endothelial dysfunction may share NO-bioavailability deficits. Safety in pregnancy is debated (STRIDER trial controversy) but post-pregnancy use is feasible.
**Evidence link:** ME/CFS endothelial literature; sFlt-1 placental dysfunction
**Falsifiable prediction:** Postpartum sildenafil 20 mg TID x 8 weeks in women with elevated postpartum sFlt-1 and ME/CFS symptoms will improve flow-mediated dilation and fatigue scores vs. placebo.
**Integration target:** ch14d; ch25b

## Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### Pre-Conception Energy Envelope Banking
**Category:** Non-pharmacological
**Certainty:** 0.18
**Mechanistic rationale:** Pregnancy increases metabolic demand by 15-20%. Patients near their energy envelope ceiling will exceed it; those with reserve may absorb the load. A structured 3-6 month pre-conception period of aggressive pacing, sleep optimization, comorbidity treatment, and CPET-guided activity reduction may expand baseline reserves.
**Evidence link:** Schacterle & Komaroff worsening fraction; pacing literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** Women completing 6-month pre-conception envelope-banking protocol will show pregnancy worsening rate <20% vs. ~29% historical baseline.
**Integration target:** ch17; ch25b

### Postpartum Vagal-Tone Restoration Protocol
**Category:** Non-pharmacological
**Certainty:** 0.15
**Mechanistic rationale:** Postpartum HRV recovery is impaired in many women; ME/CFS-prone women may show particularly poor recovery. Combine: paced breathing, gentle non-PEM-triggering movement, cold-face stimulation, structured rest. Goal: support parasympathetic restoration during the 3-6 month vulnerability window.
**Evidence link:** Slack et al. postpartum window; HRV-vagal-immune literature
**Falsifiable prediction:** Postpartum HRV (RMSSD) trajectory at 12 weeks predicts ME/CFS onset by 12 months, with <30 ms RMSSD identifying high-risk subgroup.
**Integration target:** ch17; ch25b

### Cycle-Synchronized Activity Pacing
**Category:** Non-pharmacological
**Certainty:** 0.20
**Mechanistic rationale:** Many ME/CFS women report cycle-phase symptom variation. A structured pacing protocol that allocates higher activity quotas to follicular/ovulatory phases and aggressive rest to late luteal/menstrual phases may reduce PEM frequency.
**Evidence link:** Patient anecdote; PMS-PEM overlap
**Falsifiable prediction:** Cycle-synchronized pacing reduces monthly PEM episode count by >25% compared to flat pacing in n=50 crossover trial over 4 cycles.
**Integration target:** ch17; ch25b

## Notes & Caveats

- All ideas are creative extrapolation; none constitutes clinical advice.
- Pregnancy interventions require especially stringent safety review.
- Cross-disease analogies (MS, lupus) may break down; mechanistic distinctions matter.
- Sample sizes in much of source literature are small; effect estimates noisy.
- Selection bias pervades retrospective pregnancy outcomes data.
- Hormonal interventions have known risks (thrombosis, breast cancer, etc.) requiring individualized risk-benefit.
- Several falsifiable predictions presume cohorts/biobanks not yet existing — registry creation is upstream prerequisite.
