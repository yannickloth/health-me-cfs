# Literature Survey: The Reference Range Problem in Chronic Illness
**Date:** 2026-04-21
**Scope:** How population-based laboratory reference ranges fail to detect functional deficiencies — iron, magnesium, B12, vitamin D, thyroid hormones — with particular relevance to ME/CFS

---

## Overview

Standard laboratory reference ranges are derived from population distributions, not from functional outcome data. A value within the "normal" range may still be insufficient for optimal cellular function in a given individual, particularly in the context of chronic illness where compensatory mechanisms are impaired, inflammation elevates acute-phase proteins (masking deficiencies), and tissue-level hormone conversion may be disrupted even when serum markers appear normal.

Nine papers were identified and assessed. Quality varies: the iron and thyroid domains have the strongest RCT and prospective evidence; the ME/CFS-specific functional B12 paper is from a lower-tier journal and should be used cautiously.

## Domain 1: Iron Deficiency Without Anemia

### Vaucher2012 — Iron supplementation RCT in nonanemic women with low ferritin

- **Citation:** Vaucher P, Druais PL, Waldvogel S, Favrat B. Effect of iron supplementation on fatigue in nonanemic menstruating women with low ferritin: a randomized controlled trial. *CMAJ*. 2012;184(11):1247–54.
- **DOI:** 10.1503/cmaj.110950 | **PMID:** 22777991
- **Design:** Multicentre parallel RCT, 44 primary care sites, France; observer-blinded
- **n:** 198 (102 iron, 96 placebo), ages 18–53
- **Ferritin cutoff:** <50 µg/L with normal hemoglobin (>12.0 g/dL)
- **Intervention:** Ferrous sulfate 80 mg elemental iron daily × 12 weeks vs placebo
- **Key finding:** Fatigue score decreased 47.7% (iron) vs 28.8% (placebo), p=0.02. No significant difference in quality of life, depression, or anxiety.
- **Reference range problem:** WHO threshold for iron deficiency is <15 µg/L. This trial showed fatigue benefit in women up to 50 µg/L — more than 3× the WHO cutoff. Most clinical labs flag deficiency only <12–15 µg/L.
- **Quality:** High — peer-reviewed, CMAJ, RCT, n=198, independent of intervention group.
- **Certainty:** 0.75 (moderate-high; single RCT, female-only, no ME/CFS sample)
- **Limitations:** Women only; France primary care sample; no ME/CFS patients; 12-week follow-up; no objective performance measures improved.

### Krayenbuehl2011 — IV iron RCT in nonanemic women with low ferritin

- **Citation:** Krayenbuehl PA, Battegay E, Breymann C, Furrer J, Schulthess G. Intravenous iron for the treatment of fatigue in nonanemic, premenopausal women with low serum ferritin concentration. *Blood*. 2011;118(12):3222–7.
- **DOI:** 10.1182/blood-2011-04-346304 | **PMID:** 21705493
- **Design:** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- **n:** 90 premenopausal women, ferritin ≤50 ng/mL, hemoglobin ≥120 g/L
- **Intervention:** IV iron(III)-hydroxide sucrose 800 mg vs IV placebo
- **Key finding:** In the subgroup with ferritin ≤15 ng/mL, fatigue decreased 1.8 (iron) vs 0.4 (placebo), p=0.005; 82% vs 47% reported improvement, p=0.03. Full cohort effect was smaller.
- **Reference range problem:** Confirms the functional threshold at ≤15 ng/mL is clinically meaningful, but also shows that patients up to 50 ng/mL were enrolled — well above most labs' lower limit of normal (12 ng/mL). Benefit concentrated in the most depleted subgroup.
- **Quality:** High — Blood journal, RCT, double-blind.
- **Certainty:** 0.70 (moderate; effect in subgroup rather than full ITT population; n=90)
- **Limitations:** Women only; subgroup analysis for main benefit; adverse events 21% vs 7%.

### Yokoi2017 — Meta-analysis of IDWA and fatigue

- **Citation:** Yokoi K, Konomi A. Iron deficiency without anaemia is a potential cause of fatigue: meta-analyses of randomised controlled trials and cross-sectional studies. *British Journal of Nutrition*. 2017;117(10):1422–31.
- **DOI:** 10.1017/S0007114517001349 | **PMID:** 28625177
- **Design:** Systematic review + two meta-analyses (6 RCTs, 6 cross-sectional studies)
- **Key findings:** RCT meta-analysis: iron supplementation significantly reduced fatigue in IDWA patients (effect size 0.33, p<0.0001). Cross-sectional meta-analysis: no significant association between IDWA status and fatigue (effect size 0.10, p=0.362). Intervention improves fatigue; presence of IDWA does not reliably predict fatigue in cross-section.
- **Reference range problem:** Studies in the meta-analysis used ferritin thresholds ranging from <12 to <50 µg/L, demonstrating no consensus on where "normal" ends functionally.
- **Quality:** Medium-high — British Journal of Nutrition, systematic methods, but heterogeneous thresholds across included studies limit pooled conclusions.
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Limitations:** Heterogeneous ferritin cutoffs across studies; no specific ME/CFS population; publication bias possible.

### Al-Naseem2021 — Iron deficiency without anemia: clinical review

- **Citation:** Al-Naseem A, Sallam A, Choudhury S, Thachil J. Iron deficiency without anaemia: a diagnosis that matters. *Clinical Medicine (Lond)*. 2021;21(2):107–113.
- **DOI:** 10.7861/clinmed.2020-0582 | **PMID:** 33762368
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:** IDWA is at least twice as common as iron deficiency anemia. WHO cutoff (<15 µg/L) is too low for clinical practice — ferritin <30 µg/L identifies ID with high specificity/sensitivity. In inflammatory states, the threshold rises to 100 µg/L (with TSAT <20% as confirmatory). The paper calls for IDWA to be recognized as a clinical diagnosis in its own right.
- **Reference range problem:** Explicitly addresses the mismatch between WHO reference thresholds (driven by population distributions) and clinical iron sufficiency.
- **Quality:** Medium — Clinical Medicine, peer-reviewed, but narrative review, not primary data.
- **Certainty:** 0.60
- **Limitations:** Review, not primary research; no ME/CFS data.

## Domain 2: Magnesium — Serum vs Intracellular

### Workinger2018 — Challenges in diagnosing magnesium status

- **Citation:** Workinger JL, Doyle RP, Bortz J. Challenges in the diagnosis of magnesium status. *Nutrients*. 2018;10(9):1202.
- **DOI:** 10.3390/nu10091202 | **PMID:** 30200431
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:**
  - Only ~1% of total body magnesium circulates in serum; 99% is intracellular (muscle 27%, bone 53%, other tissues 19%)
  - Serum magnesium has "no reliable correlation with total body magnesium levels or concentrations in specific tissues"
  - Serum levels are tightly defended by renal excretion — appearing normal even when total body stores are depleted
  - No validated rapid bedside test exists for true magnesium status
  - Estimated 45% of Americans are magnesium deficient by dietary standards despite normal serum levels in many
  - RBC magnesium is a better proxy than serum, but still imperfect and not standardized across labs
- **Reference range problem:** The standard serum magnesium assay is specifically the wrong test for detecting chronic intracellular depletion. Patients with symptoms of magnesium deficiency (fatigue, muscle cramps, sleep disturbance) routinely have "normal" serum levels.
- **Quality:** Medium — Nutrients (MDPI, open access), systematic narrative review, well-referenced, no primary data.
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Limitations:** No primary data; review article; MDPI journal (though Nutrients is indexed and legitimate).

## Domain 3: B12 Functional Deficiency

### RussellJones2022 — Functional B12 deficiency in CFS

- **Citation:** Russell-Jones G. Functional vitamin B12 deficiency in chronic fatigue syndrome. *International Journal of Psychiatry*. 2022;7(3):153–158.
- **DOI:** 10.33140/IJP (journal DOI prefix; article-level DOI not confirmed in PubMed)
- **PMID:** Not indexed in PubMed
- **Design:** Observational comparison, n=350 CFS individuals
- **Key findings:**
  - Majority of CFS patients show markers of functional B12 deficiency (elevated methylmalonic acid, abnormal urinary metabolites) despite normal to elevated serum B12
  - Termed "paradoxical vitamin B12 deficiency" — serum B12 is not a reliable marker of functional B12 status in CFS
  - Functional B12 activity depends on B2 (riboflavin) sufficiency; co-deficiency may explain the paradox
- **Reference range problem:** Serum B12 reference ranges (typically 200–900 pg/mL) measure circulating cobalamin, not intracellular utilization. Functional deficiency can exist with serum B12 in the upper normal or even elevated range.
- **Quality:** Low-Medium — published in a lower-tier open-access journal (Opast Publishing, not MEDLINE-indexed). The finding is biologically plausible and aligns with earlier Regland et al. work, but independent replication in a major journal is needed.
- **Certainty:** 0.35 — use cautiously; flag as hypothesis-generating
- **Limitations:** Not PubMed-indexed; journal quality uncertain; n=350 but methodology not fully peer-reviewed through high-impact process; no control group details confirmed.

## Domain 4: Vitamin D Thresholds — Bone vs Immune

### Charoenngam2020 — Immunologic effects of vitamin D

- **Citation:** Charoenngam N, Holick MF. Immunologic effects of vitamin D on human health and disease. *Nutrients*. 2020;12(7):2097.
- **DOI:** 10.3390/nu12072097 | **PMID:** 32679784
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:**
  - Classic bone-health threshold: 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) — basis for most lab reference ranges
  - Optimal immune function threshold: 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L)
  - Active vitamin D (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D) modulates T-cell and B-cell activity, cathelicidin production, and anti-inflammatory cytokine profiles
  - Individual variation in VDR gene expression means two people with identical serum 25(OH)D may have very different cellular responses
  - Significant gene expression differences in immune response begin only above 30 ng/mL
- **Reference range problem:** Most clinical labs define sufficiency as ≥20 ng/mL (for bone health). The immune-optimization threshold of 40–60 ng/mL is approximately 2–3× higher. A result of 22 ng/mL is flagged "normal" by most labs but may be functionally deficient for immune regulation.
- **Quality:** Medium — Nutrients journal, Holick is the leading expert in the field, but this is a narrative review. Holick has disclosed commercial relationships (speaking fees) that should be noted.
- **Certainty:** 0.60
- **Limitations:** Narrative review; Holick COI; optimal thresholds for immune function not established by RCT (primarily from observational and mechanistic data).

### Holick2011 — Endocrine Society vitamin D guideline

- **Citation:** Holick MF, Binkley NC, Bischoff-Ferrari HA, et al. Evaluation, treatment, and prevention of vitamin D deficiency: an Endocrine Society clinical practice guideline. *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*. 2011;96(7):1911–30.
- **DOI:** 10.1210/jc.2011-0385 | **PMID:** 21646368
- **Design:** Clinical practice guideline (evidence-based)
- **Key findings:**
  - Defines deficiency as <20 ng/mL; insufficiency as 21–29 ng/mL; sufficiency as ≥30 ng/mL
  - Recommends supplementation targeting 40–60 ng/mL for maximum extraskeletal benefit
  - Adults at risk may need 1500–2000 IU/day to achieve >30 ng/mL
- **Reference range problem:** The guideline itself identifies the gap — bone-health threshold (20 ng/mL) vs extraskeletal-benefit threshold (40–60 ng/mL) — but most clinical reference intervals report only the bone-health cutoff.
- **Quality:** High — Endocrine Society, evidence-based guideline, widely cited (>20,000 citations). Note: superseded by 2024 Endocrine Society guideline for some recommendations.
- **Certainty:** 0.80 (for the threshold values as stated; immune function benefit from higher levels is medium certainty)
- **Limitations:** 2011 guideline; some recommendations updated in 2024; extraskeletal benefit evidence has been debated in subsequent RCTs (VITAL trial showed less benefit than expected for some endpoints).

## Domain 5: Thyroid — Tissue-Level Deficiency with Normal TSH

### Panicker2009 — DIO2 polymorphism and psychological wellbeing

- **Citation:** Panicker V, Saravanan P, Vaidya B, Evans J, Hattersley AT, Frayling TM, Dayan CM. Common variation in the DIO2 gene predicts baseline psychological well-being and response to combination thyroxine plus triiodothyronine therapy in hypothyroid patients. *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*. 2009;94(5):1623–9.
- **DOI:** 10.1210/jc.2008-1301 | **PMID:** 19190113
- **Design:** Prospective cohort, genetic subgroup analysis within the Weston Area T4 T3 Study (WATTS)
- **n:** 552 subjects on T4 monotherapy
- **Key findings:**
  - The rs225014 CC genotype of DIO2 (present in 16% of sample) was associated with worse baseline psychological wellbeing on T4 (GHQ 14.1 vs 12.8 for TT genotype, p=0.03)
  - CC genotype showed greater improvement on T4+T3 combination therapy vs T4 alone: +2.3 GHQ points at 3 months (p=0.03)
  - Critically: the polymorphism had NO IMPACT on circulating thyroid hormone levels (TSH, T3, T4 all normal)
  - Mechanism: DIO2 encodes type II deiodinase, which converts T4 to active T3 intracellularly in the brain and pituitary. The Thr92Ala substitution reduces enzyme efficiency, potentially creating tissue-level T3 deficiency invisible to serum testing.
- **Reference range problem:** TSH is used as the primary clinical marker of thyroid function, and T3/T4 levels were normal in all genotype groups. Yet 16% of hypothyroid patients had measurably worse psychological function on standard T4-only treatment, explained only by a genetic variant in intracellular T4-to-T3 conversion — something serum TSH cannot detect.
- **Quality:** High — JCEM, prospective design, n=552, validated genetic analysis.
- **Certainty:** 0.70 (moderate-high; findings have been contested in replication attempts, though the general principle of tissue-level T3 deficiency is now well-supported)
- **Limitations:** Hypothyroid patients on T4, not general population or ME/CFS; p-values borderline (0.02–0.06 range in some analyses); replication mixed.

### RuizNunez2018 — Low T3 syndrome in ME/CFS

- **Citation:** Ruiz-Núñez B, Tarasse R, Vogelaar EF, Dijck-Brouwer DAJ, Muskiet FAJ. Higher prevalence of "low T3 syndrome" in patients with chronic fatigue syndrome: a case-control study. *Frontiers in Endocrinology*. 2018;9:97.
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fendo.2018.00097 | **PMID:** 29615976
- **Design:** Case-control
- **n:** 98 ME/CFS patients, 99 age/sex-matched healthy controls
- **Key findings:**
  - CFS patients showed lower FT3, total T3, total T4; elevated reverse T3 (rT3%)
  - FT3 below the reference range in 16/98 CFS patients vs 7/99 controls (OR 2.56, p=0.035)
  - TSH was normal in all groups — this is the key reference range problem
  - The overall pattern resembles a mild non-thyroidal illness syndrome (NTIS), suggesting chronic systemic illness suppresses T4→T3 conversion independent of primary thyroid disease
  - Reduced deiodination function, reduced thyroid secretory capacity
- **Reference range problem:** TSH-based thyroid screening would classify all these patients as "euthyroid." The FT3 abnormality and elevated rT3 are not routinely tested and are not part of standard reference range reporting in most labs.
- **Quality:** Medium — Frontiers in Endocrinology (peer-reviewed open-access), n=197, case-control. Frontiers has variable peer review quality.
- **Certainty:** 0.55 (moderate; biologically coherent; limited by case-control design and Frontiers publication)
- **Limitations:** Case-control, cannot establish causality; Frontiers journal; not replicated in larger ME/CFS cohort.

### Ott2011 — Hashimoto fatigue independent of TSH

- **Citation:** Ott J, Promberger R, Kober F, Neuhold N, Tea M, Huber JC, Hermann M. Hashimoto's thyroiditis affects symptom load and quality of life unrelated to hypothyroidism: a prospective case-control study in women undergoing thyroidectomy for benign goiter. *Thyroid*. 2011;21(2):161–7.
- **DOI:** 10.1089/thy.2010.0191 | **PMID:** 21186954
- **Design:** Prospective case-control (histology-confirmed Hashimoto's as "case" definition)
- **n:** 426 euthyroid women undergoing thyroid surgery; 28 (6.6%) confirmed Hashimoto's on histology
- **Key findings:**
  - Anti-TPO cutoff for true inflammation calculated at 121.0 IU/mL
  - Women above cutoff had significantly higher symptom counts (mean 6.7±2.5 vs 4.1±2.8, p<0.001)
  - Chronic fatigue was significantly more prevalent in the Hashimoto's group
  - TSH levels were statistically identical between groups (1.7±1.3 vs 1.5±1.4 µU/mL)
  - Autoimmune activity itself — not hypothyroidism — drives fatigue
- **Reference range problem:** TSH-based thyroid function is "normal" in euthyroid Hashimoto's patients. Yet these patients carry a significant fatigue burden. Anti-TPO antibodies are not part of standard thyroid function panels ordered for fatigue evaluation.
- **Quality:** High — *Thyroid* (leading specialty journal), prospective, histology-confirmed case definition.
- **Certainty:** 0.75
- **Limitations:** Women only; small Hashimoto's subgroup (n=28); surgical sample may not represent community prevalence.

## Domain 6: Nutrient Status Overview in CFS/Fibromyalgia

### Joustra2017 — Systematic review vitamin/mineral status in CFS/fibromyalgia

- **Citation:** Joustra ML, Minovic I, Janssens KAM, Bakker SJL, Rosmalen JGM. Vitamin and mineral status in chronic fatigue syndrome and fibromyalgia syndrome: a systematic review and meta-analysis. *PLoS One*. 2017;12(4):e0176631.
- **DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pone.0176631 | **PMID:** 28453534
- **Design:** Systematic review + meta-analysis; 45 studies (5 RCTs, 40 observational), 27 included in meta-analyses
- **Key findings:**
  - Vitamin E levels were lower in CFS/FMS patients vs controls — but effect disappeared in higher-quality studies
  - No consistent differences in vitamin D, magnesium, B12, iron, zinc, folate between CFS patients and controls when pooled
  - Supplementation RCTs showed no consistent clinical improvements
  - Study quality was generally poor; substantial heterogeneity across studies
  - Conclusion: "Little evidence was found to support the hypothesis that vitamin and mineral deficiencies play a role in the pathophysiology of CFS and FMS"
- **Relevance to reference range problem:** This is the necessary counterweight. The reference range problem means populations may be stratified by symptoms rather than deficiency, and standard serum measures may miss intracellular/functional deficiencies. This meta-analysis used standard serum measures — the very tests identified as inadequate in the other papers.
- **Quality:** Medium-high — PLoS One, systematic methods, but constrained by poor primary study quality.
- **Certainty:** 0.65 for the negative finding — should be interpreted as "standard serum measures show no consistent difference," not "no deficiency exists."
- **Limitations:** Relies on serum/plasma measures (not intracellular); high heterogeneity; poor primary study quality throughout; may miss functional deficiencies by design.

## Integration Notes

### The Core Argument
All nine papers collectively support a single concept: **laboratory reference ranges encode population distributions, not functional thresholds.** The mismatch between population-normal and individually-sufficient values is larger for nutrients primarily stored intracellularly (magnesium, B12, iron in stores) and hormones requiring peripheral conversion (T4→T3 via DIO2).

### Framing for ME/CFS Document
- Iron IDWA evidence (Vaucher, Krayenbuehl, Yokoi) → supports systematic screening at higher ferritin thresholds than WHO minimum
- Magnesium (Workinger) → rationale for RBC magnesium over serum magnesium when testing
- B12 functional deficiency (RussellJones) → hypothesis-level only; use `speculation` environment
- Vitamin D dual threshold (Charoenngam, Holick2011) → supports targeting 40–60 ng/mL rather than merely ≥20 ng/mL
- DIO2 and T3 (Panicker, RuizNunez) → mechanistic explanation for thyroid symptoms with normal TSH
- Hashimoto's (Ott) → autoimmune fatigue independent of thyroid function
