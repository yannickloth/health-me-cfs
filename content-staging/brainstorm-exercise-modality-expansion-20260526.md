# Brainstorm: Exercise Modality Expansion for ME/CFS

## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### H1.1 Constant Body Position as Primary PEM-Safety Criterion (Certainty: 0.50)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Constant body position eliminates the orthostatic demand fluctuations that drive autonomic compensation in ME/CFS/POTS. Upright-to-supine transitions require rapid blood redistribution (venous return adjustment, baroreflex-mediated HR changes), which exhausts autonomic reserve and depletes metabolic resources. Sports where body position remains constant (swimming, rowing, recumbent cycling) reduce autonomic demand by maintaining stable hemodynamic parameters throughout the activity, thereby raising PEM threshold.

**Evidence link:**
- Schmid et al. 2007: Water immersion decreased HR (vagal activation) while maintaining cardiac output in CHF patients—demonstrating hydrostatic preload benefit without positional change stress
- Hanna et al. 1993: Upright cycling during immersion failed to benefit from hydrostatic preload in MI patients—suggesting autonomic constraint when body position is maintained upright (not supine)
- ME/CFS paper: Orthostatic intolerance and POTS prevalence (60-80%) shows autonomic instability during positional transitions; supine positioning is consistently recommended as restorative

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a crossover study comparing activities with constant vs. varying body position at matched metabolic intensity (VO2, HR), ME/CFS patients will show:
- Lower PEM incidence (odds ratio <0.5) after constant-position activities
- Reduced autonomic stress markers (catecholamines, norepinephrine spillover, HRV turbulence) during constant-position activities
- Shorter recovery time to baseline biomarkers (lactate, cytokines) after constant-position activities

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Provides a mechanistic framework for selecting exercise modalities that align with autonomic pathophysiology. Explains why swimming and recumbent activities are frequently reported as tolerable by patients despite similar cardiovascular demand to walking.

### H1.2 Hydrostatic Pressure Augments Orthostatic Reserve via Parasympathetic Shift (Certainty: 0.68)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Water immersion increases central venous pressure via hydrostatic compression of peripheral veins, shifting autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance (vagal activation overriding Bainbridge reflex). This augments orthostatic reserve—the body's capacity to maintain cerebral perfusion during positional stress—by improving preload and stroke volume while reducing HR. In ME/CFS patients with reduced orthostatic reserve and sympathetic dominance, aquatic exercise may temporarily "recharge" autonomic capacity, allowing activity without triggering the cascade that leads to PEM.

**Evidence link:**
- Schmid et al. 2007: Water immersion decreased HR by ~10 bpm (vagal activation) while increasing cardiac index by 16-21% in all groups including CHF
- Peripheral vascular resistance decreased 21-30% during immersion—demonstrating sympatholytic effect
- Swimming below AT was tolerated even in compromised cardiac function (CHF patients reached 9.7 ml/kg/min VO2, ~4 METs)
- ME/CFS paper: POTS and orthostatic intolerance involve sympathetic dominance and reduced vagal tone; parasympathetic activation is therapeutic goal

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients performing aquatic exercise (thermoneutral water, chest-deep) will show:
- Immediate reduction in standing HR by ≥5 bpm post-immersion (vs. no change after land exercise)
- Increased HF-HRV (parasympathetic index) during and for ≥30 minutes after aquatic exercise
- Reduced orthostatic symptoms on tilt-table test performed 1 hour post-aquatic exercise vs. post-land exercise
- Delayed PEM onset (24-48 hours later) vs. equivalent-intensity land exercise

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Suggests aquatic exercise is not merely "reduced gravity" but an active autonomic modulator. Supports clinical recommendation of hydrotherapy for POTS/ME/CFS subgroups with objective biomarker rationale.

### H1.3 Water Rowing Combines Constant Position with Hydrostatic Benefit (Certainty: 0.30)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Water rowing on a seated ergometer (Concept2-style machine in pool or aquatic rowing shell) combines three protective mechanisms: (1) seated constant body position eliminates orthostatic transition stress; (2) hydrostatic pressure augments venous return and parasympathetic tone; (3) compound upper+lower body movement distributes metabolic load across larger muscle mass, reducing peak local demand. Rowing's rhythmic pattern (pull-feather-recover) may entrain breathing and HRV, further stabilizing autonomic function. Unlike swimming, rowing keeps the face above water, avoiding the head-neck stress and breath-holding demands that some patients cannot tolerate.

**Evidence link:**
- Schmid et al. 2007: Hydrostatic benefit demonstrated; rowing maintains constant seated position
- ME/CFS paper: Recumbent activities already recommended; resistance bands (isometric) are PEM-safe
- No direct evidence on water rowing in ME/CFS or POTS—this is extrapolation from component mechanisms
- Oka et al. 2014: Isometric exercises (low cardiovascular demand) are PEM-safe; rowing is dynamic but can be performed at low intensity

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, low-intensity water rowing (≤10 strokes/minute, RPE ≤10, HR ≤85% estimated AT) will show:
- Lower HR response per watt of power output compared to land rowing (due to hydrostatic preload)
- Reduced catecholamine elevation during and after exercise vs. matched-intensity land rowing
- Lower PEM incidence (OR <0.7) vs. matched-intensity land cycling over 4-week trial
- Improved orthostatic tolerance on stand-test 24 hours post-rowing vs. baseline

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Proposes a novel modality with strong theoretical foundation for the POTS/orthostatic intolerance subgroup. Addresses the patient-reported insight that "constant body position" activities are safest.

### H1.4 Resistance Bands Are Safe Due to Isometric-Like Kinematics and Compound Movement Distribution (Certainty: 0.40)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Resistance band exercises performed in seated or supine positions share PEM-protective properties with isometric training: (1) submaximal intensity can be tightly controlled via band resistance selection; (2) concentric-eccentric phases are slow and controlled, avoiding explosive power demands; (3) compound movements (e.g., seated chest press, seated row) distribute load across multiple muscle groups rather than isolating single muscles that may exceed metabolic capacity. Bands provide accommodating resistance (increasing force at end-range) that matches natural strength curves, reducing excessive demand at vulnerable joint angles. Unlike free weights, bands eliminate gravitational loading that requires stabilization energy expenditure.

**Evidence link:**
- Oka et al. 2014: Isometric yoga (static contractions) is PEM-safe and improves fatigue scores
- ME/CFS paper: Resistance bands listed as "low-impact strength maintenance" but no mechanistic detail
- hEDS/HSD comorbidity literature: Resistance bands are standard for hypermobile patients due to controlled loading
- Cardiac rehab literature: Elastic resistance training is safe for patients with reduced cardiac output

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients performing seated resistance band exercises (2 sets × 10 reps at 30% estimated 1RM, 3x/week) will show:
- No elevation in inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) post-exercise vs. baseline (unlike dynamic exercise which elevates cytokines)
- Lower HR response vs. equivalent-load free-weight exercises (due to reduced stabilization demand)
- Improved strength (estimated by band resistance progression) without PEM exacerbation over 8 weeks
- Better functional outcomes (chair stand test) vs. control group with no exercise

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Provides mechanistic justification for resistance band inclusion in safe movement protocols. Important for hEDS/HSD comorbidity where muscle strengthening is indicated but standard resistance training risks PEM.

### H1.5 Thermoneutral Water Temperature Is Critical for Autonomic Stability During Aquatic Exercise (Certainty: 0.55)

**Mechanistic rationale:** ME/CFS patients exhibit impaired thermoregulation and heightened sympathetic response to temperature stress. Water immersion below thermoneutral (32°C) triggers vasoconstriction and sympathetic activation to conserve heat, counteracting the beneficial parasympathetic shift from hydrostatic pressure. Water above thermoneutral triggers vasodilation and potential hypotension in dysautonomic patients. Only thermoneutral water (31-32°C) provides pure hemodynamic benefit without thermal autonomic stress, making aquatic exercise tolerable for ME/CFS patients with POTS comorbidity.

**Evidence link:**
- Schmid et al. 2007: All aquatic testing was at thermoneutral water temperature (32°C); no cold or warm water comparisons
- ME/CFS paper: Thermoregulatory dysfunction documented; temperature intolerance common
- POTS literature: Cold water immersion worsens orthostatic symptoms; warm water can trigger hypotension
- Cardiac rehab guidelines: 31-33°C water temperature standard for patients with cardiovascular compromise

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS/POTS patients performing aquatic exercise at matched sub-AT intensity:
- Cold water (28°C) will show: increased HR, increased catecholamines, worsened orthostatic symptoms post-exercise, higher PEM incidence
- Warm water (35°C) will show: decreased HR but increased lightheadedness, increased syncopal episodes during immersion
- Thermoneutral (32°C) will show: optimal parasympathetic shift (HF-HRV increase), lowest PEM incidence, best orthostatic tolerance post-exercise

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Provides specific, implementable guidance for aquatic therapy protocols. Explains why some patients report "water makes me worse" (non-thermoneutral pools) while others benefit (properly temperature-controlled hydrotherapy pools).

### H1.6 Compound vs. Isolated Movement HR Response Diverges in ME/CFS Due to Impaired Perfusion Redistribution (Certainty: 0.40)

**Mechanistic rationale:** In healthy individuals, compound movements (multi-joint, multi-muscle) elicit higher HR response than isolated movements because they recruit more muscle mass and generate higher total metabolic demand. However, in ME/CFS patients with impaired perfusion redistribution and reduced cardiac output, compound movements may actually elicit *lower* HR response at equivalent submaximal intensity because metabolic load is distributed across more muscle tissue, reducing peak local demand and ischemic stress. Isolated movements (e.g., bicep curl) concentrate metabolic demand in small muscle groups, potentially exceeding local perfusion capacity and triggering compensatory sympathetic activation and HR elevation disproportionate to total workload.

**Evidence link:**
- ME/CFS paper: Global hypoperfusion documented; cardiac output reduced (especially end-diastolic volumes and stroke volume)
- Two-day CPET literature: ME/CFS patients show impaired perfusion to working muscles during exercise
- Schmid et al. 2007: Swimming (compound movement) was well-tolerated in CHF patients despite reduced cardiac function
- No direct evidence comparing compound vs. isolated movement HR response in ME/CFS—this is novel extrapolation

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients performing matched-intensity exercises (matched by VO2 or Borg RPE):
- Compound movement (e.g., seated row) will show lower HR vs. isolated movement (e.g., bicep curl) at equivalent perceived exertion
- Isolated movement will show higher norepinephrine spillover and regional muscle ischemia (NIRS oxygenation) vs. compound movement
- EMG patterns will show higher local muscle activation (% MVC) in isolated movements despite matched overall workload
- Compound movement will show lower PEM incidence over 4-week trial vs. isolated movement at matched intensity

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Challenges standard exercise prescription which often focuses on isolated movements for "targeted strength." Suggests compound movements may be safer for ME/CFS physiology despite conventional wisdom that isolation is "easier."

## Category 2: Research Directions

### R2.1 RCT: Constant-Position vs. Varying-Position Exercise Modality Safety (Certainty: 0.50)

**Design:** Crossover randomized controlled trial. N=40 ME/CFS patients (Fukuda/ICC criteria with documented PEM). Each participant completes 3 intervention arms (order randomized, 4-week washout between arms):
- Arm A: Constant-position exercise (recumbent bike, seated rowing, or aquatic exercise)
- Arm B: Varying-position exercise (walking, upright cycling, or swimming with frequent position changes)
- Arm C: No-exercise control (baseline pacing)

**Intensity:** All exercise arms matched at 60% of individual anaerobic threshold (determined by CPET), 10 minutes/session, 3x/week.

**Primary outcomes:** PEM incidence (days with ≥30% symptom exacerbation), PEM severity (0-10 scale), time to recovery (hours from exercise to return to baseline).

**Secondary outcomes:** HR response (peak, area-under-curve), HRV (LF/HF ratio, RMSSD), inflammatory biomarkers (IL-6, TNF-α, cortisol), orthostatic tolerance (stand-test HR increase).

**Statistical analysis:** Paired comparisons within participants (crossover design). Primary analysis: PEM incidence difference between Arms A and B.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- H0 (null): No difference in PEM incidence between constant-position and varying-position exercise
- H1 (alternative): Constant-position exercise reduces PEM incidence by ≥30% (OR ≤0.7)

**Relevance:** Direct test of H1.1 constant-position hypothesis. Provides high-certainty evidence for exercise modality selection guidelines.

### R2.2 Cross-Over Trial: Aquatic vs. Land Exercise at Matched Intensity in POTS+ME/CFS (Certainty: 0.55)

**Design:** Crossover RCT. N=30 ME/CFS patients with comorbid POTS (confirmed by tilt-table). Each participant completes:
- Phase 1 (2 weeks): Aquatic exercise in thermoneutral pool (32°C), chest-deep, low-intensity (RPE 9-11), 10 min/session, 3x/week
- Phase 2 (2 weeks): Land exercise (upright cycling or treadmill), matched intensity by RPE and HR, same duration/frequency
- Order randomized, 1-week washout between phases

**Primary outcomes:** Orthostatic tolerance (HR increase on 10-minute stand test, symptom score 0-10), autonomic balance (HRV LF/HF ratio, catecholamines).

**Secondary outcomes:** PEM incidence/severity, quality of life (SF-36), mood (PHQ-9, GAD-7).

**Mechanistic measures:** Pre/post each phase: cardiac MRI (stroke volume, end-diastolic volume), near-infrared spectroscopy (muscle oxygenation), tilt-table with hemodynamic monitoring.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Aquatic exercise will reduce standing HR by ≥5 bpm vs. land exercise
- Aquatic exercise will increase HF-HRV (parasympathetic) vs. no change after land exercise
- Aquatic exercise will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.6) vs. land exercise
- Aquatic exercise will improve stroke volume on cardiac MRI vs. no change after land exercise

**Relevance:** Direct test of H1.2 hydrostatic pressure hypothesis. Provides objective autonomic data to support or refute aquatic therapy recommendations.

### R2.3 Dose-Finding Study: Water Rowing Protocol for ME/CFS (Certainty: 0.30)

**Design:** Sequential dose-escalation study (Phase I/II design). N=24 ME/CFS patients (mild-to-moderate severity, able to sit). Groups:
- Cohort 1 (n=8): Ultra-low dose (5 strokes/minute, 3 min/session, 2x/week)
- Cohort 2 (n=8): Low dose (10 strokes/minute, 5 min/session, 2x/week)
- Cohort 3 (n=8): Moderate dose (10 strokes/minute, 10 min/session, 3x/week)

**Duration:** 6 weeks per cohort, with PEM monitoring to define tolerability. Escalation to next dose only if <20% of current cohort experiences PEM.

**Primary outcomes:** Tolerability (PEM incidence, dropout rate), safety (adverse events, orthostatic symptoms).

**Secondary outcomes:** Aerobic capacity (6-minute walk test, CPET if available), strength (grip strength, chair stand), fatigue (Chalder scale), quality of life.

**Technique:** Water rowing performed seated in therapy pool (water at chest level, 32°C) using specialized water rowing ergometer (resistance provided by water drag, not air flywheel).

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Cohort 1 will show <10% PEM incidence, establishing baseline safety
- Cohort 2 will show 10-20% PEM incidence, likely tolerable with monitoring
- Cohort 3 will show >30% PEM incidence, exceeding safe threshold
- Dose-response relationship: longer duration (10 min) will show disproportionate PEM increase vs. frequency increase (3x/week)

**Relevance:** Direct test of H1.3 water rowing hypothesis. Establishes safe protocol parameters for future RCT. Addresses complete lack of evidence on this modality.

### R2.4 Resistance Band vs. Free-Weight Safety Study in hEDS+ME/CFS Comorbidity (Certainty: 0.40)

**Design:** Parallel-group RCT. N=60 ME/CFS patients with comorbid hypermobility spectrum disorder (hEDS/HSD, diagnosed by Beighton criteria). Randomization:
- Intervention group (n=30): Seated resistance band exercises, 2 sets × 10 reps, 30% estimated 1RM, 3x/week
- Control group (n=30): Seated free-weight exercises, matched volume/intensity, 3x/week

**Duration:** 12 weeks.

**Primary outcomes:** Joint stability (Beighton score, joint hypermobility symptom scale), PEM incidence, functional strength (chair stand test, grip strength).

**Secondary outcomes:** Pain severity (0-10 scale), fatigue (Chalder scale), anxiety/depression (HADS), HRV (LF/HF ratio), inflammatory biomarkers (IL-6, TNF-α).

**Exercise protocol:** Both groups perform identical movement patterns (e.g., chest press, seated row, shoulder press) differentiated only by resistance modality (bands vs. dumbbells). Intensity titrated individually: 30% 1RM estimated from submaximal testing (band tension equivalent to 1RM, dumbbell weight equivalent to 1RM).

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Resistance band group will show lower HR response during exercise vs. free-weight group (ΔHR <10 bpm vs. ΔHR 15-20 bpm)
- Resistance band group will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.7) vs. free-weight group
- Resistance band group will show comparable strength gains to free-weight group (non-inferiority margin: Δstrength <15%)
- Resistance band group will show better joint stability outcomes (reduced subluxation episodes) vs. free-weight group

**Relevance:** Direct test of H1.4 resistance band hypothesis. Addresses critical comorbidity exercise conflict (hEDS strengthening vs. PEM risk). Provides evidence for safe movement protocols in hypermobile ME/CFS patients.

### R2.5 Temperature-Comparison Study: Thermoneutral vs. Non-Thermoneutral Aquatic Exercise (Certainty: 0.55)

**Design:** Within-subject crossover. N=20 ME/CFS/POTS patients. Each participant completes 3 aquatic exercise sessions (order randomized, 1-week washout between):
- Session A: Cold water (28°C)
- Session B: Thermoneutral water (32°C)
- Session C: Warm water (35°C)

**Protocol:** Identical exercise intensity (RPE 10-11, HR <85% AT), duration (10 min), modality (gentle water walking + arm movements), depth (chest-level immersion).

**Primary outcomes:** Autonomic stability (HRV LF/HF ratio, catecholamines norepinephrine/epinephrine), orthostatic tolerance (stand-test HR increase pre/post session, orthostatic symptom score 0-10).

**Secondary outcomes:** Subjective comfort rating, perceived exertion (Borg scale), PEM incidence (24-48 hours post-session).

**Monitoring:** Continuous HR telemetry during immersion, pre/post blood draws for catecholamines and cortisol, pre/post tilt-table stand-test for orthostatic response.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Thermoneutral water will show: lowest HR increase, highest HF-HRV (parasympathetic), lowest catecholamine elevation, best post-session orthostatic tolerance
- Cold water will show: highest HR increase (sympathetic activation), lowest HF-HRV, highest catecholamines, worsened orthostatic tolerance
- Warm water will show: lowest HR but highest lightheadedness, potential syncopal episodes during immersion, reduced post-session orthostatic tolerance due to vasodilation

**Relevance:** Direct test of H1.5 temperature hypothesis. Provides specific evidence-based guidance for aquatic therapy pool temperature standards in ME/CFS/POTS care.

### R2.6 Compound vs. Isolated Movement HR Response Mechanism Study (Certainty: 0.40)

**Design:** Within-subject mechanistic study. N=15 ME/CFS patients (mild-to-moderate). Each participant performs:
- Compound movement: Seated row (multi-joint, multi-muscle)
- Isolated movement: Bicep curl (single-joint, single-muscle)
- Control: Rest

**Matching protocol:** Perform each movement at matched perceived exertion (Borg RPE 10-11) and matched VO2 (via metabolic cart if available, otherwise matched by HR). 3 sets × 8 reps each, 2-minute rest between sets.

**Outcome measures:**
- Primary: HR response (peak, area-under-curve during exercise, recovery time to baseline)
- Secondary: Near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) for muscle oxygenation in active muscles (biceps vs. back muscles)
- Tertiary: EMG (% maximal voluntary contraction, local vs. global activation patterns)
- Exploratory: Norepinephrine spillover (if invasive sampling available), regional blood flow (Doppler ultrasound)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Compound movement will show lower HR response vs. isolated movement at matched RPE/VO2 (ΔHR <5 bpm vs. ΔHR 10-15 bpm)
- Isolated movement will show greater local muscle deoxygenation (NIRS oxygenation <50% baseline) vs. compound movement (oxygenation >70% baseline) in active muscles
- EMG will show higher local activation (% MVC) in isolated movement (>60% MVC) vs. compound movement (<40% MVC per muscle group)
- Norepinephrine will be higher after isolated movement vs. compound movement, reflecting compensatory sympathetic response to local ischemia

**Relevance:** Direct test of H1.6 compound vs. isolated hypothesis. Provides mechanistic data to inform exercise prescription guidelines. Challenges conventional wisdom that isolated movements are "easier."

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### D3.1 Pyridostigmine Augmentation of Aquatic Exercise Parasympathetic Benefit (Certainty: 0.35)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Pyridostigmine is an acetylcholinesterase inhibitor that enhances cholinergic transmission at autonomic ganglia and neuromuscular junctions. In POTS, pyridostigmine improves orthostatic tolerance by potentiating vagal tone and reducing excessive sympathetic activation. Combining pyridostigmine with aquatic exercise may produce additive parasympathetic activation: hydrostatic pressure augments venous return (mechanical parasympathetic stimulus), pyridostigmine augments cholinergic signaling (pharmacological parasympathetic stimulus). The combined effect may lower exercise-induced sympathetic stress and raise PEM threshold, allowing longer or more intense aquatic sessions without triggering crashes.

**Evidence link:**
- POTS literature: Pyridostigmine (30-60 mg TID) improves orthostatic symptoms in 60-70% of POTS patients via enhanced vagal tone
- Schmid et al. 2007: Hydrostatic pressure produces parasympathetic shift (decreased HR, increased stroke volume)
- ME/CFS paper: POTS comorbidity (60-80%) justifies autonomic-targeted interventions; parasympathetic activation is therapeutic goal
- No direct evidence combining pyridostigmine with aquatic exercise—this is novel combination

**Proposed protocol:** ME/CFS patients with POTS comorbidity already stable on pyridostigmine (30-60 mg TID, ≥4 weeks tolerance) add aquatic exercise (thermoneutral pool, chest-deep, low-intensity RPE 10-11, 10 min/session, 3x/week). Control group: aquatic exercise alone without pyridostigmine. Duration: 8 weeks.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Pyridostigmine + aquatic exercise group will show greater reduction in standing HR (≥15 bpm decrease vs. ≥5 bpm in aquatic-only group)
- Pyridostigmine + aquatic exercise will show higher HF-HRV increase (≥30% vs. ≥10% in aquatic-only group)
- Pyridostigmine + aquatic exercise will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.5 vs. aquatic-only group)
- Pyridostigmine + aquatic exercise will allow longer session duration (15 min vs. 10 min maximum tolerated) without PEM

**Safety considerations:** Pyridostigmine contraindicated in asthma, bradycardia, mechanical GI obstruction. Monitor for cholinergic side effects (diarrhea, sweating, salivation). Start aquatic exercise at lower intensity in pyridostigmine group due to potential additive effects.

**Relevance:** Tests pharmacological augmentation of non-pharmacological intervention. Addresses POTS comorbidity specifically. Provides mechanistic justification for combined therapy.

### D3.2 Midodrine Pre-Exercise for Upright Modality Tolerance in Non-Aquatic Settings (Certainty: 0.30)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Midodrine is an alpha-1 adrenergic agonist that causes vasoconstriction and increases blood pressure, used off-label in POTS to improve orthostatic tolerance. Pre-exercise midodrine administration (10 mg 30-45 minutes before exercise) may reduce orthostatic demand during upright exercise modalities (walking, upright cycling) by augmenting peripheral resistance and reducing blood pooling. This could allow ME/CFS/POTS patients to tolerate brief upright activity sessions that would otherwise trigger PEM, facilitating functional movement (e.g., walking to bathroom, short errands) without requiring aquatic facilities.

**Evidence link:**
- POTS literature: Midodrine (5-10 mg TID) improves orthostatic symptoms and increases standing BP in 50-60% of POTS patients
- ME/CFS paper: Orthostatic intolerance is pathophysiological driver; supine positioning reduces orthostatic demand
- No direct evidence on pre-exercise midodrine for ME/CFS exercise tolerance—this is novel application

**Proposed protocol:** ME/CFS patients with POTS comorbidity already stable on midodrine (10 mg TID, ≥4 weeks tolerance) add upright exercise (walking or upright cycling) at 60% AT, 10 min/session, 3x/week. Exercise scheduled 45 minutes after midodrine dose. Control group: upright exercise without midodrine. Duration: 8 weeks.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Midodrine group will show lower standing HR increase during exercise (ΔHR <15 bpm vs. ΔHR 25-30 bpm in control)
- Midodrine group will show reduced orthostatic symptoms during exercise (lightheadedness, visual disturbance) vs. control
- Midodrine group will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.6 vs. control group)
- Midodrine group will show improved functional capacity (6-minute walk test) vs. control group

**Safety considerations:** Monitor for supine hypertension (avoid lying down within 4 hours of dose). Watch for urinary retention, piloerection, scalp tingling. Contraindicated in hypertension, cardiac disease, urinary retention. Start upright exercise at minimal intensity (2-3 minutes) and progress cautiously.

**Relevance:** Provides pharmacological bridge for patients without aquatic access. Addresses upright activity necessity for daily living. Tests mechanistic interaction between orthostatic support and exercise tolerance.

### D3.3 Low-Dose Naltrexone (LDN) Adjunct to Isometric Yoga for Anti-Inflammatory Synergy (Certainty: 0.30)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Low-dose naltrexone (1.5-4.5 mg nightly) exhibits immunomodulatory effects via transient opioid receptor blockade, triggering compensatory upregulation of endogenous opioids (endorphins, enkephalins) and reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1β). Isometric yoga (Oka et al. 2014) produces an anti-inflammatory signature (decreased TNF-α, decreased cortisol, increased DHEA-S). Combining LDN with isometric yoga may produce synergistic anti-inflammatory effects: LDN reduces baseline cytokine levels, isometric yoga further suppresses exercise-induced cytokine release. The combination may lower the inflammatory threshold that triggers PEM, allowing slightly higher activity intensity without symptom exacerbation.

**Evidence link:**
- LDN literature: In fibromyalgia and chronic fatigue, LDN (3-4.5 mg nightly) reduces pain and fatigue in 50-60% of patients via cytokine modulation
- Oka et al. 2014: Isometric yoga decreases TNF-α and cortisol, increases DHEA-S in CFS patients
- ME/CFS paper: Cytokine dysregulation is pathophysiological driver; inflammation contributes to PEM
- No direct evidence combining LDN with isometric yoga—this is novel synergy

**Proposed protocol:** ME/CFS patients (ICC criteria) already stable on LDN (3-4.5 mg nightly, ≥8 weeks tolerance) add isometric yoga (seated format, Oka protocol, 10 min/session daily). Control group: isometric yoga alone without LDN. Duration: 12 weeks.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- LDN + isometric yoga group will show greater reduction in TNF-α vs. isometric yoga alone (ΔTNF-α >30% vs. ΔTNF-α >10%)
- LDN + isometric yoga will show greater improvement in Chalder Fatigue Scale (≥5 point reduction vs. ≥2 point reduction)
- LDN + isometric yoga will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.5 vs. control)
- LDN + isometric yoga will show higher DHEA-S/cortisol ratio (≥50% increase vs. ≥20% increase)

**Safety considerations:** LDN contraindicated in opioid dependence, liver disease. Monitor for vivid dreams, sleep disruption, nausea. Start isometric yoga at minimal duration (5 minutes) and progress to full 10-minute protocol over 2 weeks.

**Relevance:** Tests pharmacological-augmentation of non-pharmacological intervention. Addresses inflammatory component of PEM pathophysiology. Low-risk adjunctive therapy with strong mechanistic rationale.

### D3.4 Beta-Blocker Prophylaxis for Aquatic Exercise Safety in POTS+ME/CFS (Certainty: 0.30)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Beta-blockers (particularly beta-1 selective like metoprolol) reduce excessive tachycardia and catecholamine response in POTS. Aquatic exercise, despite hydrostatic benefits, still produces cardiac demand and HR elevation. Pre-exercise beta-blocker administration (metoprolol 25-50 mg 1 hour before aquatic session) may blunt excessive HR response, reduce myocardial oxygen demand, and prevent the sympathetic overdrive that can trigger PEM. In POTS+ME/CFS patients with marked HR elevation during minimal activity, beta-blocker prophylaxis may make aquatic exercise accessible that would otherwise be intolerable due to tachycardia.

**Evidence link:**
- POTS literature: Beta-blockers reduce standing HR and symptoms in 40-50% of POTS patients, particularly those with hyperadrenergic phenotype
- Schmid et al. 2007: Aquatic exercise increased HR (though reduced vs. land) in CHF patients
- ME/CFS paper: POTS comorbidity involves excessive tachycardia; HR monitoring is pacing cornerstone
- No direct evidence on pre-exercise beta-blocker for aquatic exercise in ME/CFS—this is novel prophylactic use

**Proposed protocol:** ME/CFS patients with POTS comorbidity already stable on metoprolol (25-50 mg daily, ≥4 weeks tolerance) add aquatic exercise (thermoneutral pool, chest-deep, RPE 10-11, 10 min/session, 3x/week). Exercise scheduled 1 hour after metoprolol dose. Control group: aquatic exercise without beta-blocker. Duration: 8 weeks.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Beta-blocker group will show lower peak HR during aquatic exercise (<100 bpm vs. 110-120 bpm in control)
- Beta-blocker group will show reduced catecholamine elevation (norepinephrine <300 pg/mL vs. 400-500 pg/mL in control)
- Beta-blocker group will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.6 vs. control)
- Beta-blocker group will show improved exercise tolerance (able to progress to 15-minute sessions vs. stuck at 10-minute ceiling in control)

**Safety considerations:** Monitor for bradycardia (<50 bpm), hypotension, fatigue exacerbation. Avoid in asthma (non-selective beta-blockers), severe bradycardia, AV block. Start aquatic exercise at minimal intensity and monitor HR closely.

**Relevance:** Provides pharmacological safety net for aquatic exercise. Addresses POTS comorbidity specifically. Tests prophylactic vs. chronic beta-blocker timing strategies.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### S4.1 Sodium/Electrolyte Loading Prior to Water Immersion for Blood Volume Augmentation (Certainty: 0.55)

**Mechanistic rationale:** POTS and ME/CFS patients frequently exhibit hypovolemia and reduced blood volume, contributing to orthostatic intolerance. Sodium loading (3-5 g NaCl/day) is standard POTS therapy to expand plasma volume via increased osmotic retention. Pre-exercise sodium loading (1-2 g NaCl 60 minutes before aquatic exercise) may augment hydrostatic pressure benefits: expanded plasma volume enhances the preload effect of water immersion, further increasing stroke volume and reducing HR. The combined volume-expansion strategy (sodium + hydrostatic pressure) may maximize parasympathetic shift and minimize orthostatic stress during aquatic sessions, reducing PEM risk.

**Evidence link:**
- POTS literature: Sodium loading (3-10 g/day) increases plasma volume by 10-15% and improves orthostatic symptoms in 60-70% of POTS patients
- Schmid et al. 2007: Hydrostatic pressure increased central venous pressure and stroke volume in CHF patients
- ME/CFS paper: Hypovolemia documented in POTS comorbidity; orthostatic intolerance is pathophysiological driver
- No direct evidence on pre-exercise sodium loading for aquatic exercise—this is novel timing strategy

**Proposed protocol:** ME/CFS patients with POTS comorbidity perform aquatic exercise (thermoneutral pool, chest-deep, RPE 10-11, 10 min/session, 3x/week). Randomized crossover:

- 1-week washout between phases

**Falsifiable predictions:**

Use electrolyte solution (not just salt) to maintain potassium balance. Avoid in patients with MCAS (high sodium can trigger histamine release in some individuals).

**Relevance:** Low-risk, low-cost intervention. Tests optimal timing of standard POTS therapy. Provides mechanistic rationale for pre-exercise sodium loading strategy.

### S4.5 Electrolyte Solution During Water Rowing for Heat Dissipation and Hydration (Certainty: 0.45)

**Relevance:** Low-risk, low-cost intervention. Addresses thermoregulatory and orthostatic components simultaneously. May enable longer rowing sessions without triggering PEM.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### N5.1 Water Rowing Progressive Protocol for ME/CFS (Certainty: 0.25)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Water rowing combines constant body position (seated), hydrostatic pressure benefit (chest-deep immersion), and compound movement distribution (upper+lower body). A carefully titrated progressive protocol allows ME/CFS patients to gradually build tolerance without exceeding PEM threshold. The protocol emphasizes ultra-conservative progression (1-2 strokes/minute increase every 2-3 weeks, only if no PEM), frequent monitoring (12-72 hour symptom tracking), and immediate rollback at first sign of intolerance.

**Proposed protocol structure:**

*Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Baseline establishment*
- 5 strokes/minute, 2 minutes/session, 2 sessions/week
- Continuous HR monitoring (target <85% estimated AT)
- Symptom tracking at 6h, 24h, 48h post-session
- Progression criteria: No PEM for 2 consecutive sessions

*Phase 2 (Weeks 3-4): Minimal increase*
- 6-7 strokes/minute, 3 minutes/session, 2 sessions/week
- Same monitoring and progression criteria
- If PEM occurs: Return to Phase 1 for 2 weeks before re-attempt

*Phase 3 (Weeks 5-8): Gradual build*
- 8-10 strokes/minute, 5 minutes/session, 2-3 sessions/week
- Introduce 30-second rest breaks every 2 minutes
- Monitor for delayed PEM (72 hours post-session)
- Progression: Increase session duration by 1 minute every 2 weeks, only if no PEM

*Phase 4 (Weeks 9-12): Maintenance*
- 10 strokes/minute, 8-10 minutes/session, 3 sessions/week
- Supine rest 5 minutes post-session for blood redistribution
- Do not increase beyond this level without CPET confirmation of AT
- Goal: Maintenance, not fitness improvement

**Safety parameters:**
- Stop immediately if: HR > AT, Borg RPE >13, lightheadedness, shortness of breath, chest pain, nausea
- Mandatory 48-hour observation period after any increase before continuing
- Bedbound patients: Consider water walking (standing in chest-deep water, gentle walking) before attempting rowing
- Patients with severe POTS: Combine with pre-exercise sodium loading (S4.1)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- 60-70% of mild-to-moderate ME/CFS patients will tolerate Phase 1-3 progression over 12 weeks without PEM
- <20% of patients will progress to Phase 4 maintenance level
- Patients who progress to Phase 3 will show improved orthostatic tolerance (stand-test HR decrease ≥5 bpm)
- Patients who experience PEM will show predictable pattern: onset 24-48 hours post-session, recovery requiring 5-7 days

**Relevance:** Provides concrete, implementable protocol for water rowing. Addresses complete lack of evidence on this modality. Aligns with constant-position hypothesis (H1.1) and hydrostatic pressure hypothesis (H1.2).

### N5.2 Swimming Adapted Protocol for ME/CFS with POTS Comorbidity (Certainty: 0.35)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Swimming provides hydrostatic pressure benefit and horizontal posture, but introduces unique challenges: breath-holding (Valsalva), head-neck stress (cervical proprioception), and variable body position (turns, starts). A ME/CFS-adapted swimming protocol eliminates these triggers: backstroke (face always out of water), horizontal only (no turns, use pool width or walk-turns), gentle pace (below AT), thermoneutral water (32°C), and optional flotation support for severe patients.

**Proposed protocol structure:**

*Adaptation 1: Body position modifications*
- Backstroke only (face out of water, eliminates breath-holding stress)
- Horizontal straight-line swimming (no turns, use pool width, walk-turns at wall)
- Optional pool noodle or kickboard support for cervical stability

*Adaptation 2: Intensity management*
- RPE 9-11 (very light to light)
- Continuous HR monitoring (target <85% estimated AT)
- 10-15 minute sessions maximum
- Rest intervals: 30 seconds supine rest at wall every 2-3 minutes

*Adaptation 3: Environmental controls*
- Thermoneutral water (31-32°C)
- Avoid cold pools (<28°C) or warm pools (>34°C)
- Quiet environment (no lap swimming crowds, minimal auditory stimulation)

*Progressive protocol:*

*Weeks 1-2 (Baseline):*
- Backstroke, 5 minutes/session, 2 sessions/week
- Pool noodle support optional
- Focus on technique, not distance

*Weeks 3-6 (Build):*
- Backstroke, 8-10 minutes/session, 2-3 sessions/week
- Introduce 30-second rest intervals every 2 minutes
- Progress distance if comfortable (10m → 15m → 20m)

*Weeks 7-12 (Maintenance):*
- Backstroke, 10-15 minutes/session, 3 sessions/week
- Supine rest 5 minutes post-session
- Do not increase intensity or duration beyond this level

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- 50-60% of mild-to-moderate ME/CFS patients with POTS comorbidity will tolerate this adapted swimming protocol without PEM
- Backstroke will show lower HR response vs. freestyle at matched RPE (ΔHR <5 bpm vs. ΔHR 10-15 bpm)
- Thermoneutral water will show lower orthostatic symptom score vs. non-thermoneutral pools (0-10 scale, ≤3 vs. 5-7)
- Patients who tolerate adapted swimming will show improved orthostatic tolerance on stand-test (HR decrease ≥5 bpm after 8 weeks)

**Relevance:** Addresses swimming modality safety (mentioned in literature search as gap). Provides patient-friendly protocol for aquaphobic patients who can tolerate horizontal movement in water. Tests hydrostatic pressure hypothesis (H1.2) in a real-world aquatic setting.

### N5.3 Resistance Band Protocol for hEDS+ME/CFS Comorbidity (Certainty: 0.40)

**Mechanistic rationale:** hEDS/HSD patients require muscle strengthening for joint stabilization, but standard resistance training exceeds ME/CFS PEM threshold. A resistance band protocol adapted for ME/CFS safety provides controlled, low-impact strengthening that targets deep stabilizers without triggering PEM. The protocol emphasizes submaximal intensity (30% 1RM), isometric-to-concentric-eccentric progression, seated/supine positioning, and compound movements over isolation.

**Proposed protocol structure:**

*Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Isometric foundation*
- Seated isometric holds: Shoulder press hold (3 × 10 sec), Chest press hold (3 × 10 sec), Seated row hold (3 × 10 sec)
- Band resistance: Light (purple/blue band, ~2-3 lbs equivalent)
- 2 sessions/week, 5 minutes/session
- HR monitoring: Target <70 bpm during holds

*Phase 2 (Weeks 3-4): Concentric-eccentric introduction*
- Seated chest press: 2 sets × 8 reps, slow tempo (3 sec up, 3 sec down)
- Seated row: 2 sets × 8 reps, slow tempo (3 sec pull, 3 sec return)
- Band resistance: Light to medium (3-5 lbs equivalent)
- 2 sessions/week, 8 minutes/session
- Rest intervals: 30 seconds supine rest between sets

*Phase 3 (Weeks 5-8): Compound movements*
- Seated chest press + overhead press combo: 2 sets × 8 reps
- Seated row + bicep curl combo: 2 sets × 8 reps
- Leg press against wall (supine): 2 sets × 10 reps
- Band resistance: Medium (5-7 lbs equivalent)
- 3 sessions/week, 10-12 minutes/session
- Symptom tracking at 24h and 48h post-session

*Phase 4 (Weeks 9-12): Maintenance*
- Full protocol (Phase 3 exercises), 3 sessions/week, 12-15 minutes/session
- Intensity increase only if no PEM for 4 consecutive sessions
- Goal: Joint stability maintenance, not strength increase

**Safety parameters:**
- Stop immediately if: HR >85 bpm, RPE >13, joint pain >3/10, muscle trembling
- Subluxation precautions: Avoid end-range positions, maintain mid-range only
- Hyperextension precautions: Avoid locking joints, maintain slight bend throughout movement
- PEM monitoring: Track symptoms 12-72 hours post-session; reduce intensity if PEM occurs

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- 70-80% of hEDS+ME/CFS patients will tolerate Phase 1-3 progression over 12 weeks without PEM
- Patients who progress to Phase 3 will show improved joint stability (reduced subluxation episodes by ≥50%)
- Patients who progress to Phase 3 will show improved functional outcomes (chair stand time reduced by ≥2 seconds)
- Resistance band protocol will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.5) vs. matched-intensity free-weight protocol

**Relevance:** Addresses critical comorbidity exercise conflict (hEDS strengthening vs. PEM risk). Provides implementable protocol for clinicians. Tests resistance band hypothesis (H1.4) in hypermobile population.

### N5.4 Breathing-Coupled Movement for Autonomic Stabilization During Exercise (Certainty: 0.45)

**Mechanistic rationale:** ME/CFS patients exhibit impaired respiratory mechanics (hyperpnea rather than tachypnea, low ETCO2) and autonomic instability. Coupling movement to slow-paced breathing (5-7 breaths/minute) during exercise may stabilize autonomic function by entraining HRV and reducing compensatory sympathetic activation. For aquatic exercise, slow breathing maximizes hydrostatic pressure benefit by optimizing chest expansion and venous return during inhalation. For resistance band exercises, breathing-coupled movement reduces muscle tension and prevents breath-holding (Valsalva), which triggers sympathetic activation.

**Proposed protocol structure:**

*Breathing technique:*
- 5-7 breaths/minute (inhale 4 sec, exhale 5-6 sec)
- Diaphragmatic breathing (abdominal expansion, not chest)
- No breath-holding during exertion (exhale during effort phase, inhale during recovery phase)

*Integration with aquatic exercise:*
- Each stroke synchronized to breath cycle: Inhale during recovery (return), exhale during drive (power)
- If breathing rate exceeds 7 breaths/minute: Reduce stroke rate or intensity immediately
- Focus on long, slow exhalation (5-6 seconds) to maximize parasympathetic activation

*Integration with resistance band exercises:*
- Exhale during concentric phase (press/pull), inhale during eccentric phase (return)
- Pause for 1 breath cycle between repetitions if needed
- Exhale duration ≥5 seconds (count "one-one-thousand, two-one-thousand,..." up to 5)

*Progressive protocol:*

*Weeks 1-2 (Baseline breathing training):*
- Practice breathing-coupled movement in supine position without exercise (5 minutes/day)
- Focus on diaphragmatic breathing, 6 breaths/minute
- No additional exercise during this phase

*Weeks 3-6 (Integration with aquatic exercise):*
- Add breathing-coupling to aquatic protocol (N5.1 or N5.2)
- Start with slow stroke rate (5-6 strokes/minute) to match breathing rhythm
- Progress intensity only if breathing rate remains ≤7 breaths/minute

*Weeks 7-12 (Integration with resistance band exercise):*
- Add breathing-coupling to resistance band protocol (N5.3)
- Exhale ≥5 seconds during concentric phase for every repetition
- Monitor for breath-holding (increase HR >15 bpm without corresponding intensity increase)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Breathing-coupled exercise will show lower HR response vs. uncoupled exercise at matched intensity (ΔHR <5 bpm vs. ΔHR 10-15 bpm)
- Breathing-coupled exercise will show higher HF-HRV (≥30% increase vs. ≤10% increase in uncoupled exercise)
- Breathing-coupled exercise will show lower catecholamine elevation (norepinephrine <300 pg/mL vs. 400-500 pg/mL in uncoupled exercise)
- Breathing-coupled exercise will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.6 vs. uncoupled exercise)

**Relevance:** Addresses autonomic instability during exercise. Low-cost, low-risk intervention. Tests breathing-augmented exercise safety. Aligns with Buteyko-adapted breathing in ME/CFS paper.

### N5.5 Post-Exercise Horizontal Rest Protocol for Blood Redistribution (Certainty: 0.55)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Exercise, even at sub-AT intensity, produces transient blood redistribution away from splanchnic circulation and toward working muscles. In ME/CFS patients with global hypoperfusion and orthostatic intolerance, this redistribution may exceed compensatory capacity, contributing to PEM. Post-exercise horizontal rest (supine or semi-recumbent, 5-10 minutes immediately after exercise) maximizes blood return to splanchnic circulation and brain, accelerating recovery of perfusion balance and reducing the risk of delayed PEM. This protocol extends the Klimas program's supine rest concept to all exercise modalities.

**Proposed protocol structure:**

*Protocol application:*
- Immediately after any exercise session (aquatic, resistance band, rowing, walking), transition to supine position within 1 minute
- Legs elevated 15-30 degrees (pillow under calves) to enhance venous return
- Duration: 5 minutes for sessions ≤10 minutes, 10 minutes for sessions 10-20 minutes
- Environment: Quiet, darkened room, minimal stimulation

*During rest:*
- Practice diaphragmatic breathing (6 breaths/minute) to enhance parasympathetic activation
- Focus on relaxation, not sleep (sleep may disrupt circadian rhythm if too late in day)
- Monitor for symptom improvement (HR should decrease to baseline, lightheadedness should resolve)

*Rest completion criteria:*
- HR within 5 bpm of baseline
- No orthostatic symptoms (lightheadedness, visual disturbance, nausea)
- Subjective sense of "settled" (not rushed or stressed)

*If criteria not met after 10 minutes:*
- Extend rest by 5-minute increments until criteria met (max 30 minutes total)
- If criteria never met: Exercise intensity was too high; reduce intensity for next session

*Progressive protocol:*

*Weeks 1-2 (Baseline):*
- Implement post-exercise horizontal rest for all activity (even minimal exertion like walking to bathroom)
- 5 minutes supine rest after any upright activity >2 minutes
- Log rest duration and symptom response

*Weeks 3-6 (Structured):*
- Apply to exercise protocols (N5.1, N5.2, N5.3)
- Monitor for delayed PEM with vs. without post-exercise rest (crossover within-subject comparison)
- Optimize rest duration based on individual response

*Weeks 7-12 (Maintenance):*
- Standardize post-exercise horizontal rest as mandatory part of all exercise sessions
- Use rest duration as indicator of appropriate intensity: Longer rest needed = intensity too high

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Post-exercise horizontal rest will reduce time to HR baseline (≤5 minutes vs. 10-15 minutes without rest)
- Post-exercise horizontal rest will reduce orthostatic symptom duration (≤2 minutes vs. 5-10 minutes without rest)
- Post-exercise horizontal rest will reduce PEM incidence (OR <0.5 vs. exercise without rest)
- Longer rest duration (>10 minutes) will correlate with higher exercise intensity, serving as intensity indicator

**Relevance:** Low-cost, low-risk intervention. Extends Klimas program concept to all modalities. Addresses blood redistribution mechanism of PEM. Tests horizontal rest hypothesis (N1.4 in ME/CFS paper) in exercise context.

### C6.1 Bedbound Aquatic Exercise Alternatives: Water-Immersion Supine Therapy (Certainty: 0.30)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Severe/bedbound ME/CFS patients cannot access standard aquatic therapy (pool facilities require transport, changing rooms, standing). However, the hydrostatic pressure benefit demonstrated by Schmid et al. 2007 can be delivered in home settings using specialized water-immersion baths or hydrotherapy tubs. Chest-deep immersion in thermoneutral water (32°C) while supine provides parasympathetic shift, increased preload, and reduced sympathetic tone—benefits accessible without standing or cardiovascular exertion. For bedbound patients, passive water immersion may be the only form of "exercise" that provides physiological benefit without triggering PEM.

**Proposed protocol structure:**

*Equipment requirements:*
- Hydrotherapy tub or bathtub with chest-depth immersion capacity (patient sitting or lying supine with water at nipple/axilla level)
- Thermoneutral water temperature control (31-32°C)
- Transfer assistance (caregiver or mechanical lift) for patients unable to step into tub
- Non-slip mats, grab bars, and fall-prevention measures

*Protocol:*

*Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Passive immersion*
- Chest-deep immersion (axilla level), supine or semi-recumbent in tub
- Duration: 10 minutes/session, 2 sessions/week
- No movement or exercise (passive immersion only)
- Temperature: 32°C thermoneutral water
- Monitor: HR, orthostatic symptoms, comfort level

*Phase 2 (Weeks 3-4): Gentle movement*
- Chest-deep immersion, add gentle arm movements (water walking in place, arm circles)
- Duration: 15 minutes/session, 2 sessions/week
- Intensity: RPE 9-10 (very light), HR monitoring (target <70 bpm)
- Focus on movement quality, not intensity

*Phase 3 (Weeks 5-8): Structured movement*
- Chest-deep immersion, seated or supine leg movements (knee extensions, ankle pumps)
- Add gentle trunk movements (pelvic tilts, side-to-side trunk sway)
- Duration: 20 minutes/session, 2-3 sessions/week
- Intensity: RPE 10-11 (light), HR <75 bpm
- Rest intervals: 2-minute passive rest every 5 minutes of movement

*Phase 4 (Weeks 9-12): Maintenance*
- Full protocol (Phase 3 exercises), 3 sessions/week, 20-25 minutes/session
- Do not increase intensity or duration beyond this level
- Goal: Autonomic stabilization and joint mobility, not fitness improvement

*Caregiver requirements:*
- Transfer assistance (patient in/out of tub)
- Water temperature monitoring and adjustment
- HR and symptom monitoring during session
- Emergency protocol (if lightheadedness, nausea, or excessive HR elevation)

*Falsifiable predictions:*
- 40-50% of bedbound ME/CFS patients will tolerate Phase 1-3 progression over 12 weeks without PEM
- Passive immersion (Phase 1) will show parasympathetic shift (HF-HRV increase, HR decrease ≥5 bpm) without any movement
- Bedbound patients who complete Phase 3 will show improved orthostatic tolerance (stand-test HR decrease ≥5 bpm if able to stand at all)
- Bedbound patients who complete Phase 3 will show improved joint mobility (reduced contractures, improved range of motion)

*Relevance:* Addresses severe/bedbound population gap. Provides home-accessible aquatic therapy. Tests hydrostatic pressure benefit without exercise. Low-intensity intervention with potential high impact for most vulnerable patients.

### C6.2 Community Pool Accessibility Toolkit for ME/CFS Patients (Certainty: 0.40)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Many ME/CFS patients could benefit from aquatic exercise but cannot access appropriate facilities due to: (1) lack of thermoneutral temperature control, (2) excessive ambient noise/stimulation, (3) inadequate accessible entry (ramps vs. ladders), (4) locker room barriers (changing, showers), (5) scheduling constraints (only open during limited hours). A community pool accessibility toolkit provides advocacy resources and facility modification guidelines to make aquatic therapy accessible for ME/CFS patients, addressing the "access" component of the exercise modality expansion.

**Proposed toolkit components:**

*Section 1: Patient advocacy guide*
- How to request disability accommodations at community pools
- Template letters to pool management requesting temperature modifications (31-33°C)
- Legal rights under ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) or equivalent disability legislation
- How to document medical necessity (physician letter, symptom diary)

*Section 2: Facility modification guidelines for pool managers*
- Temperature control recommendations: Maintain pool at 31-33°C for therapeutic aquatic programs
- Scheduling recommendations: Designate "quiet hours" for sensory-sensitive populations (ME/CFS, autism, PTSD)
- Entry modifications: Provide pool lifts or graduated entry ramps (avoid ladder climbing)

### C6.4 Severity-Stratified Exercise Modality Selection Algorithm (Certainty: 0.55)

**Mechanistic rationale:** ME/CFS severity ranges from mild (able to work part-time) to severe (housebound/bedbound). Exercise modality safety varies dramatically across severity levels. A severity-stratified algorithm provides evidence-based modality recommendations matched to functional capacity, ensuring that patients attempt only modalities appropriate for their severity level, reducing PEM risk and maximizing potential benefit. The algorithm incorporates constant-body-position criterion (H1.1), hydrostatic pressure benefit (H1.2), and orthostatic tolerance considerations.

**Proposed algorithm structure:**

*Severity level definitions (based on ME/CFS paper severity criteria):*

*Level 1 (Mild):* Able to work or attend school part-time, independent ADLs, minimal orthostatic symptoms, can walk 500+ meters without PEM.

*Level 2 (Moderate):* Able to perform ADLs independently but not work/school, reduced activity tolerance, orthostatic symptoms during standing, can walk 100-500 meters without PEM.

*Level 3 (Severe - Housebound):* Limited to home environment, can perform basic ADLs but with PEM risk, orthostatic intolerance significant, cannot walk >100 meters without PEM.

*Level 4 (Very Severe - Bedbound):* Bedbound majority of time, requires assistance for ADLs, severe orthostatic intolerance, cannot sit upright >10 minutes without PEM.

*Modality recommendations by severity:*

*Level 1 (Mild):*
- Primary: Recumbent bike, aquatic exercise (swimming, water rowing), seated resistance bands
- Secondary: Walking (short duration, below AT), upright cycling (if tolerated)
- Avoid: Prolonged standing, high-intensity activities, GET-style progressive programs
- Monitoring: HR monitoring at AT-15 bpm, PEM tracking 12-72 hours post-session

*Level 2 (Moderate):*
- Primary: Aquatic exercise (gentle swimming, water walking), seated resistance bands, recumbent bike
- Secondary: Isometric yoga (seated format), gentle stretching (seated/supine)
- Avoid: Upright exercise, prolonged standing, activities >10 minutes without rest
- Monitoring: HR monitoring at AT-20 bpm, mandatory supine rest post-session

*Level 3 (Severe - Housebound):*
- Primary: Seated resistance bands (isometric foundation), isometric yoga (seated), gentle stretching (supine)
- Secondary: Water-immersion supine therapy (if hydrotherapy tub accessible), bed-based passive movement
- Avoid: Any upright exercise, prolonged sitting, activities >5 minutes without rest
- Monitoring: HR monitoring at <70 bpm, PEM tracking 24-96 hours post-session

*Level 4 (Very Severe - Bedbound):*
- Primary: Passive movement only (caregier-assisted range of motion), supine gentle stretching (if tolerated)
- Secondary: Isometric holds (bed-based, 5-second contractions), breathing exercises
- Avoid: Any volitional exercise beyond basic ADLs
- Monitoring: Symptom tracking only (HR monitoring may be excessive cognitive load), focus on rest over activity

*Progression criteria:*
- Move to next severity level only if: (1) Sustained tolerance (≥4 weeks) at current level, (2) No PEM for 2 consecutive weeks, (3) Functional capacity improvement (increased walking distance, reduced orthostatic symptoms)
- If PEM occurs at any level: Return to previous level for 2 weeks before re-attempting progression

*Contraindications across all levels:*
- Do not attempt exercise during active PEM
- Do not exceed AT threshold (or <70 bpm for severe patients)
- Do not perform GET-style progressive increases without physician oversight
- Do not ignore delayed symptoms (PEM onset 24-72 hours)

*Falsifiable predictions:*
- Patients following severity-stratified algorithm will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.5) vs. patients selecting modalities arbitrarily
- Level-appropriate modality selection will show higher adherence (>80% session completion) vs. mismatched modalities (<50% session completion)
- Patients who progress to next severity level will show objective functional improvement (increased 6-minute walk test distance, reduced stand-test HR)
- Algorithm-based modality selection will improve patient confidence in exercise safety (confidence score ≥7/10 vs. ≤4/10 baseline)

*Relevance:* Provides structured, evidence-based approach to exercise modality selection. Addresses severity heterogeneity in ME/CFS. Tests whether appropriate modality matching improves outcomes. Practical tool for clinicians and patients.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### M7.1 Orthostatic Demand Index (ODI) for Exercise Modality Quantification (Certainty: 0.50)

**Mathematical concept:** Define a quantitative index that captures the orthostatic demand of different exercise modalities, enabling evidence-based selection based on individual orthostatic tolerance.

**Definition:**
ODI = Σ (orthostatic stress component × intensity factor × duration factor)

Where:
- Orthostatic stress component (OSC): Body position score (0 = supine, 1 = seated, 2 = standing, 3 = upright with movement)
- Intensity factor (IF): Metabolic equivalent (MET) or VO2 as fraction of anaerobic threshold (AT)
- Duration factor (DF): Session duration (minutes)

**Example calculations:**
- Aquatic swimming (horizontal, MET 4.0, 10 min): ODI = 0 × 4.0 × 10 = 0 (minimal orthostatic demand)
- Recumbent bike (seated, MET 3.0, 10 min): ODI = 1 × 3.0 × 10 = 30 (moderate orthostatic demand)
- Upright cycling (upright, MET 4.0, 10 min): ODI = 2 × 4.0 × 10 = 80 (high orthostatic demand)
- Walking (upright with movement, MET 3.0, 10 min): ODI = 3 × 3.0 × 10 = 90 (highest orthostatic demand)

**Individual orthostatic tolerance threshold:**
ODI_threshold = k × (stand-test HR increase) × (orthostatic symptom severity)

Where k is an individual calibration constant (empirically determined).

**Application:**
Select exercise modality where ODI_modality < ODI_threshold.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- ME/CFS patients with higher stand-test HR increase will have lower ODI_threshold (require lower-ODI modalities)
- Modalities with ODI < individual threshold will show lower PEM incidence vs. modalities with ODI > threshold
- ODI will correlate with PEM risk across different modalities (higher ODI = higher PEM incidence, dose-response relationship)
- Individual ODI_threshold will increase over time with exercise tolerance improvement (quantifying progression)

**Relevance:** Provides quantitative framework for modality selection. Tests constant-body-position hypothesis (H1.1) mathematically. Enables personalized exercise prescription based on objective orthostatic tolerance measures.

### M7.2 Body Position Score (BPS) for Quantifying Positional Stability (Certainty: 0.55)

**Mathematical concept:** Define a quantitative score that captures the positional stability (or lack thereof) of different exercise modalities, aligning with the constant-body-position safety criterion.

**Definition:**
BPS = 1 - (positional change frequency × positional magnitude) / (session duration)

Where:
- Positional change frequency (PCF): Number of body position changes per minute (e.g., supine ↔ seated, standing ↔ walking, swimming turns)
- Positional magnitude (PM): Magnitude of each position change on orthostatic stress scale (0 = minor shift within same category, 1 = shift between adjacent categories, 2 = shift between non-adjacent categories)
- Session duration (SD): Total session duration in minutes

**Interpretation:**
- BPS = 1.0: Perfectly constant body position (e.g., recumbent bike, seated rowing, continuous swimming backstroke)
- BPS = 0.8-0.9: Minor positional variations (e.g., swimming with turns, resistance band exercises with posture shifts)
- BPS = 0.5-0.7: Moderate positional variations (e.g., walking with stops, upright cycling with breaks)
- BPS = <0.5: Major positional variations (e.g., circuit training, sports with frequent starts/stops)

**Example calculations:**
- Recumbent bike (10 minutes, 0 position changes): BPS = 1 - (0 × 0) / 10 = 1.0
- Seated rowing (10 minutes, 0 position changes): BPS = 1 - (0 × 0) / 10 = 1.0
- Swimming backstroke (10 minutes, 4 turns, PM = 1 each): BPS = 1 - (0.4 × 1) / 10 = 0.96
- Walking (10 minutes, 2 stops/starts, PM = 2 each): BPS = 1 - (0.2 × 2) / 10 = 0.96
- Circuit training (10 minutes, 8 station changes, PM = 1 each): BPS = 1 - (0.8 × 1) / 10 = 0.92

**Individual BPS tolerance threshold:**
BPS_threshold = m × (orthostatic symptom severity) + b

Where m and b are individual calibration constants (empirically determined).

**Application:**
Select exercise modality where BPS_modality ≥ BPS_threshold.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- ME/CFS patients with higher orthostatic symptom severity will have lower BPS_threshold (require higher BPS = more constant position)
- Modalities with BPS ≥ individual threshold will show lower PEM incidence vs. modalities with BPS < threshold
- BPS will correlate with autonomic stress markers (higher BPS = lower catecholamine elevation, lower sympathetic activation)
- BPS threshold will increase over time with exercise tolerance improvement (quantifying progression toward dynamic activities)

**Relevance:** Provides quantitative framework for constant-body-position hypothesis (H1.1). Tests position-based modality safety. Enables personalized modality selection based on orthostatic symptom burden.

### M7.3 Hydrostatic Pressure Benefit Index (HPBI) for Aquatic Modality Quantification (Certainty: 0.68)

**Mathematical concept:** Define a quantitative index that captures the hydrostatic pressure benefit of different aquatic modalities, enabling comparison with land-based modalities and optimization of aquatic protocols.

**Definition:**
HPBI = (immersion depth × immersion duration × temperature factor) / (intensity factor)

Where:
- Immersion depth (ID): Depth of water immersion as fraction of body height (0 = none, 0.5 = waist-deep, 1.0 = chest-deep, 1.5 = neck-deep)
- Immersion duration (IDur): Total time immersed (minutes)
- Temperature factor (TF): Deviation from thermoneutral temperature (TF = 1.0 at 32°C, TF = 0.8 at 28°C or 36°C, TF = 0.5 at <25°C or >38°C)
- Intensity factor (IF): Metabolic equivalent (MET) or VO2 as fraction of AT

**Interpretation:**
- HPBI > 5.0: Strong hydrostatic benefit (highly favorable aquatic modality)
- HPBI 3.0-5.0: Moderate hydrostatic benefit (favorable aquatic modality)
- HPBI 1.0-3.0: Weak hydrostatic benefit (aquatic modality with compromised parameters)
- HPBI < 1.0: Negative hydrostatic benefit (thermally stressful or intensity too high)

**Example calculations:**
- Thermoneutral swimming (chest-deep ID=1.0, 10 min, TF=1.0, MET=4.0): HPBI = (1.0 × 10 × 1.0) / 4.0 = 2.5 (moderate benefit)
- Thermoneutral water rowing (chest-deep ID=1.0, 10 min, TF=1.0, MET=3.0): HPBI = (1.0 × 10 × 1.0) / 3.0 = 3.3 (moderate-high benefit)
- Cold pool swimming (chest-deep ID=1.0, 10 min, TF=0.5, MET=4.0): HPBI = (1.0 × 10 × 0.5) / 4.0 = 1.25 (weak benefit)
- Warm pool immersion (neck-deep ID=1.5, 10 min, TF=0.8, MET=2.0): HPBI = (1.5 × 10 × 0.8) / 2.0 = 6.0 (strong benefit)

**Individual HPBI tolerance threshold:**
HPBI_threshold = h × (orthostatic tolerance) × (temperature sensitivity)

Where h is an individual calibration constant (empirically determined).

**Application:**
Select aquatic modality where HPBI_modality ≥ HPBI_threshold.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- ME/CFS patients with lower orthostatic tolerance will have higher HPBI_threshold (require stronger hydrostatic benefit)
- Aquatic modalities with HPBI ≥ individual threshold will show greater parasympathetic shift (higher HF-HRV increase) vs. modalities with HPBI < threshold
- HPBI will correlate with orthostatic symptom improvement (higher HPBI = greater reduction in stand-test HR)
- HPBI will be highest in thermoneutral, chest-deep, low-intensity modalities (confirming Schmid et al. 2007 parameters)

**Relevance:** Provides quantitative framework for hydrostatic pressure hypothesis (H1.2). Tests aquatic modality optimization. Enables personalized aquatic therapy prescription based on individual orthostatic and temperature sensitivity.

### M7.4 Compound vs. Isolated Movement Stress Ratio (CIMSR) for Muscle Perfusion Analysis (Certainty: 0.40)

**Mathematical concept:** Define a quantitative ratio that captures the differential stress on muscle perfusion between compound and isolated movements, testing H1.6.

**Definition:**
CIMSR = (compound movement local muscle oxygenation) / (isolated movement local muscle oxygenation)

Where:
- Compound movement local muscle oxygenation (CMO): NIRS-measured oxygenation in primary working muscle during compound movement (e.g., back muscles during seated row)
- Isolated movement local muscle oxygenation (IMO): NIRS-measured oxygenation in primary working muscle during isolated movement (e.g., biceps during bicep curl)

**Interpretation:**
- CIMSR > 1.2: Compound movement provides significantly better local perfusion (supports H1.6)
- CIMSR 0.9-1.2: No significant difference in local perfusion
- CIMSR < 0.9: Isolated movement provides better local perfusion (refutes H1.6)

**Normalized to workload:**
CIMSR_norm = CIMSR / (compound movement VO2 / isolated movement VO2)

This normalizes for differences in total metabolic demand.

**Individual CIMSR threshold:**
CIMSR_threshold = c × (perfusion reserve) + d

Where c and d are individual calibration constants (empirically determined from CPET perfusion measures).

**Application:**
If CIMSR ≥ threshold, prioritize compound movements. If CIMSR < threshold, prioritize isolated movements (or reduce intensity).

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- ME/CFS patients will show CIMSR > 1.2 (compound movements provide better local perfusion) vs. healthy controls (CIMSR ~1.0)
- CIMSR will correlate with HR response (higher CIMSR = lower HR response at matched RPE)
- Patients with lower perfusion reserve (from CPET) will show higher CIMSR (greater benefit from compound movements)
- CIMSR will improve over time with exercise tolerance improvement (quantifying perfusion adaptation)

**Relevance:** Provides quantitative framework for compound vs. isolated hypothesis (H1.6). Tests muscle perfusion mechanism. Enables personalized modality selection based on individual perfusion reserve.

### M7.5 PEM Risk Equation Incorporating Modality Factors (Certainty: 0.50)

**Mathematical concept:** Define a predictive equation for PEM risk that incorporates exercise modality factors (orthostatic demand, body position, hydrostatic pressure), individual factors (severity, orthostatic tolerance, metabolic reserve), and protocol factors (intensity, duration, recovery).

**Definition:**
PEM_risk = α × (ODI × severity) + β × ((1 - BPS) × orthostatic_sensitivity) + γ × ((1 - HPBI) × temperature_sensitivity) + δ × (intensity × duration / metabolic_reserve) - ε × (recovery_quality)

Where:
- ODI: Orthostatic Demand Index (M7.1)
- BPS: Body Position Score (M7.2)
- HPBI: Hydrostatic Pressure Benefit Index (M7.3)
- Intensity: Fraction of anaerobic threshold (AT)
- Duration: Session duration (minutes)
- Severity: Patient severity level (1-4 scale)
- Orthostatic_sensitivity: Individual orthostatic symptom severity (0-10 scale)
- Temperature_sensitivity: Individual temperature sensitivity (0-10 scale)
- Metabolic_reserve: Individual metabolic reserve from CPET (fraction of normal reserve)
- Recovery_quality: Post-exercise rest quality (0-10 scale, incorporating rest duration and horizontal positioning)
- α, β, γ, δ, ε: Weighting coefficients (empirically determined)

**Interpretation:**
- PEM_risk < 20: Low PEM risk (modalities likely safe)
- PEM_risk 20-40: Moderate PEM risk (modalities possibly safe with careful monitoring)
- PEM_risk > 40: High PEM risk (modalities likely unsafe)

**Application:**
Calculate PEM_risk for proposed exercise protocol. If PEM_risk < individual tolerance threshold, proceed with monitoring. If PEM_risk > threshold, modify protocol parameters (reduce intensity, increase recovery, change modality) to reduce risk.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- PEM_risk will correlate with actual PEM incidence across different modalities (higher predicted risk = higher actual PEM)
- The ODI term (α) will have highest weight in POTS+ME/CFS patients (orthostatic demand is primary PEM driver)
- The HPBI term (γ) will have highest weight in thermally sensitive patients (temperature sensitivity modulates aquatic benefit)
- PEM_risk will decrease over time with exercise tolerance improvement (metabolic_reserve increase, orthostatic_sensitivity decrease)

**Relevance:** Integrates all modality hypotheses into unified predictive framework. Provides quantitative tool for exercise prescription. Tests interactions between modality factors and individual characteristics.

### X8.4 Hypermobility Spectrum Disorder Resistance Training Parallels: PEM-Safe Adaptation (Certainty: 0.40)

**Background:** Resistance training in hypermobility spectrum disorders (hEDS/HSD) emphasizes: (1) submaximal intensity (30-50% 1RM) to avoid joint injury, (2) eccentric focus for tendon strengthening, (3) compound movements over isolation, (4) isometric exercises for joint stability, (5) slow tempo (3-0-3 or 4-0-4) to control movement. hEDS/HSD resistance training has demonstrated improvements in joint stability, subluxation rates, and pain.

**Parallels to ME/CFS:**
- Comorbidity prevalence: Significant overlap between hEDS/HSD and ME/CFS (estimated 20-30% comorbidity)
- Submaximal intensity principle: Both conditions require intensity below threshold (joints for hEDS, PEM for ME/CFS)
- Compound movement preference: Both conditions benefit from distributing load across multiple muscle groups
- Isometric value: Both conditions benefit from isometric exercises (joint stability for hEDS, PEM safety for ME/CFS)

**Adaptation for ME/CFS:**
- Lower intensity ceiling: hEDS protocols target 30-50% 1RM; ME/CFS adaptation targets 20-30% 1RM (more conservative)
- Slower tempo: hEDS uses 3-0-3 or 4-0-4; ME/CFS adaptation uses 5-0-5 (very slow)
- Shorter duration: hEDS targets 20-30 minute sessions; ME/CFS adaptation targets 8-12 minute sessions
- PEM monitoring integration: hEDS monitors for joint pain; ME/CFS adaptation adds 12-72 hour PEM tracking
- Supine positioning emphasis: hEDS uses seated/standing; ME/CFS adaptation emphasizes supine/seated only

**Proposed ME/CFS-adapted hEDS resistance protocol:**

*Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Isometric foundation*
- Seated isometric holds (shoulder press, chest press, row), 20-30% estimated 1RM, 3 × 10 sec, 2 sessions/week
- Very slow tempo: 5 sec contraction, 10 sec hold, 5 sec relaxation
- HR monitoring: Target <70 bpm

*Phase 2 (Weeks 3-6): Concentric-eccentric introduction*
- Seated resistance band exercises (chest press, row, shoulder press), 20-30% 1RM, 2 sets × 8 reps, 2-3 sessions/week
- Very slow tempo: 5 sec concentric, 2 sec isometric, 5 sec eccentric (5-2-5)
- Rest intervals: 30 sec supine rest between sets

*Phase 3 (Weeks 7-12): Compound progression*
- Seated compound movements (chest press + overhead press, row + bicep curl), 25-30% 1RM, 2-3 sets × 8 reps, 3 sessions/week
- Very slow tempo: 5-2-5 throughout
- PEM monitoring mandatory: Stop if PEM occurs, rollback 1 phase for 2 weeks

*Falsifiable predictions:*
- ME/CFS-adapted resistance protocol will show lower PEM incidence (OR <0.5) vs. standard hEDS protocol
- Patients completing Phase 3 will show improved joint stability (subluxation episodes decrease by ≥40%)
- Patients completing Phase 3 will show improved functional outcomes (chair stand time decrease by ≥2 seconds)
- Very slow tempo (5-2-5) will show lower HR response vs. standard hEDS tempo (3-0-3) (ΔHR <5 bpm vs. ΔHR 10-15 bpm)

**Relevance:** Addresses hEDS+ME/CFS comorbidity exercise conflict. Leverages established hEDS resistance training principles. Tests whether submaximal, slow-tempo resistance generalizes from joint safety to PEM safety.

### D9.1 Orthostatic-Demand-by-Modality Index (ODMI) for Personalized Modality Selection (Certainty: 0.55)

**Concept:** Develop a diagnostic index that quantifies an individual ME/CFS patient's orthostatic demand tolerance across different exercise modalities, enabling personalized modality selection based on objective physiological measures rather than trial-and-error.

**Measurement protocol:**

1. *Baseline assessment:*
   - Tilt-table test with continuous HR, BP, and catecholamine monitoring
   - Stand-test HR increase (ΔHR_stand)
   - Orthostatic symptom severity score (0-10 scale)
   - CPET to determine anaerobic threshold (AT)

2. *Modality testing (crossover, random order):*
   - Test 4 modalities at matched intensity (60% AT, 10 minutes):
     - Modality A: Recumbent bike (constant seated position)
     - Modality B: Aquatic exercise (chest-deep, thermoneutral)
     - Modality C: Upright cycling (constant upright position)
     - Modality D: Walking (upright with movement, varying position)
   - Continuous HR monitoring during and 30 minutes post-exercise
   - Catecholamine levels pre- and post-exercise
   - Orthostatic symptom score pre- and post-exercise
   - HRV (LF/HF ratio, RMSSD) pre- and post-exercise

3. *ODMI calculation:*
   - For each modality, calculate ODMI_score = (ΔHR_exercise / ΔHR_stand) × (post-exercise catecholamine / baseline) × (post-exercise symptom score / baseline)
   - Lower ODMI_score = better orthostatic demand tolerance for that modality

4. *Individual modality ranking:*
   - Rank modalities from lowest to highest ODMI_score
   - Recommend lowest ODMI_score modality as primary exercise choice

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Recumbent modalities (bike, aquatic) will show lower ODMI_score vs. upright modalities (cycling, walking)
- Patients with higher baseline ΔHR_stand will show greater ODMI_score differences between modalities
- ODMI_score will correlate with PEM incidence (higher ODMI_score = higher PEM risk)
- ODMI_ranking will be stable over 8-week period (test-retest reliability r >0.7)

**Clinical application:**
- Use ODMI_ranking to prescribe primary exercise modality
- Reassess ODMI_ranking after 12 weeks if exercise tolerance improves
- Use ODMI_score progression to quantify tolerance improvement (lower ODMI_score over time = better orthostatic adaptation)

**Relevance:** Provides objective diagnostic tool for modality selection. Reduces trial-and-error and PEM risk from inappropriate modality choice. Tests constant-body-position hypothesis (H1.1) with individual-level data.

### D9.2 Heart Rate Response Profiling for Modality Tolerance Prediction (Certainty: 0.50)

**Concept:** Develop a diagnostic profile that uses heart rate response patterns during different modalities to predict which modalities will be safest (lowest PEM risk) for individual patients.

**Measurement protocol:**

1. *Brief modality test (each modality 2-3 minutes, sufficient for HR response stabilization):*
   - Test 6 modalities in random order:
     - Supine rest (baseline)
     - Seated resistance bands
     - Recumbent bike
     - Aquatic immersion (chest-deep, no movement)
     - Upright cycling
     - Walking
   - Continuous HR telemetry during each test
   - Measure: baseline HR, peak HR, time to HR stabilization, HR recovery time (time to return to baseline after test)

2. *HR response profile calculation:*
   - For each modality, calculate:
     - ΔHR_peak = peak HR - baseline HR
     - ΔHR_stabilization = HR at 2 minutes - baseline HR
     - Recovery_time = time for HR to return to within 5 bpm of baseline
   - Create individual HR response profile: ranking of modalities by ΔHR_peak (lower = better tolerance)

3. *Modality tolerance prediction:*
   - Modalities with ΔHR_peak <10 bpm and Recovery_time <2 minutes: Predicted "high tolerance" (low PEM risk)
   - Modalities with ΔHR_peak 10-20 bpm or Recovery_time 2-5 minutes: Predicted "moderate tolerance" (possible PEM risk)
   - Modalities with ΔHR_peak >20 bpm or Recovery_time >5 minutes: Predicted "low tolerance" (high PEM risk)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- HR response profile ranking will correlate with actual PEM incidence over 4-week trial (higher ΔHR_peak = higher PEM)
- Brief 2-3 minute tests will be sufficient to predict tolerance (no need for longer sessions for diagnostic purposes)
- Aquatic immersion will show lowest ΔHR_peak for most patients (confirming hydrostatic benefit)
- Patients with higher baseline orthostatic HR increase will show greater ΔHR_peak differences between modalities

**Clinical application:**
- Use HR response profile as initial screening tool for modality selection
- For patients unable to tolerate longer exercise sessions, brief HR response testing provides modality guidance without triggering PEM
- Repeat HR response profile after 12 weeks of exercise to track tolerance improvement (lower ΔHR_peak over time)

**Relevance:** Low-risk, low-burden diagnostic tool. Provides objective modality guidance without requiring full exercise sessions. Tests whether HR response predicts PEM risk across modalities.

### D9.3 Muscle Oxygenation (NIRS) Profiling for Compound vs. Isolated Movement Tolerance (Certainty: 0.40)

**Concept:** Use near-infrared spectroscopy (NIRS) to measure local muscle oxygenation during compound vs. isolated movements, identifying which movement pattern provides better perfusion and lower ischemic stress for individual patients.

**Measurement protocol:**

1. *NIRS sensor placement:*
   - Place NIRS sensors on primary working muscles:
     - Compound movement: Back muscles (latissimus dorsi, rhomboids) for seated row
     - Isolated movement: Biceps brachii for bicep curl

2. *Movement testing (crossover, random order):*
   - Test compound movement (seated row): 2 sets × 8 reps at matched RPE (10-11)
   - Test isolated movement (bicep curl): 2 sets × 8 reps at matched RPE (10-11)
   - Continuous NIRS oxygenation monitoring during each movement
   - Measure: baseline oxygenation, minimum oxygenation (desaturation), time to recovery (return to baseline)

3. *NIRS profile calculation:*
   - For each movement, calculate:
     - Oxygenation_drop = (baseline - minimum) / baseline (fractional desaturation)
     - Recovery_time = time for oxygenation to return to within 10% of baseline
   - Compare compound vs. isolated: Calculate CIMSR = (compound oxygenation) / (isolated oxygenation) during effort phase

4. *Movement tolerance prediction:*
   - If CIMSR >1.2: Compound movement provides better perfusion (prioritize compound movements)
   - If CIMSR 0.9-1.2: No significant difference (either pattern acceptable)
   - If CIMSR <0.9: Isolated movement provides better perfusion (prioritize isolated movements)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Most ME/CFS patients will show CIMSR >1.2 (supporting H1.6)
- CIMSR will correlate with HR response (higher CIMSR = lower HR at matched RPE)
- Patients with lower perfusion reserve (from CPET) will show higher CIMSR (greater benefit from compound movements)
- NIRS oxygenation drop during isolated movements will predict PEM risk (greater desaturation = higher PEM incidence)

**Clinical application:**
- Use NIRS profiling to personalize movement pattern selection (compound vs. isolated)
- For patients with severely reduced perfusion reserve, emphasize compound movements to distribute metabolic load
- Use NIRS oxygenation as real-time biofeedback during exercise sessions (stop if desaturation exceeds individual threshold)

**Relevance:** Provides objective measure of muscle perfusion during different movement patterns. Tests compound vs. isolated hypothesis (H1.6) at individual level. Enables personalized movement pattern selection.

### D9.4 Autonomic Balance Profiling for Hydrostatic Pressure Benefit Quantification (Certainty: 0.55)

**Concept:** Use autonomic balance measures (HRV, catecholamines, baroreflex sensitivity) to quantify individual response to hydrostatic pressure immersion, identifying patients who will benefit most from aquatic exercise modalities.

**Measurement protocol:**

1. *Baseline autonomic assessment:*
   - HRV measurement: LF/HF ratio, RMSSD, HF power (5-minute resting supine and standing)
   - Catecholamines: Norepinephrine, epinephrine baseline levels
   - Baroreflex sensitivity: Sequence method from continuous BP and HR

2. *Hydrostatic immersion test:*
   - Immersion: Chest-deep in thermoneutral water (32°C), 10 minutes, no movement
   - Continuous HRV monitoring during immersion
   - Catecholamine levels pre- and post-immersion
   - Baroreflex sensitivity pre- and post-immersion

3. *Autonomic response calculation:*
   - Calculate parasympathetic shift magnitude:
     - ΔHF-HRV = HF power during immersion - baseline HF power
     - ΔLF/HF = LF/HF ratio during immersion - baseline LF/HF
     - ΔNorepinephrine = post-immersion norepinephrine - baseline norepinephrine
   - Calculate Hydrostatic Benefit Score (HBS) = (ΔHF-HRV - ΔLF/HF - ΔNorepinephrine) / baseline HRV
   - Higher HBS = greater parasympathetic benefit from hydrostatic pressure

4. *Aquatic modality recommendation:*
   - If HBS >0.3: Strong hydrostatic benefit (prioritize aquatic modalities)
   - If HBS 0.1-0.3: Moderate hydrostatic benefit (aquatic modalities acceptable, monitor response)
   - If HBS <0.1: Weak or negative hydrostatic benefit (avoid aquatic modalities, prioritize land-based)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Patients with higher baseline sympathetic dominance (high LF/HF, low HF-HRV) will show greater HBS (more benefit from hydrostatic pressure)
- HBS will correlate with PEM reduction during aquatic exercise (higher HBS = lower PEM incidence)
- Patients with HBS <0.1 may experience symptom exacerbation during aquatic exercise (negative response)
- HBS will be stable over 8-week period (test-retest reliability r >0.7)

**Clinical application:**
- Use HBS to determine whether aquatic exercise is appropriate for individual patient
- For patients with HBS <0.1, avoid aquatic modalities despite theoretical benefit
- For patients with high HBS (>0.3), aquatic exercise is priority modality
- Use HBS to track autonomic adaptation over time (increasing HBS = improving parasympathetic reserve)

**Relevance:** Provides objective measure of individual response to hydrostatic pressure. Identifies patients who will benefit from aquatic modalities vs. those who may not respond or may worsen. Tests hydrostatic pressure hypothesis (H1.2) at individual level.

### D9.5 Temperature Sensitivity Profiling for Aquatic Therapy Optimization (Certainty: 0.55)

**Concept:** Develop a diagnostic profile that quantifies individual temperature sensitivity during water immersion, identifying optimal water temperature for aquatic therapy in ME/CFS patients.

**Measurement protocol:**

1. *Temperature sensitivity baseline:*
   - Symptom response to environmental temperature changes (heat vs. cold sensitivity)
   - Baseline thermal comfort score (0-10 scale) at room temperature (22-24°C)

2. *Temperature immersion test (crossover, random order):*
   - Test 3 water temperatures in random order:
     - Cold: 28°C
     - Thermoneutral: 32°C
     - Warm: 35°C
   - Immersion: Chest-deep, 5 minutes each temperature, 30-minute washout between temperatures
   - Continuous HR monitoring during immersion
   - Symptom assessment pre- and post-immersion (orthostatic symptoms, thermal comfort, overall well-being)
   - HRV measurement (LF/HF ratio, HF power) during immersion

3. *Temperature response calculation:*
   - For each temperature, calculate:
     - ΔHR = peak HR during immersion - baseline HR
     - ΔLF/HF = LF/HF during immersion - baseline LF/HF
     - Symptom score change = post-immersion symptom score - baseline
     - Thermal comfort score during immersion (0-10 scale)
   - Calculate Temperature Tolerance Score (TTS) = Thermal comfort - (ΔHR + ΔLF/HF + Symptom change)/3
   - Higher TTS = better temperature tolerance

4. *Optimal temperature recommendation:*
   - Select water temperature with highest TTS for individual patient
   - If TTS <3 for all temperatures: Avoid aquatic immersion, consider land-based modalities

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- Most ME/CFS patients will show highest TTS at thermoneutral temperature (32°C)
- Patients with heat sensitivity will show lowest TTS at warm temperature (35°C)
- Patients with cold sensitivity will show lowest TTS at cold temperature (28°C)
- Patients with low TTS at all temperatures (<3) will experience PEM exacerbation from aquatic exercise regardless of temperature

**Clinical application:**
- Use TTS to individualize aquatic therapy water temperature (not all patients need 32°C; some tolerate 30°C or 34°C better)
- For patients with TTS <3 at all temperatures, avoid aquatic therapy despite theoretical hydrostatic benefit
- Use TTS to advocate for facility temperature accommodations (individualized vs. one-size-fits-all)
- Repeat TTS assessment after 12 weeks if aquatic tolerance improves (temperature sensitivity may improve over time)

**Relevance:** Addresses critical implementation barrier (water temperature variability). Tests temperature hypothesis (H1.5) at individual level. Enables personalized aquatic therapy prescription rather than assuming 32°C is optimal for all patients.
