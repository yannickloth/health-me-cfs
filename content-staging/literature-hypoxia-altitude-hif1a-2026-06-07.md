# Literature: Hypoxia, High Altitude, HIF1α Signalling, EPO, and ME/CFS
## Session: 2026-06-07 | research_stream: hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

---

## Summary of Literature Landscape

**Direct ME/CFS evidence:** No studies have directly examined ME/CFS patients at high altitude or in hypobaric/hypoxic chambers. The closest direct evidence comes from studies of cerebral blood flow autoregulation and CO2 reactivity in ME/CFS (Badhwar 2025, Medow 2024, Barnden 2011), which consistently show impaired cerebrovascular control that would compound hypoxic stress. Several ME/CFS studies independently converge on a tissue hypoxia hypothesis (Hoel 2021, Wirth & Scheibenbogen 2020, Kell 2022).

**Cross-disease evidence:** HIF-1α has been mechanistically linked to autoimmune tissue damage in lupus (Little 2023), and HIF-2α activation drives endothelial dysfunction in post-COVID syndrome (Ribeiro 2026). Hypoxic conditioning has demonstrated safety and potential benefit in Parkinson's disease (Janssen Daalen 2025).

**Mechanistic inference:** The HIF pathway sits at the intersection of multiple ME/CFS-relevant processes: cerebral autoregulation, metabolic adaptation to hypoxia, immune cell function, and erythropoiesis. The absence of direct altitude/ME/CFS studies is a significant research gap.

## Papers Added (12 new entries)

### 1. Badhwar et al. 2025 — Cerebrovascular Control in ME/CFS and Long COVID
**BibTeX key:** `Badhwar2025brainbloodflow`
**Journal:** Autonomic Neuroscience (2025)
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.autneu.2025.103358
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** n=12 ME/CFS, n=9 Long COVID, n=10 controls. Breathing 5% CO2, 10% O2 (hypoxia), 70° head-up tilt. Measured hemodynamic and cerebrovascular variables.

**Key findings:**
- ME/CFS vs controls: similar resting vascular function and hemodynamic responses to hypoxia, hypercapnia, and tilt (p > 0.05)
- ME/CFS showed greater reduction of cerebrovascular resistance during hypercapnia (p = 0.041) and impaired autoregulation (p = 0.042) alongside impaired cognitive-motor integration (p < 0.02)
- Long COVID showed reduced peripheral and end-tidal oxygen (p < 0.04) and less vagal withdrawal during tilt (p = 0.028)
- Unique phenotypes: Long COVID = hypoxia while upright; ME/CFS = impaired cerebrovascular control during hypercapnia

**Conclusion:** ME/CFS and Long COVID have distinct autonomic/cerebrovascular phenotypes. ME/CFS involves impaired cerebrovascular control during hypercapnia potentially reducing cognitive-motor integration.

**Limitations:** Small sample size; mild/moderate ME/CFS only; cross-sectional; single-centre. Causal direction unclear.

### 2. Medow and Stewart 2024 — Cerebral Autoregulation in ME/CFS with Orthostatic Intolerance
**BibTeX key:** `Medow2024cerebralautoregulation`
**Journal:** American Journal of Physiology-Regulatory, Integrative and Comparative Physiology (2024)
**DOI:** 10.1152/ajpregu.00071.2024
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** n=15 ME/CFS + POTS, n=11 controls. Head-up tilt (HUT) with N-back testing. Phase synchronization index (PhSI) between arterial pressure and cerebral blood velocity.

**Key findings:**
- HUT in ME/CFS: HR increased (109 vs 77 bpm), ETCO2 decreased (33.9 vs 42.8 Torr), CBFv decreased 22.5% vs 8.7% controls
- Phenylephrine corrected orthostatic cognitive decline and reduced PhSI (0.80 → 0.69), indicating improved cerebral autoregulation
- CO2 and acetazolamide did NOT improve autoregulation or cognition
- Only phenylephrine improved upright N-back performance

**Conclusion:** Impaired cerebral autoregulation in ME/CFS can be rescued by preventing hyperventilation/hypocapnia via baroreflex loading.

**Limitations:** All patients had POTS — may not generalize to non-OI ME/CFS. Small sample. Pharmacological intervention only, not altitude.

### 3. Barnden et al. 2011 — Brainstem Dysfunction and Impaired Autoregulation in CFS
**BibTeX key:** `Barnden2011brainstemCFS`
**Journal:** NMR in Biomedicine (2011)
**DOI:** 10.1002/nbm.1692
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** n=25 CFS, n=25 controls. Voxel-based morphometry, T1/T2-weighted MRI signal analysis with regression against clinical scores and hemodynamic parameters.

**Key findings:**
- Midbrain white matter volume decreased with increasing fatigue duration
- Group × hemodynamic score interactions in brainstem GM, deep prefrontal WM, caudal basal pons, hypothalamus
- Strong correlation in CFS between brainstem GM volume and pulse pressure → impaired cerebrovascular autoregulation
- Findings consistent with midbrain insult at fatigue onset disrupting multiple feedback control loops
- Astrocyte dysfunction proposed as underlying mechanism

**Conclusion:** Brainstem structural changes in CFS correlate with impaired cerebrovascular autoregulation, suggesting a central mechanism for cerebral hypoperfusion.

**Limitations:** Cross-sectional; modest sample size; single-centre; 2011 — imaging methods have advanced.

### 4. Hoel et al. 2021 — Metabolic Phenotypes in ME/CFS: Tissue Hypoxia Hypothesis
**BibTeX key:** `Hoel2021metabolicphenotypes`
**Journal:** JCI Insight (2021)
**DOI:** 10.1172/jci.insight.149217
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** n=83 ME/CFS, n=35 controls. Global metabolomics, lipidomics, hormone measurements. Three subsets identified by metabolic profiles.

**Key findings:**
- Common changes across patients: elevated energy strain, altered fatty acid/amino acid utilization
- Heterogeneous effects in 3 patient subsets with distinct energy metabolism deregulation contexts
- Authors propose exertion-triggered tissue hypoxia as driver of systemic metabolic adaptation
- Metabolic dysfunction mediates key symptoms and may be a target for intervention

**Conclusion:** ME/CFS involves exertion-triggered tissue hypoxia leading to systemic metabolic adaptation and compensation.

**Limitations:** Cross-sectional; different subsets may reflect different disease stages or etiologies. Direct hypoxia measurement not performed.

### 5. Kell and Pretorius 2022 — Ischaemia-Reperfusion Injury in Long COVID and ME/CFS
**BibTeX key:** `Kell2022IRinjury`
**Journal:** Biochemical Journal (2022)
**DOI:** 10.1042/BCJ20220154
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Comprehensive review proposing ischaemia-reperfusion (I-R) injury as pathogenic mechanism in rheumatoid arthritis, Long COVID, and ME/CFS.

**Key findings:**
- Fibrin amyloid microclots block capillaries → ischaemia on exercise initiation; reperfusion on cessation
- I-R injury manifests as oxidative stress, hyperinflammation, mast cell activation
- Explains breathlessness/fatigue and PEM
- Proposes therapeutic benefit from antioxidants, anti-inflammatories, iron chelators, safe fibrinolytics
- Extensive mechanistic evidence from multiple disease contexts

**Conclusion:** Microclot-mediated I-R injury may underpin PEM and fatigue in ME/CFS and Long COVID through repeated hypoxia-reperfusion cycles.

**Limitations:** Review, not primary data. Narrative synthesis. Microclot measurements in ME/CFS are still limited.

### 6. Wirth and Scheibenbogen 2020 — Autoantibody-Mediated Vasoconstriction → Hypoxemia in ME/CFS
**BibTeX key:** `WirthScheibenbogen2020autoantibodies`
**Journal:** Autoimmunity Reviews (2020)
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.autrev.2020.102527
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Unifying hypothesis paper based on finding of elevated β2-adrenergic receptor and M3 acetylcholine receptor autoantibodies in ME/CFS patients.

**Key findings:**
- β2AdR and M3 AChR are important vasodilators; functional autoantibodies → vasoconstriction → hypoxemia
- Muscular and cerebral hypoperfusion documented in multiple ME/CFS studies, correlated with fatigue
- Metabolic changes in ME/CFS consistent with concept of hypoxia and ischemia
- Framework integrates autoimmunity, vascular dysfunction, and metabolic changes

**Conclusion:** Autoantibody-mediated vasoconstriction leads to tissue hypoxemia that could explain many ME/CFS symptoms.

**Limitations:** Hypothesis paper; β2AdR antibodies found in subset only (~30%). Causal mechanism not proven.

### 7. Kaczmarek 2023 — Circulating miRNA Converge on Impaired Hypoxic Adaptation in ME/CFS
**BibTeX key:** `Kaczmarek2023miRNAhypoxia`
**Journal:** PLoS ONE (2023)
**DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pone.0296060
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Computational analysis of circulating miRNA changes from multiple ME/CFS studies. Target gene prediction and functional pathway analysis.

**Key findings:**
- Differentially expressed miRNAs (heterogeneous between patients) converge on same target gene set (p ≈ 0.0018)
- Predicted functional impairments: exercise hyperemia, angiogenic adaptations to hypoxia, antioxidant defenses, TGF-β signaling
- Shift towards mitochondrial fission
- 8/10 most central miRNAs upregulated by herpesviruses — possible viral origin

**Conclusion:** Circulating miRNA changes in ME/CFS converge on impaired hypoxic adaptation and angiogenesis pathways.

**Limitations:** Computational analysis only — no experimental validation. miRNA heterogeneity may reflect different etiologies.

### 8. Shan et al. 2022 — Abnormal Neurovascular Coupling Hypothesis in ME/CFS
**BibTeX key:** `Shan2022neurovascular`
**Journal:** Frontiers in Neurology (2022)
**DOI:** 10.3389/fneur.2022.954142
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Protocol and hypothesis paper. n=288 planned (91 ME/CFS, 61 chronic fatigue, 91 healthy, 45 fibromyalgia). Multimodal MRI investigating neurovascular coupling (NVC).

**Key findings:**
- Prior neuroimaging findings (fMRI signal characteristics, structural changes in hypoxia-sensitive brain regions) → abnormal NVC hypothesis
- NVC: glutamate from active neuron → Ca2+ influx → vascular dilator factors → local blood flow increase
- Proposed investigation of hemodynamic response function, respiration response function, and glutamate changes
- Deep machine learning framework for neuromarker development

**Conclusion:** Abnormal neurovascular coupling may be the neurobiological origin of ME/CFS, explaining how neural activity fails to produce adequate blood flow.

**Limitations:** Protocol paper — results pending. Cross-sectional design. NVC mechanism remains to be directly tested.

### 9. Ribeiro et al. 2026 — HIF-2α Activation in Post-COVID Endothelial Dysfunction
**BibTeX key:** `Ribeiro2026HIF2alpha`
**Journal:** Frontiers in Immunology (2026)
**DOI:** 10.3389/fimmu.2026.1770758
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** n=41 post-COVID syndrome (PCS) patients, n=24 pre-pandemic controls. In vitro: HRECs exposed to SARS-CoV-2 spike S1 and PCS plasma. HIF-2α inhibition with belzutifan.

**Key findings:**
- PCS patients: elevated erythropoietin, VEGF, and MCP-1 vs controls
- S1 exposure: ROS production, transient HIF-1α + sustained HIF-2α activation, VEGFR2 upregulation, impaired endothelial barrier
- PCS plasma: increased ROS, modest endothelial barrier alterations
- HIF-2α inhibition (belzutifan): improved endothelial barrier integrity in S1 and PCS plasma conditions

**Conclusion:** Spike protein drives HIF-2α-mediated endothelial dysfunction in post-COVID syndrome. EPO elevation may be compensatory or pathogenic.

**Limitations:** In vitro model (retinal endothelial cells); n=41 PCS, moderate severity. Relevance to ME/CFS indirect (cross-disease).

### 10. Janssen Daalen et al. 2025 — Hypoxic Conditioning in Parkinson's Disease
**BibTeX key:** `JanssenDaalen2025hypoxicPD`
**Journal:** Nature Communications (2025)
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41467-025-63324-2
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Randomized double-blind controlled multiple N-of-1 trials. n=20 PD patients. 5 interventions: continuous and intermittent hypoxia at FiO2 0.163 and 0.127, plus placebo.

**Key findings:**
- Primary endpoint met: safe and feasible
- Intermittent hypoxia at FiO2 0.163 modestly improved most participant-rated symptoms for several hours vs placebo (Bayesian posterior probability > 0.95)
- Serum EPO, BDNF, GFAP, NfL, PDGFRβ did NOT differ between interventions at 1 hour post
- No dose-dependent adverse events; well-tolerated
- 95 adverse events (1 moderate, 3 serious) — not dose-dependent, comparable to placebo

**Conclusion:** Hypoxic conditioning is safe and feasible in PD; modest short-term symptomatic improvement. No acute biomarker changes detected.

**Limitations:** PD-specific, not ME/CFS. Small (n=20). Short-term outcomes only. No EPO induction detected at 1 hour. Cross-disease inference only.

### 11. Little et al. 2023 — HIF-1 Regulates Pathogenic Cytotoxic T Cells in Lupus
**BibTeX key:** `Little2023HIF1lupus`
**Journal:** JCI Insight (2023)
**DOI:** 10.1172/jci.insight.166076
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Mouse model of lupus skin disease + human CLE tissue analysis. Transcriptomic and protein analysis of skin-infiltrating T cells. HIF-1 inhibition studies.

**Key findings:**
- Skin-infiltrating CD4+ and CD8+ T cells express high levels of HIF-1 in lupus
- Strong cytotoxic signature at transcript and protein levels
- HIF-1 inhibition abrogated skin and systemic disease with decreased T cell cytotoxic activity
- Human CLE tissue: T cell-rich infiltrate shows increased HIF-1 and cytotoxic signature
- Granzyme B-expressing T cells concentrated at skin damage sites

**Conclusion:** HIF-1 is a critical regulator of pathogenic cytotoxic T cells in autoimmune tissue damage. Identifies HIF-1 as potential therapeutic target.

**Limitations:** Mouse model with human tissue validation. Lupus-specific. Cross-disease relevance to ME/CFS indirect (shared autoimmune mechanism).

### 12. Gangwar et al. 2019 — Intermittent Hypoxia Reduces Inflammation at High Altitude
**BibTeX key:** `Gangwar2019IHTaltitude`
**Journal:** Pflügers Archiv - European Journal of Physiology (2019)
**DOI:** 10.1007/s00424-019-02273-4
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Research stream:** hypoxia-altitude-hif1a

**Study:** Human trial. Moderate normobaric IHT protocol (12% FiO2, 4h, 4 days). Global plasma proteomics. Subsequent exposure to high altitude (3250 m). n not explicitly stated in abstract.

**Key findings:**
- IHT induced acute-phase response proteins (CRP, SAA, AGP2) and altered apolipoproteins
- After altitude ascent: IH-trained volunteers had higher arterial O2 saturation and lower AMS incidence vs controls
- Trained group: lower CRP, SAA, fibrinogen at days 4 and 7 at altitude
- IHT curtailed hypoxia-induced alterations of HDL, LDL, Apo-AI, Apo-B, LCAT, CETP, PLTP

**Conclusion:** Moderate IHT attenuates hypoxia-induced inflammation and dyslipidemia during high altitude exposure, providing molecular basis for altitude acclimatization benefits.

**Limitations:** Acute IHT protocol only (4 days); unknown long-term effects. Military/Defence Research context — potential selection bias. No ME/CFS patients.

## Research Gaps Identified

1. **Zero direct studies** of ME/CFS patients at high altitude or in hypobaric/hypoxic chambers
2. **No studies** of HIF-1α or HIF-2α expression in ME/CFS patient tissues
3. **No clinical trials** of HIF-PH inhibitors (roxadustat, daprodustat, vadadustat) in ME/CFS
4. **No studies** of intermittent hypoxic training/conditioning in ME/CFS
5. **No altitude epidemiology** — does living at altitude affect ME/CFS prevalence or severity?
6. **EPO in ME/CFS** — only one small study (Winkler 2004); no modern EPO or hepcidin measurements
7. **Cerebral autoregulation** — 3 small studies exist (Badhwar, Medow, Barnden) but no altitude challenge
