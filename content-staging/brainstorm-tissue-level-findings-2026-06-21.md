## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 Active Immune Privilege Disruption as Tissue–Blood Dissociation Engine

**Rationale:** If blood is normal but six tissue compartments show pathology, the barrier between them is likely not passively leaky but actively disrupted — immune privilege (the system that sequesters tissue antigens from systemic circulation) may be actively subverted. In LC, viral antigen persistence in tissues (spike in plaque macrophages, retinal Aβ) could drive local antigen presentation that saturates or overwhelms tissue-resident regulatory mechanisms (tolerogenic DCs, regulatory T cells), breaking immune privilege. This would produce an unusual pattern: tissues harboring pathology with normal blood, because blood ignorance of tissue antigens is actively maintained until privilege breaks. In ME/CFS, similar post-infectious disruption could explain why standard blood immunology comes back normal despite patients being severely symptomatic — the pathology lives in tissue immune microenvironments, not in circulation.

**Evidence link:** Eberhardt 2023 (spike in plaque macrophages — a privileged vascular niche); Lederer 2022 (LN germinal centers — classic immune-privileged site); VanElzakker 2024 (brainstem neuroinflammation — CNS immune privilege). The aggregation across three privileged compartments supports the disruption thesis.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show elevated levels of normally-sequestered tissue-specific antigens (e.g., S100β for CNS, collagen IV for vascular basement membrane) in serum, indicating immune privilege breakdown. If these antigens are NOT elevated despite tissue pathology, privilege disruption is not the mechanism.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If true, your standard blood test misses ME/CFS because the disease hides in your tissues' "immune blind spots" — but a simple blood test for tissue-specific debris leaking into circulation could diagnose it.

### 1.2 Glycocalyx Degradation as Multi-Compartment Breakdown Initiator

**Rationale:** All six compartments share a common endothelial vulnerability — the glycocalyx, a sugar-protein layer lining every vessel wall that controls permeability, leukocyte adhesion, and mechanotransduction. SARS-CoV-2 spike protein directly degrades glycocalyx in vitro (via heparanase upregulation and ACE2-mediated shedding), and once degraded, every downstream barrier (blood-brain, blood-retinal, gut-vascular, LN sinus) becomes permissive to pathological trafficking. This predicts that the multi-compartment pattern is NOT six independent pathologies but one endothelial surface failure manifesting differently by local cell type. For ME/CFS, if any post-infectious trigger can degrade glycocalyx (viral neuraminidase, inflammatory cytokine-mediated heparanase), the same multi-compartment pattern should exist — but this has never been measured.

**Evidence link:** Eberhardt 2023 (spike in macrophages — requires glycocalyx breach for monocyte entry); Miller 2025 (retinal Aβ — requires blood-retinal barrier breach); Cañadas 2023 (corneal nerve infiltration — requires limbal vessel glycocalyx failure); Kim 2026 (gut barrier — downstream of gut vascular glycocalyx). No study directly measured glycocalyx thickness.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.20

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show reduced glycocalyx thickness on sublingual or retinal videomicroscopy (clinically available), correlating with number of affected symptom domains. If glycocalyx is normal, multi-compartment pathology requires independent barrier failures — less parsimonious.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The "sugar coating" on your blood vessels may be the first domino — when it degrades, every organ's protective barrier fails at once, explaining why post-viral illness affects so many body systems simultaneously.

### 1.3 Retinal–Brainstem Antigenic Spread via Anterograde Transport

**Rationale:** Retinal Aβ (Miller 2025) and brainstem neuroinflammation (VanElzakker 2024) may not be independent — retinal ganglion cell axons project directly to the superior colliculus (midbrain) and pretectal nuclei (brainstem) via the optic tract. If SARS-CoV-2 or its antigens enter retinal cells, anterograde axonal transport could deliver spike protein or Aβ aggregates directly to brainstem along a ~7 cm pathway — the only direct CNS entry portal not blocked by the blood-brain barrier. This would produce the brainstem-predominant TSPO signal in VanElzakker 2024 without requiring systemic brain entry. For ME/CFS, this could explain how peripheral triggers reach the brainstem (autonomic control centers) without crossing the BBB — via retinal entry of viral fragments or autoantibodies.

**Evidence link:** Miller 2025 (retinal Aβ — demonstrates retinal pathology); VanElzakker 2024 (brainstem TSPO — location matches retinal projections); known biology: retinal ganglion cell axons are unmyelinated, vulnerable to injury, and transport cargo at ~200 mm/day. Schlick 2022 (retinal microcirculation correlates with fatigue) provides indirect clinical link.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.20

**Falsifiable prediction:** In LC patients with both retinal Aβ and brainstem TSPO-PET signal, the TSPO signal will be strongest in superior colliculus and pretectum (retinal projection targets) versus other brainstem regions. If TSPO signal is uniform across brainstem, retinal-brainstem transport is not the dominant route.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Your eyes might be a direct "back door" for infection or immune molecules to reach your brainstem — explaining why vision-related symptoms (blurred vision, light sensitivity) and autonomic dysfunction often co-occur in post-viral illness.

### 1.4 Germinal Center Autoantibody Seeding of Tissue Niche Pathology

**Rationale:** Lederer 2022 validated LN FNA for germinal center (GC) B cell analysis. If GCs in LC or ME/CFS produce autoantibodies (as in autoimmune disease), the antibody specificity may not target systemic antigens but tissue-niche-specific molecules — synaptic proteins (brainstem), tight junction proteins (retina, gut), plaque-associated epitopes (arteries). This "tissue-niche antibody" hypothesis predicts that: (a) LN GCs are chronically active, (b) they produce antibodies that don't reach high systemic titers (explaining why blood autoantibody screens are often normal), and (c) these antibodies bind locally in the tissue compartment where the antigen first triggered them. The GC dysfunction could be EBV-driven (as hypothesized in MS and some ME/CFS subsets), linking viral reactivation to compartmentalized tissue pathology.

**Evidence link:** Lederer 2022 (GC method — tool to test this); Eberhardt 2023 (plaque macrophages may be local antibody target); Cañadas 2023 (corneal nerve damage could be antibody-mediated); EBV-driven GC dysfunction in MS is well-documented. No direct antibody data from LN FNA in LC or ME/CFS exists.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** LN FNA in ME/CFS patients will show expanded GC B cell populations with oligoclonal specificity for at least one tissue-niche antigen (retinal S-antigen, brainstem α3-nAChR, gut occludin), while paired serum will show low or absent titers against the same antigens. If LN GC activity is normal or antibodies are equally present in serum, niche-seeding is not the mechanism.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Your lymph nodes might be producing "tissue-specific" antibodies that attack your brainstem or eyes but never show up in standard blood tests — explaining why doctors say "your blood work is normal" while you feel anything but.

### 1.5 Microclot Compartmental Sequestration as Tissue Hypoperfusion Amplifier

**Rationale:** Saleem 2026 reviews microclots in LC and ME/CFS. Existing models treat microclots as circulating emboli causing systemic ischemia. A tissue-level reframing: microclots may be disproportionately sequestered in specific vascular beds (retina: Schlick 2022 microcirculation changes; brainstem: VanElzakker 2024 — TSPO may bind clot-associated fibrin; coronary: Eberhardt 2023 — plaque macrophages may engulf trapped clots). Microclot sequestration would create compartment-specific ischemia-and-inflammation patterns invisible to systemic clotting panels. Differential sequestration (certain beds trap clots, others clear them) could explain symptom heterogeneity — retinal sequestration → visual snow; brainstem sequestration → autonomic dysregulation; coronary sequestration → cardiac symptoms.

**Evidence link:** Saleem 2026 (microclots in both LC and ME/CFS — bridge condition); Schlick 2022 (retinal microcirculation + fatigue severity — tissue correlate); Eberhardt 2023 (plaque macrophages = clot clearance site). No study has measured clot burden per tissue bed in the same patient.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** Optical coherence tomography angiography (OCTA) in ME/CFS patients will show retinal capillary drop-out patches whose spatial distribution matches predicted microclot sequestration zones, and these patches will correlate with symptom severity (fatigue, visual snow, brain fog) better than any systemic clotting marker. If OCTA is normal despite high circulating microclots, sequestration is not happening.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Tiny blood clots in post-viral illness may not float freely in your blood — they get stuck in specific organs (your eyes, your brainstem), causing symptoms that match whichever organ traps them.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### 2.1 Re-Contact Raijmakers TSPO-PET ME/CFS Cohort for Longitudinal Follow-Up

**Rationale:** Raijmakers 2021 found null TSPO-PET in ME/CFS (n=25). VanElzakker 2024 found positive brainstem TSPO in PASC (n~20). The discrepancy may reflect: (a) ME/CFS truly lacks neuroinflammation, (b) Raijmakers' cohort had lower disease severity, (c) different PET ligands (DPA-713 vs PBR28 vs others), (d) scanner resolution insufficient to resolve brainstem. Re-contacting the Raijmakers cohort for follow-up PET with higher-resolution scanner and PASC-like methods would resolve this directly without recruiting new patients. Even a null re-replication (no change over time) would be valuable — it would suggest ME/CFS and LC have different neuroinflammatory profiles.

**Evidence link:** VanElzakker 2024 (positive PASC); Raijmakers 2021 (null ME/CFS). This is the single most critical unresolved tension in the tissue-level evidence.

**Resource estimate:** ~$300-500k (PET scans, tracer production, analysis) + 6-12 months recruitment + ethical approval amendment.

**Risk:** Likelihood of null result: moderate (if Raijmakers was correct). But both outcomes (positive or null) are informative.

**Primary outcome:** Brainstem TSPO binding potential (BPND) change from initial scan, compared to age/sex-matched PASC patients from VanElzakker-like protocol.

### 2.2 Multi-Compartment Tissue Correlation Study (Any 3 Compartments, N=50)

**Rationale:** The most impactful single experiment would be measuring pathology in ≥3 tissue compartments simultaneously in the same patients. The evidence base currently aggregates unrelated single-compartment studies — confound and selection bias are uncontrolled. A within-subject design (e.g., retinal OCTA + corneal confocal + gut permeability + LN FNA in ~50 well-phenotyped ME/CFS patients) would establish whether compartmental pathology is correlated (suggesting common mechanism) or independent (suggesting coincident but distinct mechanisms). If correlated, compartment number becomes a severity metric; if independent, treatment must be compartment-specific.

**Evidence link:** Six compartments each have validated methods (OCTA, corneal confocal, lactulose/mannitol, FNA, TSPO-PET, plaque CT), but never combined. Cost is the barrier.

**Resource estimate:** ~$500-800k (FNA adds ~$200k; PET adds ~$300k; imaging ~$100k; recruitment ~$50k). Potential for reduced-cost design: skip PET (most expensive), use 4 cheap compartments (retina, cornea, gut, LN FNA) for ~$300k.

**Risk:** Low per-compartment (methods validated); risk is attrition from LN FNA (invasive, ~15% refusal expected).

**Primary outcome:** Inter-compartment correlation matrix; if mean r > 0.4, common mechanism supported.

### 2.3 Repository Autopsy Study: ME/CFS Multi-Compartment Tissue Banking

**Rationale:** The strongest evidence for tissue pathology in ME/CFS would come from autopsy. National tissue banks (NIH NeuroBioBank, UK Brain Bank) hold ME/CFS donor tissue that has never been systematically examined for the six-compartment pathologies seen in LC. Funding a targeted re-examination of existing archived tissues (retina, brainstem, coronary artery, gut, LN, CSF) would cost far less than a prospective study and could yield immediate results. Key targets: spike-like immunoreactivity in plaque macrophages, retinal Aβ, TSPO immunohistochemistry in brainstem, GC architecture in LN.

**Evidence link:** Eberhardt 2023 (coronary method — applicable to archived FFPE blocks); Miller 2025 (retinal Aβ method — applicable to archived eyes); VanElzakker 2024 (TSPO target confirmed in LC — same ligand-binding site can be stained in fixed tissue).

**Resource estimate:** ~$150-300k (tissue acquisition from banks + immunohistochemistry + imaging + pathologist time). No patient recruitment needed.

**Risk:** Tissue quality in archives (autolysis, fixation artifacts) may prevent some assays. For retina specifically, globes are rarely collected without ocular indication — sample size may be small.

**Primary outcome:** Prevalence of compartment-specific pathology in ME/CFS vs matched controls (other chronic illness, sudden death).

### 2.4 LN FNA + Germinal Center Single-Cell Atlas in Post-Infectious Fatigue

**Rationale:** Lederer 2022's LN FNA method is validated, safe, and produces rich data (GC B cell subsets, Tfh cells, plasma cells). A single exploratory study (n=20: 10 ME/CFS, 10 healthy) with paired blood would determine: (a) whether GCs are chronically activated, (b) whether the activation pattern matches autoimmune disease or chronic infection, and (c) whether LN-derived cell subsets have blood-based correlates. This would directly test whether LN pathology explains normal blood immunology — if LN GCs are hyperactive but corresponding plasmablasts are absent in blood, the tissue-blood dissociation is confirmed.

**Evidence link:** Lederer 2022 (method validation, n=54 healthy); ME/CFS literature: no LN studies exist; this is a direct application of the LC-validated method to ME/CFS.

**Resource estimate:** ~$200-400k (FNA procedures + single-cell sequencing + flow cytometry + blood pairing). Recruit from existing well-phenotyped ME/CFS cohorts.

**Risk:** Low (FNA is safe, Lederer 2022 reports no serious adverse events). Risk is that LN pathology is absent in ME/CFS (informative null).

**Primary outcome:** GC B cell activation score (GCB/Tfh ratio, Ki67+ fraction, somatic hypermutation frequency) in ME/CFS vs controls, and its correlation with symptom severity and blood immune parameters.

### 2.5 Retinal OCTA + Corneal Confocal + Cognitive Assessment Cross-Sectional in ME/CFS

**Rationale:** Retinal OCTA (non-invasive, 5 min) and corneal confocal microscopy (non-invasive, 10 min) together provide a window into CNS small-fiber and microvascular pathology without PET radiation or lumbar puncture. Schlick 2022 (retinal microcirculation + fatigue) and Cañadas 2023 (corneal nerve loss + neuroinflammation in LC) both used these methods but no study has combined them in ME/CFS. Adding cognitive testing (processing speed, attention) would test whether retinal/corneal metrics correlate with CNS function — validating them as surrogate CNS biomarkers. If both correlate with cognition, the retina-as-CNS-window hypothesis receives strong support; if neither correlates, the eye–CNS link is weaker than hoped.

**Evidence link:** Schlick 2022 (OCTA method, fatigue link); Cañadas 2023 (corneal method, LC neuroinflammation); neuro-ophthalmic literature (retinal nerve fiber layer thinning correlates with brain atrophy in MS). Cost is low.

**Resource estimate:** ~$100-200k (OCTA machine rental + confocal microscope + cognitive battery + ~50 participants). OCTA is increasingly available in optometry clinics.

**Risk:** Low. Confounders: refractive error, glaucoma, diabetes affect retinal metrics — exclude these.

**Primary outcome:** Retinal capillary density + corneal nerve fiber density correlation with cognitive processing speed (SDMT or equivalent). Predictive model: do retinal metrics + demographics explain >30% of cognitive variance?

## Category 10: Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS

### 10.1 Tissue Findings Are SARS-CoV-2-Specific, Not General Post-Viral

**Rationale:** SARS-CoV-2 is biologically unique among viruses capable of triggering post-viral illness: it enters cells via ACE2 (widely expressed on endothelium), directly degrades glycocalyx, induces syncytia (enabling cell-to-cell spread without extracellular phase), and produces a spike protein that persists in tissues for months (Eberhardt 2023). EBV, influenza, and other ME/CFS triggers lack these specific features. The multi-compartment tissue pathology in LC may reflect SARS-CoV-2's peculiar biology rather than a general post-viral tissue pathology mechanism. If so, ME/CFS patients with non-COVID triggers would show no tissue pathology — and tissue-focused research would be a dead end for the majority of ME/CFS.

**Evidence link:** Eberhardt 2023 (spike persistence — unique to SARS-CoV-2); no comparable studies exist for EBV or influenza tissue persistence. The counterargument: if tissue pathology were EBV-specific, we would need EBV-naive ME/CFS patients to have different pathophysiology — but EBV seroprevalence in ME/CFS is near-universal (95%+), making this hard to test.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with documented non-viral triggers (e.g., Lyme, mold, chemical exposure) will show NO retinal, corneal, or LN pathology on the same assays that are positive in LC patients. If non-viral-trigger ME/CFS also shows tissue pathology, the mechanism is post-infectious, not SARS-CoV-2-specific.

**Severity (1-5):** 4 — if true, the entire tissue-level hypothesis for ME/CFS collapses for most patients.

### 10.2 Multi-Compartment Pathology Is an Acute Residual, Not Chronic Driver

**Rationale:** The tissue findings in LC may represent residual resolving pathology from acute COVID, not a chronic disease-driving mechanism. Eberhardt 2023 (coronary plaque in acute/post-acute) may reflect macrophages clearing viral debris weeks after infection — a normal process still active in LC because the antigen load was massive. VanElzakker 2024's TSPO signal may be microglial resolution activity, not ongoing neuroinflammation. If tissue pathology resolves spontaneously in most patients over 2-3 years (the natural history of LC in ~50-80% of patients), it is an epiphenomenon of recovery, not the engine of chronic illness. ME/CFS patients (median duration 5-10+ years) would have passed this window, and tissue pathology would have resolved — explaining its absence in Raijmakers 2021.

**Evidence link:** Raijmakers 2021 (null TSPO-PET in ME/CFS, ~5yr median duration) vs VanElzakker 2024 (positive in LC, ~1yr duration). No longitudinal tissue study in LC or ME/CFS exists.

**Falsifiable prediction:** LC patients with ≥3 years duration will show lower tissue pathology burden (retinal OCTA, corneal confocal, TSPO-PET) than those with <1 year duration. If chronic LC (>3yr) shows equivalent pathology to acute LC, the residual hypothesis is falsified.

**Severity (1-5):** 3 — would mean tissue-level work is relevant to early LC but not to established ME/CFS.

### 10.3 Blood Is Actually Informative — Tissue Studies Are Overinterpreted

**Rationale:** The premise "blood misses tissue pathology" is based on an assumption — that standard clinical blood work would be expected to detect these tissue changes. But the LC tissue studies used specialized research methods (PET, FNA, OCTA), not clinical blood tests. It is possible that appropriately designed blood assays (e.g., proteomics for tissue-specific proteins, cell-free DNA methylation deconvolution, extracellular vesicle cargo) WOULD detect the same tissue pathology. In that case, the "tissue > blood" argument is a straw man — blood was never tested properly. ME/CFS blood immunology may be abnormal when assayed with the right methods (as some high-plex proteomics studies suggest), and tissue examination is unnecessary.

**Evidence link:** Many LC blood proteomics studies show abnormalities (e.g., Filip 2023 multi-omic clusters, Cervia 2023 complement activation). The tissue studies do not include parallel comprehensive blood analysis in the same patients. No direct comparison of tissue vs optimized blood diagnostic performance exists.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In LC or ME/CFS patients undergoing tissue examination (any compartment), parallel high-plex plasma proteomics + EV cargo analysis will replicate the tissue diagnosis (e.g., retinal Aβ detected in plasma EV cargo) with AUC >0.85 for tissue-defined pathology. If blood EV cargo cannot detect tissue pathology, the tissue specificity claim holds.

**Severity (1-5):** 3 — would change the research emphasis (better blood methods) but not invalidate tissue pathology.

### 10.4 Inflammatory Hypothesis Mismatch: Tissue Findings Are Subclinical

**Rationale:** The tissue pathologies found — plaque macrophage activation (Eberhardt 2023), retinal Aβ (Miller 2025), corneal nerve loss (Cañadas 2023) — may be clinically silent (producing no symptoms on their own) in the general population and only coincidentally detected in LC patients. Millions of people have coronary plaque macrophages without symptoms; many have retinal Aβ without visual impairment. The findings' association with LC symptoms (fatigue, brain fog) may reflect confounding: LC patients are studied more intensively, revealing age-related or lifestyle-related tissue changes unrelated to their post-viral illness. In ME/CFS, similar subclinical tissue findings would be irrelevant to symptom generation.

**Evidence link:** Plaque macrophages are present in >50% of adults >50 with coronary disease (Eberhardt's own statistics show control coronaries also have macrophages, just less spike+); retinal Aβ increases with age; corneal nerve density decreases with age/diabetes. None of the studies controlled for all confounders (age, BMI, pre-existing cardiovascular risk, diabetes). Schlick 2022 (fatigue correlation) is the only symptomatic link, and it is cross-sectional.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a general population cohort (no LC), retinal OCTA abnormalities will correlate with age and cardiovascular risk factors but NOT with fatigue scores. If OCTA predicts fatigue independently of age/CV risk in the general population, the subclinical claim is falsified.

**Severity (1-5):** 3 — would gut the clinical relevance of tissue findings for symptom generation.

## Category 11: Null Hypothesis Assessment

### 11.1 Null in ME/CFS Tissues Would Redirect to Central/Supracellular Mechanisms

**If direct tissue examination in ME/CFS (autopsy, LN FNA, retinal OCTA, TSPO-PET) consistently finds normal tissue across all compartments**, this would be a profoundly informative result. It would rule out the entire class of "hidden tissue pathology" explanations and redirect ME/CFS pathophysiology research toward:
- **Cellular metabolic failure** without structural tissue damage (mitochondrial dysfunction, ion channel pathology)
- **Central nervous system functional disorder** (connectivity, autonomic regulation, sensory processing) without neuroinflammation
- **Systemic physiological dysregulation** (circadian, neuroendocrine, autonomic) leaving no histopathological footprint
- **Biochemical but not anatomical pathology** (metabolomics, redox state, proteostasis)

This would save the field from years of dead-end tissue-focused investigations and concentrate resources on mechanisms compatible with structurally normal tissues. The ME/CFS field would then look more like functional neurological disorder research (imaging + physiology, no biopsy) than autoimmune disease research.

**Value of this null:** HIGH — it would redirect funding and PhD projects away from expensive tissue studies toward physiology research. The opportunity cost of NOT testing the null hypothesis is substantial.

### 11.2 Null in Retina But Positive in Brainstem Would Refine the Window Hypothesis

**The retina-as-CNS-window hypothesis (existing paper environment) predicts that retinal pathology parallels brain pathology.** If ME/CFS autopsy or OCTA shows normal retina but TSPO-PET shows brainstem neuroinflammation, this hypothesis would be partially falsified — eye and brain would dissociate. The mechanism would then not be a shared barrier/vascular process but a CNS-specific one, and retinal imaging would be useless as a CNS biomarker in ME/CFS. This outcome is plausible: the blood-retinal barrier is tighter than the blood-brain barrier, and retinal cells may be more resilient to whatever drives neuroinflammation.

**Testability:** Requires simultaneous retina + brainstem assessment in the same ME/CFS patients (OCTA + MRI + TSPO-PET). No such study exists.

### 11.3 Null Would Validate Blood-Based Biomarker Programs

**Currently, many biomarker programs assume blood is informative (proteomics, metabolomics, EV panels).** If tissues are null in ME/CFS, these blood programs gain credibility — the disease signal truly is systemic or circulating, not compartmentalized. Conversely, if tissues are positive but blood normal, it would invalidate the assumption underlying millions of dollars of blood-biomarker research. Current funding decisions are being made in ignorance of this, making the null hypothesis fiscally as well as scientifically important.

## Category 12: Evidence Quality Concerns

### 12.1 Conference Presentation Dominance — Peer Review Absent

**6 of 15 papers are unpublished conference presentations** (Moschovis larazotide, Murakami CCI, Locci LN B-cells, Salmon maraviroc, Giannerelli plaque, Treadwell autonomic). Conference abstracts undergo minimal peer review (typically 1-2 sentences of feedback from a committee, not full methodological review). Key concerns: no requirement for data sharing, no reproducibility check, no statistical review, and favorable results are far more likely to be submitted than negative ones. The conference-presentation bias amplifies the file-drawer problem. Until these findings appear in peer-reviewed journals, they should be treated as preliminary signals, not evidence.

`origin: brainstorm`

---

### 12.2 No Negative Results — Full File Drawer Problem

**0 of 15 papers report null/negative findings.** This is statistically impossible if even a fraction of the hypotheses tested are false (as they certainly are). A reasonable estimate: for every positive tissue finding in LC, there are likely 1-3 negative studies that went unpublished because: (a) they were conference presentations that didn't meet the threshold, (b) negative tissue findings are harder to publish than positive, (c) small-sample studies (typical n=10-30) are underpowered but authors rarely report "failed to find" — they simply don't submit. The true effect size of the tissue-level pathologies is likely inflated relative to what a meta-analysis of all conducted (including unpublished) studies would show.

`origin: brainstorm`

### 12.3 Sample Size and Statistical Power Concerns

**Tissue studies are uniformly small:**
- Lederer 2022: n=54 (adequate for method validation, not for case-control inference)
- VanElzakker 2024: n~20
- Miller 2025: retinal explants (n~5 donors)
- Cañadas 2023: n=50
- Schlick 2022: n=30

None of these sample sizes can reliably detect moderate-effect associations (r<0.4) or control for the many covariates (age, sex, BMI, comorbidity, medications, time since infection, severity). The typical rule for multiple regression (10-20 observations per predictor) means most studies cannot adjust for more than 2-3 covariates. Published associations may reflect residual confounding rather than true tissue pathology.

`origin: brainstorm`

---

### 12.4 Long COVID to ME/CFS Extrapolation Without Justification

**All tissue findings are in LC populations.** None of the 15 papers test or claim generalizability to ME/CFS. The assumption that LC and ME/CFS share tissue-level pathology is a logical bridge built by this review, not by the evidence itself. While LC and ME/CFS share symptom profiles (fatigue, PEM, brain fog, autonomic dysfunction), they differ in: (a) trigger specificity (SARS-CoV-2 vs multiple triggers), (b) natural history (spontaneous recovery in ~50-80% of LC vs <10% in ME/CFS), (c) demographic profile (LC affects working-age adults broadly; ME/CFS has 3:1 female predominance), and (d) inflammatory signature (some LC studies show elevated IL-6, TNF-α that normalize; ME/CFS inflammatory profile is more heterogeneous and often normal). These differences weaken the extrapolation. The tissue pathology specific to LC may track with the LC-specific features, not the shared symptoms.

`origin: brainstorm`

### 12.5 Confounding by Medication and Comorbidity

**LC patients in these studies were often on medications during tissue examination:**
- Antivirals (nirmatrelvir/ritonavir)
- Anticoagulants (aspirin, DOACs — affect microclot and plaque studies)
- Statins (affect plaque inflammation — Eberhardt 2023 did not report statin use)
- Beta-blockers (affect autonomic measures in Treadwell 2025)
- Antidepressants (affect TSPO binding — can reduce signal by 20-50%)

Most studies did not report medication washout or adjust for these in analysis. In ME/CFS, medication burden is similarly high (analgesics, psychotropics, autonomic agents), and any future tissue study would face the same confounds. The tissue findings' specificity to LC (vs medication effects) is untested.

`origin: brainstorm`

### 12.6 Methodological Heterogeneity in TSPO-PET

**VanElzakker 2024 (positive brainstem) and Raijmakers 2021 (null ME/CFS) used different PET ligands (likely PBR28 vs DPA-713 or PK11195), different scanners (HRRT vs Siemens Biograph), different reference regions (cerebellum vs whole brain), and different TSPO genotype inclusion criteria.** The rs6971 polymorphism (which affects PBR28 binding by ~40%) was not reported in Raijmakers and may not have been genotyped. If Raijmakers' cohort had a higher proportion of low-affinity binders (HAB/LAB distribution differs by ancestry), the null result could be a genotyping artifact, not a true absence of neuroinflammation. This methodological confound remains unresolved and makes the two studies' discordance uninterpretable without re-analysis.

`origin: brainstorm`
