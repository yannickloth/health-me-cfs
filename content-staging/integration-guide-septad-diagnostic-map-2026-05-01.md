## Papers Anchoring Each Gap

### Gap 1: Flat entry points — no tiebreaker when multiple presentations equally prominent

**Problem:** Current map has no prevalence weighting to break ties.

**Evidence found:**

| Condition | Prevalence in ME/CFS | Source | Certainty |
|-----------|---------------------|--------|-----------|
| Hypermobility (Brighton) | 81% (FM+ME/CFS clinic sample) | Eccles2021hypermobility | 0.55 |
| hEDS (strict 2017 criteria) | 15.5% (You+ME registry) | Mudie2024hypermobility | 0.55 |
| hEDS (18% by strict criteria) | 18% (FM/ME/CFS clinic) | Eccles2021hypermobility | 0.55 |
| MCAS (clinical MCA criteria) | 25.3% (Austrian ME/CFS cohort) | Rohrhofer2025mecfsmast | 0.52 |
| MCAS (formal diagnosis) | 16.7% (Austrian ME/CFS cohort) | Rohrhofer2025mecfsmast | 0.52 |
| POTS/OI | 27% (UK cohort) | Newton2008POTSprevalence [already in bib] | — |
| GI dysmotility (symptomatic) | 75% fullness, 45% abdominal pain | Steinsvik2023GIdysmotility | 0.48 |

**Recommended Septad map change:** Assign prior probability weights in the triage tier.
Hypermobility (by any Brighton criterion) is so prevalent (81%) that it should be screened
for in all patients first — even before MCAS-first sequencing is considered. MCAS formal
diagnosis (16-25%) should come after hEDS/hypermobility screening fails to explain the
presentation. POTS/OI (27%) is nearly as common as formal MCAS.

**Null result worth noting:** The Mudie 2024 registry study (n=815) found only 15.5%
joint hypermobility, much lower than Eccles 2021 (81%). The difference is explained by
measurement method: registry self-report vs clinical Brighton assessment. The clinical
rate is the more useful prior for a diagnostic map.

### Gap 2: No prevalence-weighted priors — MCAS-first sequencing asserted, not derived

**Problem:** The current map sequences MCAS first without justification from base rates.

**Evidence found:**

The Rohrhofer 2025 Austrian study (n=687+383) provides the best ME/CFS-specific MCAS
prevalence data: 16.7% formal MCAS, 25.3% clinical MCA. The Yao 2025 POTS triad study
[already in bib as Yao2025MCASPOTStriad] shows MCAS prevalence in POTS patients varies
enormously by criteria (2-87%); therapy response (75-91%) is criteria-independent.

**Implication for sequencing logic:** MCAS is NOT the most prevalent Septad component
in ME/CFS. Hypermobility spectrum (81% Brighton positive) and GI symptoms (75% fullness)
both have higher prevalence. The current MCAS-first sequencing cannot be justified by
base rate alone.

**Defensible alternatives for MCAS-first priority:**
- Treatability: MCAS is the most actionable with existing medications
- Upstream amplification: MCA worsens all other Septad components (Wirth/Löhn 2023)
- Trigger role: MCAS may initiate or sustain the cascade (Afrin/Molderings 2020,
  Weinstock 2021) — but this is hypothesis-grade evidence (certainty 0.35-0.55)

**Recommended Septad map change:** Retain MCAS-first sequencing but add explicit rationale
footnote: "MCAS prioritized for treatment responsiveness and cascade amplification, not
prevalence. Base rate ~17% formal MCAS, ~25% clinical MCA in ME/CFS cohorts."

### Gap 3: No confirmation criteria — "disproportionate upstream improvement" not operationalized

**Problem:** The map needs a measurable test for "upstream improvement."

**Evidence found:**

The ECNM-AIM consensus (captured in Lee2025CanadaMCAS and Gulen2024mcascriteria,
both already in bib) provides the operationalized treatment response criterion:

> Confirmed MCAS requires: (1) episodic, severe, multi-system symptoms; (2) tryptase
> elevation during event: baseline × 1.2 + 2 ng/mL; OR urinary mediator >200% baseline;
> (3) symptom response to MC mediator-targeting drugs.

The Yao 2025 study [Yao2025MCASPOTStriad] found 75-91% therapy response even without
biochemical confirmation — suggesting clinical response alone may be sufficient for
provisional diagnosis in resource-limited settings.

**Operationalization proposal for Septad map:**

"Disproportionate upstream improvement" = reduction in ≥2 of the following within 4-8
weeks of MCAS-targeted treatment initiation:
- Orthostatic intolerance severity (COMPASS-31 or standing HR)
- Pain scores (NRS or PEM frequency)
- GI symptom burden (IBS-SSS or patient global)
- Fatigue severity (any validated scale)
AND no concurrent treatment change for other Septad components during the trial period.

**This operationalization is not from a single paper — it synthesizes the ECNM-AIM
criterion (Gulen 2024, Lee 2025) with the multi-system requirement and applies it
to ME/CFS context. Flag as expert synthesis in the document, not direct citation.**

### Gap 4: MCAS-induced ME/CFS — pathway absent from map

**Problem:** MCAS as primary driver of ME/CFS phenotype has no branch in the current map.

**Evidence found:**

Three papers address this pathway at different evidence levels:

1. **AfrinMolderings2020postCOVID** (certainty 0.35): Hypothesis that dysregulated MCAS
   drives post-viral ME/CFS. Mechanism: ACE2-expressing mast cells + SARS-CoV-2 trigger
   sustained MCAS escalation through epigenetic mechanisms. No controlled data.

2. **Weinstock2021LongCOVIDmast** (certainty 0.55): Survey evidence (n=136 LC, n=80 MCAS)
   showing Long-COVID symptom profile is virtually identical to pre-treatment MCAS.
   Pre-infection LC and controls were comparable, suggesting post-viral onset not pre-existing
   selection. Supports MCAS-triggered post-viral phenotype indirectly.

3. **WirthLohn2023MECFSvascular** (certainty 0.42): Bidirectional hypothesis — explicitly
   argues MCA and ME/CFS mutually trigger each other, not that MCAS causes ME/CFS.
   This paper CONTRADICTS simple MCAS-first causality.

4. **Rohrhofer2025mecfsmast** (certainty 0.52): Only 2.8% of ME/CFS patients had MCAS
   diagnosed BEFORE ME/CFS onset; 16.7% diagnosed after. This temporal sequence is the
   strongest available evidence AGAINST MCAS-as-primary-cause hypothesis.

**Recommended Septad map change:** Add a branch labeled "MCAS-triggered ME/CFS phenotype
(hypothesis)." Proposed criteria: (a) MCAS onset preceded or coincided with ME/CFS onset;
(b) PEM is present but fatigue pattern matches MCAS mediator fluctuation; (c) MCAS treatment
produces ≥50% improvement in ME/CFS core symptoms. Annotate as hypothesis-grade (certainty 0.35).

**Contradicting evidence must be stated:** The Austrian cohort data (Rohrhofer 2025) shows
97.2% of patients developed MCAS after ME/CFS onset, which is the opposite of what the
MCAS-primary-driver hypothesis would predict.

### Gap 5: Missing predisposition layer — genetics not in the map

**Problem:** HNMT variants, mast cell genetics (TPSAB1/HaT), hEDS genetics are above the map.

**Evidence found:**

**TPSAB1/Hereditary Alpha Tryptasemia (HaT):**
- HaT prevalence: ~5% Western populations (well established, multiple sources)
- Key null result (Vazquez 2022, Vazquez2022TPSAB1): HaT prevalence in hEDS/HSD patients
  (4.9%) = general population rate. HaT does NOT concentrate in hypermobility disorders.
- When HaT IS present in a hypermobility patient: OR 5.9-7.3 for dysphagia, increased
  anaphylaxis risk — shifts phenotype toward MCAS-primary presentation.
- Implication: HaT testing should be CONDITIONAL (indicated when hEDS + MCAS features
  present), not universal.

**HNMT variants:**
- The Thr105Ile HNMT polymorphism (~15-20% population) reduces histamine-N-methyltransferase
  efficiency, prolonging histamine action — relevant to MCAS symptom severity.
- No peer-reviewed ME/CFS-specific study found linking HNMT to ME/CFS or Septad components.
- Evidence base: mechanistic genetics only (GeneCards, OMIM), no controlled cohort data.
- Certainty: too low to add to map as established finding. Flag as "candidate predisposition
  marker, untested in ME/CFS cohorts."

**KIT D816V:**
- Relevant for primary (clonal) MCAS / systemic mastocytosis, not idiopathic MCAS.
- Detection note (from KITD816V literature already in bib): KIT D816V testing in blood
  useful for mastocytosis screening; negative result does not exclude idiopathic MCAS.

**Recommended Septad map change — predisposition layer:**

Add a conditional genetics layer with three testable markers:

| Marker | When to test | Clinical implication |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| TPSAB1 copy number (HaT) | hEDS + unexplained anaphylaxis or severe MCAS | Shifts to MCAS-primary phenotype if positive |
| KIT D816V (blood) | Persistent tryptase elevation + systemic symptoms | Rules in/out clonal mastocytosis |
| HNMT Thr105Ile | MCAS with histamine-dominant symptoms | May explain antihistamine dose requirements; untested in ME/CFS |
| hEDS genetic panel | Not yet available (no hEDS gene identified) | Awaiting discovery; currently diagnosis by criteria only |

**Note on null result:** The Vazquez 2022 study is an important corrective to the assumption
that hEDS and HaT cluster together. The map must not imply that all hEDS patients should be
tested for HaT.
