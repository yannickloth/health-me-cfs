## 3. Evidence Summary

Schomerus et al. (2026) make a systematic argument against psychologizing ME/CFS and Long COVID, documenting five specific harm categories: (1) iatrogenic deterioration from activation therapy, (2) invalidation of illness experience that undermines pacing, (3) neglect of biomedical diagnostic and treatment opportunities, (4) medico-legal disadvantage in disability assessment and insurance, and (5) family/social harm including child protection risks. The essay critiques the evidence base for "secondary psychologization" — the claim that psychological factors drive illness maintenance even if not causal — as unspecific, empirically unsubstantiated, and unfalsifiable. The key argument: in conditions with unknown pathophysiology, the biopsychosocial model creates an asymmetry where psychological factors can always be assumed but never disproven, systematically biasing attributions while the bio-component remains unspecified.

The strongest mechanistic claim supportable: psychologization is not merely offensive — it is clinically harmful with specific, measurable, and potentially irreversible consequences (PEM-triggered deterioration from GET, diagnostic delay from symptom dismissal, disability denial from psychosomatic framing).

What must remain speculative: the quantitative fraction of severe ME/CFS cases attributable to iatrogenic psychologization (no epidemiological study has attempted this); whether reducing psychologization alone would measurably improve outcomes.

What the evidence does NOT support: that psychological factors play no role whatsoever in the patient experience of ME/CFS — the essay explicitly states that comorbid psychiatric conditions exist and deserve treatment, adapted to PEM.
