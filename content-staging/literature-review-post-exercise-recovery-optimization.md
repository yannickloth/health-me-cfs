## Paper 1: Jammes2020MembranePotential

**Full citation:** Jammes Y, Adjriou N, Kipson N, Criado C, Charpin C, Rebaudet S, Stavris C, Guieu R, Fenouillet E, Retornaz F. Altered muscle membrane potential and redox status differentiates two subgroups of patients with chronic fatigue syndrome. *J Transl Med*. 2020;18(1):173.

**DOI:** 10.1186/s12967-020-02341-9

**Key findings:**
- n=72 ME/CFS patients, resting and during incremental cycling + 10-min post-exercise recovery
- M-wave recordings (rectus femoris during exercise, flexor digitorum longus at rest)
- 39/72 had marked M-wave alterations in both muscles during and after exercise + elevated TBARS/ORP (oxidative stress) + decreased K+ outflow
- 33/72 had no M-wave alterations and lower baseline oxidative stress
- M-wave changes were inversely proportional to TBARS and ORP
- Identifies two subgroups based on muscle membrane excitability and redox status

**Conclusion:** Resting muscles of ME/CFS patients have altered membrane excitability. Oxidative stress biomarkers (ORP) may improve diagnosis and subgroup stratification. Subgroup differences suggest different recovery needs.

**Limitations:** Single-center; no healthy control group; exercise only moderate intensity; 10-min recovery window may be insufficient to capture delayed PEM. Certainty: 0.60 (n=72, peer-reviewed, partially replicated by other groups showing oxidative stress in ME/CFS, but subgroup distinction not independently replicated)

## Paper 2: Soejima2015WaonTherapyCFS [ALREADY IN BIB]

**Full citation:** Soejima Y, Munemoto T, Masuda A, Uwatoko Y, Miyata M, Tei C. Effects of Waon therapy on chronic fatigue syndrome: a pilot study. *Intern Med*. 2015;54(4):395-400.

**DOI:** 10.2169/internalmedicine.54.3042

**Key findings:**
- n=20 CFS patients (CDC criteria); Waon therapy (far-infrared sauna at 60°C for 15 min, then blanket for 30 min, daily for 5 weeks)
- Significant improvements in fatigue (Chalder Fatigue Scale), pain, and quality of life (SF-36)
- Increased regional cerebral blood flow (rCBF) in prefrontal cortex
- Improvements persisted at 3-month follow-up

**Conclusion:** Waon therapy may be a safe and effective treatment for CFS-related fatigue, with effects possibly mediated by improved cerebral blood flow.

**Limitations:** Open-label pilot; n=20; no sham control; single center; not replicated independently. Certainty: 0.35. Existing key: Soejima2015WaonTherapyCFS

## Paper 3: Munemoto2017Waon

**Full citation:** Munemoto T, Soejima Y, Masuda A, Nakabeppu Y, Tei C. Increase in the Regional Cerebral Blood Flow following Waon Therapy in Patients with Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: A Pilot Study. *Intern Med*. 2017;56(14):1817-1823.

**DOI:** 10.2169/internalmedicine.56.8001

**Key findings:**
- n=12 CFS patients; Waon therapy 5x/week for 5 weeks
- SPECT imaging: rCBF significantly increased in bilateral prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate gyrus, and thalamus post-treatment
- Improvements correlated with reduced fatigue scores

**Conclusion:** rCBF increase may be a mechanism by which Waon therapy reduces fatigue in CFS. Suggests cerebral hypoperfusion as a therapeutic target.

**Limitations:** n=12; no control group; open-label; same group as Soejima2015; overlaps with Soejima co-authors. Certainty: 0.30

## Paper 4: Li2024MassageMeta

**Full citation:** Li J, Piao F, Zeng Q, Yan H, Bi Y. The effect of massage on patients with chronic fatigue syndrome: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Medicine (Baltimore)*. 2024;103(18):e37973.

**DOI:** 10.1097/MD.0000000000037973

**Key findings:**
- Systematic review and meta-analysis of massage therapy for CFS
- Included 5 RCTs (n=385 total)
- Massage significantly reduced fatigue (SMD = -0.89, 95% CI -1.57 to -0.21, p=0.01)
- Massage improved quality of life, anxiety, and depression
- Various massage types included (Tui Na, acupressure, Swedish)

**Conclusion:** Massage therapy may effectively alleviate fatigue in CFS patients, but quality of evidence is limited.

**Limitations:** Small number of studies; high heterogeneity; most studies from China; intervention protocols varied widely; publication bias possible. Certainty: 0.45 (meta-analysis, but small, heterogeneous)

## Paper 5: Rohrhofer2025MastCell [ALREADY IN BIB as Rohrhofer2025mecfsmast]

**Full citation:** Rohrhofer J, Ebner L, Schweighardt J, Stingl M, Untersmayr E. The Clinical Relevance of Mast Cell Activation in Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome. *Diagnostics (Basel)*. 2025;15(22):2828.

**DOI:** 10.3390/diagnostics15222828

**Key findings:**
- Review linking mast cell activation (MCA) to ME/CFS pathophysiology
- MCA releases histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, leukotrienes, cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α)
- Exercise can trigger mast cell degranulation (exercise-induced urticaria/anaphylaxis)
- MCA may contribute to PEM via: vasodilation → cerebral hypoperfusion, neuroinflammation, pain sensitization
- Suggests antihistamines, mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn), and anti-mediator drugs as potential treatments
- Notes high comorbidity: MCAS + ME/CFS ~15-30% in case series

**Conclusion:** Mast cell activation is a plausible contributor to PEM pathophysiology via vasoactive mediator release. Targeting mast cells may improve post-exercise recovery.

**Limitations:** Review; not original data; no direct measurement of mast cell mediators post-exercise in ME/CFS; MCAS diagnostic criteria still debated. Certainty: 0.40 (existing bib has 0.62 based on cohort size). Existing key: Rohrhofer2025mecfsmast

## Paper 6: Wirth2023Vascular [ALREADY IN BIB as Wirth2023]

**Full citation:** Wirth K, Löhn M. Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) and Comorbidities: Linked by Vascular Pathomechanisms and Vasoactive Mediators? *Medicina (Kaunas)*. 2023;59(5):978.

**DOI:** 10.3390/medicina59050978

**Key findings:**
- Comprehensive hypothesis: vascular dysregulation + vasoactive mediators (histamine, bradykinin, nitric oxide) link ME/CFS, MCAS, POTS, EDS
- Impaired microcirculation → delayed muscle recovery after exercise
- Vasoactive mediator release (from mast cells, endothelium) → exercise intolerance
- Explains co-occurrence of ME/CFS with MCAS, POTS, SFN
- Suggests therapeutic targeting of vascular/vasoactive pathways

**Conclusion:** Vascular pathomechanisms involving mast cell-derived vasoactive mediators provide a unifying framework for ME/CFS comorbidities and may explain delayed recovery after exercise.

**Limitations:** Hypothesis/review; not a trial; no direct experimental data. Certainty: 0.35. Existing key: Wirth2023 (note: published in Healthcare, not Medicina as stated in my search results — possible duplicate journal versions)

## Paper 7: White2023GET

**Full citation:** White PD, Abbey S, Angus B, Ball H, Buchwald D, et al. Anomalies in the review process and interpretation of the evidence in the NICE guideline for chronic fatigue syndrome and myalgic encephalomyelitis. *J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry*. 2023;94(9):745-752.

**DOI:** 10.1136/jnnp-2022-330463

**Key findings:**
- 51 authors critique NICE 2021 guideline recommending against GET
- Argue NICE misrepresented evidence: GET showed moderate benefit in multiple RCTs including PACE trial
- Claim harms not consistently demonstrated in trials
- Cite 7 "anomalies" in NICE's review process and evidence interpretation
- Represent the pro-GET position in the ongoing debate

**Conclusion:** Authors argue GET should remain an option for some ME/CFS patients, contrary to NICE 2021 recommendation.

**Limitations:** Authors include PACE trial investigators (conflict of interest); patient groups strongly oppose GET; PACE trial methodology criticized (subjective outcomes, lowered thresholds for "recovery"). Certainty: 0.40 (high-profile journal, large author list, but clear COI concerns)

## Paper 8: Vink2025GET

**Full citation:** Vink M, Partyka-Vink K. The PACE Trial's GET Manual for Therapists Exposes the Fixed Incremental Nature of Graded Exercise Therapy for ME/CFS. *Life (Basel)*. 2025;15(4):584.

**DOI:** 10.3390/life15040584

**Key findings:**
- Analysis of the PACE trial's original GET therapist manual
- Shows GET involves fixed incremental increases in exercise duration regardless of symptoms
- Argues this fixed incremental approach conflicts with "energy envelope" pacing
- Concludes harms documented by patient reports are consistent with the manual's design
- Supports NICE 2021 decision to recommend against GET

**Conclusion:** GET's fixed incremental structure is incompatible with the post-exertional nature of ME/CFS and likely harmful.

**Limitations:** Single study analysis; author is known patient advocate/critic of PACE; published in lower-tier journal. Certainty: 0.30

## Paper 9: Godlewska2024Creatine

**Full citation:** Godlewska BR, Sylvester A, Emir U, Sharpley AL, Clarke WT, et al. Six-Week Supplementation with Creatine in Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS): A Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy Feasibility Study at 3 Tesla. *Nutrients*. 2024;16(19):3308.

**DOI:** 10.3390/nu16193308

**Key findings:**
- n=14 ME/CFS patients; 6-week creatine monohydrate supplementation (20g/day × 5d loading, then 10g/day)
- MRS measured brain PCr, ATP, pH at 3T
- No significant changes in brain PCr, ATP, or pH
- Self-reported fatigue (Chalder) showed trend toward improvement (not significant in small sample)
- Feasibility: protocol acceptable, MRS at 3T feasible

**Conclusion:** Creatine supplementation did not significantly change brain energetics or fatigue in this small feasibility study. Larger trials needed.

**Limitations:** n=14; no placebo control; open-label; short duration (6 weeks); brain MRS may not capture muscle PCr changes; trend toward fatigue improvement may be placebo. Certainty: 0.30 (small feasibility, no control)

## Paper 10: Jin2026PEM [ALREADY IN BIB as Jin2026PEMMechanism]

**Full citation:** Jin H, An Y, Huang J, Luo T, Wu X. Pathophysiological mechanisms of post-exertional malaise: an integrative analysis based on the metabolism-immune-neuro interaction model. *Front Immunol*. 2026;17:1774310.

**DOI:** 10.3389/fimmu.2026.1774310

**Key findings:**
- Systematic review of PEM pathophysiology across ME/CFS and Long COVID
- Proposes metabolism-immune-neuro (MIN) interaction model
- Identifies: mitochondrial dysfunction + immune activation + neuroinflammation as interacting drivers
- Reviews evidence for cytokine dysregulation (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) post-exercise
- Discusses NLRP3 inflammasome as potential central mechanism
- Notes impaired resolution of inflammation (no SPM/resolvin data in ME/CFS)

**Conclusion:** PEM involves multi-system interactions best captured by MIN model. NLRP3 inflammasome activation and impaired resolution may be key therapeutic targets.

**Limitations:** Review; no original data; broad scope may miss ME/CFS-specific nuances. Certainty: 0.50 (existing bib has 0.55). Existing key: Jin2026PEMMechanism

## Paper 11: Lien2019Lactate

**Full citation:** Lien K, Johansen B, Veierød MB, Haslestad AS, Bøhn SK, et al. Abnormal blood lactate accumulation during repeated exercise testing in myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome. *Physiol Rep*. 2019;7(18):e14138.

**DOI:** 10.14814/phy2.14138

**Key findings:**
- n=18 ME/CFS patients, n=18 matched controls; repeated CPET on consecutive days
- ME/CFS patients: higher blood lactate at submaximal workloads vs controls
- On day 2: ME/CFS patients showed abnormal lactate accumulation at lower workloads compared to day 1
- Controls showed normal lactate handling on both days
- Suggests impaired metabolic recovery and lactate clearance as PEM mechanism

**Conclusion:** Repeated exercise reveals impaired lactate metabolism in ME/CFS, indicating reduced recovery capacity between exercise sessions.

**Limitations:** n=18; moderate sample; well-designed (2-day CPET, matched controls). Certainty: 0.55

## Paper 12: Gattoni2025CPET [ALREADY IN BIB as Gattoni2025LongCOVIDCPET]

**Full citation:** Gattoni C, Abbasi A, Ferguson C, Lanks C, Decato T, et al. Two-day cardiopulmonary exercise testing in long COVID post-exertional malaise diagnosis. *Respir Physiol Neurobiol*. 2025;331:104362.

**DOI:** 10.1016/j.resp.2024.104362

**Key findings:**
- 2-day CPET protocol in Long COVID patients with PEM
- Decreased VO2 peak and work rate on day 2
- Abnormal heart rate recovery and ventilatory efficiency
- 2-day CPET can objectively differentiate PEM from deconditioning
- Supports use of 2-day CPET as objective PEM biomarker

**Conclusion:** 2-day CPET provides objective PEM measurement in Long COVID, supporting its use as diagnostic tool.

**Limitations:** Long COVID, not ME/CFS specifically (though substantial overlap); moderate sample. Certainty: 0.50. Existing key: Gattoni2025LongCOVIDCPET

## Paper 13: Aregawi2026Noradrenergic [ALREADY IN BIB]

**Full citation:** Aregawi L, Walitt B, Sullivan P, Norato G, Benjamin R, et al. Central noradrenergic deficiency in post-infectious chronic fatigue: neurobehavioral correlates. *Brain Commun*. 2026;8(3):fcag173.

**DOI:** 10.1093/braincomms/fcag173

**Key findings:**
- Assessed central noradrenergic function in post-infectious chronic fatigue
- Reduced CSF MHPG (noradrenaline metabolite) correlated with cognitive fatigue
- Cognitive post-exertional worsening linked to noradrenergic deficiency
- Suggests cognitive PEM has distinct neurochemical basis from muscular PEM
- Implicates locus coeruleus-noradrenaline pathway

**Conclusion:** Central noradrenergic deficiency may drive cognitive domain of PEM, distinct from peripheral muscular mechanisms.

**Limitations:** Post-infectious subgroup (not all ME/CFS); correlational; small sample for CSF studies. Certainty: 0.40. Existing bib has 0.90. Existing key: Aregawi2026Noradrenergic

## Paper 14: Creatine general sports science (non-ME/CFS reference)

**Full citation:** 41579075 (Short-term creatine supplementation enhances strength, reduces fatigue, and accelerates recovery in resistance-trained athletes: a double-blind, randomized, crossover trial. *J Int Soc Sports Nutr*. 2025.)

See PMID 41579075 — used as general-population reference point only.

**Key findings (general population):**
- n=30 resistance-trained athletes; double-blind, randomized, crossover
- Creatine (20g/day × 5d + 5g/day maintenance) for 4 weeks
- Significant improvement in strength, reduced fatigue, accelerated recovery
- Muscle PCr resynthesis rate increased

**Conclusion:** Creatine accelerates recovery in healthy athletes. Relevance to ME/CFS hypothetical; Godlewska2024 found null result in brain MRS.

**Limitations:** General population, not ME/CFS; short duration; no disease comparison. Certainty: 0.70 (for general population; 0.15 for ME/CFS application)
