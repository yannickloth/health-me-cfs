### Null
- **Egg 2002**: Pupillary unrest (hippus) inversely correlated with MS fatigue (r=-0.521). Central autonomic instability does not explain fatigue in MS. Key null finding.

### Indirect Biochemical/Systemic Links
**6 indirect papers found. Key connections:**
1. **Szklarski 2021 + Azcue 2026**: ME/CFS patients have elevated autoantibodies against M3 muscarinic and α1-adrenergic receptors — the exact receptors controlling pupil constriction (M3 on iris sphincter) and dilation (α1 on iris dilator). This is the strongest mechanistic bridge: if pathogenic autoantibodies target the pupil's autonomic receptors, pupillometry should detect functional impairment.
2. **Lisowski 2025**: Anatomical confirmation — iris sphincter = M3 (parasympathetic), iris dilator = α1 (sympathetic), ciliary ganglion + Edinger-Westphal nucleus = central control.
3. **Oesch-Régeni 2025**: M3 autoantibodies are clinically actionable in ME/CFS (plasmapheresis/IVIG pilot). Even if pupil effects were not measured, the same autoantibodies targeted in treatment act on the pupil.
4. **Smit 2026 (reused `smit2026pupillary`)**: Task-evoked pupillary dynamics altered in post-COVID syndrome (n=397). PCS shares autonomic dysfunction features with ME/CFS.

## 3. Evidence Summary

**What does this evidence establish?**
Pupillary light reflex (PLR) parameters are validated, noninvasive autonomic biomarkers in several conditions (concussion, diabetic neuropathy, MS, pure autonomic failure), but have never been studied in a modern, properly-powered ME/CFS cohort. The only direct CFS pupillometry study (Sendrowski 1997) is outdated. However, strong indirect evidence from GPCR autoantibody research provides a mechanistic rationale: the M3 muscarinic and α1-adrenergic receptors controlling pupil constriction and dilation are the same receptors targeted by autoantibodies elevated in ME/CFS. This makes pupillometry a mechanistically grounded autonomic probe — not a fishing expedition.

**Strongest mechanistic claim supportable:**
Pupillary dysfunction in ME/CFS is mechanistically plausible because (a) the pupil's autonomic receptors (M3, α1) are established targets of ME/CFS-associated autoantibodies, and (b) pupillometry detects autonomic dysfunction in diseases with similar autonomic phenotypes (MS, diabetes, PAF). The clinical question is not "could it work?" but "has anyone tested it properly?" — and the answer is no.

**What must remain speculative:**
- Whether PLR abnormalities actually exist in ME/CFS (never properly tested)
- Whether PLR metrics would correlate with ME/CFS-specific features (PEM, severity, orthostatic intolerance)
- Whether pupillometry adds diagnostic value beyond existing autonomic tests (HRV, tilt-table)
- Any quantitative PLR cutoff for ME/CFS diagnosis

**What the evidence does not support:**
- That pupillometry is a validated ME/CFS biomarker (it is not — it's a validated method in other conditions with a mechanistic rationale in ME/CFS)
- That all PLR parameters track fatigue (Egg 2002 contradicts for pupillary unrest in MS)

## 4. Clinical Relevance Assessment

- **Direct patient care implications:** Currently none — pupillometry has no validated ME/CFS application. However, the method is noninvasive, takes ~30 seconds, and uses inexpensive handheld devices — if validated, it could be a low-burden outpatient screening tool accessible even to severe patients.
- **Treatment-related:** Not applicable — this is a biomarker/method topic, not a treatment.
- **Evidence type:** Cross-disease methods validation + biochemical mechanistic rationale. **No ME/CFS-specific clinical data.**
- **Severity applicability:** Potentially all severity levels (pupillometry can be performed supine/bedbound), but this is speculative — no severity-stratified data exist.
- **Clinical relevance: LOW** (research-only at this stage; no clinical application until an ME/CFS validation study is conducted). Could elevate to MEDIUM if validated.
