### 1.2 Lacrimal Gland Parasympathetic Denervation (Not Inflammation) Drives Sicca in ME/CFS

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| ID | 1.2 |
| Title | Lacrimal gland functional denervation as primary sicca mechanism in ME/CFS |
| Mechanism | The lacrimal functional unit (LFU) is innervated by parasympathetic fibres from CN VII (pterygopalatine ganglion). In Sjögren's, sicca is primarily caused by lymphocytic infiltration of lacrimal glands. In ME/CFS, where dysautonomia (parasympathetic withdrawal) is well-documented @Azcue2023sfn, sicca could arise from functional denervation of the LFU rather than autoimmune gland destruction. This would predict: (a) preserved gland architecture on imaging, (b) normal or compensatory salivary flow with pharmacological stimulation, (c) correlation of sicca severity with other autonomic measures (HRV, pupillometry), not with inflammatory markers. Distinguishing denervation-driven from inflammation-driven sicca determines treatment: cholinergic agonists (pilocarpine, cevimeline) for denervation vs immunosuppression for autoimmune. |
| Evidence link | Azcue2023sfn (parasympathetic dysfunction in ME/CFS); wu2024sjogrendryeyediagnostics (SjS inflammatory sicca); general LFU neuroanatomy. |
| Preliminary certainty | 0.30 — plausible, testable, never explicitly assessed |
| Falsifiable prediction | In ME/CFS patients with sicca (n=40) vs Sjögren's sicca controls (n=20), ME/CFS will show: (a) normal salivary gland ultrasound (no parenchymal heterogeneity, normal power Doppler), (b) Schirmer's test improvement ≥5mm after oral pilocarpine challenge (reversible denervation), (c) correlation of OSDI dry eye score with HRV measures (r≥0.5). Falsified if ME/CFS sicca patients show salivary gland ultrasound abnormalities matching Sjögren's, or if pilocarpine challenge does not improve tear production. |
| Non-specialist consequence | Dry eyes in ME/CFS may be caused by the autonomic nervous system malfunction rather than immune attack on tear glands — which would mean different treatments. |

### 1.5 Corneal Subbasal Nerve Loss Reflects Ganglionic Immune Attack Shared with Sjögren's, Not Distal Axonopathy

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| ID | 1.5 |
| Title | Trigeminal ganglion immune attack as shared mechanism in Sjögren's and ME/CFS corneal neuropathy |
| Mechanism | In Sjögren's, corneal nerve loss in CCM correlates with SFN status but also with trigeminal ganglion involvement @luzu2022cornealinnervation. The trigeminal ganglion is not protected by the blood-brain barrier — it has fenestrated capillaries, allowing circulating autoantibodies and immune cells direct access. If ME/CFS involves autoantibodies targeting ganglionic neurons (as hypothesized for DRG in @spec:sfn-interface-failure), the trigeminal ganglion would be similarly vulnerable. Corneal nerve loss on CCM would then reflect ganglionic immune attack, not distal axonopathy — explaining why corneal changes can occur without length-dependent IENFD loss. The Sjögren's literature provides the precedent: corneal CCM changes occur even in the absence of systemic SFN @luzu2022cornealinnervation. |
| Evidence link | luzu2022cornealinnervation (CCM in SjS + SFN); wang2025ivcmsjogren (CCM predicts serological activity); verges2025fibrodryeye (CCM in FM dry eye); general trigeminal ganglion vulnerability. |
| Preliminary certainty | 0.25 — builds on existing literature, testable |
| Falsifiable prediction | In ME/CFS (n=30) with CCM-detected corneal nerve loss, concurrent blink reflex testing (trigeminal nerve function) will be abnormal in ≥50%, while distal IENFD (proximal thigh biopsy to avoid length-dependent confound) will be normal. Autopsy or MRI studies of the trigeminal ganglion (if available) would show immune cell infiltration or MRI contrast enhancement. Falsified if CCM changes tightly correlate with distal IENFD and blink reflex is normal. |
| Non-specialist consequence | Eye nerve damage could start at the nerve root (where it enters the brainstem) rather than the nerve tip — meaning treatments need to reach the brain, not just the eye surface. |

### 2.2 Pilocarpine Challenge Test to Distinguish Autonomic Sicca from Glandular Sicca in ME/CFS

| Field | Content |
|---|---|
| ID | 2.2 |
| Title | Pharmacological lacrimal challenge to distinguish denervation from inflammatory sicca |
| Mechanism | Pilocarpine (cholinergic agonist, FDA-approved for Sjögren's sicca) stimulates lacrimal and salivary secretion via M3 muscarinic receptors. In Sjögren's, response to pilocarpine is blunted due to gland destruction. In autonomic sicca (functional denervation), response should be preserved or even exaggerated (denervation supersensitivity). A standardised pilocarpine challenge (5mg oral, measure Schirmer's at 0, 30, 60, 90 min) in ME/CFS sicca patients vs Sjögren's sicca controls would separate the two mechanisms. A normal response argues for autonomic sicca; blunted response suggests glandular pathology requiring Sjögren's workup (lip biopsy, SSA/SSB). Feasible as an outpatient procedure, no special equipment beyond Schirmer strips. |
| Evidence link | Hypothesis 1.2 (denervation mechanism); general pilocarpine pharmacology; naseri2025dryeye (DED in post-COVID). |

Falsified if ME/CFS and Sjögren's show similar blunted response rates. |
| Non-specialist consequence | A simple eye test using a common medication could tell whether dry eyes in ME/CFS are due to nerve dysfunction (autonomic) or gland damage (autoimmune) — with very different treatment implications. |
