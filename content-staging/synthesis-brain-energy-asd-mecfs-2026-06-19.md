### What's missing
- **GLUT1:** Zero papers on GLUT1 in ME/CFS brain tissue
- **Brain glycogen:** Zero papers on cerebral glycogen in ME/CFS (measurement difficulty acknowledged)
- **PFC-specific glucose metabolism:** Zero studies targeting PFC glucose metabolism in ME/CFS
- **Executive function + glucose link:** Zero studies bridging EF deficits to glucose metabolism in ME/CFS
- **Astrocyte-specific PET/MRS:** No astrocyte-selective imaging in ME/CFS

### Null findings
- Siessmeier (2003): no *global* glucose hypometabolism in CFS — only regional (right caudate, brainstem). This is a partial null: the more granular prediction (regional, network-level) is supported; the coarse prediction (global) is not.

### Indirect biochemical links
- **OxPHOS-to-glycolysis shift** → documented in ME/CFS PBMCs (Tomas 2017, 2020), ventricular lactate (Natelson 2017), PEM metabolomics (McGregor 2019)
- **Astrocyte dysfunction** → documented in ME/CFS fatigue pathway models (Morris 2015, Xu 2026)
- **Energy conservation as fatigue mechanism** → independently proposed by Lacourt (2018) without reference to ASD literature
- **Brain glucose hypometabolism** → FDG-PET evidence exists (Siessmeier, VanDerGucht, Mairal, Abu-Judeh) but is regionally heterogeneous
- **All four indirect pathways converge:** ME/CFS independently shows evidence for every mechanism in the ASD paper's model

## 2. Internal Contradiction Detection

**Contradiction: Global vs. regional hypometabolism**
- Siessmeier (2003): no global FDG-PET difference between CFS and controls
- VanDerGucht (2017): widespread cortical FDG-PET hypometabolism in MMF/CFS-like
- Mairal (2021): global hypometabolism in a single CFS case
- **Resolution:** This is resolvable. MMF population (VanDerGucht) may differ from idiopathic CFS (Siessmeier). The Mairal case report is n=1. Likely heterogeneity across ME/CFS subtypes/severities, not a fundamental contradiction. The pattern mirrors the ASD paper's own observation that PET studies show mixed hyper/hypometabolism depending on region and individual.

**No other contradictions detected.** The evidence converges: brain glucose metabolism is disrupted in ME/CFS, but the pattern is region-specific (not global), severity-dependent, and heterogeneous across subtypes.

## 3. Evidence Summary

### What this evidence establishes
ME/CFS independently exhibits all the core biochemical features of the ASD brain energy metabolism model: (1) regional brain glucose hypometabolism via FDG-PET, (2) elevated cerebral lactate via MRS suggesting glycolytic shift away from OXPHOS, (3) astrocyte dysfunction implicated in fatigue pathogenesis, and (4) an independently proposed energy conservation model structurally identical to the ASD paper's "energy-saving adaptation." The convergence is striking because the ME/CFS evidence was developed without reference to the ASD brain energy framework.

### Strongest mechanistic claim supportable
"Brain energy metabolism dysfunction — specifically regional glucose hypometabolism, astrocyte-mediated lactate shuttle impairment, and an OXPHOS-to-glycolysis shift — contributes to cognitive dysfunction in ME/CFS. This mechanism converges with the ASD brain energy disorder model, suggesting a cross-disease phenomenon where cerebral ATP shortfall drives cognitive sacrifice."

### What must remain speculative
- PFC-specific glucose deficits driving EF impairment (no direct imaging evidence)
- GLUT1 involvement (no ME/CFS data)
- Brain glycogen depletion (no ME/CFS data)
- Whether the mechanism is primary or secondary to neuroinflammation
- Developmental timing difference: ASD is neurodevelopmental; ME/CFS is acquired — the mechanism may be the same but the temporal trigger differs

### What the evidence does NOT support
- Global brain glucose hypometabolism as a uniform ME/CFS feature (Siessmeier 2003)
- A simple "brain-wide energy deficit" model — the evidence suggests regional/network-specific vulnerability (analogous to ASD's PFC-predominant pattern)

### Primary integration targets
- ch06 (energy metabolism) — core mechanistic integration
- ch09 (CNS / neurocognitive) — cognitive dysfunction, brain fog mechanism
- ch13 (cross-disease integrative models) — ASD-ME/CFS convergent pathway
- ch14d (cross-disease comparisons) — cross-disease energy metabolism

## 4. Clinical Relevance Assessment

- **Direct patient care implications:** LOW to MEDIUM. The framework provides mechanistic context for cognitive symptoms but does not yet yield validated biomarkers or treatments. Potential therapeutic implications (ketogenic diet, metformin, NAC targeting energy pathways) are research-stage.
- **Treatment-related data:** No ME/CFS-specific clinical trials of metabolic interventions exist for the brain energy pathway specifically. General-population data on ketogenic diet for cognition exists but has not been trialed in ME/CFS.
- **Evidence type:** Mixed — human imaging (FDG-PET, MRS), human blood biomarkers (PBMC bioenergetics), preclinical (mouse model), narrative reviews. No RCTs. No ME/CFS-specific metabolic intervention data.
- **Patient subset applicability:** Unknown. Evidence comes from mixed-severity study populations. Tomas (2020) suggests severity-dependent bioenergetic impairment — severely affected patients may exhibit more pronounced metabolic dysfunction. Applicable to: mild / moderate / severe / very severe (hypothesized gradient; confirmation needed).

**Clinical relevance: LOW — research-stage mechanistic framework.** Provides biological context for cognitive symptoms and potential therapeutic directions, but no actionable clinical protocols. **Subset: unknown (hypothesized severity gradient).**

**Decision: PROCEED**

The evidence meets and exceeds the PROCEED threshold: 4 papers at certainty ≥ 0.60, 6 at certainty ≥ 0.50, and only 3/13 (23%) below 0.40. Critically, the convergence of four independent lines across different methods (FDG-PET, MRS, PBMC bioenergetics, mechanistic reviews) makes this a well-triangulated mechanistic observation rather than a single-study finding.

The existence of a structurally identical energy conservation model independently developed in ME/CFS (Lacourt 2018) without reference to the ASD literature constitutes a particularly strong form of validation — the same mechanistic logic was derived from two different disease contexts.

The primary limitations are: (1) no PFC-specific imaging in ME/CFS, (2) no GLUT1 data, (3) no brain glycogen data, (4) no ME/CFS clinical trials of metabolic interventions targeting brain energy. These are research gaps, not evidence of absence.
