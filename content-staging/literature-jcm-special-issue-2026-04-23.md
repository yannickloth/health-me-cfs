## Paper 1: Voice and Speech Abnormalities in ME/CFS

**Citation key:** `Grach2025voiceSpeech`
**Authors:** Grach SL, Seltzer J, Orbelo DM (Mayo Clinic + ME Action Network)
**Journal:** J Clin Med 2025;14(14):4847
**DOI:** 10.3390/jcm14144847 | **PMID:** 40725538
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Low-Medium)

**Design:** Mixed-methods online survey; Pacing Narrative Study dataset (first use); n=685 enrolled, 302 qualitative respondents; AI-assisted qualitative analysis.

**Key findings:**
- 9.27% of qualitative responses mentioned voice or speech changes (38 mentions, 28 unique respondents)
- Emerged without direct prompting — patients volunteered this as part of PEM experience descriptions
- Proposes voice/speech as potential digital biomarker for PEM monitoring

**Limitations:** No acoustic measurements; self-selected sample; no control group; very low absolute rate; dataset designed for PEM not voice.

**Replication status:** Not replicated — preliminary signal only.

**Integration targets:**
- **ch20** (biomarkers): Novel candidate digital biomarker; low certainty, use `speculation` or `observation` environment
- **ch02/ch05** (clinical features): As patient-reported symptom domain; brief mention only
- Suggested environment: `speculation` — signal study, not validated

## Paper 2: Autonomic Dysfunction in ME/CFS — MCAM Multi-Site Study

**Citation key:** `MCAM2025autonomic`
**Authors:** Issa A, Lin J-MS, Chen Y, Attell J, Brimmer D, Bertolli J, Natelson BH, Lapp CW, Podell RN, Kogelnik AM, Klimas NG, Peterson DL, Bateman L, Unger ER; MCAM Study Group
**Journal:** J Clin Med 2025;14(17):6269
**DOI:** 10.3390/jcm14176269 | **PMID:** 40944028
**Certainty:** 0.75 (High)

**Design:** Multi-site cross-sectional; n=442 (301 ME/CFS, 141 healthy controls); 7 specialist clinics across USA; COMPASS-31 + NASA lean test + medical history.

**Key findings:**
- 97% of ME/CFS participants had at least one autonomic symptom (all p<0.0001 vs controls)
- Higher burden across all three assessment modalities
- OI, GI, and pupillomotor domains correlate with heightened illness severity
- Supports dysautonomia as a core feature, not epiphenomenon

**Limitations:** Cross-sectional; specialist clinic selection bias; no longitudinal follow-up.

**Replication status:** Partially replicated — MCAM is itself a multi-site replication consortium; consistent with prior smaller studies.

**Note:** Entry already existed in bib as `MCAM2025autonomic` but was incomplete (`author = {others}`, no PMID, no certainty). Updated with full author list and metadata.

**Integration targets:**
- **ch09** (autonomic nervous system): `achievement` environment — 97% prevalence, large n, multi-site
- **ch14d** (POTS/dysautonomia): Primary evidence base; severity correlation finding
- **ch20** (biomarkers): COMPASS-31 and NASA lean test as validated assessment tools
- Suggested environment: `achievement` for the 97% prevalence / severity correlation finding

## Paper 3: Pediatric Pulmonary Rehabilitation in Post-COVID-19

**Citation key:** `Goicoechea2026pediatricRehab`
**Authors:** Goicoechea-Calvo A et al. (15 authors; Spain)
**Journal:** J Clin Med 2026;15(2):535
**DOI:** 10.3390/jcm15020535 | **PMID:** 41598473
**Certainty:** 0.50 (Medium)

**Design:** Quasi-experimental (pre-post, no control); n=115 pediatric post-COVID; mean age 13.3 years; 66.1% female; 12-month multidisciplinary pulmonary rehabilitation; Spain.

**Key findings:**
- 6MWD +37 m (p<0.001, Cohen's d=0.50)
- Handgrip +2.4 kg; breathing muscle strength +15 cmH2O
- Fatigue scores -9.3 points; psychiatric symptoms -4.5 points (p<0.001)
- Well-being +11 points; muscle imaging showed improved composition

**Limitations:** No control group — cannot exclude natural recovery or regression to mean; single center; no PEM screening (post-COVID ≠ ME/CFS); diagnostic heterogeneity.

**Replication status:** Not replicated; single quasi-experimental study.

**Critical context:** Post-COVID pediatric population overlaps with but is not identical to pediatric ME/CFS. The lack of PEM screening means some participants may have had ME/CFS-like presentation where exercise could cause harm. Use cautiously — do not generalize to ME/CFS without noting this distinction.

**Integration targets:**
- **ch17** (non-pharmacological treatments): As evidence for rehabilitation in post-COVID pediatric — with explicit caveat about quasi-experimental design and no PEM screening
- **ch24** (CBT/GET critique context): Could note as example of uncontrolled rehabilitation study where improvement cannot be attributed to intervention vs. natural recovery
- Suggested environment: `observation` with `warning` about design limitations and post-COVID vs. ME/CFS distinction

## Paper 4: An Overview of Severe Myalgic Encephalomyelitis

**Citation key:** `Vink2026severeME`
**Authors:** Vink M, Vink-Niese A
**Journal:** J Clin Med 2026;15(2):805
**DOI:** 10.3390/jcm15020805 | **PMID:** 41598742
**Certainty:** 0.60 (Medium-High for descriptive claims; lower for causal claims)

**Design:** Narrative review. Authors have established publication record critiquing GET/CBT trial methodology (VinkNiese2022NICE already in bib).

**Key findings:**
- Severe ME/CFS: clinical diagnosis, no diagnostic test
- Most severe: bedbound 24/7, dark/quiet room, some require tube feeding
- 2-day CPET documents abnormal exercise response (research/disability use)
- COVID-19 expanded the post-infectious ME/CFS pool
- No effective treatments exist for severe ME/CFS
- Call for pharmacological research using advanced medical technologies

**Limitations:** Narrative review (not systematic); potential author perspective bias; no new primary data; severity definitions not uniformly operationalized.

**Replication status:** Descriptive claims about severe ME are consistent with multiple independent sources (Severe ME charity data, prior reviews). Specific treatment absence claim is well-supported.

**Integration targets:**
- **ch05** (severity spectrum): Primary target; severe ME subgroup definition, bedbound presentations, tube feeding requirement
- **ch03** (disease definition/diagnosis): Clinical diagnosis without biomarker; 2-day CPET role
- **ch14a** (functional impairment): Severity documentation, disability context
- Suggested environment: `observation` (for descriptive severity facts); `warning` (for no effective treatments claim)

## Paper 5: POTS, Menopause, and Hormone Replacement Therapy

**Citation key:** `Blitshteyn2026POTSmenopause`
**Author:** Blitshteyn S (University at Buffalo; Dysautonomia Clinic) — also guest editor of this JCM special issue
**Journal:** J Clin Med 2026;15(4):1477
**DOI:** 10.3390/jcm15041477 | **PMID:** 41753164
**Certainty:** 0.45 (Medium-Low)

**Design:** Narrative review; no systematic search reported.

**Key findings:**
- POTS: ≥30 bpm HR rise supine-to-standing, no orthostatic hypotension; predominantly reproductive-age women
- Hormonal modulation: symptoms exacerbated during menstruation; pregnancy is a trigger
- Peri-/postmenopausal POTS exacerbation: HRT may be considered
- Vaginal estrogen: safest option; transdermal estrogen + micronized progesterone for menopausal symptoms
- Long-term HRT outcomes in POTS: unknown

**Limitations:** Narrative review; no RCT evidence for HRT in POTS; author is guest editor (potential perspective considerations); limited to menopausal POTS.

**Replication status:** No RCTs; clinical expert review only.

**Integration targets:**
- **ch14d** (POTS/dysautonomia): Sex-hormone modulation of POTS; menopausal transition management
- **ch23** (epidemiology, sex differences): Why POTS/ME/CFS predominantly affects women; hormonal vulnerability windows
- Suggested environment: `observation` for hormonal modulation facts; `speculation` for HRT recommendations given absence of RCT data

## Paper 6: Telemedicine Clinic for Pediatric and Young Adult POTS

**Citation key:** `Boris2026POTStelemedicine`
**Author:** Boris JR (Jeffrey R. Boris MD LLC, Moylan PA)
**Journal:** J Clin Med 2026;15(4):1626
**DOI:** 10.3390/jcm15041626 | **PMID:** 41753313
**Certainty:** 0.55 (Medium)

**Design:** Retrospective analysis; n=277; median age 16.8 years; 88.1% female; telemedicine clinic.

**Key findings:**
- MCAS suspected: 70%
- Joint hypermobility: 78.3% (higher in females) — hEDS/HSD overlap
- Migraine: 51.6%; tension-type headache: 57.4%
- Head trauma/concussion history: ~40%
- Vestibular dysfunction and convergence disorder: previously underrecognized
- Telemedicine: feasible for comprehensive POTS evaluation

**Limitations:** Retrospective; single provider; MCAS by suspicion not formal criteria; telemedicine selection bias; no ME/CFS diagnostic assessment.

**Replication status:** Partially replicated — MCAS/hypermobility/POTS triad consistent with prior clinical cohort literature.

**Integration targets:**
- **ch14d** (POTS/dysautonomia): MCAS-POTS-hypermobility triad prevalence; telemedicine feasibility for dysautonomia patients
- **ch05** (clinical features/comorbidities): Comorbidity pattern in POTS/ME/CFS overlap
- **ch23** (epidemiology): Sex ratio, age distribution in pediatric POTS
- Suggested environment: `observation` for comorbidity prevalence data
