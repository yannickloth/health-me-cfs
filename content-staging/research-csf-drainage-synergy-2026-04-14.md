# Literature Research: CSF Drainage Synergy (CA Inhibitors + Perrin Technique)

**Date:** 2026-04-14
**Hypothesis:** CA inhibitors (acetazolamide, topiramate) reduce CSF production; Perrin Technique provides mechanical lymphatic drainage; together they may address glymphatic clearance failure from both sides (reduce input, increase output). Particularly relevant for hEDS/CCI comorbid patients with intracranial hypertension signs.

## 1. CA Inhibitors and CSF Production

### Wall2014 (IIHTT) — Landmark RCT
- **Citation:** Wall M et al. JAMA. 2014;311(16):1641-1651. doi:10.1001/jama.2014.3312. PMID:24756514
- **Design:** Multicenter RCT, n=165 IIH patients, 6 months, maximally tolerated acetazolamide (up to 4 g/d) vs placebo + low-sodium diet
- **Key findings:** Acetazolamide improved visual field function (0.71 dB difference, p=.050), reduced papilledema (p<.001), improved vision-related QoL (p=.003), and reduced weight (-4.05 kg, p<.001)
- **Quality:** Class I evidence, multicenter (38 sites), JAMA publication
- **Relevance:** Establishes acetazolamide as evidence-based first-line therapy for IIH. Demonstrates CSF production reduction via CA inhibition improves clinically meaningful outcomes. Directly supports the hypothesis that CA inhibition reduces CSF input pressure
- **Limitations:** Modest effect size on visual field; high dropout from side effects (paresthesias, dysgeusia); 50% experienced treatment-limiting adverse effects. Benefit may partially reflect weight loss. All but 4 participants were women, limiting generalizability

### Mitchell2025 — Head-to-Head Drug Comparison
- **Citation:** Mitchell JL et al. Headache. 2025;65(2):258-268. doi:10.1111/head.14897. PMID:39853738
- **Design:** Randomized sequential cross-over study, n=14 IIH patients, 2-week treatment periods with telemetric ICP monitoring. Tested acetazolamide, amiloride, furosemide, spironolactone, topiramate
- **Key findings:** All four active drugs reduced ICP marginally (acetazolamide -3.3 mmHg, furosemide -3.0, spironolactone -2.7, topiramate -2.3; no significant difference between drugs). Side effects common with acetazolamide (100%) and topiramate (93%). **Both acetazolamide and topiramate worsened fluid cognition (p=0.057 and 0.061 respectively)**
- **Quality:** First head-to-head comparison with objective ICP measurement via telemetric monitors. Small sample (n=14). Published in Headache journal
- **Relevance:** Critical negative finding for ME/CFS application: **CA inhibitors impaired cognition at doses effective for ICP reduction.** This directly challenges the hypothesis's safety for ME/CFS patients who already have cognitive dysfunction. Topiramate showed similar ICP reduction with comparable cognitive side effects
- **Limitations:** Small sample, not powered for between-drug differences, unblinded design for individual drugs

### Goyal2023 — Topiramate as Alternative First-Line
- **Citation:** Goyal A, Zarroli K. Medicine (Baltimore). 2023;102(42):e35545. doi:10.1097/MD.0000000000035545. PMID:37861536
- **Design:** Literature review
- **Key findings:** Topiramate has multiple mechanisms relevant to IIH: CA inhibition (reduces ICP), weight loss promotion, migraine prophylaxis. Safety profile comparable or superior to acetazolamide
- **Quality:** Review article, moderate quality (Medicine journal)
- **Relevance:** Supports topiramate as rational alternative/complement to acetazolamide, particularly for patients with comorbid migraine (common in ME/CFS). Weight loss benefit addresses obesity-IIH overlap
- **Limitations:** No direct comparison data; limited RCT evidence

### Wang2022 — IIH Comprehensive Review
- **Citation:** Wang MTM, Bhatti MT, Danesh-Meyer HV. J Clin Neurosci. 2022;95:172-179. doi:10.1016/j.jocn.2021.11.029. PMID:34929642
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:** IIH pathophysiology involves CSF dynamics dysregulation and venous sinus pressure. IIHTT provides Class I evidence for acetazolamide. Emerging metabolic/hormonal targets
- **Quality:** Comprehensive review, J Clin Neurosci
- **Relevance:** Provides mechanistic context for CA inhibitor use in CSF dynamics disorders

## 2. Acetazolamide in ME/CFS Context

### Medow2024 — Acetazolamide in ME/CFS with Orthostatic Intolerance
- **Citation:** Medow MS, Stewart JM. Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol. 2024;326(6):R599-R608. doi:10.1152/ajpregu.00071.2024. PMID:38682242
- **Design:** Experimental physiology study, n=15 ME/CFS+POTS patients, n=11 controls. Tested CO2, acetazolamide, and phenylephrine during head-up tilt
- **Key findings:** **Acetazolamide had NO effect on neurocognitive function during orthostatic challenge in ME/CFS.** Only phenylephrine improved upright N-back performance (ME/CFS+PE=65.6% vs control 56.9%). Acetazolamide did not improve cerebral autoregulation (phase synchronization index)
- **Quality:** Controlled experimental design, small sample, AJP-Regulatory journal
- **Relevance:** **Directly challenges the hypothesis for ME/CFS application.** In ME/CFS with OI/POTS, acetazolamide does not improve the orthostatic cognitive impairment that the hypothesis targets. However, this tested acute effects during tilt, not chronic CSF volume reduction
- **Limitations:** Small sample, acute (not chronic) administration, phenylephrine is not a viable long-term treatment

### Swenson2014 — CA Inhibitors at High Altitude
- **Citation:** Swenson ER. Subcell Biochem. 2014;75:361-386. doi:10.1007/978-94-007-7359-2_18. PMID:24146388
- **Design:** Chapter review
- **Key findings:** Acetazolamide acts by inhibiting renal and endothelial CA, opposing hypocapnic alkalosis from hypoxic ventilatory response. This augments ventilation and improves oxygenation. Also has non-CA inhibiting actions relevant to HAPE prevention
- **Quality:** Authoritative review from leading altitude medicine researcher
- **Relevance:** Provides detailed mechanistic understanding of how CA inhibitors affect CSF production and cerebral fluid dynamics

## 3. Perrin Technique — Lymphatic Drainage

### Riste2025 — Self-Help Perrin Technique for Long COVID (RCT)
- **Citation:** Riste L, Perrin R, Mulholland T, Hann M, McDonald O, Heald A. Infect Dis Ther. 2026;15(2):577-589. doi:10.1007/s40121-025-01287-z. PMID:41442105
- **Design:** Feasibility RCT of self-help Perrin Technique (self-massage, mobility exercises, breathing exercises, cold/warm packs) vs wait-list control, n=100 Long COVID patients
- **Key findings:** Improvements in Chalder Fatigue Questionnaire in intervention group at 3 months (p=0.01). High recruitment rate (90.6%), 79.3% follow-up at 3 months. Participants found intervention onerous but were keen to engage
- **Quality:** Feasibility study (not powered for efficacy); first RCT of Perrin-derived self-help intervention. Conflict of interest: Perrin developed the intervention
- **Relevance:** First RCT evidence supporting Perrin-derived lymphatic drainage approach for fatigue. Applicable to both Long COVID and ME/CFS. Supports the "increase output" side of the hypothesis
- **Limitations:** Feasibility study only; self-help (not practitioner-administered); no ME/CFS-specific data; no CSF/glymphatic measurement; large control group improvement may confound results

## 4. hEDS/CCI and Intracranial Hypertension

### Henderson2017 — Neurological Manifestations of EDS
- **Citation:** Henderson FC Sr et al. Am J Med Genet C Semin Med Genet. 2017;175(1):195-211. doi:10.1002/ajmg.c.31549. PMID:28220607
- **Design:** Expert consensus/communication
- **Key findings:** EDS associated with increased prevalence of migraine, **idiopathic intracranial hypertension**, Chiari malformation, craniocervical instability, Tarlov cysts, tethered cord. Ligamentous laxity at atlanto-occipital and atlantoaxial joints may complicate all EDS forms. CMI may be complicated by CCI or basilar invagination
- **Quality:** Expert consensus, comprehensive
- **Relevance:** Establishes the hEDS-IIH comorbidity cluster relevant to the hypothesis. 78% of Bragee2020 ME/CFS patients had intracranial hypertension signs — this review explains the EDS-IIH connection mechanistically
- **Limitations:** Epidemiological evidence "not yet available" at time of publication; anecdotal reports

### Midtlien2024 — CVD/Connective Tissue Disorder Phenotype
- **Citation:** Midtlien JP et al. Front Neurol. 2024;15:1305972. doi:10.3389/fneur.2023.1305972. PMID:38269002
- **Design:** Retrospective single-center review, n=86 patients with connective tissue disorders + cerebral venous outflow disorders
- **Key findings:** 55% had EDS diagnosis; 55.8% had POTS; 51.2% had CSF leaks; **37.2% had CCI; 25.6% had MCAS**. Despite lower opening pressures than classic IIH, headache severity and QoL were comparable, suggesting **hypersensitivity to intracranial pressures**. Allergies to medications (87.2%) and surgical tape (19.8%) frequent
- **Quality:** Retrospective, single-center, but largest series of this phenotype
- **Relevance:** **Critically important for the hypothesis:** establishes a conserved clinical phenotype of CTD+CVD+IIH+CCI+POTS+MCAS. This is precisely the patient population where combined CA inhibitor + lymphatic drainage would be considered. The pressure hypersensitivity finding suggests lower drug doses may be needed
- **Limitations:** Retrospective, single center, no control group, potential selection bias from tertiary referral center

### Frost2024 — Neuraxial Biomechanics in EDS
- **Citation:** Frost N, Barclay SJ. Front Neurol. 2024;15:1479545. doi:10.3389/fneur.2024.1479545. PMID:39719977
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Key findings:** Altered connective tissue in EDS affects neuraxial biomechanics, impacting CSF dynamics, venous congestion, and myodural bridge function. Discusses interactions between biomechanics and IIH, SIH, CCI, and tethered cord
- **Quality:** Peer-reviewed narrative review, Frontiers in Neurology
- **Relevance:** Provides mechanistic framework: in EDS, altered dural tension and myodural bridge function may impair glymphatic outflow. Supports the rationale for Perrin Technique-type manual approaches to improve lymphatic drainage
- **Limitations:** Narrative review, no primary data

### Hashemizad2025 — Spiky-Leaky Syndrome
- **Citation:** Hashemizad A et al. Front Neurol. 2025;16:1512671. doi:10.3389/fneur.2025.1512671. PMID:40313612
- **Design:** Retrospective review of metabolic exercise data, n=44 SLS patients vs 210 OIDS vs 53 controls
- **Key findings:** SLS patients (CCI + MCAS + HSD + alternating intracranial hypertension with CSF leaks) showed severe hyperventilation at rest and during exercise (low ETCO2), reduced VO2max, and reduced peak oxygen pulse compared to both OIDS and controls
- **Quality:** Retrospective, moderate evidence
- **Relevance:** Describes the clinical syndrome where the combined hypothesis is most relevant. Chronic hyperventilation may impair glymphatic clearance (ventilation-coupled CSF clearance). Suggests CO2 supplementation may be a simpler alternative to CA inhibitors for this phenotype

## Overall Evidence Assessment

**Strength of evidence for CA inhibitors reducing CSF production:** STRONG (IIHTT RCT, multiple mechanistic studies, decades of clinical use in IIH)

**Strength of evidence for topiramate as CA inhibitor:** MODERATE (review-level evidence, one small RCT showing comparable ICP reduction to acetazolamide; additional TRPV1/mast cell mechanism via Souza2024)

**Strength of evidence for Perrin Technique improving lymphatic drainage:** WEAK-MODERATE (one feasibility RCT in Long COVID with p=0.01; observational ME/CFS studies by Perrin with 86% diagnostic accuracy but no RCT; mechanistic theory sound but unvalidated)

**Strength of evidence for combined approach:** NO DIRECT EVIDENCE. No study has tested the combination. The hypothesis is speculative, extrapolating from two separate evidence bases.

**Critical safety concern:** Mitchell 2025 showed that both acetazolamide and topiramate **worsen fluid cognition** at ICP-reducing doses. ME/CFS patients already have impaired cognition. This is a serious barrier to the hypothesis.

**Relevant patient population identification:** Midtlien 2024 and Hashemizad 2025 describe precisely the phenotype (CTD+CCI+IIH+POTS+MCAS) where this approach would be considered, but prevalence is uncertain and selection bias is likely (tertiary referral centers).

## Research Gaps

1. **No study has tested combined CA inhibitor + lymphatic drainage approach** — the hypothesis remains speculative
2. **No CSF volume measurement before/after Perrin Technique** — mechanism unverified
3. **No ME/CFS-specific IIH trial with CA inhibitors** — all IIH evidence is from classical IIH population
4. **Long-term cognitive effects of CA inhibitors in ME/CFS** — unknown (Mitchell 2025 shows acute worsening; chronic adaptation unknown)
5. **Optimal dosing for ME/CFS subgroup** — classical IIH doses (up to 4g acetazolamide) likely excessive for this population given Midtlien's pressure hypersensitivity finding
