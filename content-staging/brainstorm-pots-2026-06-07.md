## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1.1: Stroke Volume → ETCO2 → Cerebral Hypoperfusion — The Missing Link Between POTS and Brain Fog
**Mechanism:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 demonstrates reduced stroke volume → lower end-tidal CO2 → impaired cerebral autoregulation via hypocapnic vasoconstriction. This establishes a direct mechanistic chain: the low-SV state characteristic of hypovolemic POTS/ME/CFS not only drives compensatory tachycardia but also actively constricts cerebral vessels through CO2-mediated vascular tone. The brain fog that patients report upon standing is not just from reduced cardiac output — it is amplified by hypocapnic cerebral vasoconstriction on top of reduced perfusion pressure. This is a multiplicative impairment: SV reduction → linear CBF drop × CO2-mediated vasoconstriction → supralinear cognitive symptom burden.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→ETCO2→CBF, Raj lab), Malik 2026 (structural brain changes), Seeley 2025 (SPECT hypoperfusion), van Campen 2020 (27% CBF reduction at 20° tilt)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFS+POTS patients, the magnitude of orthostatic cognitive impairment (psychomotor vigilance task, n-back) correlates more strongly with end-tidal CO2 change upon standing (r > 0.6) than with heart rate increment (r < 0.3) or MAP change alone. Capnometry-guided CO2 augmentation during standing (adding 1-2% CO2 to room air via face mask) normalizes orthostatic cognitive performance without affecting HR.

### H1.2: Tachycardia Treatment-Induced Worsening — HR Reduction as CBF Destabilization
**Mechanism:** If tachycardia is compensatory (Marchetta 2025, Chopra 2026) — defending cerebral perfusion when stroke volume drops — then pharmacologically reducing HR without first normalizing stroke volume creates a dangerous mismatch: CO = SV × HR, and if SV remains pathologically low and HR is artificially reduced, cardiac output falls, MAP drops, and cerebral perfusion worsens. This explains the clinical observation in Uppal 2026 (most patients report limited medication benefit) and Marchetta 2025 (symptom burden does not correlate with ivabradine HR reduction): patients with low-SV-dominant POTS may feel worse on beta-blockers/ivabradine because their compensatory mechanism has been pharmacologically amputated. The heterogeneity in treatment response corresponds to SV reserve: patients with preserved SV (hyperadrenergic POTS) benefit from HR reduction; patients with low SV (hypovolemic/neuropathic POTS) worsen.
**Evidence:** Marchetta 2025 (ivabradine symptom dissociation), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia argument), Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→CBF pathway), Uppal 2026 (patient-reported limited benefit)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** Pre-treatment SV (measured by impedance cardiography or echo during HUT) predicts treatment response to ivabradine/beta-blockers: patients in the lowest SV tertile show net worsening of orthostatic cognitive performance (Cogstate or similar) after 4 weeks of ivabradine; patients in the highest SV tertile show improvement. The SV-by-treatment interaction term is significant (p < 0.05).

### H1.3: Central Sensitization as Circuit Interpretation — Brainstem-Diencephalic Rerouting of Autonomic Afferents
**Mechanism:** Mathew 2026 (67% of POTS patients meet central sensitization criteria, Novak lab) reveals that POTS is not purely a peripheral autonomic disorder — there is a CNS amplification component. If central sensitization involves altered processing of autonomic afferent signals (from baroreceptors, chemoreceptors, nociceptors) at the NTS, parabrachial nucleus, and insular cortex, then the brain's interpretation of the body's hemodynamic state is systematically distorted. The brain may perceive "low blood flow" when MAP is adequate, driving disproportionate sympathetic activation and producing the hyperadrenergic state. This reframes POTS as a disorder of autonomic interoception — the brain's "body state map" is miscalibrated — rather than a pure efferent deficit. The SFN link (Ekman 2025, Azcue 2023) is not an alternative but a peripheral driver that can trigger or maintain this CNS amplification.
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (67% central sensitization prevalence, JAMA Netw Open, Novak lab), Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI symptoms), Chopra 2026 (compensatory viewpoint implies CNS interpretation), Novak 2022 (MCAS POTS CBF studies)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** POTS patients meeting central sensitization criteria (CSI score ≥ 40) show: (a) exaggerated cortical autonomic processing (fMRI BOLD in insula, ACC during graded HUT) compared to CSI- POTS patients matched for HR increment; (b) disproportionate HR response to identical orthostatic stimulus (head-up tilt at same angle) that correlates with CSI score (r > 0.5); (c) better response to CNS-directed interventions (duloxetine, tapentadol, cognitive retraining for interoception) than to purely peripheral interventions (midodrine, fludrocortisone).

### H1.4: The Diagnostic Mirage Operationalized — Hedge 2026 Test-Retest Unreliability as State-Dependent Physiology
**Mechanism:** Hedge 2026 (poor test-retest reliability in POTS tests, Vernino/Levine lab) is not merely a methodological nuisance — it reveals a biological truth. The supine-to-stand HR response varies day-to-day because it reflects the current state of a dynamic system: blood volume (hydration-dependent), autonomic tone (sleep-dependent, stress-modulated), inflammatory status (mast cell degranulation, cytokine milieu), and prior-day PEM (metabolic reserve). A patient who meets POTS criteria on Tuesday may not meet them on Friday because their system is in a different attractor neighborhood, not because the diagnosis is "wrong." This state-dependence is diagnostic information, not noise. The day-to-day variance itself could be a biomarker: patients with high test-retest variability may have structurally unstable autonomic circuits (high susceptibility to perturbation), while those with consistently positive tests have fixed structural impairment.
**Evidence:** Hedge 2026 (poor reliability), van Campen 2024 (two hemodynamic subtypes), existing HRV within-person fluctuation data (Aitken 2026)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** The coefficient of variation (CV) of standing HR increment across 5 repeated morning tests over 2 weeks discriminates POTS patients with high central sensitization (CSI ≥ 40, high CV) from those with SFN-confirmed neuropathic POTS (low CV) with AUC > 0.75. A composite "autonomic stability index" derived from resting HRV CV + standing HR increment CV + symptom VAS variability predicts treatment response: high-variability patients respond better to tVNS/neuromodulation; low-variability patients respond better to volume expansion/compression.

### H1.5: CBF as Unifying Hub — All POTS Symptoms Are Cerebral Perfusion Phenotypes
**Mechanism:** Malik 2026 (gray matter volume reduction, altered network connectivity), Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→ETCO2→CBF pathway), and Seeley 2025 (regional SPECT hypoperfusion) converge on CBF deficits as the central mediator of POTS symptoms. If chronic, repeated CBF drops during orthostatic stress cause cumulative brain injury (structural changes on MRI, gray matter atrophy), then POTS transitions from a benign autonomic syndrome to a progressive neurovascular disease. The regional specificity of SPECT deficits (Seeley 2025) determines the clinical phenotype: prefrontal hypoperfusion → executive dysfunction; hippocampal → memory complaints; thalamic → sensory amplification. This regional CBF mapping aligns with the central sensitization hypothesis (H1.3): hypoperfused brain regions develop compensatory hyperexcitability (sensitization) as oxygen-starved neurons upregulate synaptic gain. The distinction between "functional OI" (pediatric, reversible, H1.6) and "structural OI" (adult, cumulative brain changes) maps directly to CBF-trajectory: brief CBF drops → functional symptoms; chronic, repeated CBF drops → structural brain changes → irreversible cognitive impairment.
**Evidence:** Malik 2026, Miranda-Hurtado 2026, Seeley 2025, Novak 2022 (CBF review), van Campen 2020 (27% CBF reduction at 20° tilt)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** Regional CBF during HUT (measured by simultaneous transcranial Doppler + near-infrared spectroscopy or SPECT during tilt) correlates with domain-specific cognitive performance: prefrontal ΔCBF predicts Δexecutive function (Digit Symbol, Trails B); hippocampal ΔCBF predicts Δverbal memory; thalamic ΔCBF predicts Δpain sensitivity (QST). Structural MRI gray matter volume in POTS patients correlates with cumulative orthostatic exposure (years since onset × self-reported daily standing hours, r > 0.4). Longitudinal CBF-Cognition tracking (12-month follow-up) shows that patients whose CBF deficits worsen have measurable cognitive decline, while those who remain stable do not.

### H1.6: van Campen 2024 Subtypes as Disease Trajectory Waypoints — Hypovolemic→Hyperadrenergic Conversion
**Mechanism:** van Campen 2024 identifies two hemodynamic responses in ME/CFS+POTS: hypovolemic-like (low SV) and hyperadrenergic-like (high HR). Rather than being stable subtypes, these may represent sequential stages on a disease trajectory. Early ME/CFS: hypovolemia-predominant (low blood volume, low SV, modest tachycardia). Over time, chronic low SV → sustained baroreceptor unloading → chronic sympathetic overactivation → baroreflex resetting → progressive hyperadrenergic state. This conversion from "compensatory tachycardia" to "pathological sympathetic dominance" represents a transition from functional to structural OI (ch10 @spec:oi-distinction). The midodrine meta-analysis (Kwok 2026, RR 1.52 in pediatric) supports this: midodrine (alpha-1 agonist) should work best at the hypovolemic stage, less well once sympathetic tone is already dysregulated.
**Evidence:** van Campen 2024 (two subtypes), Chopra 2026 (compensatory argument), Errico 2025 (ANS aging), Kwok 2026 (midodrine meta-analysis), existing ANS aging hypothesis (ch10)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** In a longitudinal cohort of post-infectious ME/CFS (n ≥ 50, followed from onset for 24 months with quarterly HUT + impedance cardiography): (a) the proportion of patients with hyperadrenergic POTS (standing NE > 600 pg/mL) increases over time (baseline < 20% → 12 months > 35%); (b) the transition is marked by rising resting HR and falling resting HRV, detectable 2-3 months before the formal hyperadrenergic criterion is met; (c) patients who receive early aggressive volume expansion (fludrocortisone + salt + compression within 6 months of onset) have lower conversion rate to hyperadrenergic POTS than those on delayed/symptomatic-only treatment.

### H1.7: SFN→GI POTS Axis — The Ekman 2025 Finding as Enteric Autonomic Denervation
**Mechanism:** Ekman 2025 demonstrates SFN severity correlates with GI symptoms in POTS. This is mechanistically specific: the GI tract receives extensive small fiber innervation (enteric autonomic nerves regulating motility, secretion, and splanchnic vascular tone). SFN that affects mesenteric and enteric small fibers simultaneously impairs both splanchnic vasoconstriction (worsening orthostatic pooling) and GI motility/barrier function (producing nausea, bloating, constipation, permeability). This dual hit — cardiovascular (splanchnic pooling) + GI (enteric denervation) — creates the clinical presentation of patients who experience both orthostatic intolerance and post-prandial symptom exacerbation. The splanchnic-SFN-GI connection provides mechanistic grounding for the POGS construct (ch10 @spec:postural-gut-syndrome): if enteric small fibers are denervated, the gut mucosa is doubly vulnerable — impaired perfusion (due to dysregulated splanchnic vasoconstriction) combined with impaired epithelial maintenance (due to denervation of trophic fibers). The Ekman finding, combined with existing Azcue 2023 SFN data, suggests that GI-SFN is not secondary to dysmotility but a primary neuropathic manifestation of the same process causing peripheral SFN.
**Evidence:** Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI symptoms), Azcue 2023 (SFN in ME/CFS), ter Steege 2012 (splanchnic pooling), Novak 2022 (80% SFN in MCAS)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** (a) Punch skin biopsy IENFD at the calf will correlate with gastric emptying scintigraphy T½ (r > 0.4) in ME/CFS+POTS patients. (b) Splachnic-mesenteric Doppler ultrasound during HUT will show paradoxical vasodilation (impaired sympathetic vasoconstriction → perfusion drop despite continued pooling) in patients with low IENFD, with the degree of vasodilation correlating with GI symptom severity (GSRS score, r > 0.5). (c) Pilocarpine iontophoresis sweat response (sudomotor SFN) will predict midodrine response: patients with preserved sudomotor function respond to midodrine (intact alpha-1 signaling pathway in small fiber effector arm); patients with absent sudomotor function do not.

## 2. Research Directions

### R2.1: CBF-Titrated Ivabradine Trial — Finding the Safe HR Floor (PRIORITY #1)
**Design:** N=40 ME/CFS+POTS. Crossover: 4 weeks ivabradine 5 mg bid + 4 weeks placebo (or vice versa), with 2-week washout. Primary: change in orthostatic cognitive performance (Cogstate composite) during 10-min HUT with continuous transcranial Doppler CBF monitoring. Key stratification: baseline SV tertile by impedance cardiography. Predictions: patients in lowest SV tertile show CBF drop (≥15%) and cognitive worsening on ivabradine; patients in highest SV tertile show CBF improvement and cognitive benefit.
**Rationale:** Marchetta 2025 and Chopra 2026 show tachycardia is compensatory. But Chopra is review/opinion (low-medium certainty). This trial resolves whether HR reduction helps or harms — and in whom. The CBF measurement prevents the ethical issue of "treat first, check harm later" — CBF changes are detectable within the first dose (single-session crossover could precede the 4-week period).
**Evidence:** Marchetta 2025, Chopra 2026, Miranda-Hurtado 2026, Malik 2026
**Certainty:** 0.80 (high — this trial is needed regardless of outcome)
**Falsifiable:** Ivabradine does not reduce orthostatic CBF by > 10% in any SV tertile; or, ivabradine reduces CBF in low-SV tertile but cognitive performance tracks CBF change (r > 0.6); or, no SV-by-treatment interaction exists (all patients show similar CBF response).

### R2.2: CO2 Augmentation for Orthostatic Brain Fog — Capnometry-Guided Breathing
**Design:** N=25 ME/CFS+POTS + brain fog. Within-subject, 3 conditions during 15-min 70° HUT in randomized order: (a) normal breathing, (b) CO2-augmented breathing (add 2% CO2 to inspired air via sealed mask, maintaining ETCO2 at supine baseline), (c) paced breathing matched to rate in (b) without CO2. Primary: orthostatic cognitive performance (n-back, psychomotor vigilance) and CBF (transcranial Doppler MCA velocity). Secondary: symptom VAS (brain fog, dizziness, nausea).
**Rationale:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 shows SV→ETCO2→CBF pathway. CO2 is the most potent cerebral vasodilator. If hypocapnia drives orthostatic cognitive symptoms, normalizing CO2 during standing should improve cognition without affecting HR — a "cognitive orthostatic aid" independent of cardiovascular compensation. This is an experimental paradigm (adding CO2 to inspired air is not a clinical intervention), but it directly tests the mechanistic claim.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (ETCO2→CBF link), existing CBF autoregulation physiology, ch29 CBF model
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Falsifiable:** Normalizing ETCO2 during standing improves orthostatic cognitive performance by ≥ 15% (Cohen's d > 0.6) and MCA velocity by ≥ 10% compared to normal breathing. Paced breathing without CO2 does not produce equivalent improvement (ruling out breathing-pattern confound). The CO2 cognitive benefit is specific to patients with > 5 mmHg ETCO2 drop during standing (hypocapnic subtype).

### R2.3: Test-Retest Variability as Longitudinal Prognostic Biomarker
**Design:** N=100 ME/CFS with orthostatic symptoms (not all meeting formal POTS criteria). Baseline: 5 consecutive mornings of standardized active stand test (10 min supine, 10 min standing, continuous beat-to-beat HR/BP). Follow-up: 12 months with quarterly HUT + symptom questionnaires (COMPASS-31, CIS fatigue, SF-36). Primary: does HR increment CV at baseline predict change in COMPASS-31 at 12 months? Secondary: prediction of diagnostic category change (POTS→non-POTS transitions over time).
**Rationale:** Hedge 2026 demonstrates poor test-retest reliability. But this variability may carry prognostic signal. If some patients are "stable POTS" (low HR increment CV, fixed structural deficit) and others are "unstable dysautonomia" (high CV, dynamic system), their trajectories likely differ: stable → persistent disability, poor treatment response; unstable → fluctuating symptoms, may respond to neuromodulation, may spontaneously remit (as in pediatric POTS).
**Evidence:** Hedge 2026 (test-retest), Aitken 2026 (within-person HRV fluctuations predict symptoms), Ryabkova 2024 (ME/CFS HRV remains abnormal under stress unlike PCS)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** HR increment CV significantly predicts 12-month change in COMPASS-31, orthostatic symptom severity, or quality of life (p < 0.05), and the prediction is superior to a model using only baseline HR increment mean and age.

### R2.4: Central Sensitization × CBF Interaction — fMRI + HUT Paradigm
**Design:** N=40 ME/CFS+POTS (20 CSI+ by Mathew criteria, 20 CSI-). fMRI during graded HUT (0°→30°→50°→70°) with simultaneous CBF (ASL perfusion MRI), BOLD autonomic network (NTS, parabrachial nucleus, insula, ACC), and continuous HR/BP/ETCO2. CSI groups matched for HR increment, age, sex, and disease duration. Primary: insula BOLD response to orthostatic challenge in CSI+ vs CSI-.
**Rationale:** H1.3 (central sensitization as distorted interoception) and H1.5 (CBF as unifying hub) converge here. If central sensitization amplifies autonomic afferent processing, CSI+ patients should show exaggerated insula/ACC activation for the same degree of peripheral hemodynamic perturbation. If the CBF drop itself drives CNS amplification (hypoperfused neurons upregulate gain), then brain regions with the largest CBF deficit should show the largest BOLD response (paradoxical coupling). This study links Mathew 2026 (CSI prevalence) to Malik 2026/Seeley 2025 (CBF deficits) in a single paradigm.
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (CSI prevalence, Novak lab), Seeley 2025 (regional SPECT), Malik 2026 (structural changes), existing pain neuroimaging literature (central sensitization fMRI signatures)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** CSI+ patients show significantly greater insula and ACC BOLD activation during HUT compared to CSI- patients (p < 0.01, cluster-corrected), despite similar HR and MAP changes. Regional CBF (ASL) deficits correlate with regional BOLD activation (r > 0.4) — hypoperfused regions paradoxically show greater activation, consistent with compensatory gain upregulation.

### R2.5: Dermatome-Mapped SFN Sweat Testing for Autonomic Subtype Prediction
**Design:** N=60 ME/CFS+POTS. Comprehensive SFN assessment: punch skin biopsy (calf, thigh — IENFD + pilomotor + sudomotor innervation), quantitative sudomotor axon reflex testing (QSART at foot, distal leg, proximal leg, forearm — 4 sites), quantitative sensory testing (vibration, cold, warm, heat pain at foot + hand), gastric emptying scintigraphy, and standard HUT + impedance cardiography. Primary: does dermatomal sudomotor function pattern predict hemodynamic subtype (van Campen 2024 hypovolemic-like vs hyperadrenergic-like)?
**Rationale:** Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI symptoms) combined with Azcue 2023 (non-length-dependent SFN in ME/CFS) suggests that SFN distribution carries mechanistic information. Non-length-dependent SFN (proximal = distal involvement) suggests immune/inflammatory etiology vs length-dependent (distal > proximal) suggesting metabolic/degenerative. If autonomic SFN subtype maps to hemodynamic subtype, then a simple skin biopsy + QSART could guide treatment (neuropathic POTS → pyridostigmine; hypovolemic → fludrocortisone).
**Evidence:** Ekman 2025, Azcue 2023, Novak 2022 (80% SFN in MCAS, universal dysautonomia), van Campen 2024 (two hemodynamic subtypes)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** Non-length-dependent SFN pattern (calf IENFD ≈ thigh IENFD reduction) is associated with neuropathic POTS hemodynamics (impaired vasoconstriction, low SV, preserved NE response), while length-dependent SFN pattern is associated with hyperadrenergic POTS hemodynamics (normal SV, exaggerated NE response). The SFN-to-hemodynamic mapping has AUC > 0.70 for predicting treatment response to pyridostigmine vs fludrocortisone.

### R2.6: Combined CO2 + CBF + Cognitive Monitoring During Serial Standing — Ecological Orthostatic Stress
**Design:** N=30 ME/CFS+POTS. 2-hour protocol: alternating 15-min seated + 5-min standing periods (6 stand-seat cycles) with continuous NIRS prefrontal CBF, ETCO2 (nasal cannula), beat-to-beat BP (Finapres), and brief cognitive tests during each standing period. This creates an "office-day" orthostatic stress rather than a single HUT. Primary: trajectory of CBF and cognitive performance across repeated standing episodes — is there cumulative deterioration (each stand worse than the last) or adaptation (first stand worst, subsequent improve)?
**Rationale:** Real-life orthostatic stress involves repeated stand-sit transitions, not one 10-minute stand. ME/CFS+POTS patients often report progressive deterioration through the day (morning mostly okay, afternoon unable to stand). Repeated standing may reveal cumulative CBF decompensation not captured by single HUT — making it a better predictor of daily function.
**Evidence:** Malik 2026, Miranda-Hurtado 2026, Seeley 2025, Uppal 2026 (qualitative patient reports of cumulative decline), existing ME/CFS 2-day CPET literature (cumulative metabolic decline)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** Cognitive performance (n-back accuracy) and prefrontal CBF decline progressively across the 6 standing episodes (linear trend p < 0.05), with greater decline in patients reporting "afternoon orthostatic crash" on symptom diaries than those who do not. Single-stand HUT CBF does not predict cumulative decline (r < 0.3), making serial-standing measurement necessary for individualized activity guidance.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### DR3.1: Acetazolamide for Cerebral CO2 Retention — Reverse the Hypocapnic Vasoconstriction
**Mechanism:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→ETCO2→CBF pathway) implies that raising ETCO2 during standing could restore cerebral perfusion without altering HR or MAP. Acetazolamide, a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor, causes metabolic acidosis → compensatory hyperventilation → elevated CO2 set-point. In the POTS context, the desired effect is: acetazolamide raises tissue CO2 at the level of cerebral vessels → cerebral vasodilation → improved CBF during orthostatic stress. This is distinct from the hypocapnia-induced vasoconstriction mechanism: acetazolamide increases brain tissue CO2 even if systemic ETCO2 drops during standing. The drug is used for altitude sickness (improves cerebral oxygenation under hypoxic/ischemic conditions) — conceptually analogous to orthostatic cerebral hypoperfusion. Known safety profile. Onset: hours. Off-target: metabolic acidosis (may worsen fatigue), diuresis (may worsen hypovolemia → counterproductive).
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (CO2 pathway), acetazolamide cerebral vasodilation literature (altitude medicine, sleep apnea), Malik 2026/Seeley 2025 (CBF deficits)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** Single-dose acetazolamide 250 mg in ME/CFS+POTS patients increases orthostatic MCA velocity by ≥ 15% (transcranial Doppler during HUT) and improves cognitive performance by ≥ 10% (n-back accuracy) vs placebo. The benefit is independent of any HR or MAP change (acetazolamide's diuretic effect could reduce SV → net zero or negative HR effect; the CBF benefit must arise from the CO2 mechanism).

### DR3.2: Ivabradine + Midodrine Combination — HR Limitation + SV Support
**Mechanism:** Marchetta 2025 and Chopra 2026 argue that tachycardia is compensatory. If HR reduction is potentially harmful in low-SV patients (H1.2), the solution is not to avoid HR reduction but to simultaneously support SV. Midodrine (alpha-1 agonist) increases venous return, central blood volume, and stroke volume. Combined with ivabradine (HR reduction), the pairing could reduce tachycardia without compromising cardiac output because SV is augmented: CO = SV↑ × HR↓ ≈ CO preserved. The Kwok 2026 meta-analysis (midodrine RR 1.52 for pediatric POTS symptom response) suggests midodrine monotherapy has some efficacy; combination with ivabradine is untested but mechanistically rational.
**Evidence:** Marchetta 2025 (ivabradine symptom dissociation), Chopra 2026 (compensatory argument), Kwok 2026 (midodrine meta-analysis, RR 1.52), Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→CBF), existing cardiovascular physiology
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** In a crossover trial (ivabradine monotherapy vs ivabradine+midodrine vs midodrine monotherapy vs placebo, each 4 weeks, N=30 ME/CFS+POTS), the combination arm shows: (a) HR reduction comparable to ivabradine monotherapy — midodrine does not interfere; (b) CBF during standing (transcranial Doppler) significantly higher than ivabradine monotherapy (p < 0.05); (c) orthostatic symptom score (COMPASS-31 orthostatic subscale) significantly lower than either monotherapy.

### DR3.3: Guanfacine for Central Sensitization + Prefrontal CBF — Dual Mechanism
**Mechanism:** Guanfacine, an alpha-2A adrenergic agonist, has two relevant mechanisms for POTS+ME/CFS. (1) Central: strengthens prefrontal cortical network connectivity by reducing cAMP-mediated opening of HCN channels in dendritic spines (improving signal-to-noise in PFC circuits). In ME/CFS POTS patients with CSF catecholamine deficiency (Walitt 2024), guanfacine could improve prefrontal function without the sympathetic activation of stimulants. (2) Peripheral: alpha-2 agonism reduces central sympathetic outflow (clonidine-like), which could benefit hyperadrenergic POTS. The Mathew 2026 finding (67% central sensitization) suggests alpha-2 agonists could address both the cognitive dysfunction (brain fog) and the autonomic dysregulation (sympathetic overdrive) through a single receptor mechanism. Guanfacine is more selective for alpha-2A (prefrontal) than clonidine (which hits alpha-2B/C → more BP effects). Low dose: 0.5-1 mg at bedtime (sedation is initial side effect that attenuates).
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (central sensitization), Walitt 2024 (CSF catecholamine deficiency), guanfacine PFC physiology (Arnsten lab, Yale), existing use in Long COVID brain fog (Fesharaki-Zadeh 2023 case series)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** 4-week low-dose guanfacine (0.5-1 mg qhs) in ME/CFS+POTS with brain fog: (a) improves orthostatic cognitive performance (Digit Symbol, Trails B) by ≥ 20% vs placebo; (b) reduces standing HR by ≥ 8 bpm in hyperadrenergic subtype but not in hypovolemic subtype; (c) reduces CSI score by ≥ 10 points if baseline CSI > 40 (central sensitization responders).

### DR3.4: Droxidopa + Pyridostigmine — Sympathetic Enhancement Without Tachycardia Amplification
**Mechanism:** Droxidopa (norepinephrine prodrug) addresses CSF catecholamine deficiency (Walitt 2024) while pyridostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor) enhances parasympathetic ganglionic transmission. The combination provides balanced autonomic support rather than unopposed sympathetic drive: droxidopa→α1 vasoconstriction→improved venous return→higher SV; pyridostigmine→enhanced parasympathetic→buffered HR response (preventing excessive tachycardia despite improved vascular tone). This avoids the problem of pure midodrine (α1 agonist alone can cause reflex bradycardia or supine hypertension) and pure ivabradine (HR reduction without SV support). The pyridostigmine component may be particularly effective for the neuropathic POTS subtype (Ekman 2025 SFN→GI, where SFN impairs parasympathetic as well as sympathetic fibers — pyridostigmine enhances remaining cholinergic transmission).
**Evidence:** Kwok 2026 (midodrine meta-analysis, alpha-1 efficacy), Walitt 2024 (CSF catecholamine deficiency), Raj 2005 (pyridostigmine for POTS), Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI, suggests parasympathetic involvement)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** Droxidopa (100-200 mg tid) + pyridostigmine (30-60 mg tid) in ME/CFS+POTS: (a) increases standing SV (impedance cardiography) by ≥ 15% vs droxidopa monotherapy; (b) produces less standing HR increase than droxidopa monotherapy (pyridostigmine buffers the sympathetic HR effect); (c) improves GI symptom score (GSRS) ≥ 20% from baseline (pyridostigmine prokinetic effect + SFN enteric benefit).

### DR3.5: Tapentadol for POTS Pain + Central Sensitization — Dual NRI + MOR Agonism
**Mechanism:** Tapentadol uniquely combines norepinephrine reuptake inhibition (NRI — increases spinal NE, enhancing descending noradrenergic pain inhibition) with mu-opioid receptor agonism (MOR). For POTS patients with comorbid chronic pain and central sensitization (Mathew 2026: 67% of POTS), this dual mechanism offers: (a) spinal pain relief from NRI (addresses central sensitization by augmenting the same NE pathway that is deficient — CSF catecholamine deficit from Walitt 2024 may extend to spinal noradrenergic tone), (b) peripheral pain relief from MOR, (c) α1-adrenergic vasoconstriction from NE reuptake inhibition (similar mechanism to midodrine, providing orthostatic benefit). The NRI component may simultaneously improve orthostatic tolerance (↑ venous return) and pain (↓ central sensitization) through a single pharmacodynamic action. Contrast with duloxetine (SNRI — also increases NE but serotonin component may cause GI side effects, nausea, and initial anxiety that is poorly tolerated in POTS).
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (central sensitization, Novak lab), Walitt 2024 (CSF catecholamine deficiency), tapentadol dual mechanism pharmacology, Ekman 2025 (SFN pain in POTS)
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** 8-week tapentadol (50-100 mg bid) in ME/CFS+POTS+pain (CSI ≥ 40): (a) reduces pain VAS by ≥ 30% vs placebo; (b) reduces CSI score by ≥ 15 points (central sensitization reversal); (c) does not worsen orthostatic tolerance (standing MAP stable or improved — tapentadol NRI effect may be orthostatically beneficial).

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### SU4.1: Sodium Bicarbonate Pre-Load for Hypocapnia Prevention
**Mechanism:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026: SV↓→ETCO2↓→CBF↓. Sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) is an alkalinizing agent — it raises blood pH → suppresses ventilatory drive → increases CO2 retention → elevates baseline ETCO2. Pre-loading with NaHCO3 (3-5 g in water, 30 min before standing) could elevate ETCO2 sufficiently to prevent the hypocapnic cerebral vasoconstriction during standing, preserving CBF without affecting HR or SV. This is a nutritional CO2-retention strategy — cruder than acetazolamide (DR3.1) but safer, faster-acting, and available OTC. Risk: excessive alkalosis (headache, muscle twitching), sodium load (worsens hypertension if present), GI discomfort. Dose must be titrated.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (CO2 pathway), established acid-base physiology (NaHCO3→pH→ventilatory drive→CO2), athletic performance literature (NaHCO3 loading improves high-intensity performance via buffering — CBF pathway is analogous)
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** Single-dose NaHCO3 (0.3 g/kg, ~20 g for 70 kg person) 60 min before HUT in ME/CFS+POTS: (a) raises resting ETCO2 by ≥ 3 mmHg; (b) attenuates orthostatic ETCO2 drop by ≥ 50%; (c) reduces orthostatic MCA velocity drop by ≥ 20% vs placebo (NaCl matched for sodium load to control for volume effect). Side-effect rate > 30% (GI) would limit feasibility.

### SU4.2: Inhaled/Sublingual CO2 Microbolus — On-Demand Cognitive Orthostatic Rescue
**Mechanism:** Rather than systemic CO2 retention (acetazolamide, NaHCO3), a targeted CO2 delivery to the CNS circulation could provide on-demand cerebral vasodilation at the moment of orthostatic stress. Sublingual or inhaled CO2 (in clinical concentrations, 5-7%) produces rapid (seconds) cerebral vasodilation without affecting systemic pH or requiring pre-loading. This is a "cognitive rescue inhaler" for orthostatic brain fog episodes — analogous to how asthma patients use bronchodilator inhalers for bronchoconstriction. Practical: small CO2 cartridge device (similar to whipped cream charger) delivering 1-2 second burst of 7% CO2/93% O2 mixture through mouthpiece. Cerebral vasodilation within 5-10 seconds of inhalation. Contraindicated in panic disorder (CO2 is a panicogen), severe COPD (CO2 retention). Must be extremely low-dose to avoid dyspnea/anxiety.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (CO2 pathway), CO2 cerebral vasodilation physiology (well-established), clinical CO2 challenge literature (panic research — provides dosing safety data)
**Certainty:** 0.25
**Falsifiable:** Single inhalation of 7% CO2/93% O2 (2-second breath hold) during 10-min 70° HUT in ME/CFS+POTS: (a) increases MCA velocity by ≥ 15% within 30 seconds (transcranial Doppler), sustained for ≥ 2 minutes; (b) improves cognitive performance (brief digit-symbol substitution during the MCA velocity peak epoch) vs sham inhalation (room air); (c) CBF benefit is independent of MAP or HR change (confirming CO2 mechanism, not cardiac output effect).

### SU4.3: Thiamine (B1) + Alpha-Lipoic Acid for Small Fiber Neuropathy in POTS
**Mechanism:** Ekman 2025 demonstrates SFN correlates with GI symptoms in POTS; Azcue 2023 documents SFN in ME/CFS. Benfotiamine (fat-soluble thiamine, B1) and alpha-lipoic acid (ALA) are established interventions for diabetic peripheral neuropathy with meta-analytic evidence of benefit (SMD ~0.5-0.7 for pain, sensory improvement). Benfotiamine activates transketolase → diverts glycolytic intermediates away from AGE/RAGE → reduces oxidative stress in small fibers. ALA is a mitochondrial antioxidant that improves nerve conduction velocity and reduces neuropathic pain. Both cross the blood-nerve barrier (benfotiamine better than thiamine HCl; ALA is amphipathic). For POTS+SFN (neuropathic subtype), this neuropathy-directed approach could improve both peripheral sensory symptoms (pain, numbness) and autonomic function (vasoconstriction, sweating) if the nerve damage is metabolic/oxidative rather than autoimmune. Specific to the neuropathic POTS subtype, not hyperadrenergic or hypovolemic alone.
**Evidence:** Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI), Azcue 2023 (SFN ME/CFS), benfotiamine diabetic neuropathy meta-analyses (Stracke 2008, Haupt 2005), ALA neuropathy meta-analyses (Ziegler 2004, Mijnhout 2012)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** 12-week benfotiamine (300 mg bid) + ALA (600 mg qd) in ME/CFS+POTS with SFN (biopsy-confirmed IENFD reduction): (a) improves QST cold detection threshold at foot by ≥ 2°C; (b) improves orthostatic SV change (HUT impedance cardiography) by ≥ 10% from baseline (suggesting improved vasomotor fiber function); (c) reduces neuropathic pain (NPSI) by ≥ 30% vs placebo.

### SU4.4: Magnesium Glycinate/L-Threonate for CNS Sensitization + Vascular Tone in POTS
**Mechanism:** Mathew 2026 (67% central sensitization in POTS, Novak lab) suggests NMDA receptor hyperactivity in dorsal horn and possibly brainstem autonomic nuclei. Magnesium is the endogenous NMDA receptor channel blocker — Mg2+ blocks the NMDA receptor pore in a voltage-dependent manner. In central sensitization, Mg2+ block may be insufficient due to chronic depolarization. High-dose bioavailable magnesium (glycinate for absorption, L-threonate for CNS penetration) could: (1) enhance NMDA receptor block in sensitized spinal/brainstem circuits → reduce central sensitization, (2) provide vasorelaxation at vascular smooth muscle (magnesium is a physiological calcium channel blocker → reduces vascular tone), potentially beneficial for hyperadrenergic POTS with excessive vasoconstriction, (3) improve sleep quality (magnesium-GABAergic interaction) → secondary orthostatic benefit (better sleep = better morning autonomic function). L-threonate specifically achieves higher CNS levels than other magnesium salts (Slutsky 2010, Neuron). Dose: 200-400 mg elemental Mg as glycinate + 1000-2000 mg Mg L-threonate daily.
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (CSI prevalence, Novak lab), Mg NMDA receptor pharmacology (well-established), Slutsky 2010 (Mg L-threonate CNS levels, Neuron), magnesium POTS anecdotal/clinical use (common in patient community)
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** 8-week magnesium glycinate + L-threonate in ME/CFS+POTS with CSI ≥ 40: (a) reduces CSI score by ≥ 10 points vs placebo; (b) reduces standing HR by ≥ 5 bpm (vascular smooth muscle relaxation reduces compensatory HR requirement in hyperadrenergic subtype); (c) improves quantitative sensory testing (pressure pain threshold, temporal summation — both measures of central sensitization) by ≥ 20% vs placebo.

### SU4.5: Coenzyme Q10 (Ubiquinol) + L-Carnitine for Cardiac Energetics in Low-SV POTS
**Mechanism:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026: SV reduction is the proximal driver of the hypocapnia-CBF cascade. Improving cardiac energetics could directly improve SV by increasing myocardial contractile reserve. CoQ10 (ubiquinol) is the electron carrier between Complex I/II and Complex III in the mitochondrial ETC — essential for cardiac ATP production. L-carnitine transports long-chain fatty acids into mitochondria for beta-oxidation — the heart derives 60-70% of its ATP from fatty acid oxidation. The combination of ubiquinol + L-carnitine has precedent in heart failure (Q-SYMBIO trial, Mortensen 2014 JACC HF — CoQ10 reduced cardiovascular mortality). In POTS, the cardiac energetic deficit may be subclinical (normal EF on echo) but functionally significant during orthostatic stress when cardiac work demand increases. SV augmentation via improved mitochondrial ATP availability is a direct approach to the root cause (low SV) rather than compensatory HR reduction. Specific to hypovolemic/low-SV POTS subtype — minimal benefit for hyperadrenergic with normal SV.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→CBF), Mortensen 2014 (CoQ10 heart failure mortality reduction), existing CoQ10/carnitine ME/CFS literature (Castro-Marrero 2021 carnitine, Fukuda 2016 CoQ10 — modest fatigue benefit)
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** 12-week ubiquinol (200 mg bid) + L-carnitine (1000 mg bid) in ME/CFS+POTS with low SV (≤ 60 mL echo SV at rest): (a) increases resting SV by ≥ 10% (echo); (b) attenuates SV drop during HUT by ≥ 15% vs placebo; (c) reduces standing HR increment by ≥ 8 bpm (SV improvement → less compensatory HR demand); (d) improves orthostatic cognitive performance by ≥ 15% (Digit Symbol).

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### NP5.1: Capnometry Biofeedback for Orthostatic CO2 Stabilization
**Mechanism:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026: SV↓→ETCO2↓→CBF↓. If patients learn to monitor and voluntarily stabilize their ETCO2 during standing through paced, slightly hypoventilatory breathing (slower, deeper breaths that retain CO2), they could partially compensate for the SV-driven ETCO2 drop. Capnometry biofeedback (nasal cannula ETCO2 displayed on smartphone) provides real-time guidance: "Your CO2 is dropping — slow your breathing, take slightly deeper breaths." This trains an autonomic-bypass strategy: the brainstem-driven hypocapnia from low SV is overridden by volitional respiratory control. Feasible in patients with sufficient cognitive capacity (mild-moderate severity). This is the non-pharmacological counterpart to R2.2 (CO2 augmentation) and DR3.1 (acetazolamide). The technique is already used in anxiety/panic (capnometry-assisted breathing for hyperventilation) — CO2 biofeedback is established for respiratory dysregulation, just never applied to orthostatic hypocapnia.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (ETCO2→CBF), capnometry biofeedback in anxiety/panic (Meuret 2008), respiratory physiology (voluntary hypoventilation → CO2 retention)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** 4-week daily capnometry biofeedback training (10 min/day, seated + standing practice) in ME/CFS+POTS: (a) patients can voluntarily reduce their orthostatic ETCO2 drop by ≥ 50% (from -8 mmHg to -4 mmHg during 5-min stand); (b) reduction in ETCO2 drop correlates with reduction in orthostatic dizziness VAS (r > 0.5); (c) training effect persists at 4-week follow-up without biofeedback device (learned skill retention).

### NP5.2: CO2-Enriched Leg/Abdominal Compression — Dual Mechanical + Chemical CBF Support
**Mechanism:** Existing: ter Steege 2012 demonstrates abdominal compression reduces splanchnic pooling and improves orthostatic tolerance. This is mechanical: reducing venous pooling → higher SV → better cardiac output. Enhancement: incorporate mild CO2-enrichment into compression garments (CO2 gas is absorbed transdermally through skin, used in Japanese "CO2 bath" spa therapy for peripheral vasodilation). CO2 absorbed through the skin of compressed legs/abdomen → local vasodilation at the microvascular level → improved tissue perfusion in compressed regions → reduced ischemia signal → reduced sympathetic activation. The dual effect: (a) mechanical: compression → reduced venous pooling → SV support, (b) chemical: transdermal CO2 → local vasodilation → improved tissue oxygenation → parasympathetic shift. This is speculative: transdermal CO2 absorption amount and systemic effect are uncertain. Japanese CO2 bathing literature shows improved peripheral circulation in PAD and skin ulcer healing — mechanism is local, not systemic. But combined with compression, the localized vasodilation might complement the mechanical venous return benefit.
**Evidence:** ter Steege 2012 (compression efficacy), transdermal CO2 absorption literature (Japanese CO2 bathing, Brandi 2010, Hartmann 1997), Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (CO2→CBF)
**Certainty:** 0.20
**Falsifiable:** Abdominal compression (30-40 mmHg) + transdermal CO2 (CO2-containing gel lined in compression garment) in ME/CFS+POTS during HUT: increases MCA velocity by ≥ 10% beyond the mechanical compression-alone benefit (the incremental CO2 transdermal effect). Orthostatic symptom VAS is significantly lower for CO2-enhanced compression vs standard compression (p < 0.05). Transcutaneous CO2 partial pressure at abdominal skin under the garment is ≥ 10 mmHg above baseline (confirming transdermal absorption).

### NP5.3: Seated Calf Muscle Pump Training — Orthostatic Preconditioning Without Full Standing
**Mechanism:** The skeletal muscle pump (calf, thigh, gluteal) is the primary mechanism returning venous blood from the lower extremities during standing. In severe ME/CFS+POTS, full standing may be impossible or severely symptom-limiting, precluding "exercise" or "standing practice." Seated calf pump training — rhythmic plantarflexion/dorsiflexion while seated with legs dependent — activates the muscle pump without the full orthostatic challenge of standing. This provides partial orthostatic conditioning: muscle pump activation → increased venous return → increased SV → baroreceptor loading → reduced sympathetic outflow. Repeated seated calf pump sessions (5 min, 3x/day, seated with backrest) could: (a) improve venous return efficiency (the muscle pump "learns" through repetition — enhanced neuromuscular coordination), (b) provide baroreflex loading without full orthostatic stress (desensitizing the baroreflex to standing through graded conditioning), (c) prevent orthostatic deconditioning in patients who cannot stand. Analogous to the Dallas/Leeds POTS protocol (recumbent exercise) — this is an even lower-intensity entry point for patients who cannot tolerate recumbent cycling.
**Evidence:** Existing skeletal muscle pump physiology (Miller 2005, van Lieshout 2001), Dallas protocol (Fu 2010, Fu 2018 — recumbent exercise for POTS), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia argument — improving SV should reduce HR requirement)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** 4-week seated calf pump training (5 min, 3x/day) in severe ME/CFS+POTS: (a) reduces standing HR increment by ≥ 5 bpm at 4 weeks vs control (usual care + seated rest without calf pump); (b) does not trigger PEM in ≥ 80% of sessions (patient diary); (c) increases calf muscle pump ejection fraction (plethysmography) by ≥ 15% from baseline (training effect on muscle pump efficiency).

### NP5.4: Vertical Water Immersion (Head-Out) for Zero-Gravity Orthostatic Training
**Mechanism:** Water immersion to the neck provides: (a) hydrostatic compression of lower body and abdomen (equivalent to full-body compression garment) — external pressure counteracts gravitational pooling, (b) central blood volume expansion (300-700 mL shift from periphery to thorax) — increasing cardiac preload and SV, (c) reduced orthostatic stress (buoyancy reduces muscle work for postural maintenance). This creates a "zero-gravity orthostatic environment" where patients can practice being upright without the full hemodynamic penalty. Water temperature: thermoneutral (34-35°C) to avoid cold-induced vasoconstriction or heat-induced vasodilation. Progression: pool-based upright posture (initially floating with support → standing on pool floor → pool walking) as a bridge between supine bedrest and standing on land. Already used in some POTS rehabilitation programs (Fu lab, Dallas protocol — pool training). For severe ME/CFS+POTS patients who are bedbound except for essential transfers, this provides a graded orthostatic exposure that may prevent progression from functional to structural OI (ch10 @spec:oi-distinction).
**Evidence:** Water immersion physiology (Arborelius 1972, Epstein 1992 — classical studies), Dallas protocol (Fu 2010, 2018), ter Steege 2012 (compression benefit, analogous to water pressure), existing clinical use in POTS rehabilitation
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** 4-week water immersion orthostatic training (3x/week, 15-20 min per session, thermoneutral 34-35°C) in severe ME/CFS+POTS (bedbound > 18h/day): (a) increases orthostatic tolerance (time to presyncope during HUT) by ≥ 5 minutes at week 4 vs baseline; (b) does not increase PEM frequency vs baseline (patient diary — activity must stay within energy envelope); (c) improves QOL (SF-36 physical function subscale) by ≥ 5 points vs usual care control.

### NP5.5: Auditory Biofeedback of Cardiovascular State — Heart Sound Sonification for Standing Tolerance
**Mechanism:** Mathew 2026 (67% central sensitization = altered interoception) suggests POTS patients may have impaired ability to perceive their own cardiovascular state. Sonification — converting physiological signals into auditory feedback — could provide an explicit "body state awareness" channel that bypasses the distorted interoceptive pathways. Real-time sonification of HR, SV, or CBF (derived from wearable sensors) as changing pitch/tempo/timbre: the patient hears their cardiovascular response to standing in real time. This could: (a) improve interoceptive accuracy (patients learn to calibrate their internal sensation against the external audio feedback), (b) enable paced standing (standing for as long as heart rate or CBF remains within a safe "green zone" indicated by pleasant audio; audio changes to warn when approaching decompensation), (c) reduce anxiety around standing (the audio feedback provides certainty — "I know exactly what my heart is doing" — reducing the hypervigilance that amplifies orthostatic symptoms). Precedent: heart rate sonification in exercise training (athletes use audio HR feedback for pacing), biofeedback for anxiety disorders (HRV biofeedback with visual display). Sonification has advantages over visual display for patients with brain fog (auditory processing may remain functional when visual/attentional processing is impaired).
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (central sensitization, interoceptive distortion), biofeedback/neurofeedback literature, sonification for physiological monitoring (Dubus 2012, Pauletto 2012), HRV biofeedback in POTS (limited evidence)
**Certainty:** 0.25
**Falsifiable:** Single-session sonification feedback during 10-min stand in ME/CFS+POTS: (a) increases standing duration by ≥ 30% vs no feedback (patient stands until symptoms or HR threshold rather than predetermined time — feedback extends tolerance); (b) reduces orthostatic anxiety VAS by ≥ 2 points (0-10 scale) vs no feedback; (c) patients report the sonification as "helpful" or "very helpful" (5+ on a 7-point Likert scale) in ≥ 70% of cases.

## 6. Combinations + Access

### C6.1: Tiered POTS Protocol by Severity — Standing Ability as Triage Criterion
**Framework:** Uppal 2026 (qualitative patient experiences, Raj lab) reveals that most patients try multiple medications with limited benefit and value non-pharmacological strategies. The current "try fludrocortisone, then midodrine, then ivabradine" approach fails because it does not match mechanism to patient severity. A tiered protocol based on standing tolerance (max standing time before presyncope):

| Tier | Standing tolerance | Dominant mechanism | Intervention |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Tier 1 (bedbound) | < 30 sec | Severe hypovolemia + splanchnic pooling + SFN | Compression (abdominal + leg, donned in supine) + salt/fluid + seated calf pump → fludrocortisone sequential → water immersion as tolerated |
| Tier 2 (housebound) | 30 sec - 5 min | Hypovolemic or neuropathic POTS | Compression + salt/fluid + capnometry biofeedback + midodrine/droxidopa (α1 support) + magnesium gly+threonate (CNS sensitization) |
| Tier 3 (limited community) | 5-15 min | Neuropathic or mixed POTS | Compression + salt/fluid + seated calf pump + ivabradine (if SV preserved) or ivabradine+midodrine (if SV low) + benfotiamine+ALA (SFN) |
| Tier 4 (mild) | > 15 min | Hyperadrenergic POTS or functional OI | Salt/fluid + exercise conditioning (Dallas protocol) + propranolol/ivabradine + magnesium + CoQ10/L-carnitine |

**Rationale:** Each tier has different hemodynamic priorities. Severity-level triage prevents the "ivabradine for bedbound POTS" error (H1.2: if SV is critically low, HR reduction without SV support reduces CBF) and the "compression alone for mild POTS" inefficiency (benign treatment but insufficient). The protocol is testable in a stepped-wedge cluster trial: 4 clinics randomize to tiered protocol vs usual sequential care, with primary outcome of COMPASS-31 at 6 months.
**Evidence:** van Campen 2024 (two hemodynamic subtypes), van Campen 2020 (27% CBF reduction at 20° tilt in severe), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia), Uppal 2026 (patient-valued non-pharm strategies), Kwok 2026 (midodrine evidence), Marchetta 2025 (ivabradine symptom dissociation)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** Tiered protocol produces significantly greater improvement in COMPASS-31 orthostatic subscale at 6 months vs usual sequential care (Cohen's d > 0.4). Tier 1 patients show CBF improvement (transcranial Doppler during HUT) — specifically, they do not show the worsening that ivabradine monotherapy would produce in this subgroup.

### C6.2: Fludrocortisone + Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS) Combination — Structured Plasma Volume Expansion
**Mechanism:** Hypovolemia is documented in ME/CFS (Streeten 1998: 93.8% of females had reduced RBC mass). Fludrocortisone increases sodium retention → plasma volume expansion. ORS (glucose + sodium in WHO formulation ratio) activates the sodium-glucose co-transporter in the small intestine (SGLT1) → active sodium and water absorption → more effective than water or salt tablets alone for volume expansion. Combined: fludrocortisone → renal Na retention (prevents excretion), ORS → intestinal Na absorption (maximizes intake). The glucose in ORS is crucial — it is the co-transporter driver; NaCl tablets alone lack this mechanism. This is a low-tech, low-cost intervention accessible across all healthcare settings. The combination is already used in cholera/dehydration and postural hypotension protocols (ORS for neurogenic OH); it has not been systematically tested in POTS+ME/CFS.
**Evidence:** Streeten 1998 (blood volume reduction in ME/CFS), ORS physiology (WHO standardized formulation, SGLT1 mechanism), fludrocortisone pharmacology, existing anecdotal clinical use
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** 4-week fludrocortisone (0.1 mg qd) + ORS (2-3 L/day, WHO formula) vs fludrocortisone + water (matched volume, 2-3 L/day) in ME/CFS+POTS with hypovolemia (low plasma volume by dye dilution): (a) ORS+fludrocortisone increases plasma volume by ≥ 10% vs water+fludrocortisone (p < 0.05); (b) ORS+fludrocortisone reduces standing HR by ≥ 8 bpm vs water+fludrocortisone; (c) 24h urine volume is lower in ORS arm (confirming superior fluid retention — water alone is more rapidly excreted).

### C6.3: Ivabradine + Midodrine + Compression — The "Three-Pillar" SV-Sparing Tachycardia Management
**Mechanism:** Combining three mechanisms to reduce tachycardia without compromising SV: (1) midodrine → α1-mediated venoconstriction → increased venous return → ↑ SV → the physiological driver of tachycardia is reduced (less need to compensate for low SV); (2) compression → mechanical venous return support → additive to midodrine, reduces splanchnic+leg pooling; (3) ivabradine → reduces HR in patients whose tachycardia persists despite improved SV (hyperadrenergic component, or incomplete SV restoration). The triple combination aims to shift the patient from "low SV + compensatory tachycardia" to "improved SV + normalized HR" — addressing the root cause (SV) while preventing the symptom (tachycardia). This contrasts with ivabradine monotherapy (HR reduced but root cause untreated → CBF compromise, H1.2). The combination is sequential: start compression → add midodrine → add ivabradine only if HR remains elevated despite SV normalization confirmed by impedance cardiography or echo.
**Evidence:** Kwok 2026 (midodrine RR 1.52), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia argument), Marchetta 2025 (ivabradine symptom dissociation → SV matters), ter Steege 2012 (compression efficacy), Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→CBF pathway), H1.2 (HR reduction without SV support → worsening)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** Triple combination (compression 30-40 mmHg + midodrine 5 mg tid + ivabradine 5 mg bid) in ME/CFS+POTS with low SV: (a) reduces standing HR by ≥ 15 bpm while maintaining or increasing CBF (MCA velocity on transcranial Doppler) — the key test: HR reduction without CBF compromise; (b) improves orthostatic cognitive performance by ≥ 25% vs ivabradine monotherapy; (c) improves patient global impression of change (PGIC "much improved" or "very much improved") in ≥ 60% of patients.

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### M7.1: CO2-Mediated CBF Coupling ODE — Adding ETCO2 Variable to Existing CBF Model
**Mechanism:** The existing CBF autoregulation model (ch29 @sec:cbf-autoregulation, Equation @eq:cbf-model) captures CBF as a function of CPP (= MAP - ICP) and CVR, with CVR dynamics including CO2 reactivity g(CO2)(PaCO2) as a static function. The Miranda-Hurtado 2026 finding requires making PaCO2 an explicit dynamic variable linked to SV: low SV → reduced pulmonary perfusion → reduced CO2 exchange → falling ETCO2 (PaCO2 proxy). The extended model: add d(PaCO2)/dt = k_prod · (1 - SV/SV_healthy) - k_clear · PaCO2, where k_prod captures CO2 production rate and k_clear is ventilatory clearance. CVR dynamic equation already has g(CO2)(PaCO2); this extension makes PaCO2 endogenous to the cardiovascular subsystem rather than an exogenous parameter. The model would predict: (a) the SV threshold below which hypocapnic cerebral vasoconstriction outweighs any autoregulatory compensation, (b) the CO2 augmentation dose required to normalize CBF at a given SV deficit (informing R2.2 and DR3.1), (c) the interaction between CO2 set-point and MAP — patients with both low SV (hypocapnia) and low MAP (autonomic impairment) have supralinear CBF deficit (multiplicative interaction).
**Existing model context:** ch29 CBF model (CBF = CPP/CVR, dCVR/dt with g(CO2) term), ch29 ANS model (S, V, MAP, HR, SV). Extension: add PaCO2(t) state variable with SV-dependent production + ventilatory clearance. Coupling: PaCO2 → g(CO2)(PaCO2) → CVR → CBF. Feedback: CBF → CNS O2 → ventilatory drive (if brainstem senses low O2, hyperventilation → further PaCO2 drop → further CBF drop — positive feedback loop, potential instability at low SV).
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→ETCO2→CBF), Malik 2026 (structural brain changes), existing CBF autoregulation ODE (ch29), CO2 ventilatory control physiology (well-established)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** The extended 4-variable ODE (S, V, PaCO2, CVR, plus algebraic MAP, HR, CPP, CBF) predicts: (a) a bifurcation at a critical SV value (~40-50 mL) where the hypocapnic positive feedback destabilizes CBF (small further SV drops cause large CBF drops — a tipping point); (b) CO2 augmentation above a threshold bit CO2 > 0.02 (2% inspired CO2) stabilizes CBF for any SV > 25 mL (providing a theoretical safety margin); (c) the model fitted to individual patient HUT data (SV, ETCO2, MCA velocity simultaneously) explains ≥ 70% of CBF variance (R² > 0.70).

### M7.2: SV-HR Coupling with Compensatory Regime Transition ODE
**Mechanism:** Chopra 2026 and Marchetta 2025 argue tachycardia is compensatory — HR increases to defend CO (= SV × HR) when SV drops. The existing ANS model (ch29 @sec:orthostatic-model) treats HR as a function of S (sympathetic tone) and V (vagal tone) without explicit SV feedback. An SV-HR coupling extension would add: HR_target = CO_target / SV(t), where CO_target is the baroreflex-mediated cardiac output target needed to maintain MAP. The baroreflex operates on MAP (= CO × TPR), not on HR per se. If TPR response is also impaired (small fiber neuropathy, endothelial dysfunction), HR must compensate even more. The model would capture three regimes: (I) SV > SV_crit → HR regulated by autonomic tone (S, V), normal CO modulation; (II) SV_crit/2 < SV < SV_crit → HR compensatory response begins, baroreflex drives HR up to defend MAP, tachycardia is adaptive; (III) SV < SV_crit/2 → baroreflex saturates, HR cannot compensate further, MAP drops, CBF fails → presyncope. This regime structure directly informs ivabradine use: ivabradine is safe in regime I (SV sufficient → reducing HR is acceptable), dangerous in regime II (HR reduction → CO drops → MAP falls → CBF fails), and irrelevant in regime III (standing already impossible). The van Campen 2024 findings (two hemodynamic subtypes) map directly to regime I (hyperadrenergic — normal SV, high S) and regime II (hypovolemic — low SV, compensatory high HR).
**Existing model context:** ch29 orthostatic model (MAP, HR as functions of S, V, ΔP_grav), ch29 ANS model (dS/dt, dV/dt). Extension: add HR_target = CO_target/SV, with SV as a state variable (dSV/dt depending on blood volume, venous return, cardiac contractility). Regime-switching dynamics: HR = min(HR_m(S,V), HR_target) — whichever is higher determines actual HR.
**Evidence:** Chopra 2026 (compensatory argument), Marchetta 2025 (ivabradine dissociation), van Campen 2024 (two subtypes), Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→CBF), existing baroreflex physiology
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** The regime-transition ODE fitted to individual HUT data (SV, HR, MAP, NE simultaneously recorded during graded tilt) correctly identifies the SV_crit threshold at which HR begins rising faster than predicted by the S-only model (i.e., S alone cannot explain the HR → compensatory regime has been entered). The fitted SV_crit predicts ivabradine response: patients with resting SV > SV_crit tolerate ivabradine without CBF drop; patients with resting SV < SV_crit show CBF drop on ivabradine (AUC > 0.80).

### M7.3: Central Sensitization-Baroreflex Coupling ODE — Adding Interoceptive Gain to the ANS Model
**Mechanism:** Mathew 2026 (67% central sensitization, Novak lab) and H1.3 (distorted interoception) require extending the ANS model to include the brain's interpretation of afferent signals. The baroreflex equation (ch29: sympathetic drive S is a function of MAP_error = MAP_target - MAP_actual) assumes perfect interoception — the brain accurately detects MAP. If central sensitization amplifies afferent autonomic signals, the perceived MAP may differ from actual MAP: MAP_perceived = MAP_actual × γ(CSI), where γ(CSI) is an interoceptive gain factor that increases with central sensitization (CSI). Then S_actual = f(MAP_target - MAP_perceived) — the sympathetic response is proportional to the perceived, not actual, MAP error. If CSI is high, γ > 1, MAP_perceived is lower than actual → exaggerated sympathetic drive → hyperadrenergic state → disproportionate tachycardia. This produces a POTS subtype not captured by the existing 3-regime model (neuropathic, hypovolemic, hyperadrenergic based on autonomic parameters alone) — a "centrally sensitized POTS" where autonomic parameters (S, V, ΔP_grav) are normal but interoceptive gain γ is elevated. The model predicts: (a) CSI score correlates with γ when fitted to individual HUT data, (b) patients with high γ have exaggerated HR response to any given MAP perturbation, (c) interventions that reduce CSI (CNS-directed treatments — duloxetine, tapentadol, cognitive interoceptive retraining) should reduce γ and normalize the HR response without changing peripheral autonomic parameters.
**Existing model context:** ch29 baroreflex equation, ch29 central sensitization model (dS/dt with pain amplification), ch30 integrated model (64 variables). Extension: add γ(t) as a slow variable (weeks-to-months timescale) driven by chronic CBF drops and neuroinflammation, with treatment sensitivity. Coupling: γ → perception gain in baroreflex → altered S dynamics → altered HR → potential positive feedback (high γ → excessive S → more CBF instability → more neuroinflammation → higher γ).
**Evidence:** Mathew 2026 (CSI prevalence, Novak lab), Malik 2026 (structural brain changes — substrate for altered CNS processing), Seeley 2025 (regional SPECT hypoperfusion — correlates with CNS dysfunction), existing fMRI interoception literature (insula, ACC in autonomic disorders)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** The interoceptive gain γ fitted to individual HUT data (MAP, HR, NE simultaneously) significantly correlates with CSI score (r > 0.5). Patients with γ > 1.3 (top tertile) show ≥ 30% greater HR response at each tilt angle (30°, 50°, 70°) than patients with γ < 1.0 (bottom tertile), matched for age, sex, and resting SV. A purely peripheral model (γ = 1 for all patients) provides significantly worse fit (ΔAIC > 6) for the high-CSI subgroup.

### M7.4: SFN-Autonomic Coupling ODE — Adding Nerve Fiber Density as a Structural Parameter
**Mechanism:** Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI symptoms in POTS) and Azcue 2023 (non-length-dependent SFN in ME/CFS) suggest that SFN is not merely correlated with autonomic dysfunction but is a structural constraint on autonomic function. In the existing ANS model, the effector arm of the baroreflex is determined by sympathetic gain () — but this gain implicitly assumes intact small fiber innervation. If IENFD is pathologically reduced, the same sympathetic outflow S(t) produces less peripheral vasoconstriction, less sweat output, and less splanchnic vasomotor response. Mathematically: effective_S = S(t) × α(IENFD), where α(IENFD) is a sigmoid function that saturates at α=1 for IENFD ≥ IENFD_normal and drops below α=0.5 for IENFD < 50% of normal. This directly links the skin biopsy (IENFD) to the cardiovascular model: for the same baroreflex-driven sympathetic activation, the neuropathic POTS patient with low IENFD has less α → less vasoconstriction → greater ΔP_grav effect → lower SV → greater compensatory HR. The model predicts: (a) the degree of orthostatic HR increment is a function of IENFD when controlling for baroreflex gain (S_component), (b) midodrine (α1 agonist, bypasses nerve → directly stimulates vascular smooth muscle) should work better in low-IENFD patients because it bypasses the denervated nerve ending, (c) pyridostigmine (enhances ganglionic transmission → increases nerve ending acetylcholine release) should work worse in low-IENFD patients because there are fewer nerve endings to enhance.
**Existing model context:** ch29 ANS model (S, V, ΔP_grav). Extension: add effective_S = S × α(IENFD) with α(IENFD) parameterized from skin biopsy data. IENFD is treated as a slow structural variable (months-to-years timescale — nerve fiber density changes slowly) rather than a dynamic variable.
**Evidence:** Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI), Azcue 2023 (SFN ME/CFS), Novak 2022 (80% SFN in MCAS), existing SFN-autonomic physiology (Vinik 2013, Tesfaye 2010)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** The α(IENFD) parameter fitted to individual patient data (HUT hemodynamics + skin biopsy IENFD) significantly improves model fit (R² increase ≥ 0.10) vs a model that treats all patients as having α=1 (no SFN), specifically for patients with IENFD < 7 fibers/mm (ankle). Midodrine response (change in orthostatic SV) is inversely correlated with IENFD (r < -0.4) — the drug works better when nerve density is low because α1 receptors are intact and accessible despite denervated nerves.

### M7.5: Multi-Compartment CBF ODE — Regional Brain Perfusion Dynamics
**Mechanism:** Malik 2026 (structural brain changes: gray matter reduction, altered network connectivity) and Seeley 2025 (regional SPECT hypoperfusion correlated with cognitive scores) demonstrate that CBF deficits are regionally specific, not globally uniform. The existing CBF model (ch29) treats CBF as a single whole-brain variable — this misses regional vulnerability. A multi-compartment CBF model with 4 brain regions (prefrontal, hippocampal, thalamic, cerebellar) each with its own CVR dynamics and autoregulatory parameters would capture: (a) why some patients have executive dysfunction (prefrontal hypoperfusion dominant) and others have memory impairment (hippocampal hypoperfusion dominant), (b) the vascular steal-like phenomenon during orthostatic stress where autoregulatory capacity is differentially impaired across regions (posterior circulation may be better preserved than anterior), (c) the structural correlate: regions with the most cumulative CBF deficit show the greatest gray matter atrophy (Malik 2026) — modeled as a slow structural damage term proportional to integrated CBF deficit below a threshold.
**Existing model context:** ch29 CBF model (single compartment), ch30 integrated model (64 variables). Extension: 4 parallel CBF compartment equations each with regional CVR_i(t) dynamics, regional CO2 reactivity g(CO2)_i (may differ — posterior circulation more CO2-responsive than anterior), and a slow structural damage variable D_i(t) = ∫ max(0, CBF_threshold - CBF_i(t)) dt. Clinical mapping: executive function ≈ prefrontal CBF/D; memory ≈ hippocampal CBF/D; pain/sensory ≈ thalamic CBF/D.
**Evidence:** Malik 2026 (structural brain changes), Seeley 2025 (regional SPECT hypoperfusion → cognitive scores), regional CBF autoregulation heterogeneity literature (Willie 2011, Lucas 2010), existing CBF ODE (ch29)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** A 4-compartment CBF model fitted to regional ASL-MRI perfusion data during graded HUT better predicts domain-specific cognitive performance than a single-compartment CBF model (ΔAIC > 10 for at least 2 of 4 cognitive domains). Regional gray matter volume at 12-month follow-up correlates with the modeled cumulative CBF deficit in that region (r > 0.4), supporting the structural damage term D_i(t).

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### CD8.1: POTS ⇔ Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF) — The Low-SV Parallel
**Mechanism:** HFpEF is characterized by normal EF but impaired ventricular filling → low SV → exercise intolerance → compensatory tachycardia. This is hemodynamically identical to hypovolemic/neuropathic POTS (low SV, normal EF, compensatory HR) with different etiology (cardiac stiffness in HFpEF vs blood volume/vasomotor deficit in POTS). If the final common pathway — low SV → compensatory tachycardia → exercise intolerance — is shared, then therapeutic strategies from HFpEF could be tested in POTS: (a) SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) improve ventricular filling and reduce heart rate in HFpEF (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER trials) — could they improve SV in hypovolemic POTS? Mechanism in HFpEF is osmotic diuresis + metabolic shift — unclear if applicable to POTS. (b) Exercise training is the strongest evidence-based intervention for HFpEF (improves ventricular compliance, autonomic function) — supports the Dallas/Leeds POTS exercise protocol approach but also underscores that sustained exercise compliance is necessary (the 41% completion rate in community settings for POTS protocols may be too low to achieve the cardiac remodeling required). (c) Ivabradine in HFpEF showed benefit in a subgroup (SHIFT trial HFrEF, but EDIFY trial in HFpEF was neutral) — inconsistent results parallel the Marchetta 2025 POTS dissociation.
**Evidence:** Marchetta 2025 (ivabradine dissociation), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia), Kwok 2026 (midodrine), HFpEF hemodynamics literature (Borlaug 2011, Paulus 2013), SGLT2i HFpEF RCTs (EMPEROR-Preserved 2021, DELIVER 2022)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** Low-SV POTS patients (SV < 60 mL resting echo) have similar hemodynamic profile to HFpEF patients during HUT/CPET (comparable SV trajectory, HR compensation curve, and CBF behavior). SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin 10 mg qd, 12 weeks) in low-SV POTS: (a) does NOT reduce standing HR vs placebo (the SGLT2i mechanism — osmotic diuresis — may worsen hypovolemia, unlike in HFpEF where volume overload is the primary problem); or (b) IMPROVES SV by ≥ 10% (if the mechanism is improved cardiac energetics/metabolic shift rather than diuresis, as some HFpEF data suggest). This is a critical test of whether POTS-as-cardiac-energetic-deficit (SU4.5) or POTS-as-hypovolemia is correct.

### CD8.2: POTS ⇔ Postural Tachycardia in Spinal Cord Injury (SCI) — The Autonomic Denervation Parallel
**Mechanism:** Patients with cervical/high thoracic SCI (T6 and above) develop orthostatic hypotension and compensatory tachycardia due to disrupted descending sympathetic pathways — the brain's sympathetic command cannot reach the splanchnic and lower extremity vasculature. This is a structural, lesion-based version of neuropathic POTS: in SCI, the sympathetic preganglionic neurons are disconnected from supraspinal control; in neuropathic POTS, the postganglionic small fibers are damaged. The hemodynamic consequence is similar: failure of lower-body vasoconstriction during standing → venous pooling → reduced SV → compensatory tachycardia (or hypotension if compensation fails). SCI autonomic rehabilitation research has developed interventions that could be translated to neuropathic POTS: (a) functional electrical stimulation (FES) of leg muscles during standing → activates the muscle pump without requiring volitional movement, (b) abdominal binders + pneumatic compression devices timed to postural changes (sequentially inflated during standing transition), (c) midodrine + fludrocortisone combination is standard of care in SCI orthostatic hypotension (both FDA-approved for neurogenic OH).
**Evidence:** SCI autonomic dysfunction literature (Claydon 2006, Krassioukov 2012), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia), Kwok 2026 (midodrine meta-analysis), Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI — "peripheral SCI" analogy: SFN = partial peripheral denervation)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** FES-assisted calf muscle pump during seated-to-standing transitions in neuropathic POTS patients (SFN-confirmed, IENFD < 7 at ankle): (a) reduces standing HR increment by ≥ 10 bpm vs passive standing; (b) attenuates SV drop by ≥ 15%; (c) is tolerated without PEM in ≥ 80% of sessions (3-min FES per session — low metabolic demand due to muscle pump activation at sub-fatiguing stimulation intensity, and FES bypasses central fatigue pathways).

### CD8.3: POTS ⇔ Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) — Nocturnal Autonomic Stress Driving Daytime Orthostatic Dysfunction
**Mechanism:** OSA produces repeated nocturnal sympathetic surges (apnea → hypoxia → arousal → sympathetic burst → BP spike → vagal rebound). Each apnea-hypopnea event generates a catecholamine surge that resets baroreflex set-point, increases nocturnal BP (non-dipping), and exhausts vagal reserve. Over months-years, this produces a daytime hyperadrenergic state — elevated resting HR, reduced HRV, sympathetic predominance. This is the same autonomic signature as hyperadrenergic POTS. The question: does unrecognized OSA (common in the general population — 10-30% prevalence — and potentially higher in ME/CFS due to obesity, craniofacial features from connective tissue disorder, or supine fluid shift worsening upper airway collapsibility) contribute to POTS pathophysiology in a subset of patients? The testable prediction: patients with ME/CFS+POTS who also have OSA (AHI > 15 on sleep study) will show improvement in orthostatic tolerance and HR after 8 weeks of CPAP treatment — not because CPAP "treated POTS" but because it removed the nocturnal autonomic stressor that was sustaining the daytime hyperadrenergic state.
**Evidence:** OSA autonomic pathophysiology (Somers 1995, Narkiewicz 1998 — seminal papers on OSA sympathetic activation), Mathew 2026 (67% CSI in POTS — could OSA contribute via sleep fragmentation + nocturnal sympathetic surges), existing POTS clinical observation (some patients improve with CPAP, entirely anecdotal), Chopra 2026 (compensatory tachycardia — but in OSA-POTS overlap, the tachycardia may be pathologically driven by nocturnal stress, not compensatory)
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** Home sleep apnea testing in ME/CFS+POTS reveals AHI > 15 in ≥ 25% of patients (higher than age-matched general population prevalence). For these patients, 8 weeks of CPAP therapy: (a) reduces resting HR by ≥ 8 bpm, (b) reduces standing HR increment by ≥ 5 bpm, (c) improves morning orthostatic symptoms (COMPASS-31 orthostatic subscale, morning-specific) by ≥ 25% compared to pre-CPAP baseline.

### CD8.4: POTS ⇔ Migraine-Associated Dizziness — Shared Cerebrovascular Instability
**Mechanism:** Migraine and POTS are highly comorbid (Deb 2022, Thieben 2006: ~40% of POTS patients have migraine). The shared mechanism may be cerebrovascular instability: both conditions involve abnormal cerebral vascular reactivity — in POTS, the reactivity deficit is CO2-mediated (Miranda-Hurtado 2026); in migraine, the reactivity involves cortical spreading depression and neurovascular coupling dysfunction. If the shared substrate is a "twitchy" cerebral vasculature that over-constricts to hypocapnia (POTS) and over-dilates to cortical activation (migraine), then interventions stabilizing cerebrovascular tone would benefit both: (a) CGRP antagonists (gepants, CGRP mAbs — migraine-specific) — CGRP is the most potent cerebral vasodilator, and its blockade could paradoxically stabilize CBF (preventing both excessive constriction and excessive dilation), (b) magnesium (NMDA block + vascular smooth muscle relaxation — dual benefit for both, SU4.4), (c) riboflavin (B2 — mitochondrial support for both cortical neurons and cerebral vessels). The mechanistic link: impaired mitochondrial function in vascular smooth muscle (supporting both cerebral vasoconstriction against hypocapnia and neurovascular coupling during neuronal activation) is the common deficit. ME/CFS+POTS with comorbid migraine would represent the severe end of a cerebrovascular instability spectrum.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (CO2→CBF), Malik 2026 (structural brain changes — could be mediated by repeated migraine+CBF events), Deb 2022 (POTS-migraine comorbidity), CGRP migraine literature (Goadsby 2017, Edvinsson 2018), cerebral vascular physiology
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFS+POTS+migraine patients: (a) the CO2 reactivity of cerebral vessels (MCA velocity change per mmHg ETCO2 change, measured by transcranial Doppler CO2 challenge) is higher (steeper slope) than in POTS without migraine (p < 0.05) — indicating cerebrovascular hyperreactivity; (b) 12-week CGRP monoclonal antibody (erenumab 70-140 mg monthly) reduces both monthly migraine days (≥ 50% reduction, established efficacy) AND orthostatic dizziness VAS (≥ 30% vs baseline) — suggesting dual cerebrovascular benefit; (c) the orthostatic benefit is not explained by reduced pain catastrophizing or improved mood (mediation analysis).

## 9. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### D9.1: SV-CO2-CBF Triad — A Composite Orthostatic Index for POTS Subtyping
**Description:** Current POTS diagnosis is univariate (ΔHR ≥ 30 bpm within 10 min of standing). This diagnostic threshold has poor test-retest reliability (Hedge 2026) and does not distinguish subtypes. The SV-CO2-CBF triad — simultaneous measurement of stroke volume (impedance cardiography or echo), end-tidal CO2 (capnography), and cerebral blood flow velocity (transcranial Doppler MCA) during a 10-minute HUT — provides a 3-dimensional orthostatic profile: (a) ΔSV: magnitude of preload failure (hypovolemic vs normal SV), (b) ΔETCO2: degree of hypocapnic cerebral vasoconstriction (Miranda-Hurtado 2026 pathway activation), (c) ΔCBF_v: resultant cerebral perfusion deficit. The triad generates a 3-parameter patient vector that can be clustered into subtypes: "hypovolemic-hypocapnic" (↓SV, ↓ETCO2, ↓CBF), "hyperadrenergic-normocapnic" (↔SV, ↔ETCO2, ↓CBF via other mechanism — perhaps endothelial dysfunction), "neuropathic-CO2-normal" (↓SV, ↔ETCO2, ↓CBF — impaired autoregulation cannot compensate even with normal CO2), "centrally-sensitized" (↔SV, ↔ETCO2, ↔CBF, but ↓CBF paradoxically due to interoceptive distortion — H1.3). This 3-parameter stratification would be more reproducible than the single ΔHR metric and would directly guide treatment: hypovolemic-hypocapnic → volume expansion + CO2 retention; neuropathic-CO2-normal → midodrine + pyridostigmine; centrally-sensitized → CNS-directed treatment.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (SV→ETCO2→CBF), Malik 2026 (CBF deficits), Seeley 2025 (regional perfusion), van Campen 2024 (two hemodynamic subtypes), Hedge 2026 (ΔHR unreliability)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** Cluster analysis of SV-ETCO2-CBF HUT data from 100 ME/CFS+POTS patients yields at least 3 clusters with distinct treatment response patterns: each cluster has a dominant effective treatment (volume expansion for one, midodrine for another, CNS-directed for a third) with AUC > 0.75 for treatment response prediction. The SV-ETCO2-CBF composite has significantly better 4-week test-retest reliability (ICC > 0.70) than ΔHR alone (ICC < 0.50 per Hedge 2026).

### D9.2: CO2 Reactivity Index — A Cerebral Vasoreactivity Biomarker for POTS Brain Fog
**Description:** The degree of hypocapnic cerebral vasoconstriction determines orthostatic cognitive symptoms in POTS (H1.1, H1.5, Miranda-Hurtado 2026). A standardized "CO2 reactivity index" — the slope of MCA velocity change per unit ETCO2 change during a standardized breath-hold/hyperventilation challenge — would quantify individual cerebrovascular sensitivity to CO2. Patients with a steep slope (MCA velocity drops > 3% per mmHg ETCO2 decrease) would be "CO2-hyperreactors" — their orthostatic brain fog is substantially driven by hypocapnic vasoconstriction and would benefit from CO2-retention strategies (capnometry biofeedback NP5.1, acetazolamide DR3.1, NaHCO3 SU4.1). Patients with a flat slope (< 1.5% per mmHg) have other mechanisms for their orthostatic CBF deficits (impaired autoregulation, endothelial dysfunction, stiff vessels). The CO2 reactivity index would be measured during supine rest (transcranial Doppler + capnography, 30-sec breath hold → hypercapnia, 60-sec paced hyperventilation → hypocapnia), taking ~3 minutes and adding no risk beyond standard transcranial Doppler.
**Evidence:** Miranda-Hurtado 2026 (CO2→CBF pathway), established CO2 reactivity testing methodology (Ringelstein 1988, Markus 1994 — transcranial Doppler CO2 reactivity has been used for decades in cerebrovascular disease), Malik 2026/Seeley 2025 (CBF deficits in POTS)
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable:** CO2 reactivity index measured during supine rest predicts orthostatic CBF change during HUT (r > 0.6): patients with steeper CO2 reactivity slopes show larger MCA velocity drops during standing (because the ETCO2 drop that occurs during standing [Miranda-Hurtado pathway] is amplified by their higher CO2 sensitivity). The CO2 reactivity index predicts response to CO2 augmentation (R2.2) better than baseline ETCO2 drop alone (ΔR² > 0.10).

### D9.3: Day-to-Day HR Increment Coefficient of Variation — Autonomic Stability Index
**Description:** Hedge 2026 demonstrates poor test-retest reliability of the supine-to-stand HR test. As argued in H1.4, the variability is information, not noise. An "autonomic stability index" (ASI) can be calculated from 5 consecutive morning HR increment measurements over a 2-week period (patients perform a standardized home active stand test each morning using a validated wearable — chest strap or PPG device with HR accuracy ± 2 bpm — and report symptoms via app). ASI = (HR increment SD / HR increment mean) × 100, representing the coefficient of variation. High ASI (> 30%) indicates labile autonomic state — the patient's orthostatic response is sensitive to day-to-day fluctuations (hydration, sleep, PEM, mast cell degranulation). Low ASI (< 15%) indicates fixed structural impairment — the orthostatic response is consistently abnormal, consistent with structural SFN or fixed blood volume deficit. ASI would serve dual diagnostic purpose: (a) screening — a patient with mean HR increment of 25 bpm and ASI of 35% might not "meet POTS criteria" on any single test day but would on some days → the ASI flags them as having dynamic orthostatic intolerance, (b) treatment selection — high ASI patients may respond to interventions targeting state-dependent factors (tVNS for autonomic stability, MCAS treatment for histamine-driven fluctuations, sleep optimization); low ASI patients need structural interventions (volume expansion, compression, midodrine). ASI is directly derived from Hedge 2026's finding — it operationalizes the test-retest unreliability as a biomarker rather than a nuisance.
**Evidence:** Hedge 2026 (poor test-retest), Aitken 2026 (within-person HR/HRV fluctuations predict same-day symptoms), H1.4 (state-dependent physiology hypothesis)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** ASI from 5 home-based morning HR increment tests over 2 weeks significantly predicts: (a) 6-month trajectory — high ASI patients show greater change in POTS status (some remit, some worsen) than low ASI patients (stable — ANOVA category × time interaction p < 0.05); (b) treatment response — high ASI patients respond better to tVNS (4-week, left tragus, 25 Hz) than low ASI patients, while low ASI patients respond better to fludrocortisone + compression; (c) ASI correlates with wrist-actigraphy sleep variability (sleep timing SD > 90 min → high ASI, r > 0.4) and resting HRV CV (r > 0.5) — confirming ASI captures a broader autonomic stability phenotype.

### D9.4: Post-Standing I-FABP/LPS Spike — A POGS Biomarker for Orthostatic Gut Barrier Failure
**Description:** The POGS construct (ch10 @spec:postural-gut-syndrome, certainty 0.40) proposes that upright posture → splanchnic hypoperfusion → gut barrier failure → LPS translocation. The falsifiable prediction in the existing speculations (ch10) is: "Plasma LPS and I-FABP will increase 2-4 hours after 20-minute seated position (vs supine baseline) in severe ME/CFS+POTS patients but not in ME/CFS-only or POTS-only patients without wheat consumption." Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI symptoms in POTS) strengthens this prediction by showing that SFN in POTS directly involves GI innervation — providing a structural basis for why POTS patients specifically (with SFN affecting enteric nerves) would have impaired gut barrier maintenance during hypoperfusion stress. The diagnostic idea: a "postural gut permeability challenge" where patients consume a standardized wheat-containing meal (activating zonulin pathway and priming tight junctions), then undergo a 30-min seated orthostatic challenge (seated upright, feet on floor — moderate orthostatic stress achievable even in severe patients), with blood drawn at baseline (supine), immediately after seated challenge, and 2 hours later. The test is positive if: (I-FABP at 2h / I-FABP baseline) > 1.5 AND (LPS at 2h / LPS baseline) > 1.3. The test identifies POGS patients who specifically need wheat elimination + abdominal compression to prevent orthostatic gut barrier failure, beyond standard POTS treatment. The biomarker is directly actionable — positive test → abdominal compression before meals, wheat elimination, possibly mast cell stabilizers (ketotifen, cromolyn) to protect gut mast cells from ischemia-triggered degranulation.
**Evidence:** POGS construct (ch10 @spec:postural-gut-syndrome, certainty 0.40), Ekman 2025 (SFN→GI symptoms), van Wijck 2011 (exercise-induced splanchnic hypoperfusion → I-FABP/LPS rise), ter Steege 2012 (splanchnic pooling in POTS), van Campen 2020 (27% CBF reduction at 20° tilt in severe → inferred splanchnic equivalent)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** ≥ 40% of severe ME/CFS+POTS patients (bedbound > 18h/day) will have a positive postural gut permeability challenge, vs ≤ 10% of moderate ME/CFS without POTS and 0% of healthy controls. Among POGS-positive patients: (a) 4 weeks of wheat elimination reduces post-postural I-FABP spike by ≥ 50%; (b) abdominal compression (30-40 mmHg abdominal binder) during the seated challenge normalizes the I-FABP and LPS response (ΔI-FABP < 1.5, ΔLPS < 1.3); (c) POGS-positive patients have lower IENFD at the thigh (proximal SFN) than POGS-negative POTS patients (p < 0.05) — confirming enteric SFN as the structural basis for POGS.

### D9.5: Glycocalyx Shedding Markers in POTS — Syndecan-1 + Heparan Sulfate as Endothelial Damage Biomarkers
**Description:** The complement-glycocalyx destruction speculation (ch10 @spec:complement-glycocalyx-pots, certainty 0.35) proposes that complement activation degrades the endothelial glycocalyx, impairing flow-mediated vasodilation and contributing to orthostatic intolerance. The falsifiable prediction: "ME/CFS+POTS patients will show elevated circulating glycocalyx degradation markers (syndecan-1, heparan sulfate, hyaluronan) compared to ME/CFS without POTS and healthy controls." The diagnostic idea operationalizes this: a simple plasma syndecan-1 ELISA (commercially available, ~$20/test, 2-hour turnaround) could provide the first direct evidence of glycocalyx pathology in POTS. If syndecan-1 is elevated specifically in POTS-comorbid ME/CFS (compared to ME/CFS-no-POTS), it supports the specificity of endothelial glycocalyx damage to the orthostatic phenotype. If syndecan-1 correlates with ΔHR during standing (r > 0.4), it links glycocalyx degradation to POTS severity. If syndecan-1 spikes after exercise challenge (PEM induction), the complement→glycocalyx→POTS-worsening pathway is supported. The biomarker has clinical utility: patients with high syndecan-1 would be candidates for glycocalyx-restorative interventions (sulodexide — existing speculation ch14k; rhamnan sulfate — emerging glycocalyx therapy in sepsis models; vitamin C + glycosaminoglycan precursors). The diagnostic kit is straightforward: syndecan-1 ELISA, heparan sulfate competitive binding assay, hyaluronan ELISA-like assay — all commercially available, require only plasma (EDTA tube), and are stable after freeze-thaw.
**Evidence:** ch10 @spec:complement-glycocalyx-pots (cert 0.35), Becker 2015 glycocalyx review, Ostrowski 2015 glycocalyx shedding, existing glycocalyx biomarker literature (syndecan-1 in sepsis, trauma, cardiac surgery — well-established biomarker of endothelial damage)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** Plasma syndecan-1 levels are significantly elevated in ME/CFS+POTS vs ME/CFS-no-POTS vs healthy controls (ANOVA p < 0.01, post-hoc: POTS > no-POTS > control). Syndecan-1 correlates with: (a) ΔHR during HUT (r > 0.4), (b) C3a/C5a plasma levels (r > 0.4 — supporting complement→glycocalyx pathway), (c) flow-mediated dilation (brachial artery FMD, r < -0.4 — glycocalyx degradation → impaired endothelial function). Post-exercise (CPET at 70% predicted VO2max): syndecan-1 increases at 6h post-exertion in POTS-comorbid patients but not in no-POTS patients, correlating with post-exertional symptom worsening (PEM VAS at 24h, r > 0.5).
