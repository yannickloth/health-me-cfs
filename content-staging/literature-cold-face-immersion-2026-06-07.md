## Paper-by-Paper Summary

### 1. Kinoshita et al. 2006 — Circ J (PMID: 16723802)
- Title: Cold-water face immersion per se elicits cardiac parasympathetic activity
- N=8 healthy volunteers (5 female, 3 male)
- Design: Within-subject, 6 protocols isolating FI effect from body position/breath-holding
- Key: Bradycardia from cold-water FI attributed **solely** to cardiac vagal activity — independent of bending over and breath-holding
- HRV measured via wavelet transformation; lnHF significantly increased only with cold-water protocols
- Temperature: ~10-15°C cold water
- Quality: Medium — small N, no replication, no pathological population
- Certainty: 0.55

### 2. Al Haddad et al. 2010 — Eur J Appl Physiol (PMID: 19882167)
- Title: Influence of cold water face immersion on post-exercise parasympathetic reactivation
- N=13 healthy men
- Design: Crossover, intermittent exercise followed by 5-min recovery with or without CWFI
- Key: CWFI accelerated post-exercise parasympathetic reactivation:
  - LnHF higher (p=0.004)
  - Ln rMSSD higher (p=0.026)
  - HRR (heart rate recovery) faster (p=0.002)
- Temperature: Not specified in abstract
- Quality: Medium — randomized crossover, but healthy males only
- Certainty: 0.60

### 3. Heath & Downey 1990 — Clin Sci (PMID: 2155739)
- Title: The cold face test (diving reflex) in clinical autonomic assessment
- N=16 healthy adults (9F, 7M), age 21-35
- Design: Systematic methodological study testing temperature (0, 5, 10, 15°C), placement (whole face, unilateral, forehead), duration (20, 40, 60, 120s)
- Key findings:
  - **Optimal: 0°C compresses, bilateral, 40s** — maximum bradycardia + peripheral vasoconstriction
  - **22% HR reduction** at optimal parameters (~15-20 bpm drop from baseline ~70 bpm)
  - Finger blood flow ↓ 72%, toe ↓ 59%, calf ↓ 44%
  - Good within-subject repeatability across days, times, seasons
  - Significant between-subject variability
  - 0°C well-tolerated; 120s objectionable to some
- Quality: Medium-high — rigorous methodology, established standard parameters
- Certainty: 0.70

### 4. Caspers et al. 2011 — Scand J Med Sci Sports (PMID: 21083770)
- Title: Diving reflex: can the time course of heart rate reduction be quantified?
- Meta-analysis of 8 studies (from 890 screened)
- Key: Time course of diving bradycardia fits monoexponential decay:
  - Apneic facial immersion: **τ = 10.4s** (time constant of HR decay)
  - Apnea in air alone: slower (τ = 16.2s)
  - Fit: r² > 0.93 for exponential function
- Diving reflex useful for diagnosing efferent cardiovascular autonomic pathway integrity
- During exercise: parasympathetic partially offset by sympathetic activity
- Quality: High — meta-analysis methodology
- Certainty: 0.75

### 5. Panneton & Gan 2020 — Front Neurosci (PMID: 32581683)
- Title: The mammalian diving response: inroads to its neural control
- Comprehensive review of brainstem circuitry
- Key pathway: Trigeminal nerve (anterior ethmoidal branch, V1) → medullary dorsal horn (MDH) → NTS → vagal efferent (nucleus ambiguus) + RVLM sympathetic
- Described as the **most powerful autonomic reflex known**
- Diving bradycardia abolished by atropine or vagal cold block
- Circuit inhibits baroreceptor and chemoreceptor reflexes during diving
- Relevance: Direct neuroanatomical pathway from trigeminal → NTS → vagus parallels proposed tVNS mechanism but engages additional pathways
- Quality: High — authoritative review from lab with 30+ years of DR research (NIH-funded)
- Certainty: 0.85

### 6. Schlader et al. 2016 — Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol (PMID: 27511278)
- Title: Sustained increases in blood pressure elicited by prolonged face cooling in humans
- N=10 healthy adults
- Design: 15-min face cooling (0°C ice bag) vs sham (34°C)
- Key:
  - MAP ↑ 83→106 mmHg (sustained entire 15 min)
  - **RMSSD ↑ 61→165 ms** (first 2 min only, then returned to baseline)
  - Forearm vascular resistance ↑ significantly
  - Differential time course: **parasympathetic peaks early (first 2 min), sympathetic/sustained**
- Implication: FC has two-phase autonomic response — brief vagal surge, sustained peripheral vasoconstriction
- Safety note: Healthy population; BP increase of +23 mmHg sustained for 15 min may be concerning in dysautonomia
- Quality: Medium — well-controlled, N=10
- Certainty: 0.65

### 7. Gorini Pereira et al. 2024 — Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol (PMID: 38436059)
- Title: Activation of cardiac parasympathetic and sympathetic activity occurs at different skin temperatures during face cooling
- N=12 healthy (4F, 8M), age 25±3
- Design: Sequential temperature steps (34, 28, 21, 14, 7, 0°C), each 5 min
- Key temperature thresholds:
  - **Sympathetic activation (MAP ↑) at ≤7°C**: 13.1±10.3 mmHg at 7°C; 25.2±7.8 mmHg at 0°C
  - **Parasympathetic activation (RMSSD ↑, HF ↑) only at 0°C**: RMSSD +160.6±208.9 ms; HF +11,450±14,555 ms²
  - Peak parasympathetic requires colder stimulus than peak sympathetic
- Clinical implication: **Only 0°C reliably engages the vagal bradycardic response** — warmer temperatures (10-15°C) may produce sympathetic activation without parasympathetic
- Quality: Medium-high — elegant dose-response design, well-powered
- Certainty: 0.75

### 8. Shamsuzzaman et al. 2014 — Auton Neurosci (PMID: 24368150)
- Title: Sympathetic nerve activity and simulated diving in healthy humans
- N=56 healthy (24F, 32M), mean age 39
- Design: Facial cold + apnea (simulated diving) vs individual stimuli
- Key:
  - Simulated diving = **synergistic** sympathetic + parasympathetic activation (exceeds sum of individual stimuli)
  - MSNA ↑ markedly during early AND late phases
  - **Significant bradycardia only during LATE phase** of simulated diving
- Relevance: MSNA increase during diving reflex may be problematic in dysautonomia with sympathetic hyperactivation
- Safety: BP and vascular resistance ↑ markedly; may trigger symptoms in POTS
- Quality: Moderate-high — largest N of any study reviewed (N=56), Mayo Clinic
- Certainty: 0.70

### 9. Martinez et al. 2025 — Respir Physiol Neurobiol (PMID: 40096873)
- Title: Breath-hold diving as a tool to harness a beneficial increase in cardiac vagal tone
- Comprehensive review of central mechanisms
- Key:
  - Trigeminal afferents (nose/forehead) → pre-Bötzinger complex and KF nucleus → cardiac vagal motor neurons
  - **Hebbian plasticity** in respirHRV/diving core circuit → regular training can yield **permanent increase in CVT**
  - BH-D does not cause structural cardiac remodeling (unlike other exercise)
  - Proposed as safe, non-invasive approach for sympathetic hyperactivity (elderly, CV predisposition)
- Relevance: Supports conditioning hypothesis — repeated diving reflex activation may produce sustained vagal enhancement via plasticity at NTS-vagal synapse
- Quality: Moderate — review, no primary data
- Certainty: 0.55

### 10. Konishi et al. 2016 — Clin Auton Res (PMID: 26795189)
- Title: Diurnal variation in the diving bradycardia response in young men
- N=18 young men
- Design: Diving reflex test (face immersion 1.9-3.1°C + apnea) at 9am, 1pm, 5pm
- Key:
  - **Maximal bradycardia in morning** (9am)
  - Peak R-R interval progressively shorter at 1pm and 5pm
  - LnHF higher in morning → greater vagal tone
- Implication: Diurnal variation must be controlled in studies; best vagal response in morning
- Quality: Medium — male only, within-subject
- Certainty: 0.60

### 11. Janczak et al. 2022 — J Physiol Pharmacol (PMID: 36515628)
- Title: Effects of trigeminal nerve stimulation on parameters derived from pulse wave analysis
- N=15 healthy (10M, 5F), age 20.3±1.3
- Design: 30s facial cooling, pulse wave analysis pre/post
- Key:
  - **HR ↓ 76.8→66.6 bpm (~10 bpm drop, p=0.0038)**
  - Peripheral DBP ↑ 68→70.9 mmHg (p=0.028)
  - LV contractility ↑ (time to peak pressure ↓)
  - Aortic compliance ↑ (reflected wave return delayed)
- Relevance: Provides precise HR drop magnitude (~10 bpm) for 30s FC in young healthy adults
- Quality: Low-medium — small N, no control group
- Certainty: 0.50

### 12. Schlader et al. 2018 — Exp Physiol (PMID: 29450933)
- Title: Face cooling reveals a relative inability to increase cardiac parasympathetic activation during passive heat stress
- N=10 healthy adults
- Design: FC (0°C) during normothermia vs heat stress (ΔTint = +1.0°C)
- Key:
  - Heat stress **attenuates** FC-evoked parasympathetic activation (HRV ↑ blunted)
  - Heat stress **abolishes** FC-evoked vascular resistance increase
  - MAP increase attenuated: 19±7 mmHg (normothermia) vs 2±7 mmHg (heat stress)
- Relevance: Thermoregulatory state modulates diving reflex — hot patients may not mount vagal response. Relevant for ME/CFS with thermoregulatory dysfunction.
- Quality: Medium — well-designed, small N
- Certainty: 0.65

## Critical Question Answers

### What is the magnitude of bradycardia (bpm drop)?
- **Heath 1990**: ~22% HR reduction from baseline (~15-20 bpm) at 0°C, 40s
- **Janczak 2022**: ~10 bpm drop (77→67 bpm) for 30s FC
- **Caspers 2011 meta-analysis**: τ=10.4s monoexponential decay; HR reaches near-minimum by ~30s
- Range: **10-20 bpm drop** in healthy young adults; dose-dependent on temperature and duration

### What water temperature is optimal?
- **Gorini Pereira 2024**: Vagal activation (RMSSD, HF) **only at 0°C**; 7°C gave sympathetic only
- **Heath 1990**: 0°C > 5°C > 10°C > 15°C for bradycardia magnitude
- **Kinoshita 2006**: Significant vagal activation only with cold (not warm) water
- **Conclusion: 0-4°C is optimal for vagal activation** — warmer (10-15°C) may produce symp-only response without the desired vagal surge

### Safety in autonomic dysfunction populations?
- **Zero safety data** in ME/CFS, POTS, or any dysautonomia population — all 12 studies = healthy subjects
- **Key safety concerns identified:**
  - BP ↑ 23 mmHg sustained (Schlader 2016) — concerning in hyperadrenergic POTS
  - MSNA ↑ markedly (Shamsuzzaman 2014) — may worsen sympathetic hyperactivation
  - Peripheral vasoconstriction 44-72% (Heath 1990) — problematic in vasomotor constriction bias
  - Two-phase response: vagal surge (brief) + sympathetic (sustained)
- **Hypothetical risk:** Post-immersion vasodilation rebound could trigger orthostatic symptoms

### Face-only vs whole-body — does it avoid systemic sympathetic response?
- **No** — face cooling alone **does elicit** systemic sympathetic activation: MSNA ↑ (Shamsuzzaman 2014), FVR ↑ (Schlader 2016), MAP ↑ (Schlader 2016)
- But face-only spares trunk/limb cold receptors → less total cold afferent input than WBC
- Relative to whole-body: **less** sympathetic activation, but not **zero** sympathetic activation
- The claim "face-only spares systemic sympathetic response" from ch17 is **partially incorrect** — face cooling does produce sympathetic activation, just less than WBC
- **Revised statement needed:** Face immersion produces a **coordinated** autonomic response (vagal + sympathetic co-activation) characteristic of the diving reflex, not pure vagal activation. The advantage over WBC is anatomical specificity and absence of whole-body thermoregulatory stress, not absence of sympathetic activation.

### How does diving reflex compare to tVNS (mechanism convergence on NTS)?
- **Diving reflex:** Trigeminal (V1) → medullary dorsal horn → NTS (ventrolateral subnucleus) → nucleus ambiguus (vagal motor) + RVLM (sympathetic)
- **tVNS:** Auricular branch of vagus (Arnold's nerve, ABVN) → NTS → vagal efferent
- **Convergence point:** Both activate NTS → vagal efferent pathway
- **Divergence:** Diving reflex also directly activates RVLM → peripheral sympathetic (vasoconstriction). tVNS has no direct sympathetic component.
- **Advantage of CFI:** Engages a more robust, phylogenetically ancient reflex circuit; no device needed; no electrical side effects
- **Disadvantage:** Cannot selectively activate vagal without sympathetic co-activation; no parameterized dosing
- **Conclusion:** tVNS provides purer vagal activation; CFI provides stronger but less selective autonomic response. For conditioned vagal plasticity, CFI may be superior (Martinez 2025 plasticity evidence).

## Gaps Identified

1. **CRITICAL: Zero safety or efficacy data in any dysautonomia population** — this is the single largest gap
2. No dose-response data for autonomic conditioning (repeated CFI over days/weeks)
3. No CFI + HRV biofeedback combination studies
4. Diurnal variation not explored in patient populations
5. No comparison of CFI vs tVNS head-to-head in any population
6. Effect of baseline vagal tone on CFI response magnitude unknown
7. No studies with ME/CFS-specific outcome measures (orthostatic tolerance, PEM, symptom scores)
8. Rebound orthostatic effects post-CFI not measured in any study
9. Long-term plasticity/habituation with repeated CFI not studied
10. Optimal protocol parameters for clinical use (duration, frequency, temperature, timing) not established
