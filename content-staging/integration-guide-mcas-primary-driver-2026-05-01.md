## Papers Added to references.bib (5 new entries)

### 1. Theoharides2024ANS
**Citation:** Theoharides TC, Twahir A, Kempuraj D. "Mast cells in the autonomic nervous system and potential role in disorders with dysautonomia and neuroinflammation." _Annals of Allergy, Asthma & Immunology_. 2024;132(4):440–454.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.anai.2023.10.032 | **PMID:** 37951572
**Certainty:** 0.62

**Key findings:**
- Mast cells are located perivascularly near nerve endings in carotid bodies, heart, and hypothalamus
- CRH activates hypothalamic mast cells via CRHR-1 → VEGF + tryptase + neurotensin + CXCL8 release
- VEGF disrupts blood–brain barrier → amplifies neuroinflammation
- Identifies POTS, ME/CFS, Long COVID, ASD as candidate conditions for mast cell–ANS axis involvement
- Mast cell activation at carotid bodies may impair baroreceptor reflex → orthostatic heart rate instability
- Positions MCAS as upstream initiator of dysautonomia, not merely comorbidity

**Supports:** MCAS as primary driver (mechanistic review)
**Limitations:** Review only; no original patient data; causal direction in ME/CFS not directly demonstrated

### 2. Valent2021criteria
**Citation:** Valent P, Akin C, Bonadonna P, et al. "Updated Diagnostic Criteria and Classification of Mast Cell Disorders: A Consensus Proposal." _HemaSphere_. 2021;5(11):e646.
**DOI:** 10.1097/HS9.0000000000000646 | **PMID:** 34901755
**Certainty:** 0.85

**Key findings:**
- MCAS diagnostic triad: (1) episodic multi-system symptoms; (2) tryptase ≥20% above individual baseline + 2 ng/mL within 4-hour window; (3) response to mast cell–directed treatment
- CD30 as SM minor criterion; bone marrow mastocytosis (BMM) defined as distinct entity
- Hereditary alpha-tryptasemia (HαT) recognized — confounds baseline tryptase interpretation
- Distinguishes primary (clonal), secondary (IgE-driven), and idiopathic MCAS

**Supports:** Diagnostic criteria anchor — needed for any MCAS Domain 6 assessment protocol
**Limitations:** Contested by Afrin/Molderings consensus-2 criteria; tryptase formula requires individual baseline

### 3. Conway2024BeyondMCAS
**Citation:** Conway AE, Verdi M, Shaker MS, et al. "Beyond Confirmed Mast Cell Activation Syndrome: Approaching Patients With Dysautonomia and Related Conditions." _JACI: In Practice_. 2024;12(7):1738–1750.
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jaip.2024.03.019 | **PMID:** 38499084
**Certainty:** 0.58

**Key findings:**
- Large fraction of dysautonomia/hEDS/POTS patients fail strict MCAS criteria but respond to mast-cell–directed therapy
- Psychiatric comorbidities worsen in parallel with physical symptoms — suggests shared biological upstream driver
- Empirical therapy trial justified even without confirmed laboratory criteria
- Treatment ladder: H1/H2 antihistamines → cromolyn (GI-predominant) → ketotifen (stabilizer + antihistamine) → omalizumab (refractory)

**Supports:** MCAS as comorbidity with treatment implications; indirectly supports primary driver hypothesis
**Limitations:** Expert opinion; no original data; no ME/CFS cohort

### 4. Lee2025CanadaMCAS
**Citation:** Lee E, Picard M. "Diagnosis and management of mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS) in Canada: a practical approach." _Allergy, Asthma & Clinical Immunology_. 2025;21(1):49.
**DOI:** 10.1186/s13223-025-00998-9 | **PMID:** 41272881
**Certainty:** 0.65

**Key findings:**
- ECNM–AIM endorsed stepwise guideline: (1) confirm episodic multi-system presentation; (2) exclude secondary causes; (3) obtain biomarkers; (4) evaluate for clonal disorder
- Urinary mediator panels (N-methylhistamine, LTE4, PGD2 metabolite) available via Mayo Clinic Labs — clinically accessible alternative to acute tryptase
- H1 antihistamine prophylaxis first line for recurrent cases

**Supports:** Domain 6 diagnostic workup protocol — provides clinically actionable testing sequence
**Limitations:** Guideline without original data; Canadian context; urinary mediators require normal baseline reference

### 5. Yao2025MCASPOTStriad
**Citation:** Yao L, Subramaniam K, Raja KM, et al. "Association of POTS, HSD, and MCAS in young patients; prevalence, overlap and response to therapy depends on the definition." _Frontiers in Neurology_. 2025;16:1513199.
**DOI:** 10.3389/fneur.2025.1513199 | **PMID:** 40352770
**Certainty:** 0.52

**Key findings:**
- n=100 young POTS patients; MCAS prevalence: 2% (strict), 37% (moderate), 87% (clinical)
- Therapy response 75–91% regardless of which criteria set used — clinical response spans all groups
- Most common positive biomarker: plasma histamine; then chromogranin A
- Full triad (POTS + HSD + MCAS): 1–34 patients depending on criteria

**Supports:** High clinical response rate suggests broader MCAS diagnostic criteria capture real biology; relevant for ME/CFS-POTS overlap patients
**Limitations:** Young patients only; single-centre; no ME/CFS cohort; no placebo control

## Evidence Assessment: MCAS as Primary Driver vs. Comorbidity

| Paper | Claim supported | Strength |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| Rohrhofer2025mecfsmast (already in bib) | MCAS prevalent in ME/CFS (16–25%); associated with higher OI | Medium — prevalence, not causal |
| Theoharides2024ANS | Mast cell–ANS axis: mechanistic upstream pathway for dysautonomia | Medium — mechanistic, not ME/CFS-specific |
| Wirth2023 (already in bib) | Histamine-mediated vasodilation as ME/CFS comorbidity mechanism | Medium — mechanism, not causal hierarchy |
| Kohno2021potsmast (already in bib) | 42% of POTS patients have elevated mast cell mediators; PGD2/histamine more sensitive than tryptase | Medium — POTS not ME/CFS |
| Yao2025MCASPOTStriad | High therapy response irrespective of diagnostic criteria — suggests real biological effect | Medium — young POTS patients |

**Current evidence level:** MCAS as **frequent, clinically significant comorbidity** with **mechanistic plausibility** as an upstream dysautonomia driver in a subset. Direct evidence that MCAS is the *rate-limiting upstream cause* of ME/CFS (rather than a co-occurring condition sharing triggers) does not yet exist in peer-reviewed form. The null-result direction (Mayo Clinic retrospective, JACI 2026: none of 110 hEDS/POTS patients with suspected MCAS met strict criteria) provides important caution against overdiagnosis.

**Proposed framing:** "MCAS as upstream dysautonomia driver in a biological phenotype subset" rather than "MCAS as primary cause of ME/CFS." This is epistemically honest and avoids overclaiming.

## Suggested Domain 6 Assessment Criteria (MCAS/Mast Cell Domain)

**Name:** Domain 6 — Mast Cell / Histaminergic Dysregulation

### Inclusion criteria (≥2 required):

| Criterion | Test | Threshold | Notes |
|-----------|------|-----------|-------|
| Episodic multi-system symptoms | Clinical history | ≥2 organ systems, episodic | Skin + GI + neuro + cardiovascular |
| Orthostatic intolerance with histamine pattern | Tilt-table or NASA lean test | Heart rate rise ≥30 bpm + flushing/urticaria | Histamine-mediated vasodilation |
| Elevated urinary N-methylhistamine | 24h urine (Mayo Labs) | >1.29× upper normal | Collect during symptomatic period |
| Elevated urinary LTE4 | 24h urine (Mayo Labs) | >1.36× upper normal | Highest acute sensitivity (~36-fold elevation) |
| Elevated serum tryptase (reactive) | Acute draw within 4h of episode | ≥20% above individual baseline + 2 ng/mL | Requires known personal baseline |
| Response to H1/H2 antihistamine trial | 4-week empirical trial | ≥30% improvement in symptom score | Use validated scale (e.g., COMPASS-31) |

### Exclusion (secondary MCAS):
- Active IgE-mediated allergy (cofactor-dependent food allergy, hymenoptera venom)
- Systemic mastocytosis (bone marrow biopsy if tryptase >20 ng/mL persistently)
- Hereditary alpha-tryptasemia (genetic test if baseline tryptase >8 ng/mL)

### Rationale for biomarker selection:
- Tryptase alone misses majority of mast-cell-associated POTS (positive in only 9% in Kohno2021)
- PGD2 metabolite (2,3-dinor-11β-PGF2α) and LTE4 have higher sensitivity than tryptase for non-anaphylactic MCAS
- Urinary panel (NMH + LTE4 + PGD2 metabolite) available as home collection via Mayo Clinic Labs (Voelker2024urinemast)
- Plasma histamine is most common positive in POTS/MCAS overlap (Yao2025MCASPOTStriad)

## Suggested Treatment Protocol for Domain 6–Positive Patients

**Tier 1 — First line (weeks 1–4):**
- Cetirizine 10 mg daily (H1) + famotidine 20 mg twice daily (H2)
- Low-histamine diet trial (4 weeks)
- Trigger avoidance (heat, exercise, stress, specific foods)

**Tier 2 — Add if partial response (weeks 4–8):**
- Cromolyn sodium (oral) 100–200 mg four times daily before meals (GI-predominant symptoms)
- Quercetin 500 mg twice daily (natural mast cell stabilizer; Theoharides2012)
- Ketotifen 1–2 mg at bedtime (H1 antihistamine + mast cell stabilizer)

**Tier 3 — Specialist referral (weeks 8+):**
- Omalizumab (anti-IgE): for IgE-driven component or refractory urticaria
- Rule out systemic mastocytosis if tryptase persistently >20 ng/mL
- Small fibre neuropathy workup if autonomic symptoms predominate (Novak2022)

**Evidence base rating:** Tier 1 — Low-quality (case series, clinical consensus); Tier 2 — Very low (mechanistic + expert opinion); Tier 3 — Indirect evidence from non-ME/CFS populations.

**Warning:** No randomised controlled trial of mast cell stabilisation in ME/CFS exists. Weinstock2024Ketotifen proposes MCAS-POTS-EDS treatment implications but is review-based. Steinberg1996 (terfenadine RCT in CFS) was negative. Treatment recommendations are empirical.

## Null Result / Contradicting Evidence to Note

**Mayo Clinic retrospective (JACI 2026):** 110 adults with hEDS/POTS and suspected MCAS across all Mayo sites (2017–2025). None met established MCAS diagnostic criteria. 95% had unconfirmed MCAS labels; 66% had psychiatric comorbidities. This is important counterevidence that should be cited alongside the Rohrhofer/Yao data to avoid overclaiming. However, the PMID was not yet indexed at time of writing — add when available (S0091-6749(25)01704-X, JACI online first 2026).
