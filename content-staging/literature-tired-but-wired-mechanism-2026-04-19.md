# Tired But Wired: Integrated Mechanism of Sleep-Onset Failure in ME/CFS

## /integrate-topic

## Summary

The book covers autonomic dysfunction during sleep (ch02), sympathetic persistence (ch02), circadian disruption (ch09), and AQP4/norepinephrine interaction (ch15) as separate topics. What is missing is the integrated framing of "tired but wired" — the specific sleep-onset failure mechanism where physical exhaustion coexists with neural hyperarousal — as a coherent, multi-component autonomic failure that compounds downstream sleep dysfunction.

## Key Points for Integration

### The autonomic handoff failure (target: ch08 or ch09, new subsection)

Sleep onset requires a coordinated sympathetic-to-parasympathetic transition:
1. Declining cortisol
2. Rising melatonin (DLMO)
3. Increasing parasympathetic vagal tone
4. Decreasing norepinephrine/epinephrine
5. Peripheral vasodilation (heat dumping → core temperature drop of 1–1.5°C)
6. Progressive suppression of wake-promoting orexin neurons

In ME/CFS, this handoff fails at multiple points simultaneously:

- **Sympathetic dominance persists** — HRV studies show reduced parasympathetic tone even during sleep. The sympathetic branch doesn't disengage at night.
- **Norepinephrine stays elevated** — downstream consequence: AQP4 suppression blocks glymphatic clearance BEFORE sleep even begins. The same neurochemical keeping the patient awake also blocks the brain's waste-clearance system.
- **Temperature drop fails** — autonomic dysfunction impairs peripheral vasodilation. Core temperature stays elevated, hypothalamic sleep gate doesn't open. Patients notice cold extremities at bedtime (vasoconstriction persists when it should reverse).
- **Orexin temporal dysregulation** — López-Amador et al. 2025 found reduced orexin-A overall, but temporal pattern may be dysregulated: inappropriate wakefulness at night, inappropriate sleepiness during day.

### The inflammatory co-driver (target: ch15, add to existing sections)

Independent of autonomic failure, peripheral inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α) cross into the brain and act on hypothalamic sleep/arousal circuits. Effect is paradoxical:
- Cytokines increase sleep pressure (sickness behaviour: brain wants to force rest)
- Cytokines fragment sleep architecture (inflammatory state disrupts oscillatory coordination)

Result: two systems pulling in opposite directions. Inflammatory drive pushes toward sleep; sympathetic activation + inflammatory sleep disruption prevent sleep from being restorative. Patient is trapped in the middle.

### Downstream cascade of failed sleep onset

Every hour in the tired-but-wired state:
- Sustained sympathetic activation → elevated norepinephrine → AQP4 suppression → pre-emptive glymphatic blockade
- Delayed sleep onset → reduced total slow-wave sleep opportunity
- Accumulated sympathetic activation → fragmented sleep once it arrives
- Next-day consequences compound: brain fog, pain, PEM vulnerability

### Connection to headache qualitative description (target: ch02 or ch03)

The morning headache quality in ME/CFS is characteristically different from migraine or tension headache. Patients describe it as "intoxicated," "poisoned," "hungover without drinking" — a deep diffuse heaviness consistent with impaired overnight glymphatic clearance leaving metabolic waste (glutamate, lactate, inflammatory cytokines) in brain parenchyma. This qualitative description is mechanistically accurate: the patient IS intoxicated — by their own uncleaned metabolic debris.
