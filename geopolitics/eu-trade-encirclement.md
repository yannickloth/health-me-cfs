# The Compass Has One Arrow Left

Look at a map of the European Union's trade connections in early 2022. Then look at the same map in March 2026.

East: sanctioned shut. Southeast: bombed. The Levant: rubble. Africa: coups. Latin America: seized. The Arctic: contested. Canada: under pressure.

What's left open is the Atlantic. Westward. To the United States.

Each of these closures has its own justification — a real one. Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Iran's nuclear program. Venezuelan narco-trafficking. Sahel instability. None of what follows requires assuming secret coordination between hostile powers. But the pattern deserves scrutiny: when every closure benefits the same actor, and the cumulative result is rising EU dependency on a single partner, the question of whether this is coincidence or something more becomes unavoidable.

The EU — 450 million consumers, roughly $19–20 trillion in GDP — built its postwar prosperity on trade diversification. That diversification is eroding. Some of it was the EU's own doing: three decades of voluntary dependence on Russian gas, against repeated American warnings, left Europe structurally exposed when the invasion came. But the speed and breadth of what followed goes beyond the correction of past mistakes.

This article traces the closures, one direction at a time. It does not claim to know the intent of the actors involved — that would require access to decision-making we don't have. What it does is map the structural outcome and ask whether the pattern is worth worrying about.

## Iran as Transit Hub — What Was Being Built

### The infrastructure pipeline

Before February 28, 2026, Iran was becoming the most important transit node in Eurasian overland trade — not because of ideology, but geography. Iran sits where East-West corridors (China to Europe) and North-South corridors (Russia to the Indian Ocean) cross.

A caveat upfront: most of what follows concerns an infrastructure *pipeline*, not a functioning network. The China-Iran rail corridor dispatched 40 cargo trains in 2025 — a large increase from the seven trains over the preceding seven years, but still negligible compared to the 16,000+ annual trips on the established China-Europe rail route through Kazakhstan and Russia. What Iran represented was not a mature alternative but an accelerating one, with committed capital and multilateral agreements locking in the trajectory. Whether it would have reached scale is now unknowable. Here is what was being built.

**The Iran-China 25-Year Cooperation Program**, signed on March 27, 2021, committed approximately $400 billion in Chinese investment across Iranian railways, ports, energy infrastructure, and telecommunications. China secured discounted oil — a minimum 12% below benchmark, plus an additional 6-8% risk-adjusted compensation — while Iran gained the infrastructure to become the central node of China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) in the Middle East ([Iran-China 25-Year Cooperation Program, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93China_25-year_Cooperation_Program); [Rasanah IIIS: Iran-China Strategic Partnership](https://rasanah-iiis.org/english/position-estimate/the-iran-china-25-year-comprehensive-strategic-partnership-challenges-and-prospects/)).

On May 25, 2025, the first freight train from Xi'an arrived at Iran's Aprin dry port, launching a direct rail link. Transit time: roughly 15 days, compared to 30–40 by sea. By late 2025, 40 cargo trains had been dispatched — up from seven over the entire preceding seven years. Small numbers in absolute terms, but the trajectory mattered: the route was operational, costs were falling, and multilateral tariff harmonization was underway ([Ponderwall: China-Iran Rail Corridor](https://ponderwall.com/index.php/2025/05/25/china-iran-rail-corridor/); [SpecialEurasia: Iran-China Corridor Impact](https://www.specialeurasia.com/2025/06/09/iran-china-railway-eurasia/)).

But the China-Iran corridor was only one piece. A six-nation meeting in Tehran in May 2025 — involving Iran, China, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and Turkey — established uniform transport tariffs for the entire China-Central Asia-Iran-Turkey-EU railway route. A follow-up meeting in Beijing in August 2025 formalized these agreements further ([Eurasianet: China Building Up Southern Corridor via Iran](https://eurasianet.org/china-building-up-southern-corridor-via-iran-as-response-to-us-push-to-expand-middle-corridor); [Railway Gazette: China-Iran Railway Corridor](https://www.railwaygazette.com/freight/china-iran-railway-corridor-co-operation/68790.article)).

The **International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC)** added a perpendicular axis. In October 2025, Iran, Russia, and Azerbaijan finalized a comprehensive roadmap to increase transit volume to 15 million tons annually. In November 2025, the three countries signed a Memorandum of Understanding on the western branch of the INSTC. The critical missing link — the Rasht-Astara railway along the Caspian coast — was under active construction, with land acquired for 160 kilometers and Iran and Russia agreeing in January 2025 to commence building ([Tehran Times: Iran-Russia-Azerbaijan Railway MOU](https://www.tehrantimes.com/news/520951/Iran-Russia-Azerbaijan-expand-railway-co-op-in-North-South); [Caspian Post: Rasht to Astara Corridor](https://caspianpost.com/iran/iran-expands-key-trade-corridor-from-rasht-to-astara-linking-to-europe); [Russia's Pivot to Asia: INSTC Deal](https://russiaspivottoasia.com/new-instc-russia-azerbaijan-iran-deal-is-hugely-significant-analysis/)).

On November 30, 2025, Iran and Turkey announced the construction of a $1.6 billion railway — the Marand-Cheshmeh Soraya line — to create a direct Iran-Turkey-EU rail connection. The two countries set a bilateral trade target of $30 billion annually ([Iran International: Iran-Turkey Rail Link](https://www.iranintl.com/en/202511309621); [Times of Israel: Iran-Turkey Rail Link](https://www.timesofisrael.com/iran-and-turkey-agree-to-build-key-trade-rail-link-along-ancient-silk-road/)).

On October 22, 2025, Afghanistan, Iran, and Turkey signed a trilateral railway MOU in Istanbul focused on the Herat-Mazar-i-Sharif line, adding yet another feeder route ([South Asian Desk: Afghanistan-Iran-Turkey Railway MOU](https://southasiandesk.com/afghanistan-iran-turkey-railway-mou/25/10/2025/)).

The **Five Nations Railway Corridor** — connecting China, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Afghanistan, and Iran over 2,100 kilometers — was simultaneously being developed, linking China to Iran's Persian Gulf ports of Chabahar and Bandar Abbas. Uzbekistan announced plans to build a terminal at Chabahar, and Kazakhstan declared its intention to construct a transport and logistics terminal at Shahid Rajaee Seaport ([Five Nations Railway Corridor, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Nations_Railway_Corridor); [Astana Times: Southern Corridor Critical for Central Asia](https://astanatimes.com/2026/02/why-southern-corridor-through-iran-is-critical-for-central-asia/)).

In September 2024, Iran and Iraq signed 14 cooperation agreements, including a framework to jointly build the Shalamcheh-Basra railway, envisioned as extending through Iraq to Syria and the Mediterranean coast at Latakia — an east-west rail transit corridor from the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean Sea ([Al Jazeera: Shalamcheh-Basra Railroad](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/9/6/why-is-the-shalamcheh-basra-railroad-so-important-to-iran-and-iraq)).

In 2024, Russia, Kazakhstan, Turkmenistan, and Iran signed the "Eastern Corridor Roadmap" for coordinated development of the Caspian eastern route.

The Istanbul-Tehran-Islamabad rail service was scheduled to reopen in January 2026.

Iran had also negotiated the extension of its natural gas export contract to Turkey, currently set to expire in November 2026.

By late 2025, Iran was party to at least ten major transit agreements or frameworks with nine countries (China, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Turkey, Russia, Azerbaijan, Afghanistan, Iraq), forming the emerging keystone of both East-West and North-South overland corridors. Total transit cargo through Iran reached 22 million tons in 2024, a threefold increase from 2020 levels. None of this infrastructure was mature. All of it was accelerating.

### February 28, 2026

On February 27, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Al-Busaidi announced a "breakthrough" — Iran had agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and to submit to full IAEA verification. He said peace was "within reach." This came less than two days after US and Iranian negotiators held indirect talks in Geneva through Omani mediation ([UK House of Commons: US-Israel Strikes on Iran](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10521/)).

On February 28, the United States and Israel attacked Iran.

The security justifications were real. Iran had entered 2026 with 440 kg of uranium enriched to 60% purity — enough, if further enriched, for as many as ten nuclear weapons. Its ballistic missile program threatened the region. Years of negotiation had not produced a durable constraint. Israel, which has faced existential threats throughout its history, confronted a nuclear-threshold adversary with active proxy networks attacking Israeli territory. These are genuine security imperatives, not manufactured pretexts ([Britannica: 2026 Iran Conflict](https://www.britannica.com/event/2026-Iran-Conflict); [IISS: US-Israel Campaign in Iran](https://www.iiss.org/online-analysis/online-analysis/2026/02/the-usisrael-campaign-in-iran--further-assessments/)).

The timing, though, is hard to reconcile with a purely nonproliferation motive. The Omani breakthrough had just been announced. Whether the decision to strike predated the diplomatic opening — making the timing coincidental — or whether the breakthrough was itself a factor, we don't know. The Trump administration offered "various and conflicting explanations" for the war: imminent threat, preemption, destroying missile capabilities, securing natural resources, regime change ([Al Jazeera: US-Israeli Strategy Against Iran](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/16/the-us-israeli-strategy-against-iran-is-working-here-is-why); [Mondoweiss: How Aligned Are US and Israel's Goals?](https://mondoweiss.net/2026/03/how-aligned-are-the-u-s-and-israels-goals-in-iran/)). The multiplicity of justifications does not tell us which one mattered most. It does tell us the administration wanted the war for more than one reason.

The consequences were immediate and devastating for global trade. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps effectively shut the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint through which approximately 20% of global petroleum and liquefied natural gas passes. Over 3,000 vessels were stranded. Tanker traffic dropped approximately 70%. Oil prices surged 45% to above $110 per barrel. Oil exports from the region fell 60%, from 25 million barrels per day to around 10 million. China's $100+ billion in BRI infrastructure investment in Iran was jeopardized ([Wikipedia: 2026 Strait of Hormuz Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Strait_of_Hormuz_crisis); [CNN: Red Sea Lifeline Also at Risk](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/17/business/red-sea-trade-chokepoint-iran-war-intl); [WEF: Global Price Tag of War](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/03/the-global-price-tag-of-war-in-the-middle-east/); [NPR: Iran War Enters Fourth Week](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/21/nx-s1-5755539/iran-war-fourth-week)).

Every agreement listed above — the 25-Year Program, the six-nation tariff framework, the INSTC roadmap, the Rasht-Astara railway, the Iran-Turkey line, the Five Nations Corridor, the Shalamcheh-Basra project, the Chabahar terminals, the Eastern Corridor Roadmap, the Istanbul-Tehran-Islamabad service — was simultaneously disrupted or destroyed. Some were operational. Most were under construction or in advanced planning. All of them were cancelled by the same event.

What was attacked was not a single country's infrastructure. It was the emerging node of a Eurasian overland trade network — still immature, but with committed capital from nine governments and a trajectory that, had it continued, would have offered the EU a non-maritime, non-US-dependent route to Asian goods within the decade.

## Direction by Direction

### East — Russia

The eastern corridor was the first to close, and the EU bears significant responsibility for its own exposure. For three decades, Europe chose cheap Russian pipeline gas over diversification — a choice the United States repeatedly warned against. When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, the EU's dependency was 45% of gas imports, 27% of crude oil, and nearly half its coal.

The correction was swift and necessary. By 2025, Russian gas had fallen to roughly 12% of EU imports. Coal imports stopped in August 2022. Crude dropped below 3%. On January 26, 2026, EU countries formally adopted Regulation EU/261/2026, converting the REPowerEU roadmap into binding law: all Russian pipeline and LNG imports phased out within two years ([Al Jazeera: EU Moves to Ban Russian Energy by 2028](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/10/20/eu-moves-to-ban-russian-energy-imports-by-2028); [Al Jazeera: EU Agrees to End Russian Gas by 2027](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/3/eu-agrees-deal-to-end-russian-gas-purchases-by-2027); [EC: REPowerEU](https://energy.ec.europa.eu/strategy/repowereu-phase-out-russian-energy-imports_en)).

Nobody seriously argues this was wrong. Russia's invasion violated international law, and energy dependence on a hostile power was an unacceptable security risk. But the structural consequence remains: the eastern corridor is legally prohibited, not merely disrupted, and the question of who filled the gap matters enormously (see below).

### Southeast — the Levant

The route through Iraq, Syria, and Lebanon was never viable in modern terms and has only deteriorated. Syria's civil war left infrastructure requiring an estimated $216 billion in reconstruction (World Bank). The proposed Iraq-Syria-Lebanon oil corridor is "a patchwork of aging, damaged, and non-operational segments." Lebanon's Tripoli refinery has been dormant since 1992 ([Middle East Forum: Syria's Economic Revival](https://www.meforum.org/mef-observer/hope-and-anxiety-over-syrias-economic-revival)).

Iraq cannot secure its own territory. A 2026 Lebanon war is now running alongside the Iran conflict. The Soufan Center's 2026 forecast: "conflicts in Gaza, Lebanon, Libya, Sudan, and Yemen are unlikely to fully resolve in 2026" ([Soufan Center: Middle East Forecast 2026](https://thesoufancenter.org/intelbrief-2026-january-9/); [SpecialEurasia: Middle East Geopolitical Risk 2026](https://www.specialeurasia.com/2025/12/28/middle-east-risk-2026/); [Wikipedia: 2026 Lebanon War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Lebanon_war)). This direction was closed before the current crisis. The crisis ensures it stays closed.

### Southeast (alternative) — IMEC and Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia is building the Landbridge railway connecting Red Sea ports to the Arabian Gulf — construction started in 2025, targeted for the late 2020s, capacity of 50+ million tons annually ([Saudi Logistics Consulting: Landbridge Project 2026](https://saudilogisticsconsulting.com/insights/articles/saudi-landbridge-project-2026-updates-railway-logistics)).

This infrastructure feeds the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC), a US-backed initiative announced at the G20 that routes through UAE, Saudi Arabia, Israel, and Greece. IMEC explicitly bypasses Iran and China. It received additional momentum from the EU-India trade deal signed in January 2026 ([Atlantic Council: IMEC Report](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/in-depth-research-reports/report/the-india-middle-east-europe-economic-corridor-connectivity-in-an-era-of-geopolitical-uncertainty/); [GMF: IMEC's Comeback](https://www.gmfus.org/news/imecs-comeback); [IMEC Official Site](https://www.imec.international/)).

An important nuance: the EU is not a passive victim of IMEC — it is a named co-author. Commissioner von der Leyen was present at the G20 announcement. The EU voluntarily signed on to a corridor that bypasses both Iran and China. Whether this reflects genuine EU strategic choice or acquiescence to a framework designed in Washington is debatable. What is not debatable is that IMEC replaces Iran-based corridors with a route running entirely through US-allied states.

### South — Africa

Africa is more complicated than the other directions. EU-Africa trade runs primarily through established Mediterranean routes (Morocco, Egypt, Tunisia) and Atlantic shipping — not through the landlocked Sahel, where nine coups since 2020 have made headlines (Burkina Faso twice, Chad, Gabon, Mali twice, Niger, Sudan). The Sahel instability is real and concerning, but it was never an EU trade corridor ([Africa Center: Ten African Security Trends 2025](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/2025-security-trends-graphics-sudan-sahel-nigeria-somalia-drones-china/); [Amani Africa: Gathering Storm 2026](https://amaniafrica-et.org/the-gathering-storm-facing-africa-in-2026-entrenching-conflicts-fractured-order-and-eroding-agency/); [Chatham House: Africa in 2026](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/01/africa-2026-global-uncertainty-demands-regional-leadership)).

Where Africa matters for this analysis is in critical minerals and the competition to control them. The US-backed Lobito Corridor in Central Africa is designed explicitly to counter Chinese dominance over minerals needed for the energy transition. China has invested heavily across the continent, but instability poses growing challenges — the question is increasingly "whether China will be compelled to deploy military forces to protect its investments" ([War on the Rocks: Will China Intervene to Protect Investments in Africa?](https://warontherocks.com/2024/10/will-china-intervene-directly-to-protect-its-investments-in-africa/)). Africa is not a closed door for the EU, but it is a contested one — and the contest is primarily between the US and China, with Europe a secondary player.

### Southwest — Latin America and the "Donroe Doctrine"

On January 3, 2026, US forces captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro, transferring him to New York on narco-terrorism charges. Venezuela holds approximately 300 billion barrels of proven oil reserves — about 17% of the global total ([Wikipedia: 2026 US Intervention in Venezuela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_United_States_intervention_in_Venezuela); [TIME: How Trump's Venezuela Takeover Could Change the World](https://time.com/7343019/venezuela-trump-oil-china/)).

The counter-narcotics justification is real. So is the structural consequence. Before the intervention, 81.7% of Venezuela's oil exports went to China. The seizure severed this flow overnight — tankers heading to China were left idling in Venezuelan waters. The Trump administration announced all Venezuelan oil would move through "authorized channels consistent with US law." Combined with the Iran war (which shut the Strait of Hormuz), roughly 17% of China's total oil imports were cut off simultaneously from two separate directions ([Columbia CGEP: Venezuela-China Oil Flows Threatened](https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/venezuela-china-oil-ties-severely-impacted-by-us-action/); [Anadolu Agency: US Intervention Shifts Venezuelan Oil Control](https://www.aa.com.tr/en/economy/us-intervention-shifts-venezuelan-oil-control-china-loses-ground/3792394); [Bloomberg: What Maduro Capture Means for China](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-05/china-what-ousting-of-venezuela-s-maduro-means-for-taiwan-oil-imports)).

At his January 3 press conference, Trump invoked and "superseded" the Monroe Doctrine, coining the "Donroe Doctrine." The December 2025 National Security Strategy made it formal: "reassert and enforce the Monroe Doctrine to preserve U.S. preeminence in the Hemisphere and deny outside powers control of strategic locations and assets." Beijing recognized the language as targeting Chinese influence — China had invested approximately $67 billion in Venezuela since 2007 ([Wikipedia: Donroe Doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donroe_Doctrine); [PIIE: Trump's Monroe Doctrine Aimed at China](https://www.piie.com/blogs/realtime-economics/2026/trumps-latter-day-monroe-doctrine-aimed-china); [CNBC: Donroe Doctrine and China](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/11/trump-venezuela-greenland-donroe-doctrine-china.html); [ODI: Trump's Monroe Doctrine 2.0](https://odi.org/en/insights/trumps-monroe-doctrine-beijings-next-move/); [Atlantic Council: Trump Corollary in Effect](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/the-trump-corollary-is-officially-in-effect/)).

The EU attempted a countermove: on January 17, 2026, it signed the EU-Mercosur trade deal, creating a 700-million-person trading zone described as a "strategic hedge against US-China competition." But the treaty text is not ratification — France, Ireland, Austria, Hungary, and Poland voted against it, and the European Parliament sent it to the Court of Justice for a two-year review. As a functioning trade alternative, Mercosur is years away at best ([Foreign Policy: EU-Mercosur as Strategic Hedge](https://foreignpolicy.com/2026/01/14/eu-mercosur-trade-deal-trump-donroe-doctrine/); [CNN: EU-Mercosur Landmark Agreement](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/17/americas/mercosur-eu-trade-agreement-latam-intl); [Brussels Report: EU-Mercosur Strategic Moment](https://www.brusselsreport.eu/2026/03/02/europes-strategic-moment-the-eu-mercosur-agreement-moves-forward)).

### North — the Arctic

In 2025, Chinese shipping operators completed a record 14 container voyages through the Northern Sea Route, including the first direct China-to-UK container transit — the *Istanbul Bridge*, Ningbo-Zhoushan to Felixstowe in 20 days, 40% faster than the Suez route ([CNN: China Arctic Shipping Route](https://www.cnn.com/2025/10/03/climate/china-arctic-shipping-northern-sea-route); [Eurasia Review: Polar Silk Road](https://www.eurasiareview.com/26012026-the-polar-silk-road-chinas-strategic-arctic-ambitions-analysis/)).

China formalized the "Polar Silk Road" in its 2018 Arctic white paper. By 2025, 95% of international Arctic transit flowed between Russia and China — Russia controls the Northern Sea Route, enforcing a regime of permits, fees, and mandatory icebreaker escorts. Western operators have largely withdrawn, making it a de facto Russia-China duopoly ([Stimson: Northern Sea Route Myth of Sino-Russian Cooperation](https://www.stimson.org/wp-content/files/file-attachments/Stimson%20-%20The%20Northern%20Sea%20Route%20-%20The%20Myth%20of%20Sino-Russian%20Cooperation.pdf); [Geopolitical Monitor: Arctic Strategic Stakes](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/arctic-geopolitics-strategic-stakes-for-china-russia-and-the-u-s/)).

The US is contesting this space. Trump has pushed to acquire Greenland (see below). In October 2025, the US signed an icebreaker production deal with Finland — four Finnish-built, then seven US-built vessels. The Congressional Research Service identifies the Arctic as an area of increasing "geopolitical competition" ([Chatham House: Russia and China Expanding in Arctic](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2025/10/when-it-comes-arctic-security-europe-must-not-forget-about-economics)). The Arctic route is not closed, but it is becoming a three-way contest where the EU has no icebreakers, no Arctic military presence, and no seat at the negotiating table.

### Northwest — Canada

Canada is a deeply US-integrated economy — 75% of Canadian exports go to the US, by design and by geography, not by coercion alone. But Trump's "51st state" rhetoric, combined with tariff pressure, is worth noting in the context of EU diversification options.

After the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs (6-3) on February 25, 2026, Trump replaced them with Section 122 tariffs at 15% on all imports including Canada. The 2026 USMCA review carries the implicit threat of revision. Prime Minister Carney has responded by pursuing non-US market diversification — aiming to double non-US exports within a decade. Ninety percent of Canadians oppose annexation ([The Hill: Trump Annexation Threat](https://thehill.com/homenews/administration/5072926-trump-annexation-canada-trade-threat/); [Al Jazeera: Trump Effect on Canada](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/2/12/trump-effect-how-us-tariffs-51st-state-threats-are-shaking-up-canada); [Fraser Institute: Canada Weathers Trump's Second Term](https://www.fraserinstitute.org/commentary/year-review-canada-weathers-first-year-trumps-second-term)). Whether the EU-Canada relationship can grow significantly enough to matter is an open question — the structural barriers (transatlantic shipping capacity, regulatory divergence, established supply chain inertia) are formidable.

### West — the Atlantic, to the United States

The direction that remains fully open — and where dependency is growing fastest.

US LNG supplied 24% of EU LNG imports in early 2021. By the third quarter of 2025, it reached 57–60% (estimates vary by dataset). If current contracts materialize and EU gas demand reduction stalls, US LNG could reach 75–80% of EU LNG imports by 2030 ([IEEFA: EU Risks 80% US LNG Dependency by 2030](https://ieefa.org/resources/eu-risks-new-energy-dependence-us-could-supply-80-its-lng-imports-2030); [EU Council: Where Does EU Gas Come From?](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/infographics/where-does-the-eu-s-gas-come-from/)).

Norway, the EU's largest pipeline supplier at roughly 30% of all gas imports, is experiencing declining output — widening the gap that US LNG fills. Qatar and Australia also supply the EU with LNG, but neither at the scale nor the growth rate of the US, and neither with the political leverage that comes from being simultaneously the EU's security guarantor and its dominant energy supplier ([Natural Gas Intel: Norwegian Decline Opens Path for US LNG](https://naturalgasintel.com/news/norwegian-natural-gas-output-decline-widens-path-for-more-us-lng-to-europe/)).

The EU-US trade deal, agreed in summer 2025, imposed a 15% tariff on most EU industrial exports while granting exemptions to most US goods entering the EU. The EU committed to over €640 billion in US energy purchases, more than €500 billion in US economy investments, and roughly €35 billion in US-made AI chips. EU officials used words like "capitulation" and "coercion" to describe it. The US position — that Europe should pay more for its own defense and reduce its trade surplus — has its own legitimacy, and European populations increasingly accept this argument. But the terms of the deal go well beyond burden-sharing ([CAP: Trump's Trade Deals as Corporate Short-Termism](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/the-trump-administrations-trade-deals-are-the-epitome-of-corporate-style-short-termism-in-foreign-policy-and-the-american-people-will-pay-the-price-for-years-to-come/)).

## Who Benefits — and Does It Matter?

### The US-Israel question

The US and Israel both had genuine reasons to act against Iran. Israel faced a nuclear-threshold adversary with active proxy networks — a security situation that, for a country of nine million people in a hostile region, is existential in a way that outsiders sometimes underestimate. The US had its own nonproliferation concerns, its own regional security interests, and its own domestic political dynamics ([Al Jazeera: US and Israeli Interests May Soon Diverge](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/2/us-and-israeli-interests-may-soon-diverge-on-iran); [Al Jazeera: Geopolitical Analysis of Imposed War](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/10/geopolitical-analysis-of-the-imposed-war-against-iran); [Arab Center DC: US-Israel War on Iran](https://arabcenterdc.org/resource/the-us-israel-war-on-iran-analyses-and-perspectives/)).

The question this article raises is not whether these justifications were real — they were. It is whether, among the available options (including the Omani diplomatic track), the option chosen happened to also serve interests beyond security. The security justification was sufficient to act. But sufficiency is not the same as exclusivity: a decision can be genuinely motivated by security and simultaneously produce trade-route consequences that benefit the decision-maker's other interests.

| Actor | Security motivation (real) | Concurrent structural effect (observable) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Israel | Eliminate nuclear-threshold adversary, degrade proxy networks | Regional rival removed |
| USA | Nonproliferation, regional stability | Iran-based transit corridors destroyed; IMEC alternative gains momentum; EU pushed toward US-controlled energy and trade channels |

We do not have access to the internal deliberations that would tell us how much weight trade-route effects carried in the decision. What we can observe is that the option chosen — among several available — was the one that maximized these secondary structural effects.

### Who profits

The Center for American Progress characterized the administration's approach as "corporate-style short-termism in foreign policy." Whether that is fair, the corporate beneficiaries of rising EU-US dependency are identifiable and their gains are measurable. US LNG producers saw their EU market share climb from 24% toward a projected 75–80%. US oil companies gained pricing power at $110/barrel while absorbing Venezuelan reserves worth some 300 billion barrels. Digital trade provisions embedded in the EU-US deal served the tech sector. NATO spending pressure linked to trade concessions served the defense industry ([CAP: Trump's Military Intervention Serves Big Oil](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/trumps-military-intervention-in-venezuela-serves-big-oil-not-the-american-people/); [Global Witness: Why the US Attacked Venezuela](https://globalwitness.org/en/campaigns/fossil-fuels/why-the-us-attacked-venezuela-oil-sanctions-and-maduro/); [Democracy Collaborative: Venezuela's Oil Seizure](https://www.democracycollaborative.org/whatwethink/venezuelas-oil-seizure)).

The fact that companies profit from geopolitical shifts does not, by itself, prove those shifts were engineered for that purpose. US umbrella companies profit from rain. But when an administration has been described — by its own allies — as transactional and corporate-influenced, and when the same administration has coined an explicit doctrine of hemispheric dominance, and when every major foreign policy action it takes produces measurable benefits for the same corporate constituencies, the "just a coincidence" explanation starts to require more defense than the structural one.

## Russia's Role

The EU Council president called Russia "the only winner of the US-Israel war on Iran" ([Al Jazeera: Russia the Only Winner](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/10/russia-the-only-winner-of-us-israel-war-on-iran-eu-council-president)). That is a political statement from an interested party — the EU Council president has reasons to frame the outcome in terms that validate EU policy. But the underlying data is worth examining on its own terms.

### What Russia lost

This is the part of the analysis that most commentators skip. Russia was itself a party to the Iranian transit agreements. It had co-invested in the INSTC, the Rasht-Astara railway, and the Eastern Corridor Roadmap. It stood to benefit from southward trade access to the Indian Ocean through Iran, and from reducing its own dependence on Chinese-controlled routes. The destruction of Iran's transit role was not cost-free for Moscow — it destroyed infrastructure Russia had co-funded and routes Russia intended to use.

### What Russia gained — and whether it outweighs the losses

The simplest explanation for Russia's passivity — no advanced air defense systems provided, no precision munitions, no UN Security Council veto, just verbal condemnation and minimal intelligence sharing — is military constraint. Russia is pinned down in Ukraine, its systems partially depleted, and direct confrontation with the US would be existential. You don't need a strategic masterstroke to explain why Russia didn't rush to Iran's defense; impotence is sufficient ([Carnegie: Why Are China and Russia Not Rushing to Help Iran?](https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2026/03/china-russia-rescue-iran); [Foreign Affairs: Why Is Russia Watching Iran Burn?](https://www.foreignaffairs.com/russia/why-russia-watching-iran-burn); [Chatham House: Iran War Exposes Limits of Russia's Leverage](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/03/iran-war-exposes-limits-russias-leverage-fragmenting-regional-order)).

But impotence and windfall are not mutually exclusive. Russia's fossil fuel revenues rose from $501 million per day in January 2026 to $554 million per day within two weeks of the strikes — an incremental gain of roughly $53 million per day, or about $19 billion annualized. To put this in context, Russia's pre-2022 fossil fuel revenue averaged $700–800 million per day, so the "windfall" still leaves Russia below its pre-sanctions baseline. It is a partial recovery, not a triumph. Still, it is a recovery that Western sanctions had been designed to prevent, and it arrived courtesy of a Western military operation. As one Ukrainian outlet headlined: "Russia couldn't fix its oil revenues. The US Air Force did it" ([Euronews: Russia Pocketing Billions](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/03/19/russia-pocketing-billions-from-two-weeks-of-war-in-iran-data-shows); [Euromaidan Press: Russia Couldn't Fix Its Oil Revenues](https://euromaidanpress.com/2026/03/20/russia-iran-war-oil-revenues-sanctions/); [Bloomberg: Russia Oil Revenue Surge](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/russia-s-big-gulf-war-oil-win-is-yet-to-fill-putin-s-war-chest)).

Beyond revenue, the war reshuffled energy supply chains in Russia's favor. With Iranian heavy crude off the market, Asian refineries designed for that grade are switching to Russian Urals blend. Tankers carrying Russian oil have been changing course mid-ocean as Asian economies scramble for alternatives to Gulf supplies shut out by the Hormuz closure ([Al Jazeera: Will Russian Oil Be the Biggest Winner?](https://www.aljazeera.com/features/2026/3/20/will-russian-oil-be-the-biggest-winner-in-the-us-israel-war-on-iran); [The Diplomat: Iran War Boosts Russia's Role in Asia Energy](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/how-the-iran-war-could-boost-russias-role-in-asias-energy-future/); [Carnegie: How Trump's Wars Are Boosting Russian Oil Exports](https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2026/03/us-china-russia-iran-oil)). The destruction of Iran-based transit corridors also makes Russia's Northern Sea Route the only non-maritime alternative for China-Europe freight — increasing China's dependence on Russian goodwill at a moment when analysts describe Russia as fearing Chinese "creeping encroachment" in Siberia and the Arctic ([Arctic Institute: Russia-China Northern Sea Route](https://www.thearcticinstitute.org/pragmatic-approach-conceptual-divergences-russia-china-relations-case-northern-sea-route/); [PIR Center: Dragon, Bear, and Hard Times](https://pircenter.org/en/editions/%E2%84%96-3-12-2026-dragon-bear-and-hard-times-the-current-state-and-prospects-of-russian-chinese-relations/)).

And the oil windfall directly funds the war in Ukraine — the conflict that closed the EU's eastern trade direction in the first place. Sanctions had squeezed Russian revenues to post-invasion lows by January 2026; the Iran war reversed this within weeks ([FPRI: From Tehran to Donbas](https://www.fpri.org/article/2026/03/from-tehran-to-donbas-what-the-iran-war-means-for-russia-and-ukraine/); [Axios: Russia and Oil Exporters Are Winning](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/11/oil-iran-china-russia)).

Whether the gains outweigh the losses is debatable. Russia lost transit infrastructure it co-funded, but gained revenue, supply-chain positioning, and leverage over China. A marginal argument — but one that favors the hypothesis that Russia's passivity, whatever its cause, was not irrational.

### Convergent outcomes without convergent planning

| Actor | Stated position | Observable gains |
| --- | --- | --- |
| USA | Nuclear threat, drug trafficking, hemispheric security | Transit route control, rising LNG market share, Venezuelan oil, EU trade deal |
| Israel | Existential nuclear threat | Nuclear program degraded, proxy networks weakened |
| Russia | Verbal condemnation (no material support for Iran) | Oil revenue recovery, supply-chain repositioning, Arctic leverage, Ukraine war funding |

The US and Israel coordinated openly — joint strikes, shared targeting, combined military operations. What did not require coordination was Russia's role. Moscow pursued its own interests from the sideline, and the structural outcome — Iran's transit network destroyed, China's supply lines disrupted, the EU's alternatives narrowed — served all three actors without all three needing to agree on anything. The US and Israel acted together for their own converging reasons. Russia benefited by doing nothing.

## China's Silence — the Taiwan Trade

If the EU is the party being cornered by this pattern, China is the party being cut off. The Iran war destroyed China's emerging overland corridor to Europe, closed the Strait of Hormuz (through which roughly 20% of China's oil imports transited), and came just two months after the US seized Venezuela — which had been sending 81.7% of its oil exports to China. In total, approximately 17% of China's oil imports were severed from two directions simultaneously.

China's response was near-silence.

Beijing issued diplomatic condemnations and provided covert support — intelligence sharing, satellite navigation, radar systems, electronic warfare technologies that augmented Iran's targeting capabilities ([Small Wars Journal: China-Iran Intelligence Cooperation](https://smallwarsjournal.com/2026/03/20/china-iran-intelligence-2026-war/); [Al Jazeera: How Russia and China Help Iran See the Battlefield](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/12/the-war-of-signals-how-russia-and-china-help-iran-see-the-battlefield)). But no troops, no overt military assistance, no Security Council veto. China let its $400 billion strategic partner — and $100+ billion in BRI infrastructure investment — absorb the full force of a US-Israeli military operation without meaningful defense ([The Diplomat: The Decapitation of Iran — What Tehran's Chaos Means for China](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/the-decapitation-of-iran-what-tehrans-chaos-means-for-china/)).

Why? The answer appears to be Taiwan.

During a pre-war phone call between Xi Jinping and Trump, the Chinese readout emphasized Taiwan while barely mentioning Iran. Xi drew a red line on Taiwanese independence and warned against planned US arms sales. Trump subsequently delayed a multibillion-dollar arms package to Taiwan. Beijing, it seems, traded Iran for a concession on its core interest ([Al Jazeera: China's Silence on Iran Reveals Its True Priorities](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/19/chinas-silence-on-iran-reveals-its-true-priorities)).

The calculation behind this has a brutal logic. Taiwan is existential for Beijing — it is the one issue that could trigger a direct US-China war. Iran is a partner, but not an ally in the formal sense; China has no mutual defense treaty with Tehran. Beijing assessed that the conflict posed no existential economic threat if limited to airstrikes under four months. And — critically — any future Iranian government, whatever its political orientation, will remain economically dependent on Chinese investment. Iran has no alternative patron of comparable scale. China can wait ([Euronews: China Treads Carefully on Iran War](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/03/18/china-treads-carefully-on-iran-war-as-it-balances-energy-security-and-neutrality); [Chatham House: China Is Playing the Long Game Over Iran](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/02/china-playing-long-game-over-iran)).

What this reveals about China's structural position is as important as what it reveals about the EU's. The world's second-largest economy, holding $100 billion in committed infrastructure investment in Iran, could not — or would not — protect its own strategic partner when the US decided to act. Beijing publicly denounces "might makes right," but as Al Jazeera's analyst observed, "its abandonment of Iran when core interests clash reveals that might still sets the limits of principle."

For the EU, the lesson is sobering. If China cannot shield its own $400 billion partnership from US military action, the EU — which has no comparable military capability and is already deeper in US dependency — has even less room to maneuver independently. The Iran war was a demonstration of what happens when US interests and a partner's interests collide. Europe watched. The question is whether it understood what it was watching.

## India — the Linchpin Under Pressure

India deserves its own section, because it sits at the intersection of both competing corridor architectures — the US-backed one (IMEC) and the non-Western one (INSTC/Chabahar) — and the war has damaged India's position in both.

### The dual corridor anchor

The India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor — IMEC — has India in its name for a reason. Without Indian ports (Mumbai, Mundra), Indian throughput, and Indian cooperation, there is no US-backed alternative to the Chinese routes. Trump called IMEC "one of the greatest trade routes in all of history" in early 2025. Modi and Trump jointly identified it as a strategic priority. The EU-India trade deal signed in January 2026 was partly designed to give IMEC momentum ([Atlantic Council: IMEC Connectivity Report](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/in-depth-research-reports/report/the-india-middle-east-europe-economic-corridor-connectivity-in-an-era-of-geopolitical-uncertainty/); [TRENDS: IMEC on Pause](https://trendsresearch.org/insight/imec-on-pause-how-and-when-the-corridor-can-regain-momentum-amid-india-u-s-friction/); [Al Jazeera: India-Israel Axis — IMEC and I2U2](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/26/india-israel-axis-what-are-the-imec-corridor-i2u2-grouping-modi-spoke-of)).

But India was simultaneously the southern anchor of the non-Western alternative. Chabahar Port — India's $120+ million investment in Iran's southeastern coast — was its gateway to Afghanistan and Central Asia, bypassing Pakistan entirely. The INSTC, the 7,200 km multimodal network connecting Mumbai to Moscow through Iran, was designed to give India trade access to Russia and Europe without passing through either Chinese or US-controlled corridors. India had signed a 10-year operational contract for Chabahar in 2024 and was pouring in equipment as recently as August 2025 ([Republic World: India's Golden Gate to Central Asia Teeters](https://www.republicworld.com/world-news/india-s-golden-gate-to-central-asia-teeters-khamenei-s-assassination-threatens-chabahar-port-and-instc-dreams); [Maritime Gateway: India Keeping Tab on Chabahar Port](https://www.maritimegateway.com/india-keeping-tab-on-chabahar-port-instc-projects/)).

The war destroyed the second corridor and made India more dependent on the first — the one controlled by the US.

### What the war did to India

The damage was immediate and multi-dimensional.

The Strait of Hormuz closure cut off roughly 50–53% of India's crude oil supply — about 2.5 to 2.8 million barrels per day. India imports 88% of its crude, and over half of it transited Hormuz. Oil surged from $70 to above $100/barrel, feeding inflation, weakening the rupee, and threatening GDP growth. India's strategic petroleum reserves cover roughly 9.5 days; oil marketing companies hold an additional 64.5 days of storage — still below the 90-day minimum ([IEEFA: India's Oil Strategy Amid US-Iran Tensions](https://ieefa.org/resources/energy-power-play-indias-oil-and-gas-strategy-amid-us-iran-tensions); [American Bazaar: India Faces Energy Risks](https://americanbazaaronline.com/2026/03/16/india-faces-energy-risks-as-hormuz-closure-disrupts-global-oil-flows-476917/); [Bloomberg: India Growth at Risk](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/india-growth-seen-at-risk-as-iran-war-shows-no-sign-of-easing)).

US airstrikes targeted military facilities adjacent to Chabahar port. The India-funded Shahid Beheshti terminal was reportedly not hit directly, but the military installations surrounding it were struck on day one. The Chabahar-Zahedan railway — the critical missing link for INSTC, scheduled for 2026 completion — now faces indefinite delay as Iran shifts from civil engineering to wartime mobilization. The US declined to extend India's sanctions waiver for Chabahar operations beyond April 2026 ([NewsX: US Strikes Near Chabahar Port](https://www.newsx.com/world/chabahar-port-attack-us-iran-israel-war-news-update-tehran-gulf-of-oman-united-states-iran-port-missile-strike-183529/); [TRT World: Did India Withdraw from Chabahar Under US Pressure?](https://www.trtworld.com/article/e9605c09d611)).

Four hundred thousand metric tons of basmati rice sat stranded at Indian ports. Aviation losses reached an estimated Rs 80,500 crore (~$9.6 billion) weekly. Nine million Indian workers in Gulf countries — generating approximately $135 billion in annual remittances — faced an uncertain future ([The Wire: Why India Must Assert Itself](https://m.thewire.in/article/diplomacy/non-alignment-is-not-always-a-good-foriegn-policy)).

### The 30-day waiver — and what it reveals

The most telling detail is how the US managed India's energy crisis. The Trump administration issued Treasury General License 133: a 30-day waiver allowing Indian refineries to purchase Russian oil stranded at sea — the very Russian oil the US had spent years pressuring India to stop buying. The waiver covers Russian-origin crude loaded before March 5, valid until April 4 ([CNBC: US Offers India 30-Day Waiver](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/06/us-india-waiver-russian-oil-iran-war-energy-supply-worries-.html); [Euronews: US Offers India Waiver](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/03/06/us-offers-india-30-day-waiver-to-buy-stranded-russian-oil-amid-iran-war-shocks)).

The contradiction is plain. The US created the energy crisis (by attacking Iran and closing Hormuz), then offered the relief (a temporary waiver to buy Russian oil), on terms it controls (30 days, revocable). As CNN headlined: "Trump wanted India off Russian oil. His war with Iran is now undermining that goal" ([CNN: Trump Wanted India Off Russian Oil](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/10/india/india-russia-oil-middle-east-war-intl-hnk)).

India went from being a sovereign multi-aligned power — trading with the US, Russia, Iran, Israel, and the Gulf simultaneously — to being managed by 30-day US Treasury waivers. The same pattern as the EU: dependency increasing through crisis management, with the US controlling both the problem and the solution.

### Modi's silence

India's response has been near-silence. PM Modi visited Israel on February 25–26 — less than 48 hours before the strikes began. The visit elevated the relationship to a "special strategic partnership" with 16 agreements signed. Whether Modi was briefed on the impending operation is disputed; Israel's ambassador to India denied it, stating "the opportunity presented itself only after Prime Minister Modi had departed" ([Al Jazeera: Modi Visits Israel](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/2/25/indias-modi-visits-israel-whats-on-the-agenda-and-why-it-matters); [Bloomberg: Modi Faces Backlash Over Israel Trip](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/modi-faces-backlash-over-israel-trip-days-before-iran-attacks); [Outlook India: The Curious Timing of Modi's Israel Visit](https://www.outlookindia.com/international/the-curious-timing-of-pm-modis-israel-visit); [The Diplomat: Modi Puts India Firmly in Israel-US Camp](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/modi-puts-india-firmly-in-the-israel-us-camp/)).

Since the strikes, India has expressed "deep concern" and called for "dialogue and restraint." It is the only founding BRICS member that has not condemned the US-Israeli operation outright. India's opposition slammed the silence as "ill-timed" alignment with Israel; The Wire argued that "non-alignment was never intended as a mode of silence" ([Nikkei Asia: India's Opposition Slams Modi's Israel Visit](https://asia.nikkei.com/spotlight/iran-tensions/india-s-opposition-slams-modi-s-ill-timed-israel-visit-before-iran-attack); [The Wire: Why India Must Assert Itself](https://m.thewire.in/article/diplomacy/non-alignment-is-not-always-a-good-foriegn-policy)).

### What India's position means for the EU

India's situation is a mirror of the EU's — and in some ways more revealing, because India was supposed to be the anchor of the US-designed alternative corridor. If IMEC is the replacement for Iran-based routes, and IMEC depends on India, and India is itself being squeezed by the same war that destroyed the Iranian routes — burning through oil reserves, losing Chabahar, accepting 30-day US waivers — then the US-backed alternative is not as independent of US leverage as it appears. The corridor that is supposed to give the EU and India more options runs through a country that just demonstrated it cannot resist US pressure on any major dimension: energy, defense, or diplomacy.

The pattern extends beyond Europe. China traded Iran for a concession on Taiwan. India traded its multi-alignment for continued US partnership. The EU traded energy independence for US LNG. In each case, a crisis — often created or intensified by US action — reduced the other party's options and increased its dependency. Whether this is strategy or opportunism, the structural result is the same: the US holds more cards after each crisis than it held before.

## The Other Actors Caught in Between

### Turkey — the corridor that lost its eastern terminus

Turkey was supposed to be the western anchor of the Iran transit network. The $1.6 billion Marand-Cheshmeh Soraya railway would have created a direct Iran-Turkey-EU rail connection; bilateral trade was targeted at $30 billion annually. Turkey also markets itself to China as the western terminus of the Middle Corridor linking Asia to Europe.

The war paralyzed all of this. For Turkish exporters, Iran was the primary land bridge to Central Asian markets — that bridge is now impassable. Three Iranian ballistic missiles have been intercepted over Turkish airspace since February 28, with NATO air defense assets shooting them down on March 4, 9, and 13. Ankara has absorbed these violations while refusing to declare which side it is on — a calculated performance of non-alignment designed to extract leverage from every party ([FDD: Turkey's Red Lines in Iran Conflict](https://www.fdd.org/analysis/2026/03/17/what-are-turkeys-red-lines-in-the-iran-conflict/); [FDD: Turkey Condemns US Strikes on Iran](https://www.fdd.org/analysis/2026/03/01/backing-the-tehran-regimes-survival-turkey-condemns-u-s-strikes-on-iran/); [Modern Diplomacy: Iran's Weakening Is Turkey's Opportunity — and Its Trap](https://moderndiplomacy.eu/2026/03/03/irans-weakening-is-turkeys-opportunity-and-its-trap/)).

Erdoğan condemned both the US-Israeli strikes and Iran's retaliation, expressed "sadness" at Khamenei's killing, and called for negotiations. Turkey faces economic fallout (trade disruption, refugee pressure, energy costs), security risks (missiles over its territory), and the loss of its Iran corridor — but it also sees opportunity. With Iran weakened, Turkey could emerge as a more dominant regional power. Israel's INSS assessed that "Iran's stability is a Turkish national security interest" — but Turkey's actions suggest it is already positioning for a post-Iran regional order ([INSS: Iran's Stability as Turkish Security Interest](https://www.inss.org.il/publication/turkey-iran-2026/); [Turkish Minute: Turkey Faces Risks Amid Iran War](https://www.turkishminute.com/2026/03/02/turkey-faces-economic-security-and-migration-risks-amid-iran-war-experts-warn/); [TRENDS: Decoding Turkey's Strategic Calculus](https://trendsresearch.org/insight/decoding-turkiyes-strategic-calculus-on-the-war-in-iran/)).

Turkey's position matters for the EU because Turkey controls the Bosphorus and Dardanelles straits — the only maritime passage between the Mediterranean and the Black Sea. If Turkey tilts toward the US camp, another chokepoint falls under US-allied control. If it maintains its multi-alignment act, it becomes the last genuinely independent transit state between Europe and Asia — and the pressure on it to choose sides will only intensify.

### The Gulf states — attacked by their own ally's enemy

Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Oman have all been struck by Iranian missiles or drones since the war began — even though none launched attacks on Iran from their territory. The UAE alone has absorbed nearly 2,000 Iranian missiles and drones. The Jebel Ali port, multiple hotels, and Abu Dhabi port infrastructure have been hit ([CNBC: Gulf States' Defensive Stance Won't Last Forever](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/19/gulf-states-iran-attacks-retaliation-strikes-energy-oil-gas-water-strait-of-hormuz.html); [ME Council: Limits of Gulf Neutrality](https://mecouncil.org/publication/limits-gulf-neutrality-us-israel-iran-war/)).

The Gulf states tried to hedge. They maintained economic ties with Iran while hosting US military bases — the classic GCC balancing act. The war exposed the limits of this strategy. They cautioned the US against regime change, denied permission to use their territory for strikes, and attempted neutrality. Iran attacked them anyway. Saudi Arabia's foreign minister warned that "the patience being exhibited is not unlimited" ([Arab Center DC: GCC States and the War on Iran](https://arabcenterdc.org/resource/the-gcc-states-and-the-war-on-iran-rethinking-responses-to-unwanted-consequences/); [Chatham House: Should Gulf States Join the War?](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/03/should-gulf-arab-states-join-war-against-iran)).

The Hormuz closure forced Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Kuwait, and Iraq to cut oil production as storage filled and barrels had nowhere to go. Paradoxically, the production cuts combined with price surges could improve Saudi Arabia's budget deficit from 5.8% of GDP to 3–3.5% if prices average $80/barrel — making the Gulf states crisis beneficiaries despite being under physical attack ([Semafor: The Gulf's Shock Absorber Is Still Oil](https://www.semafor.com/article/03/19/2026/in-the-iran-war-the-gulfs-shock-absorber-is-still-oil)).

For the broader analysis, the Gulf states matter because they host IMEC's physical infrastructure. Saudi Arabia's Landbridge railway, the UAE's port networks, and Qatar's LNG facilities are the backbone of the US-backed alternative corridor to Europe. These assets are now under Iranian missile fire. The US-designed replacement for the Iran corridors runs through countries that are themselves being attacked because of the war that destroyed the Iran corridors. The circularity is remarkable.

And when the war ends, analysts expect "strategic continuity" — the Gulf states will remain US-aligned, host US bases, and serve as IMEC infrastructure. Iran's regional deterrent will be degraded. The Gulf states' hedging strategy with Iran will have been forcibly resolved in the US's favor ([Foreign Policy: After the War, Expect Strategic Continuity from Iran's Arab Neighbors](https://foreignpolicy.com/2026/03/20/iran-trump-israel-gulf-saudi-arabia-uae-oman-qatar/); [Al Jazeera: US-Israeli War Could Rewrite Gulf Security Calculations](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/1/the-us-israeli-war-on-iran-could-rewrite-gulf-security-calculations)).

### The Pacific front — AUKUS, the Quad, and the architecture forming around China

While the Iran war dominates headlines, the US is simultaneously building a dense security architecture across the Pacific. The Quad (US, Japan, Australia, India) provides the diplomatic framework. AUKUS (US, UK, Australia) provides the nuclear submarine and advanced technology backbone. Bilateral alliances with Japan, South Korea, and the Philippines provide the forward-deployed military presence. Japan opened a permanent mission to NATO in Brussels in January 2025. The US is consulting with Japan, Canada, New Zealand, and South Korea on AUKUS Pillar II advanced capability projects ([USSC: Moving the NATO-IP4 Partnership Forward](https://www.ussc.edu.au/beyond-alignment-moving-the-nato-ip4-partnership-forward); [Wiley: From Lattice Alliances to an Asian NATO?](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/app5.70070); [NATO Veterans: China-Japan-South Korea Dynamics for NATO](https://nato-veterans.org/china-japan-south-korea-security-dynamics-implications-for-nato-decision-making-in-the-indo-pacific/)).

This is not a single alliance — it is a web of interlocking arrangements that collectively surrounds China's maritime periphery from the Sea of Japan to the Strait of Malacca. Japan is rearming ($70 billion, offensive missiles, combat drones). Australia is acquiring nuclear submarines. South Korea is expanding defense cooperation. The Philippines is granting enhanced base access. Taiwan is the fulcrum: its semiconductor monopoly makes its defense a technology-security imperative, not just a geopolitical one.

The EU is absent from this architecture. It has no Pacific military presence, no seat in the Quad, no role in AUKUS, and no bilateral defense treaties in the region. Europe's access to the fastest-growing economic zone on Earth is mediated entirely through US-led structures — and the price of access is alignment with US positions on China.

## The Three-Front Structure

Step back from the individual closures and a larger geometry emerges.

The United States is positioned on two ocean flanks — the Atlantic facing Europe, the Pacific facing Asia — and the landmass between them, Eurasia, is fragmenting.

On the **Atlantic front**, the EU is being drawn into deepening dependency: US LNG replacing Russian gas, US fabs replacing Taiwanese chips, US weapons replacing European defense industry, US trade deals extracting trillion-euro commitments. Every eastern alternative for the EU — Russia, Iran, the Arctic — is either sanctioned, destroyed, or contested.

On the **Pacific front**, a military-technological alliance architecture is forming around China: AUKUS submarines, Quad diplomacy, Japanese rearmament, Philippine base access, Korean defense cooperation. China's own outreach corridors — the BRI through Iran, Venezuelan oil, the Polar Silk Road — have been severed or degraded. Taiwan, the semiconductor chokepoint, sits under increasing pressure with its backup supply being built in Arizona, not Shenzhen.

In the **Eurasian interior** between these two ocean flanks, the picture is fragmentation. Iran is destroyed. Afghanistan is under Taliban rule. India-Pakistan exchanged missile fire in 2025. Central Asia is forced to choose between the US-backed Middle Corridor and Russian-controlled northern routes. The Sahel has suffered nine coups. The Levant is rubble. Turkey absorbs Iranian missiles while calculating its next move. The Gulf states are under attack from Iran while hosting the US-backed replacement corridor.

The old geopolitical theorists — Mackinder, Spykman — debated whether power flows from controlling the Eurasian "Heartland" or the coastal "Rimland." What we may be observing is a third option: you don't need to control the Heartland if you can ensure it remains fractured, and you don't need to invade the Rimland if you can bind it through dependency. Control both ocean flanks, keep the interior broken, and the rest follows.

This is a hypothesis, not a proven strategy. We don't know whether anyone in Washington thinks in these terms. But the structural outcome — fragmented Eurasia, dependent Europe, contained China, managed India, cornered Gulf — is consistent with this reading regardless of whether it was planned.

| West (USA) | 24% of EU LNG | 57–60% of EU LNG, rising to 75–80% | US becomes gatekeeper of EU energy + chips + defense |

Two things stand out in this table. First, the EU's trade alternatives have narrowed across every direction simultaneously — some through the EU's own past choices (Russian gas dependency), some through events beyond its control (Levant destruction), some through US actions (Venezuela, Iran, Donroe Doctrine), and some through great-power competition (Arctic, Africa). The causes are heterogeneous. The structural result is uniform.

Second — and this is the dimension that receives too little attention — the closures don't just isolate the EU. They sever the EU-China commercial link from multiple directions at once. The overland corridor through Iran: destroyed. The maritime route through Hormuz: blocked. Venezuelan oil to China: seized. The Arctic: controlled by Russia, contested by the US. The only major EU-China trade route still functioning at full capacity is maritime via the Cape of Good Hope — which adds 10–14 days and 30% fuel costs compared to the Suez route, structurally disadvantaging Chinese goods relative to American goods that cross the Atlantic in 7–10 days.

The hypothesis that presents itself — and it is a hypothesis, not an accusation — is this: when an administration that describes its foreign policy as transactional and has coined a doctrine of hemispheric dominance is presented with a series of crises, each of which can be resolved in multiple ways, did it consistently choose the option that maximized EU dependency and minimized China's access to European markets? We don't have access to the internal deliberations. We can observe the pattern of choices and their cumulative effect.

## Beyond Trade Routes — the Wider Dependencies

The analysis so far concerns physical corridors. But the EU's shrinking optionality extends into domains where geography matters less: semiconductors, defense procurement, mineral supply, and the emerging architecture of Pacific alliances. Not all of these are directly connected to the trade-route closures described above — global instability has many causes, and it would be intellectually dishonest to recruit every bad development into a single thesis. But several of these dynamics materially compound the EU's position and deserve examination on their own terms.

### Semiconductors and Taiwan

Taiwan produces over 60% of the world's semiconductors and more than 90% of the most advanced chips below 7 nanometers. Taiwan and South Korea together account for 100% of sub-10nm production. The EU has less than 10% of global capacity and zero production at advanced nodes — its facilities make mature chips (14nm and above) for automotive and industrial use ([EST Think Tank: Semiconductors as Key Strategic Assets](https://esthinktank.com/2025/11/25/semiconductors-as-key-strategic-assets-navigating-global-and-european-security-challenges/); [ScienceDirect: Taiwan's Semiconductor Industry and Geopolitical Challenges](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0308596125000485)).

China's military pressure on Taiwan is intensifying — a 7% defense spending increase to $277 billion at the 2026 NPC, the largest war game around Taiwan since 2022 in December 2025 — and while invasion is not the most likely near-term outcome, the risk of miscalculation is rising ([AEI: China & Taiwan Update March 2026](https://www.aei.org/articles/china-taiwan-update-march-1-2026/); [Defcon Level: Taiwan-China Conflict 2026](https://www.defconlevel.com/taiwan-china-conflict)). A full US-China conflict over Taiwan would cost roughly $10.6 trillion in the first year — 9.6% of global GDP. For European automakers alone, 18% of semiconductor needs come from Taiwan: BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Volkswagen would lose up to 1.9 million vehicles ([Insurance Journal: The $10 Trillion Fight](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/international/2026/02/12/857770.htm)).

What matters for the dependency question is where the backup supply is being built. TSMC is investing $140 billion in Arizona fabs. Its only European fab — in Dresden — makes mature-node automotive chips, not the advanced processors that drive AI and mobile computing. The EU Chips Act aims to reach 20% of global production by 2030, but that target concerns all nodes, not the advanced ones that carry the most strategic weight. If Taiwan comes under sustained pressure, the advanced chips flow through America first. Not because the US closed a route, but because it is the only place where an alternative is being built at scale.

### Japan's rearmament and the Pacific architecture

Japan's defense budget hit ¥11 trillion ($70 billion) in fiscal 2025 — achieving the 2% GDP target two years early. The fiscal 2026 budget rose a further 9.4% in yen terms to ¥9 trillion; the apparent drop in dollar terms (to roughly $58 billion) reflects yen depreciation, not a spending cut. Japan is on track to become the world's third-largest defense spender ([USNI News: Japan Defense Spending $70 Billion](https://news.usni.org/2025/12/03/japan-poised-to-increase-defense-spending-to-70-billion-2-of-its-gdp); [Euronews: Japan Third-Largest Defence Spender](https://www.euronews.com/business/2025/12/26/japan-on-track-to-become-worlds-third-largest-defence-spender); [PBS: Japan Record Defense Budget](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/japans-cabinet-approves-record-defense-budget-aiming-to-deter-china-as-tensions-grow)).

The shift is qualitative, not just fiscal. Japan is acquiring offensive missile capability (Type-12 with 1,000km range, ¥970 billion), developing a next-generation fighter with Britain and Italy (deployment 2035, with AI-operated drone wingmen), and deploying the SHIELD unmanned system across air, sea, and underwater domains. Prime Minister Takaichi has stated that Chinese military action against Taiwan could trigger a Japanese military response ([The Diplomat: Japan Accelerates Defense Buildup](https://thediplomat.com/2025/12/japan-accelerates-defense-buildup-with-record-budget-and-expanded-unmanned-capabilities/); [Defense News: Japan Moving from Self-Defense to Military Buildup](https://www.defensenews.com/global/asia-pacific/2025/12/31/pacifist-japan-moving-from-exclusive-self-defense-to-military-buildup/); [International Crisis Group: Embracing Arms — Securing Japan](https://www.crisisgroup.org/rpt/asia-pacific/japan-united-states-china/351-embracing-arms-securing-japan-new-era-crisis)).

All of this happens under the US security umbrella. The US is building a Pacific alliance architecture — Japan, South Korea, Australia, the Philippines — and the EU is not part of it. Europe has no seat at the table in the fastest-growing economic region, faces mounting pressure to align with US positions on China (tariffs, technology export controls, semiconductor restrictions) or risk exclusion, and is caught in a military-industrial feedback loop: Japan and Australia buy US weapons, strengthening the US defense base that Europe's own 63% post-2022 procurement dependence feeds into ([Bruegel: Europe's Dependence on US Foreign Military Sales](https://www.bruegel.org/policy-brief/europes-dependence-us-foreign-military-sales-and-what-do-about-it)).

### North Korea and Russia's technology exchange

Russia is funding North Korea's nuclear programme and sharing submarine, stealth, and missile technologies — in return for ammunition, weapons, and manpower for the Ukraine war. The partnership has moved from transactional to strategic ([RUSI: Russia Actively Funding North Korea's Nuclear Programme](https://www.rusi.org/explore-our-research/publications/commentary/russia-now-actively-funding-north-koreas-nuclear-programme); [Heritage Foundation: Russia to Supercharge North Korea's Nuclear Program](https://www.heritage.org/defense/report/the-potential-russia-supercharge-north-koreas-nuclear-and-missile-program); [Chatham House: North Korea and Russia's Dangerous Partnership](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2024/12/north-korea-and-russias-dangerous-partnership/how-north-korea-benefits)).

North Korea tested its Hwasong-20 ICBM in 2025 and intends to complete missile factory construction by 2026. Kim Jong Un's five-year military plan targets tactical nuclear warheads, nuclear submarines, and submarine-launched ballistic missiles. Russian technology transfer is projected to enable significant advances within three to five years ([The Diplomat: Russia's Support for North Korea's Nuclear Modernization](https://thediplomat.com/2025/04/what-russias-support-means-for-north-koreas-nuclear-modernization/); [CRS: North Korea's Nuclear Weapons and Missile Programs](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF10472)).

The European dimension is indirect but real. A nuclear-emboldened North Korea ties down US military resources in the Pacific, making the American security guarantee to Europe more strained and more expensive to maintain. Every North Korean provocation justifies additional US arms sales to Japan, South Korea, and Australia — further growing the US defense-industrial base that European procurement increasingly depends on. And Russia's willingness to transfer nuclear-applicable technology as a bargaining chip introduces a kind of instability that no trade corridor — physical or strategic — can be insulated from.

### India and Pakistan — the May 2025 crisis

On the night of May 6–7, 2025, India launched Operation Sindoor: drone and missile strikes on nine targets in Pakistani-held Kashmir and Pakistan proper. It was India's most extensive military operation since 1971 — and the first full-scale use of combat drones between nuclear-armed states. Over four days, cross-border exchanges hit 11 Pakistani cities before a ceasefire on May 10 ([Wikipedia: 2025 India-Pakistan Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_India%E2%80%93Pakistan_crisis); [Stimson Center: Four Days in May](https://www.stimson.org/2025/four-days-in-may-the-india-pakistan-crisis-of-2025/); [Foreign Policy: The Year That Took India and Pakistan to the Brink](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/12/25/india-pakistan-conflict-kashmir-south-asia/)).

The ceasefire held, but as 2025 ended, dialogue remained limited, the Indus Waters Treaty was suspended, and commercial flights were disrupted ([Al Jazeera: What Did India and Pakistan Gain and Lose?](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/5/14/what-did-india-and-pakistan-gain-and-lose-in-their-military-standoff); [CSIS: What Led to the Crisis Between India and Pakistan?](https://www.csis.org/analysis/what-led-recent-crisis-between-india-and-pakistan)).

For the trade-route analysis, the relevance is specific: the IMEC corridor — the US-backed alternative to Iran-based routes — depends on Indian stability and regional cooperation. The 2025 crisis demonstrated how quickly that assumption can break down between two nuclear powers. Meanwhile, the INSTC connecting India, Iran, and Russia is strained at the Iran end (war) and complicated at the India end (ongoing tension with Pakistan, Taliban-controlled Afghanistan on the overland branch). The corridor is not severed by the India-Pakistan crisis in the way it is by the Iran war — the main INSTC routing goes by sea from India to Bandar Abbas — but the broader point holds: the alternatives to Atlantic trade all run through regions where nuclear-armed states have recently exchanged fire or where active conflicts are underway.

### Central Asia — the mineral corridor question

Central Asian states have practiced what analysts call "omni-alignment" — maintaining productive relationships with China, Russia, the EU, and the US simultaneously. The EU upgraded relations to a strategic partnership at the Samarkand summit in April 2025, focusing on transport, energy, and critical raw materials. MOUs with Kazakhstan (2022) and Uzbekistan (2024) target rare earths, uranium, and lithium — resources essential to the energy transition ([Caspian Post: Central Asia's Strategic Balancing](https://caspianpost.com/analytics/central-asia-s-strategic-balancing-russia-china-and-the-west-in-competition); [Clingendael: The Geopolitics of Central Asia](https://www.clingendael.org/pub/2025/central-asia-emerging-from-the-shadows/2-the-geopolitics-of-central-asia/); [Times of Central Asia: 2025 — The Year Central Asia Stepped Onto the Global Stage](https://timesca.com/2025-the-year-central-asia-stepped-onto-the-global-stage/)).

China is now the largest trading partner for most Central Asian republics, surpassing Russia. The China-Central Asia Summit in Astana (June 2025) formalized long-term cooperation ([IDOS: Geopolitics and Development in Central Asia](https://www.idos-research.de/fileadmin/user_upload/pdfs/publikationen/discussion_paper/2026/DP_2.2026.pdf); [SpecialEurasia: Central Asia Geopolitical Risk 2026](https://www.specialeurasia.com/2025/12/21/central-asia-risk-2026/)).

The Iran war hit these countries hard. Iran's transit volumes had tripled since 2020; the Astana Times warned in February 2026 that "the southern corridor through Iran is critical for Central Asia" — weeks before it was destroyed ([Astana Times: Southern Corridor Critical for Central Asia](https://astanatimes.com/2026/02/why-southern-corridor-through-iran-is-critical-for-central-asia/); [Times of Central Asia: Iran War Highlights Vulnerable Southern Corridors](https://timesca.com/iran-war-highlights-central-asias-vulnerable-southern-trade-corridors/)). Central Asian states now face a choice between the Middle Corridor (US-supported, bypassing Iran and Russia) and Russian-controlled northern routes. Their neutrality is eroding. The EU's mineral diversification strategy depends on getting those materials to Europe — and every transit corridor runs through territory controlled by actors with their own agendas.

### Greenland — rare earths and the minerals pincer

Greenland holds an estimated 36–42 million metric tons of rare earth oxides — potentially the world's second-largest reserve after China. In June 2025, the US Export-Import Bank sent a letter of interest to Critical Metals Corp for a $120 million loan for the Tanbreez mine — the Trump administration's first overseas mining investment. The company's stock rose 116% in early 2026 ([CSIS: Greenland, Rare Earths, and Arctic Security](https://www.csis.org/analysis/greenland-rare-earths-and-arctic-security); [TIME: Why Is Trump So Intent on Acquiring Greenland?](https://time.com/7344877/trump-greenland-annexation-threats-purchase-national-security-economic/); [CNBC: Tech Investors Assess Greenland Mining](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/12/tech-investors-probe-mining-viability-if-us-acquires-greenland-ceo.html)).

In January 2026, Trump announced a framework deal with NATO including Greenland mineral rights. Eighty-five percent of Greenlanders oppose joining the US. The deposits are vast but remote — under ice, in areas where extraction costs run 5 to 10 times higher than elsewhere. This is not an imminent threat to EU mineral access; it is a long-term structural one ([Fortune: Trump's Greenland Billions](https://fortune.com/2026/01/07/trump-greenland-billions-decades-mineral-experts/); [CNN: Greenland Rare Earth Minerals Trump](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/22/business/greenland-rare-earth-minerals-trump); [Euronews: Greenland's Value Explained](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/01/15/greenlands-value-explained-could-trump-really-buy-the-danish-island)).

Greenland is a Danish autonomous territory — which makes it, in effect, the EU's back door to rare earth independence from China. If the US secures mineral rights, Europe loses its last major non-Chinese, non-US-controlled source. China controls 60–70% of global rare earth processing. The US is building domestic alternatives. The EU has no processing capacity. The result: China controls refining, the US is securing the deposits. Europe has neither.

### Suez, the Cape, and the Ben Gurion Canal question

The Suez Canal handles approximately 12–15% of global trade and is the EU's primary maritime link to Asia. Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping — linked to the Gaza conflict and now the Iran war — forced major container lines to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10–14 days and roughly 30% in fuel costs. Standard Asia-Europe voyage times rose from 21–25 days (Suez) to 35–45 days (Cape) ([SeaRates: Suez Canal and Red Sea Shipping 2026](https://www.searates.com/blog/post/suez-canal-and-red-sea-shipping-in-2026-risks-routes-and-operational-impact); [Seko Logistics: Asia-Europe Trade Lane Guide 2026](https://www.sekologistics.com/en/resource-hub/knowledge-hub/asia-europe-trade-lane-guide-mastering-the-worlds-largest-shipping-corridor-in-2025/)).

A speculative but worth-noting development: Israel has revived discussion of the Ben Gurion Canal — a 260–300 km waterway from Eilat on the Red Sea through the Negev to the Mediterranean near Gaza/Ashkelon. Cost estimates range from $16 to $55 billion. No construction has begun and no funding has been approved; this is a concept, not a project. But Netanyahu's December 2025 recognition of Somaliland — interpreted by analysts as securing the canal's southern approach in the Gulf of Aden — suggests the concept is being actively prepared for rather than merely discussed ([Horn Review: Ben Gurion Canal and Ethiopia's Ras Dumera](https://hornreview.org/2026/01/23/the-ben-gurions-canal-project-ethiopias-ras-dumera-and-securing-the-southern-gate-sentinel/); [The New Arab: What Is Israel's Ben Gurion Canal Plan and Why Gaza Matters](https://www.newarab.com/news/what-israels-ben-gurion-canal-plan-and-why-gaza-matters); [Times of Israel: Ben Gurion Canal — Vision Amidst Upheaval](https://blogs.timesofisrael.com/the-ben-gurion-canal-vision-amidst-upheaval/)). If it were ever built, it would give the US-Israel axis influence over both Mediterranean-to-Red Sea chokepoints. That is a long-horizon concern, not a present reality — but it fits the pattern.

What is a present reality: the Cape route penalizes China-EU trade by adding time and cost that the US-EU Atlantic route does not bear. Every extra week of transit is a competitive advantage for American goods arriving in European ports in 7–10 days.

### Defense procurement

In the immediate aftermath of Russia's 2022 invasion, EU member states purchased approximately 63% of their additional military equipment from the US. The pattern has persisted ([Bruegel: Europe's Dependence on US Foreign Military Sales](https://www.bruegel.org/policy-brief/europes-dependence-us-foreign-military-sales-and-what-do-about-it)).

The EU is pushing back. The SAFE initiative allocates €150 billion in defense loans, aiming to unlock €800 billion total. Germany's 2025–2026 procurement directs only 8% of its $83 billion budget to US systems, with the bulk going to European programs — F127 frigates, Eurofighter Tranche 5, IRIS-T air defense. The Defense Readiness Roadmap 2030, unveiled October 2025, targets four flagship programs ([Carnegie: Rebalancing the Transatlantic Defense-Industrial Relationship](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2025/12/rebalancing-the-transatlantic-defense-industrial-relationship-regional-pragmatism-in-northeastern-europe?lang=en); [EPC: America's New Defence Strategy and Europe's Moment of Truth](https://www.epc.eu/publication/americas-new-defence-strategy-and-europes-moment-of-truth/); [BE Horizon: Europe and the 2026 US Defense Strategy](https://behorizon.org/europe-and-the-2026-u-s-defense-strategy/)).

These are real countermeasures. But buying US weapons systems creates maintenance contracts, training pipelines, ammunition dependencies, and interoperability requirements that persist for decades. The 2026 US National Defense Strategy has made the transatlantic shift explicit: Europe is "capable and therefore accountable for its own defense." The era of automatic American primacy is ending by reclassification, not withdrawal. Europe must spend more — and much of that spending, for now, flows back to the US defense-industrial base.

## What this means — and what it doesn't

This article has traced a pattern. Every major EU trade direction has been closed, degraded, or contested within a four-year window. The closures have heterogeneous causes — some are the EU's own doing (three decades of Russian gas dependency), some result from genuine security crises (Iran's nuclear program, Russia's invasion), some from US policy choices (Venezuela, the Donroe Doctrine, tariffs), and some from broader geopolitical instability (Sahel coups, India-Pakistan). The causes are different. The structural result is the same: the EU's trade alternatives have narrowed, and dependency on the United States — in energy, technology, defense, and market access — has grown.

A parallel effect, less discussed, is the severing of the EU-China commercial link. The overland corridor through Iran: destroyed. Maritime through Hormuz: blocked. Venezuelan oil to China: seized. The Arctic: Russian-controlled, US-contested. The only high-capacity China-EU route still functioning runs via the Cape of Good Hope, at a 30% cost premium that structurally advantages American goods. The closures don't just isolate Europe — they separate Europe from its largest source of manufactured imports.

Whether any of this was designed is a question we cannot answer from the outside. What we can observe is that an administration describing itself as transactional, operating under a self-declared doctrine of hemispheric dominance, and extracting €1.175 trillion in commitments from the EU in a single trade deal, has — at every juncture where multiple options were available — chosen the one that most increased European dependency and most reduced Chinese access. That pattern may be coincidence. It may be opportunism — each crisis exploited for maximum leverage without any grand plan. Or it may be something more deliberate. The structural outcome is the same regardless of which explanation is correct.

### What the EU has — and what it lacks

The EU is not without leverage. Its combined GDP of roughly $19–20 trillion, its regulatory power (the "Brussels effect" that forced global tech companies to comply with GDPR), its market size, and its renewable energy transition all represent structural advantages that a purely route-focused analysis can understate. The EU fined Meta $1.3 billion in a single penalty. It sets global product standards. Its market access is something the US — and China — need.

But leverage in standard-setting and regulation is not the same as leverage in energy supply, semiconductor manufacturing, military capability, or transit corridor control. The EU can regulate American tech companies; it cannot manufacture its own advanced chips. It can fine companies that violate its data rules; it cannot build an alternative to the Strait of Hormuz. The domains where Europe has strength (regulation, market access, standards) are not the same domains where dependency is growing (energy, chips, defense, physical trade routes).

The EU-Mercosur deal, if ratified, would create a 700-million-person trading zone — but it is stalled in courts. The EU Chips Act targets 20% of global production by 2030 — but at mature nodes, not the advanced ones that carry strategic weight. The SAFE defense initiative aims for €800 billion in spending — but current procurement still flows heavily to US platforms. The EU-Central Asia mineral partnerships are promising — but the transit corridors to deliver those minerals are exactly the ones closing.

The deeper problem is not any single dependency — it is what the cumulative dependency means for Europe's capacity to compete in the next round of global economic competition. AI, the energy transition, critical minerals, advanced manufacturing: in every domain that will define the next two decades, the EU's access to inputs now flows through a single partner who has demonstrated — explicitly, repeatedly, and by doctrine — that it views the relationship as transactional. Every year the dependency deepens, the terms get worse. Every countermeasure the EU has is either stalled, insufficient, or depends on corridors that are closing.

If this situation persists, Europe is not just cornered. It is structurally excluded from the next economic cycle — locked into the role of a wealthy but dependent market, consuming what its partner produces and selling what its partner permits, on terms its partner sets.

When every direction but one leads to a closed door, and the remaining door has terms attached, you are not trading — you are being cornered. The question for European leaders is whether they recognize this before the last corridor closes, or after.

### The Russia question — a hypothesis

If this analysis is even partially correct, there is one structural observation that follows and that European policymakers may not want to hear.

Of all the closed corridors described in this article, only one was closed by EU decision rather than by military destruction, third-party seizure, or uncontrollable instability. The eastern corridor — Russia — is closed by European sanctions. Those sanctions were justified, necessary, and remain in force for good reason. But they are also the one closure the EU has the sovereign power to reverse.

Every other broken corridor requires reconstruction of destroyed infrastructure (Iran), stabilization of failed states (Levant, Sahel), resolution of nuclear standoffs (India-Pakistan), or negotiation with the very power whose dependency the EU is trying to escape (US-controlled IMEC, US-gated Venezuela, US-contested Arctic). None of these are within the EU's unilateral control. The Russian sanctions are.

And Russia is not just any corridor. It is the geographic keystone of Eurasian connectivity. The INSTC runs through it. The Northern Sea Route is controlled by it. Central Asian minerals reach Europe through it or through Iran — and Iran is destroyed. The Caucasus transit routes require Russian acquiescence or US mediation. You can route around Iran. You can route around the Sahel. You cannot route around Russia without passing through corridors that are either US-controlled, Chinese-controlled, or broken.

A EU-Russia rapprochement — built around a resolution to the Ukraine conflict — would simultaneously reopen eastern energy diversification (reducing US LNG dependency), reactivate the INSTC's northern leg, unlock Central Asian mineral transit via northern routes, and begin reconstituting the Eurasian economic space that the three-front structure depends on keeping fragmented. It would also reduce Russia's growing dependence on China — something Moscow itself fears, as the "Dragon, Bear, and Hard Times" analysis from Russia's own PIR Center makes clear.

This is, of course, precisely what the three-front structure is designed to prevent — if it is designed at all. A permanently fractured EU-Russia relationship serves every dimension of the pattern described in this article: it keeps the eastern door closed, it maintains EU energy dependency on US LNG, it funds the US defense industry through NATO procurement, and it ensures the Eurasian interior remains broken.

And here is the deepest irony of the Iran war: by generating a $19 billion annual oil windfall for Russia, the US-Israeli strikes on Iran are directly funding Russia's war in Ukraine — the very conflict that prevents EU-Russia reconciliation. The war that closed the southeastern corridor is simultaneously making it harder to reopen the eastern one. Whether this circularity is accidental or structural, it tightens the trap.

None of this is an argument for lifting sanctions prematurely or rewarding Russian aggression. It is an observation that without Russia, no other Eurasian corridor can be stabilized for sustained commerce at the scale the EU needs. The Iran routes are destroyed and require a decade to rebuild. The Arctic requires Russian cooperation. Central Asian transit depends on Russian or Iranian infrastructure — and Iran's is gone. The only path to a non-US-dependent Eurasian trade architecture runs through a political settlement with Moscow.

That settlement may be years away, or it may be impossible. But if European leaders cannot find a way to resolve the Ukraine conflict and rebuild a working relationship with Russia — however difficult, however distasteful — then the structural trap described in this article is not temporary. It is permanent. And a permanently dependent Europe is a Europe that has lost its seat at the table of great power competition — not because it was conquered, but because every door was closed, one justified closure at a time.
