# The Compass Has One Arrow Left: A Structural Analysis of the EU's Shrinking Trade Route Options

**Every direction closed. Every closure justified. Every closure benefits the same actor.**

---

This is not a conspiracy theory. This is a structural analysis. Every event discussed here has its own legitimate justification — nuclear non-proliferation, counter-narcotics, sovereignty defense, hemisphere security. None of what follows requires assuming secret coordination between hostile powers. It only requires looking at a map, counting the doors, and noticing which ones are still open.

The European Union — the world's largest single market at 450 million consumers and $18.3 trillion GDP — is systematically losing independent access to global trade partners. Direction by direction, the routes connecting the EU to alternative suppliers, markets, and transit corridors are being closed, contested, or destabilized. What remains open is a single westward channel: the Atlantic, leading to the United States.

Whether this is design or emergence is an academic question. The structural result is observable and measurable.

## Part I: Iran — The Keystone That Was Destroyed

### The Agreement Web

Before February 28, 2026, Iran had become the most important transit node in Eurasian overland trade. Not because of ideology, but because of geography: Iran sits at the exact crossroads where East-West corridors (China to Europe) and North-South corridors (Russia to the Indian Ocean) intersect.

The scale of agreements built around this geographic reality was staggering.

**The Iran-China 25-Year Cooperation Program**, signed on March 27, 2021, committed approximately $400 billion in Chinese investment across Iranian railways, ports, energy infrastructure, and telecommunications. China secured discounted oil — a minimum 12% below benchmark, plus an additional 6-8% risk-adjusted compensation — while Iran gained the infrastructure to become the central node of China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) in the Middle East ([Iran-China 25-Year Cooperation Program, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iran%E2%80%93China_25-year_Cooperation_Program); [Rasanah IIIS: Iran-China Strategic Partnership](https://rasanah-iiis.org/english/position-estimate/the-iran-china-25-year-comprehensive-strategic-partnership-challenges-and-prospects/)).

On May 25, 2025, the first freight train from Xi'an, China, arrived at the Aprin dry port in Iran, officially launching a direct rail link between the two countries. Transit time: approximately 15 days, compared to 30-40 days by sea. By late 2025, 40 cargo trains had been dispatched from China to Iran, compared to only seven over the previous seven years — a 570% increase in a single year ([Ponderwall: China-Iran Rail Corridor](https://ponderwall.com/index.php/2025/05/25/china-iran-rail-corridor/); [SpecialEurasia: Iran-China Corridor Impact](https://www.specialeurasia.com/2025/06/09/iran-china-railway-eurasia/)).

But the China-Iran corridor was only one piece. A six-nation meeting in Tehran in May 2025 — involving Iran, China, Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and Turkey — established uniform transport tariffs for the entire China-Central Asia-Iran-Turkey-EU railway route. A follow-up meeting in Beijing in August 2025 formalized these agreements further ([Eurasianet: China Building Up Southern Corridor via Iran](https://eurasianet.org/china-building-up-southern-corridor-via-iran-as-response-to-us-push-to-expand-middle-corridor); [Railway Gazette: China-Iran Railway Corridor](https://www.railwaygazette.com/freight/china-iran-railway-corridor-co-operation/68790.article)).

The **International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC)** added a perpendicular axis. In October 2025, Iran, Russia, and Azerbaijan finalized a comprehensive roadmap to increase transit volume to 15 million tons annually. In November 2025, the three countries signed a Memorandum of Understanding on the western branch of the INSTC. The critical missing link — the Rasht-Astara railway along the Caspian coast — was under active construction, with land acquired for 160 kilometers and Iran and Russia agreeing in January 2025 to commence building ([Tehran Times: Iran-Russia-Azerbaijan Railway MOU](https://www.tehrantimes.com/news/520951/Iran-Russia-Azerbaijan-expand-railway-co-op-in-North-South); [Caspian Post: Rasht to Astara Corridor](https://caspianpost.com/iran/iran-expands-key-trade-corridor-from-rasht-to-astara-linking-to-europe); [Russia's Pivot to Asia: INSTC Deal](https://russiaspivottoasia.com/new-instc-russia-azerbaijan-iran-deal-is-hugely-significant-analysis/)).

On November 30, 2025, Iran and Turkey announced the construction of a $1.6 billion railway — the Marand-Cheshmeh Soraya line — to create a direct Iran-Turkey-EU rail connection. The two countries set a bilateral trade target of $30 billion annually ([Iran International: Iran-Turkey Rail Link](https://www.iranintl.com/en/202511309621); [Times of Israel: Iran-Turkey Rail Link](https://www.timesofisrael.com/iran-and-turkey-agree-to-build-key-trade-rail-link-along-ancient-silk-road/)).

On October 22, 2025, Afghanistan, Iran, and Turkey signed a trilateral railway MOU in Istanbul focused on the Herat-Mazar-i-Sharif line, adding yet another feeder route ([South Asian Desk: Afghanistan-Iran-Turkey Railway MOU](https://southasiandesk.com/afghanistan-iran-turkey-railway-mou/25/10/2025/)).

The **Five Nations Railway Corridor** — connecting China, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Afghanistan, and Iran over 2,100 kilometers — was simultaneously being developed, linking China to Iran's Persian Gulf ports of Chabahar and Bandar Abbas. Uzbekistan announced plans to build a terminal at Chabahar, and Kazakhstan declared its intention to construct a transport and logistics terminal at Shahid Rajaee Seaport ([Five Nations Railway Corridor, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_Nations_Railway_Corridor); [Astana Times: Southern Corridor Critical for Central Asia](https://astanatimes.com/2026/02/why-southern-corridor-through-iran-is-critical-for-central-asia/)).

In September 2024, Iran and Iraq signed 14 cooperation agreements, including a framework to jointly build the Shalamcheh-Basra railway, envisioned as extending through Iraq to Syria and the Mediterranean coast at Latakia — an east-west rail transit corridor from the Persian Gulf to the Mediterranean Sea ([Al Jazeera: Shalamcheh-Basra Railroad](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/9/6/why-is-the-shalamcheh-basra-railroad-so-important-to-iran-and-iraq)).

In 2024, Russia, Kazakhstan, Turkmenistan, and Iran signed the "Eastern Corridor Roadmap" for coordinated development of the Caspian eastern route.

The Istanbul-Tehran-Islamabad rail service was scheduled to reopen in January 2026.

Iran had also negotiated the extension of its natural gas export contract to Turkey, currently set to expire in November 2026.

**In summary: by late 2025, Iran was party to at least twelve major transit agreements with nine different countries, forming the keystone of both East-West (China-to-EU) and North-South (Russia-to-Indian Ocean) overland trade corridors. Container traffic on the China-Iran route was growing at 260% year-over-year.**

### The Destruction

On February 27, 2026, Oman's Foreign Minister Badr Al-Busaidi announced a "breakthrough" — Iran had agreed to never stockpile enriched uranium and to submit to full IAEA verification. Al-Busaidi said peace was "within reach." This came less than two days after US and Iranian negotiators held indirect talks in Geneva through Omani mediation ([UK House of Commons: US-Israel Strikes on Iran](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/cbp-10521/)).

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel attacked Iran.

The stated justifications were legitimate: Iran's nuclear program was approaching breakout capacity, its ballistic missile program threatened the region, and years of negotiation had failed to produce a durable agreement. These are real security concerns, not pretexts ([Britannica: 2026 Iran Conflict](https://www.britannica.com/event/2026-Iran-Conflict); [IISS: US-Israel Campaign in Iran](https://www.iiss.org/online-analysis/online-analysis/2026/02/the-usisrael-campaign-in-iran--further-assessments/)).

But the timing invites scrutiny: why attack after Iran agreed to full verification?

The Trump administration offered "various and conflicting explanations" for the war — imminent Iranian threat, pre-emption of Iranian retaliation, destroying missile capabilities, preventing nuclear weapons, securing Iran's natural resources, and regime change ([Al Jazeera: US-Israeli Strategy Against Iran](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/16/the-us-israeli-strategy-against-iran-is-working-here-is-why); [Mondoweiss: How Aligned Are US and Israel's Goals?](https://mondoweiss.net/2026/03/how-aligned-are-the-u-s-and-israels-goals-in-iran/)).

The consequences were immediate and devastating for global trade. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps effectively shut the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint through which approximately 20% of global petroleum and liquefied natural gas passes. Over 3,000 vessels were stranded. Tanker traffic dropped approximately 70%. Oil prices surged 45% to above $110 per barrel. Oil exports from the region fell 60%, from 25 million barrels per day to around 10 million. China's $100+ billion in BRI infrastructure investment in Iran was jeopardized ([Wikipedia: 2026 Strait of Hormuz Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Strait_of_Hormuz_crisis); [CNN: Red Sea Lifeline Also at Risk](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/17/business/red-sea-trade-chokepoint-iran-war-intl); [WEF: Global Price Tag of War](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2026/03/the-global-price-tag-of-war-in-the-middle-east/); [NPR: Iran War Enters Fourth Week](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/21/nx-s1-5755539/iran-war-fourth-week)).

Every single agreement listed above — the 25-Year Program, the six-nation tariff framework, the INSTC roadmap, the Rasht-Astara railway, the Iran-Turkey rail link, the Five Nations Corridor, the Shalamcheh-Basra line, the Chabahar terminals, the Eastern Corridor Roadmap, the Istanbul-Tehran-Islamabad service — was simultaneously disrupted or destroyed.

Iran was not just a country that was attacked. It was the keystone of an emerging Eurasian overland trade architecture. Removing it didn't close one route. It closed an entire network.

## Part II: The Compass — Direction by Direction

### East: Russia — Blocked

The eastward route to China through Russia was severed not by the 2026 Iran war, but by the Ukraine conflict beginning in 2022 and the EU's resulting sanctions regime.

The EU's dependency on Russian gas fell from 45% of overall imports in 2021 to approximately 12% in 2025. On January 26, 2026, EU countries formally adopted Regulation EU/261/2026, phasing out all Russian pipeline and liquefied natural gas imports — turning the REPowerEU roadmap into binding law. The remaining 35 billion cubic meters of Russian gas imported annually is scheduled to be fully eliminated within two years ([Al Jazeera: EU Moves to Ban Russian Energy by 2028](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/10/20/eu-moves-to-ban-russian-energy-imports-by-2028); [Al Jazeera: EU Agrees to End Russian Gas by 2027](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/3/eu-agrees-deal-to-end-russian-gas-purchases-by-2027); [EC: REPowerEU](https://energy.ec.europa.eu/strategy/repowereu-phase-out-russian-energy-imports_en)).

The justification is legitimate: Russia's invasion of Ukraine violated international law, and the EU's security required reducing dependence on a hostile power. But the structural effect is clear: the eastern door is shut.

### South-East: The Levant — Rubble

The alternative southeastward route through the Arab Middle East — Iraq, Syria, Lebanon — is not viable, and has not been for years.

Syria's civil war left infrastructure in ruins. World Bank reconstruction estimates range from $140 billion to $345 billion, with a conservative best estimate of $216 billion. The proposed Iraq-Syria-Lebanon oil corridor is described as "a patchwork of aging, damaged, and non-operational segments." Lebanon's Tripoli refinery has been dormant since 1992 ([Middle East Forum: Syria's Economic Revival](https://www.meforum.org/mef-observer/hope-and-anxiety-over-syrias-economic-revival)).

Iraq still "struggles to exercise a monopoly of armed force" in its own territory. A 2026 Lebanon war is now ongoing alongside the Iran conflict. The Soufan Center's 2026 forecast notes that "conflicts in Gaza, Lebanon, Libya, Sudan, and Yemen are unlikely to fully resolve in 2026" ([Soufan Center: Middle East Forecast 2026](https://thesoufancenter.org/intelbrief-2026-january-9/); [SpecialEurasia: Middle East Geopolitical Risk 2026](https://www.specialeurasia.com/2025/12/28/middle-east-risk-2026/); [Wikipedia: 2026 Lebanon War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Lebanon_war)).

No trade corridor can function through active war zones with destroyed infrastructure.

### South-East (alternative): Saudi Arabia — US-Aligned

Saudi Arabia is building the Saudi Landbridge railway connecting Red Sea ports to the Arabian Gulf, with construction begun in 2025 and operations targeted for the late 2020s, expected to move over 50 million tons of freight annually ([Saudi Logistics Consulting: Landbridge Project 2026](https://saudilogisticsconsulting.com/insights/articles/saudi-landbridge-project-2026-updates-railway-logistics)).

However, Saudi Arabia's infrastructure projects serve the **India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC)** — a US-backed initiative announced at the G20 that explicitly bypasses both Iran and China. IMEC routes through US allies (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Israel, Greece) and received additional momentum from the EU-India trade deal signed in January 2026 ([Atlantic Council: IMEC Report](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/in-depth-research-reports/report/the-india-middle-east-europe-economic-corridor-connectivity-in-an-era-of-geopolitical-uncertainty/); [GMF: IMEC's Comeback](https://www.gmfus.org/news/imecs-comeback); [IMEC Official Site](https://www.imec.international/)).

IMEC is not a Chinese route to Europe. It is the American-sponsored replacement.

### South: Africa — Unstable

Since 2020, nine coups have occurred across the Sahel region — Burkina Faso (twice), Chad, Gabon, Mali (twice), Niger, and Sudan — all Belt and Road Initiative members except Burkina Faso. Security has deteriorated under each military junta. The Sahel has become, in the words of Amani Africa, a ground for "armed non-state actors, military juntas, and great powers alongside drug smugglers and human-trafficking networks that form routes toward Europe" ([Africa Center: Ten African Security Trends 2025](https://africacenter.org/spotlight/2025-security-trends-graphics-sudan-sahel-nigeria-somalia-drones-china/); [Amani Africa: Gathering Storm 2026](https://amaniafrica-et.org/the-gathering-storm-facing-africa-in-2026-entrenching-conflicts-fractured-order-and-eroding-agency/); [Chatham House: Africa in 2026](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/01/africa-2026-global-uncertainty-demands-regional-leadership)).

China has invested heavily in African infrastructure, but political instability — wars, coups, civil unrest — poses challenges that "may undermine China's peaceful rise and compel it to deploy military and security forces to protect its investments" ([War on the Rocks: Will China Intervene to Protect Investments in Africa?](https://warontherocks.com/2024/10/will-china-intervene-directly-to-protect-its-investments-in-africa/)).

Africa is not a reliable alternative trade corridor.

### South-West: South America — The "Donroe Doctrine"

On January 3, 2026, the United States military captured Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro in a military operation, transferring him to New York to face narco-terrorism and drug trafficking charges. Venezuela holds the world's largest proven oil reserves — approximately 300 billion barrels, or 17% of the global total ([Wikipedia: 2026 US Intervention in Venezuela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_United_States_intervention_in_Venezuela); [TIME: How Trump's Venezuela Takeover Could Change the World](https://time.com/7343019/venezuela-trump-oil-china/)).

The stated justification — counter-narcotics, democratic restoration — is a legitimate law enforcement interest. But the structural consequences extend far beyond Venezuela.

Before the intervention, 81.7% of Venezuela's oil exports went to China. The US intervention severed this flow immediately: tankers heading to China were left idling in Venezuelan waters. The Trump administration announced that all Venezuelan oil would be transported through "authorized channels consistent with US law." Approximately 17% of China's total oil imports — from Iran and Venezuela combined — were cut off ([Columbia CGEP: Venezuela-China Oil Flows Threatened](https://www.energypolicy.columbia.edu/venezuela-china-oil-ties-severely-impacted-by-us-action/); [Anadolu Agency: US Intervention Shifts Venezuelan Oil Control](https://www.aa.com.tr/en/economy/us-intervention-shifts-venezuelan-oil-control-china-loses-ground/3792394); [Bloomberg: What Maduro Capture Means for China](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-05/china-what-ousting-of-venezuela-s-maduro-means-for-taiwan-oil-imports)).

During his January 3 press conference, Trump explicitly invoked and "superseded" the Monroe Doctrine, coining the **"Donroe Doctrine"**. The December 2025 National Security Strategy aims to "reassert and enforce the 'Monroe Doctrine' to preserve U.S. preeminence in the Hemisphere and deny outside powers control of strategic locations and assets" — language Beijing recognized as targeting Chinese influence. Since 2007, China had invested approximately $67 billion in Venezuela alone ([Wikipedia: Donroe Doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donroe_Doctrine); [PIIE: Trump's Monroe Doctrine Aimed at China](https://www.piie.com/blogs/realtime-economics/2026/trumps-latter-day-monroe-doctrine-aimed-china); [CNBC: Donroe Doctrine and China](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/11/trump-venezuela-greenland-donroe-doctrine-china.html); [ODI: Trump's Monroe Doctrine 2.0](https://odi.org/en/insights/trumps-monroe-doctrine-beijings-next-move/); [Atlantic Council: Trump Corollary in Effect](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/dispatches/the-trump-corollary-is-officially-in-effect/)).

The EU, sensing the trap, signed the EU-Mercosur trade deal on January 17, 2026 — creating a 700-million-person trading zone explicitly described as a "strategic hedge against US-China competition." But France, Ireland, Austria, Hungary, and Poland voted against it, and the European Parliament sent it to the Court of Justice for a two-year review ([Foreign Policy: EU-Mercosur as Strategic Hedge](https://foreignpolicy.com/2026/01/14/eu-mercosur-trade-deal-trump-donroe-doctrine/); [CNN: EU-Mercosur Landmark Agreement](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/17/americas/mercosur-eu-trade-agreement-latam-intl); [Brussels Report: EU-Mercosur Strategic Moment](https://www.brusselsreport.eu/2026/03/02/europes-strategic-moment-the-eu-mercosur-agreement-moves-forward)).

### North: The Arctic — Contested

The Arctic represents the last non-US-controlled route between China and Europe. In 2025, Chinese shipping operators completed a record number of container voyages through the Northern Sea Route, with the first container vessel completing a China-Europe transit in roughly 20 days — 40% faster than the Suez Canal route ([CNN: China Arctic Shipping Route](https://www.cnn.com/2025/10/03/climate/china-arctic-shipping-northern-sea-route); [Eurasia Review: Polar Silk Road](https://www.eurasiareview.com/26012026-the-polar-silk-road-chinas-strategic-arctic-ambitions-analysis/)).

China formalized the "Polar Silk Road" in its 2018 Arctic Policy white paper as an extension of the BRI. By 2025, 95% of international Arctic transit traffic flowed from Russia to China. Russia controls the Northern Sea Route and enforces a regulatory regime requiring foreign vessels to seek permission, pay fees, and accept icebreaker escorts ([Stimson: Northern Sea Route Myth of Sino-Russian Cooperation](https://www.stimson.org/wp-content/files/file-attachments/Stimson%20-%20The%20Northern%20Sea%20Route%20-%20The%20Myth%20of%20Sino-Russian%20Cooperation.pdf); [Geopolitical Monitor: Arctic Strategic Stakes](https://www.geopoliticalmonitor.com/arctic-geopolitics-strategic-stakes-for-china-russia-and-the-u-s/)).

However, western operators have largely withdrawn from the Arctic route, making it a de facto Russia-China duopoly. The US is actively contesting this space: Trump has expressed interest in acquiring Greenland — a key Arctic chokepoint — and the US Congressional Research Service identifies the Arctic as an area of increasing "geopolitical competition" ([Chatham House: Russia and China Expanding in Arctic](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2025/10/when-it-comes-arctic-security-europe-must-not-forget-about-economics)).

If the US gains significant Arctic influence, the last non-US-controlled China-Europe route would be compromised.

### North-West: Canada — Coerced

Trump's "51st state" rhetoric toward Canada, combined with tariff coercion, represents an attempt to eliminate even the possibility of the EU diversifying North American trade partnerships away from the US.

On February 25, 2026, after the Supreme Court struck down IEEPA tariffs (6-3), Trump immediately replaced them with Section 122 tariffs: 15% on all imports including Canada. The 2026 review of USMCA carries the threat of revision or exit. Ninety percent of Canadians oppose annexation, and Prime Minister Carney has stated it "will never happen," but he has responded by aggressively pursuing new markets in Asia and Europe — doubling non-US exports within a decade — revealing that he understands the structural pressure being applied ([The Hill: Trump Annexation Threat](https://thehill.com/homenews/administration/5072926-trump-annexation-canada-trade-threat/); [Al Jazeera: Trump Effect on Canada](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/2/12/trump-effect-how-us-tariffs-51st-state-threats-are-shaking-up-canada); [Fraser Institute: Canada Weathers Trump's Second Term](https://www.fraserinstitute.org/commentary/year-review-canada-weathers-first-year-trumps-second-term)).

### West: The United States — The Only Open Door

With every other direction blocked, contested, or destabilized, the transatlantic route to the United States stands as the EU's primary stable, high-capacity trade channel.

The numbers tell the story of accelerating dependency. US LNG supplied 24% of EU LNG imports in early 2021. By the third quarter of 2025, this reached 60%. If the EU fulfills all its supply deals and gas demand reduction efforts falter, US LNG could reach 75-80% of EU imports by 2030. By that date, 40% of the EU's total gas and LNG imports could come from a single supplier: the United States ([IEEFA: EU Risks 80% US LNG Dependency by 2030](https://ieefa.org/resources/eu-risks-new-energy-dependence-us-could-supply-80-its-lng-imports-2030); [EU Council: Where Does EU Gas Come From?](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/infographics/where-does-the-eu-s-gas-come-from/); [Natural Gas Intel: Norwegian Decline Opens Path for US LNG](https://naturalgasintel.com/news/norwegian-natural-gas-output-decline-widens-path-for-more-us-lng-to-europe/)).

Norway, the EU's largest pipeline supplier at roughly 30% of all gas imports, is experiencing declining output — widening the path for yet more US LNG.

The EU-US trade deal, agreed to in summer 2025 at one of Trump's golf courses, was described by EU officials using words such as "capitulation," "blackmail," and "coercion" ([CAP: Trump's Trade Deals as Corporate Short-Termism](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/the-trump-administrations-trade-deals-are-the-epitome-of-corporate-style-short-termism-in-foreign-policy-and-the-american-people-will-pay-the-price-for-years-to-come/)).

## Part III: The Convergent Interests Model

### US-Israel Convergence

The US and Israel share an interest in degrading Iran's military capabilities but diverge on the depth of desired transformation. Israel appears to want "durable incapacitation or some form of regime destabilization," while the US faces greater risk from the chaos, civil war, and refugee flows that could follow regime collapse ([Al Jazeera: US and Israeli Interests May Soon Diverge](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/2/us-and-israeli-interests-may-soon-diverge-on-iran); [Al Jazeera: Geopolitical Analysis of Imposed War](https://www.aljazeera.com/opinions/2026/3/10/geopolitical-analysis-of-the-imposed-war-against-iran); [Arab Center DC: US-Israel War on Iran](https://arabcenterdc.org/resource/the-us-israel-war-on-iran-analyses-and-perspectives/)).

The convergence model works as follows:

| Actor | Primary motivation | Secondary benefit | Political cover |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Israel | Eliminate existential nuclear threat | Remove regional rival, weaken proxy networks | US military capability makes operation possible |
| USA | Nuclear non-proliferation | Destroy Iran as China-EU transit hub, boost IMEC, force EU onto US-controlled routes | Israeli security threat provides political legitimacy |

Neither actor could pursue its secondary benefit alone. Israel lacks the military capacity for sustained operations against Iran without US support. The US lacks the domestic political justification for attacking a country over trade route competition. Together, each provides what the other needs.

### The Corporate Dimension

The administration pursuing these policies has been characterized as "corporate-style short-termism in foreign policy" by the Center for American Progress. The beneficiaries of EU trade dependency on US-controlled channels are directly identifiable:

- **US LNG corporations**: Market share rising from 24% to a projected 80% of EU LNG imports
- **US oil majors**: Now controlling Venezuelan reserves (300 billion barrels) while benefiting from $110/barrel Iran-war pricing
- **US tech sector**: Digital trade provisions embedded in coerced agreements
- **US defense industry**: NATO spending pressure linked to trade concessions
- **Individual actors**: Elon Musk has been noted as having "grown even wealthier through serving in Trump's Administration"

## Part IV: The Silent Beneficiary — Russia

The most provocative dimension of this analysis is not the US-Israel convergence. It is Russia's role.

The EU Council president himself stated it plainly: **"Russia is the only winner of the US-Israel war on Iran"** ([Al Jazeera: Russia the Only Winner](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/10/russia-the-only-winner-of-us-israel-war-on-iran-eu-council-president)).

### What Russia Did Not Do

Russia did not defend its partner Iran. It did not provide advanced air defense systems, despite having them. It did not supply precision munitions or advanced fighters, claiming they were needed in Ukraine. It did not use its UN Security Council veto to block US action. It offered verbal condemnation and some intelligence sharing — the minimum performative response ([Carnegie: Why Are China and Russia Not Rushing to Help Iran?](https://carnegieendowment.org/russia-eurasia/politika/2026/03/china-russia-rescue-iran); [Foreign Affairs: Why Is Russia Watching Iran Burn?](https://www.foreignaffairs.com/russia/why-russia-watching-iran-burn); [Chatham House: Iran War Exposes Limits of Russia's Leverage](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/03/iran-war-exposes-limits-russias-leverage-fragmenting-regional-order)).

### What Russia Got

**Oil revenue windfall**: Russia's fossil fuel revenues rose from a post-invasion low of $501 million per day in January 2026 to $554 million per day within two weeks of the attack — an annual windfall of approximately $19 billion. Russia earned €7.7 billion ($8.3 billion) from fossil fuel exports in the first 15 days of March alone. One headline captured it perfectly: "Russia couldn't fix its oil revenues. The US Air Force did it." ([Euronews: Russia Pocketing Billions](https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/03/19/russia-pocketing-billions-from-two-weeks-of-war-in-iran-data-shows); [Euromaidan Press: Russia Couldn't Fix Its Oil Revenues](https://euromaidanpress.com/2026/03/20/russia-iran-war-oil-revenues-sanctions/); [Bloomberg: Russia Oil Revenue Surge](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/russia-s-big-gulf-war-oil-win-is-yet-to-fill-putin-s-war-chest); [Sunday Guardian: $150M Daily Windfall](https://sundayguardianlive.com/world/iran-israel-war-russia-reaping-up-to-150-million-daily-windfall-from-oil-as-iran-war-disrupts-global-supply-revenue-jumps-14-following-closure-of-strait-of-hormuz-175919/)).

**Replacement of Iran as China's oil supplier**: With Iranian heavy crude off the market, refineries designed to process this grade must switch to Russian Urals blend. ### The Question This Analysis Poses

This article does not claim to prove conspiracy. It claims something more modest and more troubling: **structural convergence**.

Every door that closed had a legitimate reason to close. Russia's invasion of Ukraine was real. Iran's nuclear program was real. Venezuela's narco-state status was real. African instability is real. Levant destruction is real. Arctic competition is real.

But the cumulative effect — every closure benefiting the same actor, every closure reducing EU autonomy, every closure funneling trade toward a single western channel — cannot be adequately explained by coincidence alone.

The question is not whether the US manufactured each crisis. The question is whether, presented with a series of crises each offering a convergent benefit, the US — guided by an explicitly transactional administration, funded by corporate interests, and operating under a self-declared doctrine of hemispheric dominance — chose the option that maximized trade dependency at every single juncture.

If the answer to even some of these is yes, then the EU faces not a series of unfortunate events, but a structural trap. And the response required is not crisis management, but strategic reorientation.

## Part VI: The Wider Chessboard — Second-Order Effects

The encirclement described in Parts I–V accounts for the direct closure of trade routes. But the structural trap is deeper than trade corridors alone. A series of concurrent geopolitical dynamics — each with its own logic, each independently documented — compounds the EU's predicament by closing off the secondary options that might otherwise provide escape routes.

### The Taiwan Semiconductor Trap

Taiwan produces over 60% of the world's semiconductors and more than 90% of the most advanced chips (sub-7nm). Taiwan and South Korea collectively produce 100% of chips under 10 nanometers. The EU has less than 10% of global semiconductor capacity, and zero production at advanced nodes. Europe's AI initiatives rely overwhelmingly on externally manufactured chips: EU facilities produce only mature-node chips (>14nm), while leading-edge AI accelerators require sub-7nm processes available only from TSMC and Samsung ([EST Think Tank: Semiconductors as Key Strategic Assets](https://esthinktank.com/2025/11/25/semiconductors-as-key-strategic-assets-navigating-global-and-european-security-challenges/); [ScienceDirect: Taiwan's Semiconductor Industry and Geopolitical Challenges](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0308596125000485)).

China continues to ramp military pressure toward its 2027 readiness goals. During the 2026 National People's Congress, Beijing committed to a 7% defense spending increase to approximately $277 billion. A December 2025 war game was the largest around Taiwan since 2022. The risk is not only invasion — it is sustained pressure, miscalculation, and crisis escalation ([AEI: China & Taiwan Update March 2026](https://www.aei.org/articles/china-taiwan-update-march-1-2026/); [Defcon Level: Taiwan-China Conflict 2026](https://www.defconlevel.com/taiwan-china-conflict)).

In the most extreme case, a US-China conflict over Taiwan would cost the global economy approximately **$10.6 trillion** — roughly 9.6% of global GDP in the first year alone, eclipsing both the Covid-19 pandemic and the 2007–09 financial crisis. For European automakers alone, 18% of semiconductor needs come from Taiwan: BMW, Mercedes-Benz, and Volkswagen would lose up to 1.9 million vehicles in 2026 ([Insurance Journal: The $10 Trillion Fight](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/international/2026/02/12/857770.htm)).

The structural effect is not that Taiwan has been lost, but that **the only alternative supply is being built in the United States**. TSMC is investing $140 billion in US fabs in Arizona. But TSMC's only European fab — in Dresden, Germany — produces mature-node chips for automotive and industrial use, not the advanced AI and mobile processors that drive the modern economy. The advanced chips flow through America first. The US becomes the **gatekeeper** of semiconductor access for Europe — not by closing a route, but by being the only alternative manufacturer when the primary source is under threat.

### Japan's Rearmament — A US-Aligned Pacific Architecture

Japan approved a record defense budget of $70 billion for fiscal 2025, achieving the 2% GDP target two years early. For fiscal 2026, the cabinet approved a further increase to ¥9 trillion ($58 billion), up 9.4%. Japan is on track to become the **world's third-largest defense spender** after the US and China ([USNI News: Japan Defense Spending $70 Billion](https://news.usni.org/2025/12/03/japan-poised-to-increase-defense-spending-to-70-billion-2-of-its-gdp); [Euronews: Japan Third-Largest Defence Spender](https://www.euronews.com/business/2025/12/26/japan-on-track-to-become-worlds-third-largest-defence-spender); [PBS: Japan Record Defense Budget](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/world/japans-cabinet-approves-record-defense-budget-aiming-to-deter-china-as-tensions-grow)).

This is not merely quantitative. Japan has made a qualitative break from its postwar pacifist constitution:

- ¥970 billion ($6.2B) for "standoff" offensive missile capability, including Type-12 missiles with 1,000km range
- ¥160 billion ($1B) for a next-generation fighter jet jointly developed with Britain and Italy (deployment 2035), with AI-operated combat drone wingmen
- ¥100 billion ($640M) for the SHIELD unmanned drone system — surveillance and coastal defense drones on air, sea surface, and underwater
- Prime Minister Takaichi stated any Chinese military action against Taiwan could be grounds for a Japanese military response

([The Diplomat: Japan Accelerates Defense Buildup](https://thediplomat.com/2025/12/japan-accelerates-defense-buildup-with-record-budget-and-expanded-unmanned-capabilities/); [Defense News: Japan Moving from Self-Defense to Military Buildup](https://www.defensenews.com/global/asia-pacific/2025/12/31/pacifist-japan-moving-from-exclusive-self-defense-to-military-buildup/); [International Crisis Group: Embracing Arms — Securing Japan](https://www.crisisgroup.org/rpt/asia-pacific/japan-united-states-china/351-embracing-arms-securing-japan-new-era-crisis))

Japan's rearmament occurs entirely under the US security umbrella. The US is constructing an Asian alliance architecture — Japan, South Korea, Australia, the Philippines — that resembles a Pacific NATO. The EU is not part of this structure.

The implications for Europe are indirect but significant:

1. **No seat at the table** in the fastest-growing economic region
2. **Pressure to align** with US positions on China (tariffs, technology export controls, semiconductor restrictions) or risk exclusion from the security-trade nexus
3. A **military-industrial feedback loop**: Japan and Australia buy US weapons systems → the US defense-industrial complex grows → Europe, having purchased 63% of its post-2022 military equipment from the US, is locked deeper into the same dependency ([Bruegel: Europe's Dependence on US Foreign Military Sales](https://www.bruegel.org/policy-brief/europes-dependence-us-foreign-military-sales-and-what-do-about-it))

### North Korea as Russia's Force Multiplier

The North Korea–Russia relationship has moved beyond the merely transactional to become a longer-term strategic and ideological partnership. Russia is now **actively funding North Korea's nuclear programme** and sharing technologies related to submarines, stealth aircraft, missile design, and space systems — in return for ammunition, weapons, and manpower for the Ukraine war ([RUSI: Russia Actively Funding North Korea's Nuclear Programme](https://www.rusi.org/explore-our-research/publications/commentary/russia-now-actively-funding-north-koreas-nuclear-programme); [Heritage Foundation: Russia to Supercharge North Korea's Nuclear Program](https://www.heritage.org/defense/report/the-potential-russia-supercharge-north-koreas-nuclear-and-missile-program); [Chatham House: North Korea and Russia's Dangerous Partnership](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2024/12/north-korea-and-russias-dangerous-partnership/how-north-korea-benefits)).

North Korea tested its Hwasong-20 ICBM in 2025 and intends to complete construction of missile factories by 2026. Kim Jong Un's five-year plan (2021–2026) targets tactical nuclear warheads, nuclear-powered submarines, and submarine-launched ballistic missiles. Russia's technology transfer is projected to enable significant DPRK advances across the next three to five years ([The Diplomat: Russia's Support for North Korea's Nuclear Modernization](https://thediplomat.com/2025/04/what-russias-support-means-for-north-koreas-nuclear-modernization/); [CRS: North Korea's Nuclear Weapons and Missile Programs](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF10472)).

For the EU, this operates through three channels:

1. **Resource diversion**: A nuclear-emboldened North Korea ties down US military resources in the Pacific, making Europe's own security guarantee more uncertain and more expensive
2. **Arms industry acceleration**: North Korean threats justify massive US arms sales to Japan, South Korea, and Australia, further strengthening the US defense-industrial complex that Europe is itself dependent on
3. **Proliferation as precedent**: Russia's willingness to transfer nuclear-applicable technology to a pariah state for tactical advantage demonstrates that corridor stability (whether physical or strategic) cannot be assumed when nuclear proliferation is treated as a bargaining chip

### India-Pakistan: The Southern Alternative That Just Destabilized

On the night of May 6–7, 2025, India launched **Operation Sindoor** — drone and missile strikes on nine targets in both Pakistani-held Kashmir and Pakistan proper, including alleged terrorist headquarters. It was India's most extensive military operation since the 1971 war. Over the next four days, cross-border drone, missile, and artillery exchanges hit 11 Pakistani cities. It was the **first full-scale use of combat drones between two nuclear-armed states** ([Wikipedia: 2025 India-Pakistan Crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_India%E2%80%93Pakistan_crisis); [Stimson Center: Four Days in May](https://www.stimson.org/2025/four-days-in-may-the-india-pakistan-crisis-of-2025/); [Foreign Policy: The Year That Took India and Pakistan to the Brink](https://foreignpolicy.com/2025/12/25/india-pakistan-conflict-kashmir-south-asia/)).

A ceasefire was declared on May 10, but as 2025 ended, India-Pakistan relations remained in crisis: dialogue is limited, the Indus Waters Treaty (a key water-sharing agreement) is suspended, and commercial flights between the countries are disrupted ([Al Jazeera: What Did India and Pakistan Gain and Lose?](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/5/14/what-did-india-and-pakistan-gain-and-lose-in-their-military-standoff); [CSIS: What Led to the Crisis Between India and Pakistan?](https://www.csis.org/analysis/what-led-recent-crisis-between-india-and-pakistan)).

For the EU, this has specific structural consequences:

1. **The IMEC corridor runs through a nuclear crisis zone**: The India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor — the US-backed alternative to China's BRI — depends on Indian stability and regional cooperation. The 2025 crisis demonstrated that India's neighborhood is one miscalculation away from nuclear escalation.
2. **The INSTC is doubly broken**: The International North-South Transport Corridor connecting India, Iran, and Russia is now disrupted at both ends — Iran is at war, and India-Pakistan is in crisis. Afghanistan, the third leg, remains under Taliban rule.
3. **Every alternative corridor to the US-Atlantic route has a nuclear-armed instability on it**: Russia (east), North Korea (Pacific), India-Pakistan (south), Iran (southeast). This is not coincidence — it is the geography of nuclear proliferation meeting the geography of trade.

### Kazakhstan and Central Asia: Omni-Alignment Under Pressure

Central Asian states have mastered what analysts call "omni-alignment" — maintaining productive relationships with China, Russia, the EU, and the US simultaneously. In 2025, Central Asia "stepped onto the global stage," with the EU upgrading relations to a strategic partnership at the Samarkand summit (April 2025), focusing on transport connectivity, energy security, and critical raw materials ([Caspian Post: Central Asia's Strategic Balancing](https://caspianpost.com/analytics/central-asia-s-strategic-balancing-russia-china-and-the-west-in-competition); [Clingendael: The Geopolitics of Central Asia](https://www.clingendael.org/pub/2025/central-asia-emerging-from-the-shadows/2-the-geopolitics-of-central-asia/); [Times of Central Asia: 2025 — The Year Central Asia Stepped Onto the Global Stage](https://timesca.com/2025-the-year-central-asia-stepped-onto-the-global-stage/)).

China has become the largest trading partner for most Central Asian republics, surpassing Russia. The China-Central Asia Summit in Astana (June 2025) formalized long-term cooperation. The EU signed Memoranda of Understanding with Kazakhstan (2022) and Uzbekistan (2024) on critical raw materials — rare earths, uranium, lithium — essential for the energy transition ([IDOS: Geopolitics and Development in Central Asia](https://www.idos-research.de/fileadmin/user_upload/pdfs/publikationen/discussion_paper/2026/DP_2.2026.pdf); [SpecialEurasia: Central Asia Geopolitical Risk 2026](https://www.specialeurasia.com/2025/12/21/central-asia-risk-2026/)).

But the Iran war just destroyed Central Asia's southern transit corridor. Iran's transit volumes had been growing 300% since 2020; the Astana Times warned in February 2026 that "the southern corridor through Iran is critical for Central Asia" — weeks before it was destroyed ([Astana Times: Southern Corridor Critical for Central Asia](https://astanatimes.com/2026/02/why-southern-corridor-through-iran-is-critical-for-central-asia/); [Times of Central Asia: Iran War Highlights Vulnerable Southern Corridors](https://timesca.com/iran-war-highlights-central-asias-vulnerable-southern-trade-corridors/)).

The structural consequence: Central Asian states are being forced to choose between the **Middle Corridor** (US-supported, bypassing Iran and Russia) and Russian-controlled northern routes. Their carefully maintained neutrality is eroding. The EU's raw material diversification strategy depends on corridors it does not control and on countries whose "omni-alignment" is under precisely the kind of pressure that produces alignment with the strongest bidder.

### Greenland: The Last Independent Mineral Source

Trump's Greenland push is about more than the Arctic shipping route. The island holds an estimated **36–42 million metric tons of rare earth oxides** — potentially the world's second-largest reserve after China. In June 2025, the US Export-Import Bank sent a letter of interest to Critical Metals Corp for a $120 million loan for the Tanbreez rare earth mine in Greenland — the Trump administration's first overseas mining investment. The company's NASDAQ stock rose 116% in early 2026 ([CSIS: Greenland, Rare Earths, and Arctic Security](https://www.csis.org/analysis/greenland-rare-earths-and-arctic-security); [TIME: Why Is Trump So Intent on Acquiring Greenland?](https://time.com/7344877/trump-greenland-annexation-threats-purchase-national-security-economic/); [CNBC: Tech Investors Assess Greenland Mining](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/12/tech-investors-probe-mining-viability-if-us-acquires-greenland-ceo.html)).

Greenland is strategically positioned along the GIUK Gap (Greenland-Iceland-UK) — a critical surveillance chokepoint in the Arctic. In January 2026, Trump announced a framework deal with NATO over Greenland's future, including mineral rights. Eighty-five percent of Greenlanders oppose leaving Denmark to join the US, but the economic pressure is real: Denmark provides $600 million annually in subsidies, and the island's mineral deposits — while vast — are in remote, ice-covered areas where extraction costs are 5 to 10 times higher than elsewhere ([Fortune: Trump's Greenland Billions](https://fortune.com/2026/01/07/trump-greenland-billions-decades-mineral-experts/); [CNN: Greenland Rare Earth Minerals Trump](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/22/business/greenland-rare-earth-minerals-trump); [Euronews: Greenland's Value Explained](https://www.euronews.com/business/2026/01/15/greenlands-value-explained-could-trump-really-buy-the-danish-island)).

Greenland is currently a **Danish autonomous territory** — making it the EU's back door to rare earth independence from China. If the US secures mineral rights (or the territory itself), Europe loses its last non-Chinese, non-US-controlled rare earth source. China controls 60–70% of global rare earth processing. The US is building domestic alternatives. **The EU has no processing capacity.** The result is a **minerals pincer**: China controls processing, the US controls the major non-Chinese deposits. Europe is structurally locked out.

### The Suez Question: Maritime Chokepoints and the Ben Gurion Canal

The Suez Canal — Egyptian-controlled, handling approximately 12–15% of global trade — is the EU's critical maritime link to Asia. But it is now compromised from multiple directions simultaneously.

Houthi attacks on Red Sea shipping (linked to the Gaza conflict and now the Iran war) forced major container lines to reroute around the **Cape of Good Hope**, adding 10–14 days and approximately 30% in fuel costs to every Asia-Europe transit. Standard voyage times rose from 21–25 days (via Suez) to 35–45 days (via Cape). The cost differential is staggering: while ships save $300,000–$700,000 in canal fees, the added fuel costs and vessel-time costs far exceed the savings for most cargo types ([SeaRates: Suez Canal and Red Sea Shipping 2026](https://www.searates.com/blog/post/suez-canal-and-red-sea-shipping-in-2026-risks-routes-and-operational-impact); [Seko Logistics: Asia-Europe Trade Lane Guide 2026](https://www.sekologistics.com/en/resource-hub/knowledge-hub/asia-europe-trade-lane-guide-mastering-the-worlds-largest-shipping-corridor-in-2025/)).

But the deeper structural concern is what comes next. Israel has been developing the concept of the **Ben Gurion Canal** — a 260–300 kilometer waterway from the port of Eilat on the Red Sea through the Negev Desert to the Mediterranean coast near Gaza/Ashkelon. Cost projections range from $16 to $55 billion. In late December 2025, Netanyahu formally recognized the Republic of Somaliland as an independent state — interpreted by analysts as securing the canal's southern approach in the Gulf of Aden ([Horn Review: Ben Gurion Canal and Ethiopia's Ras Dumera](https://hornreview.org/2026/01/23/the-ben-gurions-canal-project-ethiopias-ras-dumera-and-securing-the-southern-gate-sentinel/); [The New Arab: What Is Israel's Ben Gurion Canal Plan and Why Gaza Matters](https://www.newarab.com/news/what-israels-ben-gurion-canal-plan-and-why-gaza-matters); [Times of Israel: Ben Gurion Canal — Vision Amidst Upheaval](https://blogs.timesofisrael.com/the-ben-gurion-canal-vision-amidst-upheaval/)).

If built, the Ben Gurion Canal would be an **Israeli-controlled (and therefore US-allied) alternative to the Suez** — giving the US-Israel axis effective influence over both Mediterranean-to-Red Sea transit chokepoints. Egypt — historically non-aligned and the guarantor of Suez neutrality — would lose its strategic leverage. The EU would face a maritime corridor where both options (Suez and Ben Gurion) are controlled by actors with whom it has transactional, not symmetrical, relationships.

The only remaining maritime alternative — the Cape of Good Hope — is open but imposes a **structural competitiveness penalty** on China-EU trade that the US-EU Atlantic route does not face. Every week added to transit time is a week of advantage for US-manufactured goods that arrive in European ports in 7–10 days.

### EU Defense Dependency: The Circle Closes

The final dimension of the trap is military. In the immediate aftermath of Russia's 2022 invasion, EU member states purchased approximately **63% of their additional military equipment from the United States**. This pattern has persisted: the US defense-industrial base is the dominant supplier for the European rearmament effort ([Bruegel: Europe's Dependence on US Foreign Military Sales](https://www.bruegel.org/policy-brief/europes-dependence-us-foreign-military-sales-and-what-do-about-it)).

The EU is responding. The SAFE initiative (Security Action for Europe) allocates €150 billion in long-term loans for defense investment, with ambitions to unlock €800 billion in total spending. Germany's 2025–2026 procurement plan allocates only 8% of its $83 billion annual budget to US systems, directing the bulk to European programs — F127 frigates, Eurofighter Tranche 5, IRIS-T air defense. The EU's Defense Readiness Roadmap 2030, unveiled October 2025, includes four flagship initiatives: Eastern Flank Watch, Drone Defense, Air Shield, and Space Shield ([Carnegie: Rebalancing the Transatlantic Defense-Industrial Relationship](https://carnegieendowment.org/research/2025/12/rebalancing-the-transatlantic-defense-industrial-relationship-regional-pragmatism-in-northeastern-europe?lang=en); [EPC: America's New Defence Strategy and Europe's Moment of Truth](https://www.epc.eu/publication/americas-new-defence-strategy-and-europes-moment-of-truth/); [BE Horizon: Europe and the 2026 US Defense Strategy](https://behorizon.org/europe-and-the-2026-u-s-defense-strategy/)).

But the 2026 US National Defense Strategy has made the shift explicit: Europe is seen as "capable and therefore accountable for its own defense." The era of automatic American primacy in European security is ending — not by withdrawal, but by reclassification. Europe must now spend more, but the spending itself deepens the structural relationship: buying US weapons systems creates maintenance contracts, training pipelines, ammunition supply chains, and interoperability requirements that bind European forces to American platforms for decades.

The result is a **triple dependency** that no single trade deal or defense initiative can resolve:

- **Energy**: 57% of EU LNG from the US, rising to 75–80% by 2030
- **Technology**: Advanced semiconductors routed through US fabs; US software, cloud, and payments infrastructure embedded in European commerce
- **Security**: 63% of post-2022 military procurement from the US; NATO nuclear umbrella; intelligence sharing frameworks

### What the EU Can Do

The EU-Mercosur deal is a start — but it's stalled in courts. Canada's pivot toward Europe is an opportunity — but Canada is under enormous pressure. The EU's renewable energy transition reduces gas dependency — but takes decades. The EU's defense integration reduces security dependency — but NATO remains dominant. The EU Chips Act aims to reach 20% of global semiconductor production by 2030 — but the Dresden fab produces mature nodes, not the advanced chips that drive AI and mobile computing. The EU-Central Asia strategic partnership offers mineral diversification — but the transit corridors to bring those minerals to Europe are exactly the ones being closed.

The most urgent strategic question facing Brussels is not any single crisis. It is the pattern: **when every direction but one leads to a closed door, and the remaining door has terms attached, you are not trading — you are being cornered.**

The question is whether European leaders can recognize the cumulative architecture of dependency before the last corridor closes — and whether the political will exists to rebuild optionality in an environment where every alternative has been made more expensive, more dangerous, or more distant than the path of least resistance: westward, across the Atlantic, to the United States.
