\section{Personal Symptom Log}
\label{sec:appendix-i-personal-symptoms}

This appendix records longitudinal symptom data for the patient case documented in this
volume. Symptom severity is self-reported on a 0--10 scale unless otherwise noted.
All entries are dated; entries without numerical severity scores use descriptive
qualitative ratings.

\subsection*{Log Entries}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection*{2026-03-13}
\label{sym:2026-03-13}

\begin{description}
  \item[Fatigue] Mild crash state. Diffuse full-body fatigue worsening toward end of
    afternoon. Attributed to significant sleep deprivation (approximately 4~hours,
    night of 2026-03-11 to 2026-03-12) with 1--2 day delayed onset, and possible
    compounded rebound from 3 consecutive days of modafinil (Provigil) use
    (10--12 March).
  \item[Headache] Borderline/mild earlier in day; present by end of afternoon alongside
    full-body fatigue.
  \item[PEM] Mild/suspected. Post-sleep-deprivation malaise consistent with
    post-exertional pattern.
  \item[General weakness] Present. Patient strongly prefers lying down; social engagement
    possible if necessary but requires active effort.
  \item[Temperature dysregulation] Sensation of cold throughout the morning (no fever).
  \item[Activity tolerance] Very limited. Patient unable to cook; low threshold for
    activity-triggered worsening.
  \item[Cognitive status] Functional but effortful. Reasoning intact; rest strongly
    preferred over engagement.
  \item[Neurological observation --- Acrophobia (progressive worsening)] Patient reports
    progressive worsening of acrophobia over recent years. Previously limited to real-world
    height exposure; now also triggered by videos and television showing heights. Triggered
    episodes produce rapid-onset malaise and accelerated pulse. The extension from
    real-world to screen-mediated triggers is consistent with central sensitization
    broadening effective stimuli over time. Likely facilitated by: autonomic dysregulation
    (exaggerated sympathetic response); central sensitization (amplified fear processing);
    and reduced stimulation threshold secondary to ME/CFS. Acrophobia itself is not caused
    by ME/CFS. Flag for physician discussion. If impacting daily life, adapted CBT or EMDR
    may be appropriate, paced for energy capacity.
\end{description}
