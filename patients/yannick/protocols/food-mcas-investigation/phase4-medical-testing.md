# Phase 4: Medical Confirmation — Urine Mediator Testing

## Prerequisites

- At least one confirmed food trigger from Phase 3 (clear improvement on elimination + clear worsening on rechallenge)
- A physician willing to order the tests

---

## What to request from your doctor

### Tests to order

1. **24h urine N-methylhistamine** (primary marker)
   - Most specific mast cell mediator in urine
   - Stable in urine (unlike serum tryptase, which is often normal in MCAS)

2. **24h urine prostaglandin D2 metabolites** (11-beta-prostaglandin F2-alpha)
   - Second most useful marker
   - More specific to mast cells than histamine (which can come from basophils too)

3. **24h urine leukotriene E4** (optional, adds specificity)
   - Useful if the first two are borderline

### Collection protocol

You will do **two separate 24h urine collections:**

#### Collection A: Baseline (clean day)

**3 days before collection:**
- Avoid all confirmed trigger foods
- Eat from the "safe" foods list for the relevant category
- Avoid alcohol
- Avoid NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin — these affect prostaglandin measurement)
- Continue your normal medications and supplements (but note them)
- Try to have a calm, low-exertion 3 days (to minimize non-food confounders)

**Collection day:**
- Start collection at first morning void (discard it), then collect ALL urine for 24 hours
- Keep the container refrigerated or on ice
- Continue eating clean/safe foods
- Note the start and end time
- Note all foods eaten during the collection day
- Deliver to lab as instructed

#### Collection B: Trigger day (1-2 weeks after Collection A)

**2 days before collection:**
- Eat normally (no restrictions)

**Collection day:**
- Same 24h collection procedure
- **Eat confirmed trigger foods at breakfast and lunch** (e.g., aged cheese, cured meat, tomato sauce if high-histamine was your confirmed trigger)
- Eat a generous amount — don't be shy, you want to provoke the response
- Note all foods eaten and times
- Same storage and delivery to lab

### Timing between collections
- At least 1 week apart (allows mediator levels to normalize)
- Ideally both on similar days of the week (e.g., both on a Tuesday) to control for routine

---

## Interpreting results

### Positive result (supports food-triggered MCAS)
- N-methylhistamine and/or prostaglandin D2 metabolites **significantly higher** in Collection B vs Collection A
- "Significantly higher" = generally >1.5x increase, but discuss with your physician
- Even if both are within "normal range," a clear paired difference is meaningful

### Negative result (does not support food-triggered MCAS)
- No meaningful difference between collections
- This does NOT mean the food doesn't bother you — it means the mechanism may not be mast cell degranulation (could be other immune pathways, direct gut effects, etc.)
- Your Phase 3 elimination/rechallenge results remain valid regardless

### Borderline result
- Small increase in one marker, no change in others
- Consider repeating the paired test
- Consider adding leukotriene E4 if not done initially

---

## What to bring to the doctor appointment

Prepare a folder (digital or printed) with:

1. **This protocol document** (summarized on one page)
2. **Phase 2 analysis** — showing the food-symptom correlations found
3. **Phase 3 results** — showing elimination improvement + rechallenge worsening
4. **Your request:**
   - "I've done a structured elimination-rechallenge protocol and identified [food category] as a likely trigger for my symptoms. I'd like to do paired 24h urine collections for mast cell mediators to confirm whether this is mast cell-mediated."
5. **Specific test names:**
   - 24h urine N-methylhistamine
   - 24h urine 11-beta-prostaglandin F2-alpha
   - (Optional) 24h urine leukotriene E4

### If your doctor is unfamiliar with this approach
- MCAS diagnostic criteria include elevated mast cell mediators (Valent et al. 2019 consensus)
- Paired testing (baseline vs provocation) is more sensitive than single measurements
- The elimination/rechallenge data you've collected is the standard clinical approach for food intolerance — the urine testing adds objective biochemical evidence

---

## After Phase 4

### If confirmed (positive paired test)
- You have strong evidence for food-triggered mast cell activation
- Discuss with your physician:
  - Long-term avoidance of confirmed triggers
  - Possible trial of mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn sodium, ketotifen) before meals
  - Whether H1/H2 antihistamine combination could help on days with higher exposure
  - Nutritional adequacy of the modified diet (dietitian referral if multiple categories are triggers)

### If not confirmed (negative paired test)
- The food still bothers you (Phase 3 showed that) — the mechanism is just not mast cell-mediated
- Continue avoiding triggers based on Phase 3 evidence
- Discuss with physician whether other mechanisms are worth investigating (gut permeability, direct immune effects, etc.)
