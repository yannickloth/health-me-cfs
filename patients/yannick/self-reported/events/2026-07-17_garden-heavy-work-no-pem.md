## Aripiprazole PEM-Protection: Mechanism Hypotheses

Aripiprazole is a partial agonist at D2/D3 receptors and 5-HT1A, antagonist at 5-HT2A. At microdose levels (1mg), the primary mechanistic candidate is **dopaminergic microglial modulation** rather than antipsychotic-level receptor occupancy.

### Candidate Mechanism 1: Microglial Stabilisation

Dopamine receptors (D2, D3, D4) are expressed on microglia. D2-like agonism suppresses microglial activation — reduces pro-inflammatory cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6), nitric oxide production, and reactive oxygen species from activated microglia.

Exertion in ME/CFS is thought to trigger a neuroinflammatory cascade mediated by primed/sensitised microglia. If LDA's microglial D2 partial agonism raises the activation threshold, the post-exertional neuroimmune response may fail to cross into symptomatic territory.

**Supporting:** Microglial priming/sensitisation hypothesis of ME/CFS PEM (Glassford2015, Nakatomi2014, Mackay2019). D2 agonist suppression of microglia is well-established in neuroinflammatory models (e.g., Yan2015 dopamine-microglia review).

**Testable prediction:** If this mechanism dominates, PEM protection should strengthen over weeks (cumulative microglial tone shift) and should be most apparent for neuro-dominant PEM symptoms (brain fog, headache, RLS, flu-like state).

### Candidate Mechanism 2: Dopaminergic Restoration of Central Fatigue Signalling

Low-dose D2/D3 partial agonism may restore deficient tonic dopamine signalling in mesocorticolimbic circuits. If ME/CFS involves a hypodopaminergic state (as suggested by PET/SPECT studies showing reduced striatal D2 receptor binding and dopamine synthesis), then LDA may normalise central fatigue perception and effort-reward processing.

In this view, the pre-LDA PEM cascade is partly a failure of central fatigue detection — the brain fails to register accumulating metabolic debt, permits overexertion, then crashes when homeostasis is irrecoverably breached. LDA restores the "fatigue signal," enabling more accurate self-regulation during exertion.

**Problems with this mechanism for the present observation:**
- 5h chainsaw work is extreme exertion regardless of signalling fidelity — this isn't about stopping earlier
- The expected outcome even with perfect signalling would still be some PEM, given the magnitude
- This mechanism predicts *better pacing*, not *PEM elimination at extreme loads*

However, it may be a contributing factor if restoration of central dopamine tone improves cellular energy allocation regulation at a more fundamental level than conscious effort perception.

### Candidate Mechanism 3: 5-HT1A Agonism → ANS Stabilisation

Aripiprazole is a potent partial agonist at 5-HT1A autoreceptors. 5-HT1A activation in the raphe nuclei reduces serotonergic tone, which has downstream effects on autonomic regulation. If PEM involves a sympathetic-parasympathetic crash (post-exertional ANS destabilisation), 5-HT1A-mediated autonomic stabilisation could dampen the post-exertional cascade.

**Supporting:** ANS dysfunction is well-documented in ME/CFS (orthostatic intolerance, reduced HRV, exaggerated sympathetic response to orthostatic stress). If LDA normalises autonomic recovery after exertion, the substrate for PEM may not develop.

**Testable prediction:** HRV monitoring during/after exertion on vs. off LDA would show preserved parasympathetic rebound.

### Candidate Mechanism 4: Anti-Inflammatory via D2 on Peripheral Immune Cells

Dopamine receptors are expressed on T cells, B cells, NK cells, and monocytes. D2 agonism suppresses T cell proliferation and shifts toward Th2/Treg profiles. If PEM involves a post-exertional Th1/Th17 flare targeting muscle and CNS tissue, LDA may attenuate this peripheral immune component.

**Problems:** Crosstalk between peripheral and central immune compartments makes it difficult to isolate. If LDA were acting solely peripherally, we would expect preserved CNS PEM symptoms (brain fog, headache) even if muscle symptoms resolved. The complete absence of all PEM domains argues for a mechanism with CNS reach.

### Likely Configuration

Multiple mechanisms operating simultaneously, with **microglial stabilisation as the primary acute mediator** (explains speed of effect — present by day 8, potentially sooner) and dopaminergic/ANS stabilisation as chronic contributors that build over weeks. The peripheral immune component may be secondary or irrelevant.

## Caveats and Limitations

1. **Single data point.** One event does not establish a causal relationship. Spontaneous fluctuation in PEM susceptibility is possible.
2. **"Good day" coincidence.** Prior pattern includes occasional good days followed by crashes when patient overexerts *on* those days (see Auchan event). However, that pattern was "feel good → overexert → crash next day," not "overexert → feel fine next day."
3. **Sleep factor.** Sleep quality in the days leading up to Jul 17 is not documented. If patient was unusually well-rested, that could contribute.
4. **Delayed PEM not excluded.** 24h is within the PEM window (12-48h). Observe tomorrow (Jul 19) before confirming complete absence. Some PEM episodes in this patient have had ~20h onset (WaterRower); others next-day. 48h onset is possible but less common.
5. **LDA still in early phase.** Only 8 days at therapeutic dose. Full dopaminergic receptor adaptation takes 2-4 weeks. Effects may evolve.
6. **Confounding: no Provigil.** Unlike Auchan event, no stimulant masking was present. This removes a confound but also means the exercise was performed with unaltered fatigue perception — making the no-PEM outcome *more* striking, not less.
