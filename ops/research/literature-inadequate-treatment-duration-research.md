# Literature Summary: Inadequate Treatment Duration in Research

## Overview

This topic examines the methodological critique that negative/null treatment trial results in chronic illness (Lyme, ME/CFS, post-infectious syndromes) may reflect inadequate treatment intensity/duration rather than true inefficacy. The paradigmatic example from Lyme disease is extended to ME/CFS treatment trials.

## Search Strategy

24 PubMed queries; 15 papers finally included. See `search-log-inadequate-treatment-duration-research-2026-07-15.md` for full details.

## Included Papers (15)

### Lyme Disease — Landmark Trials

1. **Klempner 2001** — Two RCTs (n=78+51) of 90d antibiotics for persistent Lyme. **Negative result.** Critiques: heavily pre-treated patients (avg 4.7yr illness, 3+ prior courses); underpowered; stopped early; treatment may be insufficient; co-infections not addressed. *Certainty: 0.85*

2. **Fallon 2008** — RCT (n=37) of 10wk IV ceftriaxone for Lyme encephalopathy. **Positive at 12wk, relapse at 24wk.** Cognitive improvement significant but not sustained after drug stopped. Pain/physical function improvement sustained. *Certainty: 0.70*

3. **Krupp 2003 (STOP-LD)** — RCT (n=55) of 28d ceftriaxone for post-Lyme fatigue. **Fatigue improved (RR 3.5), cognition did not.** *Certainty: 0.72*

### Lyme Disease — Methodological Critiques

4. **DeLong 2012** — Biostatistical review of all 4 Lyme retreatment RCTs. **All negative trials were underpowered.** Krupp and Fallon showed significant benefit. Treatment effects consistent with continued infection. *Certainty: 0.78*

5. **Cameron 2006** — Generalizability critique of Klempner trials. **Selection bias toward chronic refractory patients** invalidates generalizability. *Certainty: 0.60*

6. **Feng 2019** — Mouse model: Borrelia persister/biofilm requires **combination therapy**, not monotherapy. Standard antibiotics cannot eradicate all morphological variants. *Certainty: 0.65*

7. **Horowitz 2020** — Dapsone combination for chronic Lyme (case series, n=40). 98% improved, 45% remission ≥1yr after failing standard protocols. *Certainty: 0.30*

8. **Rogerson 2020** — ILADS vs IDSA guidelines: 210 patients treated per ILADS (longer duration, combinations). **Majority improved significantly.** *Certainty: 0.45*

9. **Popov 2026** — Review of tick-borne co-infections. Standard Lyme antibiotics do NOT cover Babesia (protozoan), Bartonella, or viral agents. **Monotherapy insufficient when co-infections present.** *Certainty: 0.55*

### ME/CFS — Treatment Trials with Duration Critique

10. **Montoya 2013** — RCT of valganciclovir for CFS (n=30, 6mo). Primary outcome missed significance; **secondary outcomes all positive** (mental fatigue p=0.039, FSS p=0.006, cognition p=0.025). Authors called for larger/longer trials. *Certainty: 0.68*

11. **Watt 2012** — Retrospective (n=61) valganciclovir for CFS. 52% responded. **Longer treatment duration → better response** (p=0.0002). *Certainty: 0.55*

12. **Fluge 2015** — Phase II rituximab for ME/CFS (n=29, open-label). 64% responded. **Response lag mean 23 weeks** (8-66wk). Short trials would miss effect. *Certainty: 0.62*

13. **Fluge 2019 (RituxME)** — Phase III rituximab (n=151, double-blind, 12mo). **Negative.** 26% vs 35.1% placebo response. High placebo rate. B-cell hypothesis not supported. *Certainty: 0.80*

14. **Strayer 2020** — Rintatolimod Phase III (n=208). Post-hoc: **disease duration 2-8 years** showed 2x improvement. <2 or >8yr: no response. *Certainty: 0.55*

15. **Smith 2015** — NIH systematic review of 35 ME/CFS treatment trials. **Most trials limited by size AND duration.** Rintatolimod only treatment with any evidence (low strength). *Certainty: 0.85*

## Key Findings

1. **Lyme paradigm: the negative trials were negative for reasons that don't disprove efficacy.** Klempner enrolled patients with avg 4.7yr illness who had already failed 3+ antibiotic courses — a population that may have irreversible pathology. DeLong's re-analysis showed all 4 retreatment RCTs were underpowered; Krupp and Fallon actually found significant benefit.

2. **Duration matters directly.** Watt found longer valganciclovir treatment → better response (p=0.0002). Fallon's cognitive improvement was lost at 24wk when drug stopped. Fluge Phase II showed 23-week mean response lag.

3. **Co-infection coverage is a critical confound.** Popov 2026 documents that standard Lyme regimens do not cover Babesia, Bartonella, or viruses. Horowitz and Rogerson show better outcomes with combination strategies. This directly parallels the ME/CFS question: if persistent HHV-6/EBV drives symptoms, valganciclovir alone misses the co-infection picture.

4. **ME/CFS trials show the same pattern.** Montoya's positive valganciclovir RCT was dismissed as "inconclusive" because the primary outcome missed significance — but secondary outcomes all favored drug, and the trial was n=30. Fluge's Phase II (positive, 64% response) was contradicted by Phase III (negative) — but the Phase II was open-label and the Phase III had 35.1% placebo response.

5. **Disease duration at treatment onset is a critical variable.** Strayer 2020 found that rintatolimod only works in patients with 2-8 years disease duration — not less, not more. This suggests a therapeutic window.

6. **Biochemical plausibility for the duration critique:** B. burgdorferi forms persister/biofilm variants (Feng 2019) requiring combination therapy. JHHV-6 establishes latency within host chromosomes (Pantry 2013), making viral eradication inherently difficult.

## Certainty Distribution

- High (≥0.70): Klempner, Fallon, Krupp, DeLong, Fluge2019, Smith
- Medium (0.40-0.69): Cameron, Feng, Montoya, Watt, Fluge2015, Strayer, Popov, Rogerson
- Low (<0.40): Horowitz

## Gaps Identified

1. **No direct replication of the treatment-duration critique in ME/CFS.** While individual trials mention duration limitations, no dedicated analysis exists analogous to DeLong's biostatistical review for ME/CFS.

2. **Co-infection studies in ME/CFS are absent.** Unlike Lyme (where Babesia/Bartonella are recognized co-pathogens), the virome/co-infection burden in ME/CFS is poorly characterized. Studies on HHV-6 + EBV + CMV + Coxsackie co-occurrence are needed.

3. **Safety data for long-term antibiotics in Lyme is sparse.** My "long-term antibiotic adverse effects" query returned zero results. Safety surveillance beyond treatment trials is needed.

4. **Herpesvirus antiviral trials for ME/CFS are outdated.** The Montoya and Watt valganciclovir trials are from 2012-2013. No modern antivirals (maribavir, letermovir) have been tested.

5. **The placebo response problem** — Fluge2019 had 35.1% placebo response rate vs 26% rituximab. Understanding (and minimizing) placebo response in ME/CFS trials is critical for future treatment-duration studies.

6. **No trials have tested extended-duration vs standard-duration in a head-to-head design** for ME/CFS. This is the most direct way to test the hypothesis.
