# TSPO PET Imaging in ME/CFS: Literature Summary

**Date:** 2026-05-07
**Research Stream:** tspe-pet-imaging
**Status:** CRITICAL GAP IDENTIFIED

---

## Executive Summary

**FINDING:** The literature on TSPO PET imaging specifically in ME/CFS is **extremely limited and contradictory**.

- **Single published ME/CFS study** (Raijmakers 2021) found **NO neuroinflammation** using TSPO PET
- **No whole-body TSPO PET studies** in ME/CFS exist in PubMed
- **No [11C]DPA-713 studies** in ME/CFS exist in PubMed
- The Michelle James (Stanford) study mentioned in briefing **does not appear in PubMed** (likely conference-only or preprint)

**Key Mechanistic Insight:** Peripheral inflammation may **reduce TSPO tracer brain influx** via BBB transport limitations (Barzon 2026), potentially masking neuroinflammation in ME/CFS.

---

## Published Studies in ME/CFS

### 1. Raijmakers et al. (2021) - NULL Finding

**Citation:** `Raijmakers2021TSPOCFS`
**Journal:** Neurology Neuroimmunology and Neuroinflammation
**Sample:** n=9 CFS (women), n=10 Q fever fatigue, n=9 healthy controls
**Tracer:** [11C]-PK11195 (first-generation)
**Finding:** **No significant difference** in TSPO binding between CFS and healthy controls
**Key observation:** Negative correlation between TSPO binding and symptom severity in CFS
**Certainty:** 0.45 (medium - small sample, single study, not replicated)

**Clinical implication:** Contradicts hypothesis that neuroinflammation is a core feature of ME/CFS. However, limitations:
- First-generation tracer (poor signal-to-noise)
- Small sample (n=9)
- Only brain imaging, not whole-body
- Single study, not replicated

**Relevance to briefing:** If Michelle James found **increased TSPO signal** in ME/CFS, this directly **contradicts** Raijmakers 2021. Possible explanations:
- Different tracers (PK11195 vs DPA-713)
- Different methodology (brain-only vs whole-body)
- Different patient cohorts
- One study has methodological error

## Related Fatigue Syndromes

### 2. Alshelh et al. (2020) - Gulf War Illness (POSITIVE Finding)

**Citation:** `Alshelh2020GWITSPO`
**Journal:** Brain, Behavior, and Immunity
**Sample:** n=15 Gulf War Illness veterans, n=33 healthy controls
**Tracer:** [11C]PBR28 (second-generation)
**Finding:** **Widespread cortical elevations** in TSPO signal (precuneus, prefrontal, motor, somatosensory cortices)
**Certainty:** 0.50 (medium - moderate sample, not replicated)

**Clinical implication:** Demonstrates that TSPO PET **CAN** detect neuroinflammation in fatigue-related conditions. Shares symptom overlap with ME/CFS (fatigue, cognitive problems, musculoskeletal pain).

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Positive finding here vs negative finding in Raijakers ME/CFS study suggests:
- Different pathophysiology between GWI and ME/CFS
- OR methodological differences matter (tracer, sample size)
- OR ME/CFS neuroinflammation is more subtle/regional

## Critical Mechanistic Studies

### 3. Barzon et al. (2026) - BBB Transport Limitation

**Citation:** `Barzon2026TSPOBBB`
**Journal:** Brain, Behavior, and Immunity
**Sample:** 358 TSPO PET scans (healthy, depression, schizophrenia)
**Tracers:** [11C]-PK11195, [18F]-DPA714, [11C]-PBR28
**Finding:** **Peripheral inflammation (CRP) correlates with reduced TSPO tracer brain influx** (K1)
**Certainty:** 0.65 (medium-high - large sample, multi-tracer, peer-reviewed)

**Mechanistic insight:** If ME/CFS involves systemic inflammation (plasma cytokines), TSPO PET may **under-estimate** brain TSPO due to BBB transport issues.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Explains potential discrepancy: ME/CFS may have neuroinflammation invisible to TSPO PET because tracer can't cross BBB efficiently
- Supports hypothesis that peripheral mechanisms drive ME/CFS symptoms even if brain TSPO appears normal
- Consistent across three tracers (robust finding)
- No ME/CFS patients, but findings generalizable

**This is the MOST CRITICAL study for interpreting TSPO PET results in ME/CFS.**

## TSPO Tracer Properties

### 4. Khaing et al. (2026) - Whole-Body Biodistribution

**Citation:** `Khaing2026LW223`
**Journal:** European Journal of Nuclear Medicine and Molecular Imaging
**Sample:** Healthy volunteers (no patient data)
**Tracer:** [18F]LW223 (third-generation, rs6971 polymorphism-independent)
**Finding:** Whole-body biodistribution recapitulates known TSPO expression (hepatobiliary elimination, intestine critical organ)
**Certainty:** 0.50 (medium - healthy volunteers only, no patient data)

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates feasibility of whole-body TSPO PET imaging with modern tracers
- Shows TSPO is expressed in peripheral tissues (consistent with whole-body imaging approach)
- No data on ME/CFS patients

**If Michelle James used [11C]DPA-713 for whole-body imaging, this study shows the approach is technically feasible.**

## TSPO in Related Clinical Contexts

### 5. Yasuno et al. (2025) - DPA-713 in Alzheimer's Disease

**Citation:** `Yasuno2025DPA713AD`
**Journal:** Neuroimmunomodulation
**Sample:** n=23 AD or prodromal AD patients
**Tracer:** [11C]-DPA-713
**Finding:** Plasma IL-6 predicts behavioral symptoms; **TSPO-PET does NOT**
**Certainty:** 0.45 (medium - small sample, different disease)

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates DPA-713 tracer in human clinical use
- Shows **disconnect** between peripheral inflammation (plasma IL-6) and central TSPO signal
- Suggests peripheral inflammation may have neuropsychiatric effects **independent of neuroinflammation**
- Supports hypothesis that peripheral mechanisms may drive ME/CFS symptoms even if brain TSPO is normal

**This reinforces the idea that peripheral inflammation (which ME/CFS likely has) may not correlate with brain TSPO signal.**

## TSPO Specificity Issues

### 6. Chaney et al. (2023) - TREM1 vs TSPO in Multiple Sclerosis

**Citation:** `Chaney2023TREM1MS`
**Journal:** Science Translational Medicine
**Type:** Mouse model of multiple sclerosis (EAE)
**Tracers:** 64Cu-TREM1 mAb, [18F]-GE-180 (TSPO)
**Finding:** TREM1-PET 14-17x more sensitive than TSPO-PET for detecting infiltrating myeloid cells
**Certainty:** 0.30 (low - preclinical, no human ME/CFS data)
**Author:** Michelle James is senior author (Stanford)

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates that **TSPO-PET has limitations in specificity**
- TSPO is expressed in both central microglia AND peripheral myeloid cells
- Newer targets (TREM1) may be more specific for peripheral immune cell infiltration
- **This is NOT an ME/CFS study** (mouse model of MS)

**Michelle James is a TSPO imaging researcher at Stanford, but this paper is on TREM1 in MS, not ME/CFS.**

### 7. Lucot et al. (2022) - TREM1 vs TSPO in Parkinson's Disease

**Citation:** `Lucot2022TREM1PD`
**Journal:** Journal of Nuclear Medicine
**Type:** Mouse model of Parkinson's disease (6-OHDA)
**Tracers:** 18F-GE-180 (TSPO), 64Cu-TREM1 mAb
**Finding:** **18F-GE-180 detected inflammation in brain AND peripheral tissues** (blood, spleen)
**Certainty:** 0.30 (low - preclinical, no human ME/CFS data)
**Author:** Michelle James is senior author (Stanford)

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Confirms TSPO tracers detect peripheral inflammation, not just brain
- Supports whole-body imaging approach (TSPO signal in blood, spleen)
- Demonstrates TSPO's dual role in central and peripheral immunity
- **This is NOT an ME/CFS study** (mouse model of PD)

## Critical Gaps in Literature

### 1. ME/CFS-Specific Evidence
- **Only ONE published TSPO PET study** (Raijmakers 2021) - negative finding
- **NO whole-body TSPO PET study** in ME/CFS
- **NO [11C]DPA-713 study** in ME/CFS
- **NO Michelle James ME/CFS study** in PubMed (contradicts briefing assumption)

### 2. Tracer Limitations
- First-generation tracers (PK11195) have **poor signal-to-noise** (used in Raijmakers study)
- Second-generation tracers (PBR28, DPA-714, GE-180) have **genetic polymorphism issues** (rs6971)
- Third-generation tracers (LW223) address polymorphism but are new and expensive
- **BBB transport limitation:** Peripheral inflammation reduces tracer brain influx (Barzon 2026)

### 3. Interpretation Challenges
- TSPO is expressed in both central microglia AND peripheral myeloid cells
- Whole-body imaging cannot distinguish peripheral vs central signal without careful analysis
- **Peripheral inflammation may mask brain neuroinflammation** by reducing tracer BBB transport
- TSPO elevation could indicate:
  - Neuroinflammation (microglial activation)
  - Peripheral immune cell infiltration
  - Mitochondrial dysfunction (TSPO also involved in mitochondrial physiology)
  - All three simultaneously

### 4. Replication and Sample Size
- **Raijmakers 2021:** n=9 CFS patients, not replicated
- **Alshelh 2020 (GWI):** n=15 patients, not replicated
- **All other studies:** Preclinical (mouse models) or healthy volunteers
- No large-scale (n>100) TSPO PET studies in fatigue syndromes

## Mechanistic Context: TSPO Biology

### TSPO Functions
- **18 kDa translocator protein** (formerly PBR - peripheral benzodiazepine receptor)
- Located on **outer mitochondrial membrane**
- Upregulated in **activated microglia** (brain) and **peripheral immune cells** (monocytes, macrophages)
- Involved in mitochondrial cholesterol transport, steroidogenesis, apoptosis
- **Dual role:** Inflammation marker + mitochondrial function regulator
- Genetic polymorphism (rs6971) affects binding affinity for second-generation tracers

### Blood-Brain Barrier Implications (CRITICAL FINDING)

**Barzon 2026 finding:** Peripheral inflammation reduces TSPO tracer brain influx rate constant (K1)

**Implications for ME/CFS:**
- If ME/CFS involves **systemic inflammation** (plasma cytokines), brain TSPO may be **under-detected**
- This complicates interpretation of negative findings (Raijmakers 2021)
- May explain why ME/CFS patients show normal brain TSPO despite symptoms consistent with neuroinflammation
- Consistent with hypothesis that peripheral mechanisms drive ME/CFS symptoms

### Peripheral vs Central Signal

**TSPO expression:**
- Brain: microglia, astrocytes, endothelial cells (BBB)
- Periphery: monocytes, macrophages, spleen, bone marrow, heart, lungs, liver, kidneys

**Whole-body TSPO PET detects:**
- Brain + bone marrow + spleen + heart + lungs + liver + kidneys
- Cannot distinguish source of signal without additional data (plasma cytokines, flow cytometry)

## Recommendations for ME/CFS Research

### Immediate Actions
1. **Locate Michelle James ME/CFS study:** Check Stanford website, conference proceedings (Berlin conference), preprint servers
2. **Replicate Raijmakers 2021 with modern tracer:** Use PBR28, DPA-714, or LW223 in ME/CFS
3. **Measure BBB permeability:** Use K1 influx rate (Barzon 2026 methodology) to control for BBB effects
4. **Correlate with peripheral biomarkers:** Plasma cytokines, flow cytometry of peripheral immune cells
5. **Consider whole-body imaging:** Include peripheral organs (bone marrow, spleen) to assess systemic inflammation

### Future Directions
1. **TREM1-PET in ME/CFS:** James et al. 2023, 2022 show TREM1 more specific for infiltrating myeloid cells
2. **Multi-tracer approach:** Compare TSPO, TREM1, and mitochondrial tracers (e.g., FDG-PET for metabolism)
3. **Longitudinal studies:** Track TSPO signal before and after PEM triggers
4. **Sample size:** Aim for n>30 per group to detect moderate effect sizes
5. **Genetic stratification:** Test rs6971 polymorphism for second-generation tracer studies

## Conclusion

**The literature on TSPO PET imaging in ME/CFS is extremely limited.**

- Only one published study (Raijmakers 2021) found NO neuroinflammation in ME/CFS
- No whole-body TSPO PET studies in ME/CFS
- No DPA-713 studies in ME/CFS
- The Michelle James Stanford study mentioned in briefing does not appear in PubMed

**Key mechanistic insight:** Peripheral inflammation may reduce TSPO tracer brain influx (Barzon 2026), potentially masking neuroinflammation in ME/CFS. This complicates interpretation of negative findings.

**Alternative hypothesis:** ME/CFS may involve peripheral inflammation and mitochondrial dysfunction without significant central microglial activation, or central activation that is invisible to TSPO PET due to BBB transport limitations.

**Urgent need:** Replication with modern tracers, whole-body imaging, and BBB permeability assessment.
