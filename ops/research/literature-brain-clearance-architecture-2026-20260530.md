## Anchor Paper 1: Yang et al. — Neuronal Protein Tracing (Cell 2026)

**Citation:** Yang A, Chayama Y, Rao N, et al. Physiological brain clearance architecture revealed by neuronal protein tracing. Cell. 2026 May 29. DOI: 10.1016/j.cell.2026.04.048.

**Key Findings (from news coverage, GEN, Bioengineer.org, AlzForum):**
- Engineered neurons in mice to express ZsGreen fluorescent protein; tracked its natural exit routes without disrupting physiology as CSF tracer injection does
- **"Nearest exit" principle:** Each brain region drains waste through the anatomically closest exit route — upper forebrain → dorsal exits; deep structures (striatum) → ventral exits
- **Corrected misconception:** Very little neuronal waste drains to cervical lymph nodes (contradicting CSF tracer studies that misidentified drainage routes by conflating CSF flow with actual protein clearance)
- Primary exit routes: dura mater, skull bone marrow niches, nasal cavity
- **Border immune niches:** Slower clearance at certain borders allows resident immune cells to sample neuronal proteins, contributing to CNS immune self-tolerance education
- **Inflammation disrupts routing:** Acute inflammation model → ZsGreen leaked into bloodstream, bypassing normal routes
- **Alzheimer's model:** 5xFAD mice → waste trapped inside brain parenchyma, unable to exit efficiently
- **Regional clearance kinetics:** Different brain borders clear proteins at different rates; variable kinetics likely reflect distinct immune surveillance functions

**Quality:**
- Journal: Cell (top-tier, peer-reviewed)
- Institution: Gladstone Institutes (Andrew Yang lab)
- Design: In vivo mouse protein tracing, validated against CSF tracer methods
- Replication: First publication of this method; no independent replication yet
- Certainty: 0.75 (Cell-tier, rigorous method; mouse model only; method novelty means replication pending)

**ME/CFS Relevance:** HIGH. The paper establishes:
1. Inflammation directly disrupts brain clearance routing (waste leaks into bloodstream instead of proper exit routes) — directly relevant to the neuroinflammatory component of ME/CFS
2. Regional clearance heterogeneity — different brain regions have different exit ZIP codes, potentially explaining region-specific cognitive symptoms
3. Dementia model shows clearance trapping — supports ME/CFS glymphatic-neurodegeneration risk model

## Anchor Paper 2: Toscano et al. — MR-AIV Glymphatic Velocimetry (Science Advances 2026)

**Citation:** Toscano JD, Guo Y, Wang Z, Vaezi M, Mori Y, Karniadakis GE, Boster KAS, Kelley DH. MR-AIV reveals in vivo brain-wide fluid flow with physics-informed AI. Science Advances. 2026 May 27. DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.aeb0404.

**Key Findings (from Rochester news, Neuroscience News, EurekAlert):**
- **MR-AIV framework:** Magnetic Resonance Artificial Intelligence Velocimetry — physics-informed neural networks decode DCE-MRI to reconstruct 3D fluid velocity fields
- **Dual-speed glymphatic flow pattern:**
  - Fast surface flow: ~3 μm/s across cortical surfaces, perivascular spaces
  - Slow deep tissue flow: ~0.1 μm/s through brain parenchyma (~30× slower)
- Quantitatively distinguishes **advective** (fast, perivascular, ~3 μm/s) from **diffusion-driven** (slow, interstitial, ~0.1 μm/s) transport
- Provides tissue permeability estimates and pressure field maps — quantities inaccessible by other methods
- Method validated in mice (n=5); <2% reconstruction error against synthetic ground truth
- Opens door to healthy vs. diseased brain comparisons, young vs. old, and eventual human translation

**Quality:**
- Journal: Science Advances (top-tier, AAAS, peer-reviewed)
- Institutions: URochester, Brown, U Copenhagen (Kelley lab, Nedergaard collaborator)
- Design: Physics-informed machine learning on in vivo DCE-MRI; validated against synthetic benchmarks
- Limitations: n=5 mice, not yet applied to disease models or humans
- Certainty: 0.75 (Science Advances-tier; method is sound; small n, preclinical only)

**ME/CFS Relevance:** HIGH. Provides:
1. Quantitative framework for measuring glymphatic velocities — could be applied to ME/CFS patients to test whether slow deep tissue flow is impaired
2. The dual-speed distinction maps onto the paper's existing model: fast perivascular NE-driven flow vs. slow interstitial diffusion that would be affected by AQP4 depolarization
3. Methodological blueprint for the DTI-ALPS studies the paper already calls for in ME/CFS
