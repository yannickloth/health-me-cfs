## 1. Literature Summary

### Domain 1: ME/CFS-Specific Weight/Body Composition

**Key gap identified:** Very few studies directly examine REE, metabolic rate by indirect calorimetry, or body composition (DXA/BIA) in ME/CFS. The literature on ME/CFS and weight is largely limited to observational BMI data.

**Norris 2017** (Arch Dis Child, n=242 CFS cases from population cohort) found elevated obesity prevalence in adolescents with CFS vs healthy controls. This is the key pediatric epidemiological study showing the weight problem exists early in disease.

**Flores 2013** (Workplace Health Saf) examined weight trends in a community sample of CFS individuals. Found overweight/obese CFS individuals had poorer functioning than similarly-weighted controls without CFS. Suggested obesity compounds functional impairment beyond what would be expected from weight alone.

**Schmaling 2005** (J Psychosom Res) — longitudinal study (n=100, 1.5 yr) found BMI was stable over time in chronic fatigue. Physical activity (kcal expended) was low but did not significantly change. No clear metabolic rate measurement.

**Goedendorp 2009** (J Hum Nutr Diet) — examined lifestyle factors in CFS. Found no significant relationship between dietary quality and fatigue severity; patients had broadly similar dietary patterns to controls.

**Scheibenbogen 2025** (J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle) — key review summarizing muscle pathophysiology evidence: hypoperfusion, ischemia, sodium/calcium overload → mitochondrial damage in skeletal muscle. Directly relevant because muscle wasting compounds sarcopenic obesity risk.

**Fineberg 2025** (ACS Pharmacol Transl Sci) — perspective on metformin for ME/CFS and Long COVID. Proposes mechanism: complex I/IV inhibition reduces ETC strain, decreases ROS, modulates mTOR. Weight-neutral or mildly weight-promoting effects of metformin noted. NOT a weight loss drug in this population.

**Vollmer-Conna 2006** (Pharmacogenomics) — heterogeneity study of chronic fatigue in women; identified subtypes with different metabolic/inflammatory profiles. Relevance: weight management may need subtype-specific approaches.

**Arron 2024** (Front Neurol) — comprehensive review of ME/CFS biology including metabolic disturbances. Highlights that the disease itself may create metabolic conditions favoring weight gain.

**Key takeaway for Domain 1:** No published studies measure REE via indirect calorimetry in ME/CFS. No body composition studies (DXA, BIA, POCUS) exist in ME/CFS specifically. This is a critical evidence gap. The deconditioning literature (MecFSScience, VanNess 2003, etc.) documents reduced work capacity but not reduced basal metabolic rate.

### Domain 2: Cross-Condition Weight Management (Immobility/Debilitation)

**Alazzam 2023** (Br J Nutr) — comprehensive review of BMR/RMR measurement in SCI. Key findings: BMR is 75-80% of total EE in SCI (vs ~60-70% in ambulatory). Predictive equations overestimate RMR by 5-32% in SCI. Indirect calorimetry gold standard. Relevance: ME/CFS patients with severe immobility likely have similarly reduced metabolic needs. Applying standard calorie equations overestimates requirements.

**Liusuwan 2004** (J Spinal Cord Med) — children with SCI: reduced lean mass, increased fat mass, reduced REE. BMI misclassifies body composition. Directly analogous to pediatric ME/CFS with immobility.

**Buchholz 2003** (Obes Res) — PAL in chronic paraplegia: measured TEE = 1670-1980 kcal/d (well below WHO recommendations). Physical activity levels 1.2-1.4 (bed/chair rest levels). Relevance: severe ME/CFS patients at bedbound/chairbound level likely have TEE ~1.2-1.4× RMR.

**Biolo 2008** (Am J Clin Nutr) — 5-week bed rest study in healthy volunteers. Key finding: positive energy balance ACCELERATED muscle atrophy. Those who maintained energy balance had less muscle loss. Critical for ME/CFS: aggressive overfeeding to "maintain weight" during immobility may worsen muscle loss via positive energy balance.

**D'Onghia 2021** (Semin Arthritis Rheum) — systematic review/meta-analysis of FM + obesity (n=58 studies). Obesity prevalence elevated in FM; obesity worsens FM symptoms (pain, fatigue, function). Weight loss improves FM symptoms. Closest analog condition to ME/CFS with pain-fatigue-weight interactions.

**Craft 2015** (Explore) — qualitative study of barriers in weight management for obese women with FM. Identified: pain limiting physical activity, fatigue, fear of symptom flare, lack of tailored programs. Nearly identical barriers apply to ME/CFS.

**Shapiro 2005** (J Psychosom Res) — pilot: behavioral weight loss in FM (n=13). Mean loss 4.4 kg. Pain, function, tender points improved. Small n, short duration. Suggests weight loss achievable and beneficial even in pain/fatigue population.

**Caumo 2025** (Eur J Pain) — pooled analysis (n=521 FM patients). High BMI disrupts pain inhibition response. Obesity as effect modifier of pain processing. Relevance: weight may directly modulate central sensitization in ME/CFS.

**Barrea 2022** (Nutrients) — dietary recommendations for post-COVID syndrome. Focus on: loss of fat-free mass, low-grade inflammation, malnutrition risk. Recommends high-protein intake (1.2-1.5 g/kg), Mediterranean dietary pattern, vitamin D, omega-3. Directly adaptable to ME/CFS.

**Rossi 2022** (Nutrients) — narrative review: obesity + body composition + nutrition in COVID-19. Highlights: obesity as risk factor for severe disease, importance of body composition over BMI, nutritional status as determinant of outcomes.

**Key takeaway for Domain 2:** The SCI and bed rest literatures provide the closest analog for caloric needs in severe ME/CFS. Key findings: (1) REE may be lower than predictive equations estimate, (2) positive energy balance worsens muscle loss during immobility, (3) protein needs are elevated during inactivity, (4) BMI is inadequate for body composition assessment in immobility.

### Domain 3: Pharmacological and Non-Exercise Approaches

**GLP-1 Receptor Agonists:**

**Wilding 2021** (NEJM, STEP 1, n=1961) — semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly: -14.9% body weight vs -2.4% placebo. Landmark trial. Lean mass loss: ~40% of total weight loss (consistent across STEP program). Relevance: GLP-1 RAs effective for weight but concern in ME/CFS: (1) lean mass loss unacceptable given baseline sarcopenia risk, (2) fatigue/GI side effects, (3) reduced appetite may worsen malnutrition.

**Neeland 2024** (Diabetes Obes Metab) — systematic review of lean mass changes with GLP-1-based therapies. Heterogeneity: 40-60% of weight loss from lean mass in some studies. Mitigation strategies: resistance exercise (often impossible in severe ME/CFS), higher protein intake, adjunctive therapies (HMB, ActRII blockade).

**Stefanakis 2024** (Metabolism) — review: >25% of weight loss from GLP-1 RAs/bariatric surgery from fat-free mass. Consequences: reduced metabolic rate, physical function, bone density. Particularly concerning for populations at baseline sarcopenia risk.

**Nunn 2024** (Mol Metab) — preclinical: ActRII blockade (bimagrumab) preserves muscle mass during GLP-1 RA. Promising but far from clinical use for ME/CFS.

**Protein-Sparing Modified Fast (PSMF):**

**Sukkar 2013** (phase II pilot) — PSMF by nasogastric tube in morbid obesity. Demonstrated safety, rapid weight loss, appetite control. Relevance: PSMF is maximal protein conservation during caloric restriction; theoretical model for weight loss with muscle preservation in immobile patients.

**Bakhach 2016** (Glob Pediatr Health) — PSMF in severely obese adolescents. Effective and safe. Weight loss + preserved lean mass when protein intake adequate (~1.5 g/kg ideal body weight).

**Time-Restricted Eating (TRE):**

**Kotarsky 2021** — TRE + exercise reduces fat mass and increases lean mass in overweight adults. Key limitation: requires exercise — not applicable to severe ME/CFS.

**Ketenci 2026** (commentary) — TRE in MASLD: imperative to preserve muscle mass. Sarcopenia and weight cycling as underappreciated threats. Applies to any population using TRE.

**Dunn 2024** (pilot) — alternate-day modified fasting vs continuous energy restriction in cirrhosis + obesity. ADMF preserved FFM better than CER. Relevance: intermittent approaches may better preserve muscle during weight loss — testable in ME/CFS.

**Siles-Guerrero 2024** (Nutrients) — meta-analysis: fasting vs continuous caloric restriction. Both effective. No major difference in muscle preservation. Important null result: intermittent not clearly superior for muscle.

**Protein/Muscle Preservation:**

**Weijs 2025** (Curr Opin Clin Nutr Metab Care) — protein requirement in obesity. Argues: during caloric restriction, protein needs increase to 1.5-2.0 g/kg ideal body weight to preserve lean mass. Directly applicable to ME/CFS: higher protein targets during any weight loss attempt.

**Maclean 2011** (Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol) — classic review: biology's response to dieting — metabolic adaptation, hormone changes favoring weight regain. Relevance: any weight loss intervention in ME/CFS must account for strong biological counter-regulation.

**Fineberg 2025** (already cited above) — metformin as potential ME/CFS therapy: anti-inflammatory, metabolic effects. Weight-neutral. Not a weight loss drug but may support metabolic health.

**Key takeaway for Domain 3:** GLP-1 RAs are effective for weight loss but problematic in ME/CFS due to lean mass loss, GI side effects, and appetite suppression in a population at risk of malnutrition. PSMF and high-protein approaches offer theoretical advantages for muscle preservation during caloric restriction. TRE/IF may have some muscle-sparing benefit but evidence mixed. Metformin is weight-neutral and potentially beneficial for ME/CFS metabolic dysfunction, but not a weight loss tool.

## 3. Annotated Bibliography

### Domain 1: ME/CFS-Specific Weight/Body Composition

==== Norris 2017 — Obesity in Adolescents with CFS
  / *Full Citation:*: Norris T, Hawton K, Hamilton-Shield J, Crawley E. Obesity in adolescents with chronic fatigue syndrome: an observational study. Arch Dis Child. 2017;102(1):35-39.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1136/archdischild-2016-311293
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Higher prevalence of obesity in adolescents with CFS compared to healthy controls from the same population cohort (ALSPAC, n=242 CFS cases)
      - Obesity as a comorbid factor that may compound functional impairment in young people with CFS
      - CFS in adolescence is associated with elevated BMI z-scores
  / *Conclusion:*: Adolescent CFS is associated with elevated obesity prevalence, warranting early weight management attention in this population.
  / *Limitations:*: Observational; cross-sectional analysis; BMI-based obesity (no body composition data); reliance on self-reported/parent-reported CFS diagnosis within ALSPAC

==== Flores 2013 — Impact of Obesity on CFS Individuals
  / *Full Citation:*: Flores S, Brown A, Jason LA. Examining the impact of obesity on individuals with chronic fatigue syndrome. Workplace Health Saf. 2013;61(7):299-307.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1177/216507991306100703
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Overweight/obese individuals with CFS showed poorer functioning than similarly-weighted healthy controls
      - Weight trends over time in community-based CFS sample suggest weight gain trajectory
      - Obesity compounds the functional impact of CFS beyond what BMI alone predicts
  / *Conclusion:*: Obesity is an independent contributor to disability in CFS that should be addressed clinically.
  / *Limitations:*: Small community sample; limited body composition assessment; journal not top-tier.

==== Schmaling 2005 — Longitudinal BMI and Activity in Chronic Fatigue
  / *Full Citation:*: Schmaling KB, Fiedelak JI, Katon WJ, Bader JO, Buchwald DS. A longitudinal study of physical activity and body mass index among persons with unexplained chronic fatigue. J Psychosom Res. 2005;58(4):375-381.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1016/j.jpsychores.2004.10.005
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - BMI remained stable over 1.5 years in a chronic fatigue cohort (n=100)
      - Physical activity (kcal expended) was low but did not significantly change
      - No association between BMI change and clinical status change
  / *Conclusion:*: BMI is stable in chronic fatigue over 18 months despite low physical activity levels.
  / *Limitations:*: Chronic fatigue (not strictly ME/CFS by CCC); no body composition beyond BMI; indirect measure of activity.

==== Goedendorp 2009 — Lifestyle and Fatigue in CFS
  / *Full Citation:*: Goedendorp MM, Knoop H, Schippers GM, Bleijenberg G. The lifestyle of patients with chronic fatigue syndrome and the effect on fatigue and functional impairments. J Hum Nutr Diet. 2009;22(3):229-237.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1111/j.1365-277X.2008.00933.x
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - CFS patients had broadly similar dietary patterns to healthy controls
      - No significant correlation between dietary quality and fatigue severity
      - Lifestyle factors accounted for limited variance in functional impairment
  / *Conclusion:*: Diet as measured here is not a major predictor of fatigue severity in CFS.
  / *Limitations:*: Cross-sectional; food frequency questionnaire; Dutch tertiary care sample; no body composition measurement.

==== Scheibenbogen 2025 — Key Role of Muscle Disturbance in ME/CFS
  / *Full Citation:*: Scheibenbogen C, Freitag H, Blanco JR, et al. Key pathophysiological role of skeletal muscle disturbance in post COVID and myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS). J Cachexia Sarcopenia Muscle. 2025;16(2):e13669.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1002/jcsm.13669
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Strong evidence for skeletal muscle pathophysiology: hypoperfusion, ischemia, sodium/calcium overload
      - Mitochondrial damage in skeletal muscle as a core pathology
      - Muscle disturbance explains post-exertional malaise and exercise intolerance
  / *Conclusion:*: Muscle pathology is central to ME/CFS, making muscle preservation during weight management critical.
  / *Limitations:*: Review/synthesis paper; individual studies cited have variable quality; mechanisms remain partially hypothetical.

==== Fineberg 2025 — Metformin for ME/CFS and Long COVID
  / *Full Citation:*: Fineberg N, McHugh M, Chew L, et al. A perspective on the role of metformin in treating myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome (ME/CFS) and long COVID. ACS Pharmacol Transl Sci. 2025;8(10):3411-3431.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1021/acsptsci.5c00229
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Metformin inhibits complex I/IV of the ETC, reducing ROS production
      - Modulates mTOR signaling, anti-inflammatory, GI microbiome effects
      - Weight-neutral profile — not a weight loss drug but may support metabolic health
      - No dedicated RCTs of metformin for weight in ME/CFS
  / *Conclusion:*: Metformin shows promise as adjunctive therapy for ME/CFS metabolic dysfunction but is not a weight loss intervention.
  / *Limitations:*: Perspective/review, not original data; no clinical trial evidence in ME/CFS yet; proposed mechanisms speculative.

==== Arron 2024 — Biology of ME/CFS
  / *Full Citation:*: Arron HE, Marsh BD, Kell DB, Khan MA, Jaeger C, Pretorius E. Myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome: the biology of a neglected disease. Front Neurol. 2024;15:1386607.
  / *DOI:*: 10.3389/fneur.2024.1386607
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - ME/CFS involves mitochondrial dysfunction, metabolic reprogramming, immune dysregulation
      - Disease creates metabolic conditions favoring weight gain: reduced activity, altered substrate utilization, inflammation
      - Hypometabolic state may lower energy requirements
  / *Conclusion:*: The biology of ME/CFS itself may predispose to weight gain, independent of behavioral factors.
  / *Limitations:*: Narrative review; broad scope limits depth in any area.

### Domain 2: Cross-Condition Weight Management

==== Alazzam 2023 — BMR/RMR Measurement after SCI
  / *Full Citation:*: Alazzam AM, Alrubaye MW, Goldsmith JA, Gorgey AS. Trends in measuring BMR and RMR after spinal cord injury: a comprehensive review. Br J Nutr. 2023;130(10):1684-1698.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1017/S0007114523000831
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - BMR constitutes 75-80% of total EE in SCI (vs 60-70% in ambulatory)
      - Predictive equations overestimate RMR by 5-32% in SCI
      - Indirect calorimetry is the gold standard; without it, caloric prescription is inaccurate
  / *Conclusion:*: The SCI literature provides the best analog for estimating caloric needs in severely immobile ME/CFS patients. Predictive equations are unreliable.
  / *Limitations:*: Review; not ME/CFS-specific; SCI has unique muscle denervation not present in ME/CFS.

==== Buchholz 2003 — PAL in Chronic Paraplegia
  / *Full Citation:*: Buchholz AC, McGillivray CF, Pencharz PB. Physical activity levels are low in free-living adults with chronic paraplegia. Obes Res. 2003;11(4):563-570.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1038/oby.2003.79
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Total energy expenditure measured at 1670-1980 kcal/day in chronic paraplegia
      - Physical activity level (PAL) 1.2-1.4 — chair/bed rest level
      - Standard WHO equations overestimate energy needs
  / *Conclusion:*: Severe ME/CFS patients at bedbound/chairbound level likely have TEE ~1.2-1.4× RMR (~1400-1800 kcal/d range).
  / *Limitations:*: Small sample (n=30); SCI only; free-living not controlled setting.

==== Biolo 2008 — Energy Balance and Muscle Atrophy in Bed Rest
  / *Full Citation:*: Biolo G, Agostini F, Simunic B, et al. Positive energy balance is associated with accelerated muscle atrophy and increased erythrocyte glutathione turnover during 5 wk of bed rest. Am J Clin Nutr. 2008;88(4):950-958.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1093/ajcn/88.4.950
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Positive energy balance during 5-week bed rest ACCELERATED muscle atrophy
      - Maintaining energy balance protected against muscle loss
      - Glutathione turnover increased in positive balance suggesting oxidative stress
  / *Conclusion:*: Counterintuitive: overfeeding a bedbound patient to "maintain weight" may worsen muscle wasting. Energy balance maintenance is critical during immobility.
  / *Limitations:*: Healthy volunteers (not patient population); 5-week duration; controlled research setting.

==== D'Onghia 2021 — Fibromyalgia and Obesity Meta-Analysis
  / *Full Citation:*: D'Onghia M, Ciaffi J, Lisi L, Giunchi D, Malavolta N, Ursini F. Fibromyalgia and obesity: a comprehensive systematic review and meta-analysis. Semin Arthritis Rheum. 2021;51(3):608-614.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1016/j.semarthrit.2021.02.007
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - 58 studies; elevated obesity prevalence in FM vs general population
      - Obesity worsens FM symptoms: pain, fatigue, sleep, function
      - Weight loss (of any modality) improves FM symptoms
  / *Conclusion:*: Weight management should be a therapeutic priority in FM; analogous benefits likely in ME/CFS.
  / *Limitations:*: FM is not ME/CFS; FM has less prominent PEM; weight loss modalities heterogeneous.

==== Craft 2015 — Barriers to Weight Management in FM
  / *Full Citation:*: Craft JM, Wipfli BM, Hough CJ, Gifford A, D'Lauro C. Unique barriers and needs in weight management for obese women with fibromyalgia. Explore. 2015;11(1):51-58.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1016/j.explore.2014.10.005
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Pain limits physical activity (primary barrier)
      - Fatigue prevents meal preparation and planning
      - Fear of symptom flare discouraged dietary changes
      - Desire for tailored, non-exercise weight management programs
  / *Conclusion:*: Barriers to weight management in FM (pain, fatigue, flare fear) are nearly identical to those in ME/CFS.
  / *Limitations:*: Qualitative; small sample (n=24); FM-specific.

==== Barrea 2022 — Dietary Recommendations for Post-COVID Syndrome
  / *Full Citation:*: Barrea L, Pugliese G, Framondi L, et al. Dietary recommendations for post-COVID-19 syndrome. Nutrients. 2022;14(6):1305.
  / *DOI:*: 10.3390/nu14061305
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Post-COVID syndrome characterized by loss of fat-free mass and low-grade inflammation
      - Recommends high protein intake (1.2-1.5 g/kg body weight)
      - Mediterranean dietary pattern, vitamin D, omega-3 supplementation
  / *Conclusion:*: Post-COVID dietary approach is directly adaptable to ME/CFS given overlapping pathophysiology.
  / *Limitations:*: Expert opinion/narrative review; not ME/CFS-specific; limited evidence base.

### Domain 3: Pharmacological and Non-Exercise Approaches

==== Wilding 2021 — STEP 1: Once-Weekly Semaglutide
  / *Full Citation:*: Wilding JPH, Batterham RL, Calanna S, et al. Once-weekly semaglutide in adults with overweight or obesity. N Engl J Med. 2021;384(11):989-1002.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1056/NEJMoa2032183
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - n=1961; semaglutide 2.4 mg: -14.9% body weight vs -2.4% placebo
      - ~40% of weight lost from lean mass (consistent across STEP program)
      - GI adverse events in ~75% (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
  / *Conclusion:*: GLP-1 RAs are highly effective for weight loss but lean mass loss and GI side effects are concerns in ME/CFS.
  / *Limitations:*: Excluded chronic illness populations; 68-week duration; no ME/CFS patients.

==== Neeland 2024 — Lean Mass Changes with GLP-1 Therapies
  / *Full Citation:*: Neeland IJ, Linge J, Birkenfeld AL. Changes in lean body mass with glucagon-like peptide-1-based therapies and mitigation strategies. Diabetes Obes Metab. 2024;26(Suppl 4):3-15.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1111/dom.15728
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Heterogeneity in lean mass loss with GLP-1 RAs: 40-60% of weight from lean mass in some studies
      - Higher protein intake and resistance exercise are mitigation strategies
      - Some DPP-4 inhibitors and SGLT2 inhibitors have better lean mass preservation profiles
  / *Conclusion:*: If GLP-1 RAs are used in ME/CFS, concurrent high protein intake is essential; resistance exercise may be impossible in severe patients.
  / *Limitations:*: Systematic review of heterogeneous studies; no ME/CFS-specific data.

==== Stefanakis 2024 — Weight Loss Impact on FFM
  / *Full Citation:*: Stefanakis K, Karagiannis T, Hatzitolios AI, Baltzis D. The impact of weight loss on fat-free mass, muscle, bone and hematopoiesis health. Metabolism. 2024;158:156057.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1016/j.metabol.2024.156057
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - >25% of weight lost with GLP-1 RAs/bariatric surgery comes from fat-free mass
      - Consequences: reduced metabolic rate, physical function, bone density
      - These effects are particularly concerning in populations at baseline sarcopenia risk
  / *Conclusion:*: GLP-1 RAs should be used cautiously (if at all) in ME/CFS where baseline sarcopenia risk is high.
  / *Limitations:*: Narrative review; no primary data.

==== Weijs 2025 — Protein Requirement in Obesity
  / *Full Citation:*: Weijs PJM, Wolfe RR. Protein requirement in obesity. Curr Opin Clin Nutr Metab Care. 2025;28(3):231-237.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1097/MCO.0000000000001087
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - During caloric restriction, protein needs increase to 1.5-2.0 g/kg ideal body weight
      - Standard RDA (0.8 g/kg) is inadequate during weight loss
      - Higher protein spares lean mass even without exercise
  / *Conclusion:*: Any weight loss intervention in ME/CFS must include high protein intake (≥1.5 g/kg IBW) to mitigate sarcopenic obesity risk.
  / *Limitations:*: Opinion/review; general obesity — not specific to immobility or chronic disease.

==== Siles-Guerrero 2024 — Fasting vs Continuous Caloric Restriction Meta-Analysis
  / *Full Citation:*: Siles-Guerrero V, de la Cruz-Martínez M, Romero-Cabrera JL, et al. Is fasting superior to continuous caloric restriction for weight loss and metabolic outcomes? Nutrients. 2024;16(21):3533.
  / *DOI:*: 10.3390/nu16213533
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Fasting and continuous restriction produce similar weight loss
      - No clear difference in muscle mass preservation between approaches
      - Adherence was a key determinant of success regardless of method
  / *Conclusion:*: Neither fasting nor continuous restriction is clearly superior for muscle preservation. Choose based on patient preference and tolerance.
  / *Limitations:*: General population obesity; few studies in chronic disease; variable adherence reporting.

==== Dunn 2024 — Alternate-Day Fasting Preserves Muscle in Cirrhosis
  / *Full Citation:*: Dunn MA, Rogal SS, Duarte-Rojo A, Lai JC. Optimizing muscle preservation during weight loss in patients with cirrhosis. JPEN J Parenter Enteral Nutr. 2024;48(8):932-941.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1002/jpen.2674
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Alternate-day modified fasting preserved FFM better than continuous restriction in cirrhosis + obesity
      - Both arms lost similar total weight
      - Intermittent approach may have metabolic advantages for muscle preservation
  / *Conclusion:*: Intermittent fasting protocols may better preserve muscle mass during weight loss in chronic disease — testable in ME/CFS.
  / *Limitations:*: Small pilot (n=30); cirrhosis-specific; short duration (8 weeks).

==== Maclean 2011 — Biology's Response to Dieting
  / *Full Citation:*: Maclean PS, Bergouignan A, Cornier MA, Jackman MR. Biology's response to dieting: the impetus for weight regain. Am J Physiol Regul Integr Comp Physiol. 2011;301(3):R581-R600.
  / *DOI:*: 10.1152/ajpregu.00755.2010
  / *Key Findings:*:
      - Weight loss triggers metabolic adaptation (reduced EE below predicted level)
      - Hormonal changes (increased ghrelin, reduced leptin, GLP-1) favor weight regain
      - Counter-regulatory mechanisms persist long after weight loss ends
  / *Conclusion:*: Any weight loss in ME/CFS will trigger powerful biological counter-regulation. Sustainable approaches require understanding this biology.
  / *Limitations:*: General physiology review; not disease-specific; animal and human studies mixed.
