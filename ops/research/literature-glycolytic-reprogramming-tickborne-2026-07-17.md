# Glycolytic Reprogramming in Tick-Borne Pathogens — Literature Summary

**Search date:** 2026-07-17
**Topic:** Do Borrelia, Bartonella, and Babesia drive glycolytic reprogramming in host cells analogous to viral glial reprogramming, and does this link to ME/CFS?

---

## Papers (N=9 added)

### 1. Dong2026BorreliaGlycolysis — Glycolytic reprogramming in host response to B. burgdorferi

- **Citation:** Dong Y, Chen Y, Luo Y, Liu M, Song C, Chen X, Yang F, Luo Q, Zhou G. Glycolytic reprogramming in host response to Borrelia burgdorferi. *Experimental and Therapeutic Medicine*. 2026;32(1):187.
- **DOI:** 10.3892/etm.2026.13182
- **PMID:** 42199349
- **Evidence type:** In vitro (THP-1 human monocytic cell line)
- **Quality:** Moderate — bioinformatics + in vitro validation. Single cell line. Moderate sample size for RT-qPCR validation. Journal: Exp Ther Med (IF ~2.4).
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Key findings:** 63 differentially expressed glycolysis-related genes in Bb infection. LDHA and TXN identified as key diagnostic genes by LASSO/SVM-RFE. RT-qPCR confirmed LDHA and TXN upregulation in Bb-infected THP-1 cells. Extracellular glucose↓ and lactate↑ in culture supernatants. Immune infiltration analysis linked these genes to Tregs, γδ T cells, CD4 memory resting T cells, and monocytes.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Indirect. Demonstrates that Borrelia drives a Warburg-like glycolytic shift in human monocytes (↑LDHA → ↑lactate). LDHA upregulation with lactate accumulation parallels the glycolytic reliance described in ME/CFS CD8+ T cells (Mandarano2020), but the direction differs — Mandarano describes *failed* Warburg; Dong describes pathogen-driven *induction* of glycolysis in host cells. Implication: tick-borne pathogen glycolysis may compound pre-existing glycolytic deficits.
- **Seed paper status:** VERIFIED — matches the Spandidos seed. Exp Ther Med (Spandidos journal). Correct.

### 2. Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage — B. burgdorferi induces trained immunity with glycolytic reprogramming in macrophages

- **Citation:** Barriales D, Martín-Ruiz I, Carreras-González A, et al. Borrelia burgdorferi infection induces long-term memory-like responses in macrophages with tissue-wide consequences in the heart. *PLoS Biology*. 2021;19(1):e3001062.
- **DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pbio.3001062
- **PMID:** 33395408
- **Evidence type:** In vitro + in vivo (mouse)
- **Quality:** High — comprehensive transcriptomics, proteomics, metabolomics; in vivo mouse experiments with glycolysis inhibition; PLoS Biol (IF ~9.6). 28 authors.
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Key findings:** Bb infection induces broad transcriptomic/proteomic changes in heart with profound downregulation of mitochondrial components. Bb-exposed macrophages show augmented glycolytic output, increased spirochetal binding/internalization, reduced inflammatory responses (memory-like). Glycolysis inhibition in vitro reduces TNF production by memory macrophages. In vivo glycolysis inhibition *reverses* the memory phenotype, recovers tissue mitochondrial components, decreases inflammation and spirochetal burdens. **This is a critical paper**: it demonstrates that Bb-induced glycolytic reprogramming is long-lived, reversible by metabolic intervention, and has tissue-wide consequences.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Medium. Demonstrates (a) pathogen-induced glycolytic shift persists as trained immunity; (b) persists after initial infection stimulus; (c) is reversible by glycolysis inhibition. Directly parallels the viral glial reprogramming documented in Rodrigues 2025/Wendeln 2018. Extends the concept to bacterial (Borrelia) infection — not just viral. The in vivo demonstration in heart tissue provides a model for how tick-borne infections could drive persistent metabolic changes in ME/CFS-relevant tissues.

### 3. Kerstholt2022BorreliaLactate — B. burgdorferi alters monocyte glucose metabolism and lactate production

- **Citation:** Kerstholt M, van de Schoor FR, Oosting M, et al. Identifying platelet-derived factors as amplifiers of B. burgdorferi-induced cytokine production. *Clinical and Experimental Immunology*. 2022;210(1):53-67.
- **DOI:** 10.1093/cei/uxac073
- **PMID:** 36001729
- **Evidence type:** In vitro + human cohort (GWAS)
- **Quality:** Moderate-high — human primary cells from two cohorts; GWAS integration; multi-experiment. Clin Exp Immunol (IF ~4.3).
- **Certainty:** 0.60
- **Key findings:** Bb exposure decreases baseline glycolysis (assessed by lactate production) in human monocytes. MFAP3L gene identified via GWAS as factor influencing lactate production after Bb exposure. MFAP3L mediates lactate/cytokine production via ERK2 activation and platelet degranulation. Platelet-derived factors (CXCL7, CCL5) elevated in LB patients vs healthy. Contrasts with Dong2026 (which found ↑lactate) — Kerstholt shows Bb *decreases* baseline glycolysis, suggesting a more complex, time/cell-type dependent effect.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Indirect. Demonstrates Bb directly modulates monocyte lactate production, a key immunometabolic hub. The MFAP3L → ERK2 → lactate pathway is a potential mechanism connecting tick-borne infection to the glycolytic/metabolic dysregulation seen in ME/CFS. Also links platelets (elevated in ME/CFS) to lactate regulation — novel cross-tissue immunometabolic mechanism.

### 4. Oosting2016BorreliaCytokine — Foundational study of Borrelia-induced cytokine responses with metabolic implications

- **Citation:** Oosting M, Kerstholt M, Ter Horst R, et al. Functional and Genomic Architecture of Borrelia burgdorferi-Induced Cytokine Responses in Humans. *Cell Host & Microbe*. 2016;20(6):822-833.
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.chom.2016.10.006
- **PMID:** 27818078
- **Evidence type:** Human in vitro (primary monocytes from two healthy cohorts)
- **Quality:** High — n=~500 from two cohorts; functional genomics; Cell Host Microbe (IF ~30).
- **Certainty:** 0.70
- **Key findings:** Non-genetic and genetic host factors influence Borrelia-induced cytokines. Age impairs IL-22 responses. Genetic variants in immune genes identified. Monocyte glucose metabolism is altered by Bb (referenced in Kerstholt 2022 as prior work). Established the human functional genomics framework for studying Bb-host immune-metabolic interactions.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Weak indirect. Provides foundational context that Bb infection produces heritable and variable effects on cytokine/immune responses. Establishes the framework within which Kerstholt and subsequent papers identified the lactate/metabolic axis. Relevant to ME/CFS because it grounds Bb immunometabolism in human primary cell data (not just cell lines).

### 5. Bernard2020BorreliaTrained — Borrelia induces trained immunity in synoviocytes

- **Citation:** Bernard Q, Hu LT. Innate Immune Memory to Repeated Borrelia burgdorferi Exposure. *Journal of Immunology*. 2020;205(12):3383-3389.
- **DOI:** 10.4049/jimmunol.2000686
- **PMID:** 33168577
- **Evidence type:** In vitro + ex vivo (mouse)
- **Quality:** Moderate — J Immunol (IF ~4.8). Short report format. Limited mechanistic depth.
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Key findings:** Murine fibroblast-like synoviocytes display trained immunity upon Bb exposure, correlating with Lyme arthritis susceptibility. Skin fibroblasts do not, correlating with absence of skin symptoms. Trained phenotype in FLS is affected by the cell environment and depends on host genetic background. Demonstrates **cell-type specific** trained immunity to Borrelia.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Weak indirect. Adds to the evidence that Borrelia induces trained immunity (innate immune memory) — a phenomenon increasingly linked to ME/CFS pathogenesis (Humer2025). Cell-type specificity (synoviocytes but not skin fibroblasts) suggests that Borrelia-induced reprogramming may be tissue-restricted, which could explain some PTLDS symptom heterogeneity.

### 6. Lynch2023LDHBorrelia — Borrelia burgdorferi depends on glycolysis; LDH as drug target

- **Citation:** Lynch A, Pearson P, Savinov SN, Li AY, Rich SM. Lactate Dehydrogenase Inhibitors Suppress Borrelia burgdorferi Growth In Vitro. *Pathogens*. 2023;12(7):962.
- **DOI:** 10.3390/pathogens12070962
- **PMID:** 37513809
- **Evidence type:** In vitro (axenic culture)
- **Quality:** Moderate — Pathogens (IF ~3.7). In vitro only, no host cells. Single species.
- **Certainty:** 0.45
- **Key findings:** Bb has a highly reduced genome and relies heavily on glycolysis for carbon metabolism. LDH inhibitors (gossypol, AT-101, oxamate, galloflavin, stiripentol) suppress Bb growth dose-dependently. Establishes that Bb's own metabolism is obligately glycolytic, making host glycolytic environment relevant for pathogen survival. Relevance: if host cells are glycolytically reprogrammed (Dong, Barriales), this may provide a permissive metabolic niche for Bb persistence.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Very indirect. Links to persistence hypothesis: a host glycolytic environment could favor Bb persistence, contributing to ongoing immune activation in PTLDS/ME/CFS. Glycolysis inhibition could be both host-directed therapy and anti-borrelial simultaneously.

### 7. Kempf2005BartonellaHIF1 — Bartonella henselae activates HIF-1α in host cells

- **Citation:** Kempf VAJ, Lebiedziejewski M, Alitalo K, et al. Activation of hypoxia-inducible factor-1 in bacillary angiomatosis: evidence for a role of hypoxia-inducible factor-1 in bacterial infections. *Circulation*. 2005;111(8):1054-1062.
- **DOI:** 10.1161/01.cir.0000155608.07691.b7
- **PMID:** 15723970
- **Evidence type:** In vitro + in vivo (human tissue immunohistochemistry)
- **Quality:** High — Circulation (IF ~40). Multi-method validation: immunofluorescence, Western blot, EMSA, reporter gene, siRNA, microarray, IHC on BA tissue lesions.
- **Certainty:** 0.70
- **Key findings:** B. henselae activates HIF-1α in infected host cells. Gene microarray shows B. henselae activates hypoxia-response genes. HIF-1α is essential for B. henselae-induced VEGF expression (siRNA inhibition). Infection increases oxygen consumption and causes **cellular hypoxia and decreased ATP levels** in host cells. Pilus-negative variant does not activate HIF-1α or VEGF, establishing the surface protein's role. **Landmark paper**: first demonstration of HIF-1α involvement in any bacterial infection.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Medium. B. henselae → HIF-1α stabilization → cellular hypoxia → decreased ATP → glycolytic shift. This directly parallels the viral glial reprogramming mechanism (HIF-1α stabilization → glycolytic shift → lactate). Extends the concept from viral to bacterial pathogens. The decreased ATP in host cells is directly relevant to ME/CFS energy metabolism dysfunction. Endothelial HIF-1α activation → VEGF → vascular permeability is also relevant to ME/CFS endothelial dysfunction (Appel2024).

### 8. Ohmori2004BabesiaGlucose — Babesia-infected RBCs show increased glucose uptake

- **Citation:** Ohmori T, Adachi K, Fukuda Y, Tamahara S, Matsuki N, Ono K. Glucose uptake activity in murine red blood cells infected with Babesia microti and Babesia rodhaini. *Journal of Veterinary Medical Science*. 2004;66(8):945-949.
- **DOI:** 10.1292/jvms.66.945
- **PMID:** 15353845
- **Evidence type:** Animal (mouse)
- **Quality:** Low-moderate — J Vet Med Sci (IF ~1.0). Small n, animal model, older. No mechanistic depth beyond glucose transport.
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Key findings:** Babesia-infected RBCs (both B. microti and B. rodhaini) show increased uptake of 2-deoxy-D-glucose and L-glucose compared to uninfected RBCs. Uptake is concentration-dependent with linear kinetics, suggesting **de novo, non-transporter-mediated glucose uptake systems** develop in infected RBCs. Two distinct uptake systems identified: one common to both species, another with higher activity in B. microti. Sodium-independent, temperature-sensitive.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Weak indirect. Demonstrates that Babesia-infected RBCs develop increased glucose consumption — effectively a glycolytic reprogramming of the erythrocyte. In ME/CFS, RBC metabolic abnormalities have been reported (deformability, oxidative stress). Babesia-driven RBC glucose consumption could contribute to systemic metabolic stress. Also provides a mechanism for the hemolysis-driven metabolic pathway (more glucose consumed → more lactate → more hemolysis → free heme → oxidative stress).
- **Note:** Low certainty; small n (pooled mouse blood), single assay method, no human data.

### 9. Fitzgerald2021PTLDSMetabolic — PTLDS patients have distinct metabolomic profiles

- **Citation:** Fitzgerald BL, Graham B, Delorey MJ, et al. Metabolic Response in Patients With Post-treatment Lyme Disease Symptoms/Syndrome. *Clinical Infectious Diseases*. 2021;73(7):e2342-e2349.
- **DOI:** 10.1093/cid/ciaa1455
- **PMID:** 32975577
- **Evidence type:** Human (PTLDS patients vs cured Lyme controls)
- **Quality:** High — Clin Infect Dis (IF ~11.8). Untargeted LC-MS metabolomics. Multi-timepoint longitudinal design. Two-cohort validation.
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Key findings:** Observable metabolic differences between PTLDS and non-PTLDS patients at multiple time points. Differentially abundant metabolites include glycerophospholipids, bile acids, and acylcarnitines (not glycolysis per se). Greater metabolic variability in PTLDS patients. 6-40 metabolites can discriminate PTLDS from non-PTLDS at defined time points. Validated in second cohort.
- **Link to ME/CFS:** Indirect (but important). Demonstrates that persistent symptoms after Lyme disease have a measurable metabolic signature — PTLDS is not just subjective. The metabolic pathways altered (glycerophospholipids → membrane remodeling, acylcarnitines → mitochondrial β-oxidation, bile acids → gut-liver axis) suggest systemic metabolic dysregulation relevant to ME/CFS. While direct glycolytic metabolites were not the discriminating features, the broad metabolic disruption is consistent with upstream glycolytic/mitochondrial dysfunction. **Null finding**: no direct glycolytic metabolite signal in PTLDS serum metabolomics — suggests host glycolytic changes may be cell-type specific (monocyte/macrophage/tissue) and not detectable in serum.

## Null/negative findings

- Fitzgerald2021: PTLDS serum metabolomics did NOT identify glycolysis as the discriminating pathway. Discriminating metabolites were phospholipid, bile acid, and acylcarnitine — not lactate, pyruvate, or hexose phosphates. This does not rule out cell-type-specific glycolytic reprogramming but indicates serum metabolomics may not capture it.
- Kerstholt2022: Bb exposure *decreased* baseline glycolysis (lactate) in monocytes — opposite of the ↑lactate in Dong2026. Suggests time-dependent or experimental-condition-dependent bidirectional metabolic effects. Neither is truly "negative" — they reflect different phases/aspects of host metabolic response.
- No competing papers found that specifically reject glycolytic reprogramming in tick-borne infections — the field is too new.

## Competing explanations for post-Lyme fatigue (not glycolysis-dependent)

- Autoimmunity (P class antibodies, molecular mimicry): PTLDS autoimmunity literature (e.g., 33735220, 30400667)
- Persistent infection/live spirochetes: ongoing debate (Feng2019BorreliaPersister already in bib)
- Neurologic/inflammatory (cytokine, complement): various mechanisms
- These do NOT contradict glycolytic reprogramming — they may coexist or operate at different levels
