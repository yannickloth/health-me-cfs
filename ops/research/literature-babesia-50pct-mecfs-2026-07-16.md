# Literature Summary: Babesia ~50% Prevalence in ME/CFS

**Date:** 2026-07-16
**Compiler:** AI literature search agent
**Topic:** Babesia prevalence and Babesia–ME/CFS links

---

## Executive Summary

A single recent study (Breitschwerdt et al. 2025) found *Babesia* DNA in 24% (12/50) and *Bartonella* DNA in 26% (13/50) of chronic fatigue patients with neurological symptoms — combined ~46%, which likely generated the "~50% Babesia" claim. No case-control comparison was performed, so whether this exceeds background seroprevalence (3–6% in endemic US regions) is unknown. The broader literature on Babesia–ME/CFS remains extremely sparse: PubMed returns only 3 papers linking Babesia to chronic fatigue, and only 1 (the same Breitschwerdt paper) explicitly uses ME/CFS terminology. The 1996 MacDonald study found **zero** Babesia seropositivity in 47 CFS cases, a notable null result. No independent replication exists.

**Bottom line:** The "50% Babesia" claim is an over-interpretation. The actual Babesia-alone rate is 24% (95% CI: 12–36%), and the study lacks healthy controls. The evidence does not support revising the existing ch07 text, which correctly notes evidence is sparse.

## Papers Evaluated

### 1. Breitschwerdt et al. 2025 (KEY PAPER)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | *Babesia and Bartonella Species DNA in Blood and Enrichment Blood Cultures from People with Chronic Fatigue and Concurrent Neurological Symptoms* |
| **Authors** | Breitschwerdt EB, Maggi RG, Bush JC, Kingston E |
| **Journal** | Pathogens 2025;15(1):2 |
| **PMID** | 41598986 |
| **DOI** | 10.3390/pathogens15010002 |
| **Design** | Cross-sectional; PCR + enrichment blood culture |
| **Sample** | n=50 self-selected (fatigue ≥6mo + ≥1 neurological symptom) |
| **Results** | Babesia: 24% (12/50, 95% CI: 12–36%); Bartonella: 26% (13/50, 95% CI: 13.8–38.2%); Both: 2/50 |
| **Combined** | 46% (23/50) infected with Babesia or Bartonella |
| **Control** | None |
| **Funding** | Steven & Alexandra Cohen Foundation; 60 Degrees; Galaxy Diagnostics |
| **COI** | Breitschwerdt (CSO), Maggi (CTO) of Galaxy Diagnostics |
| **Certainty** | **0.45** — pilot, no controls, small n, significant COI |

**Assessment:** This is the paper generating the "50% Babesia" claim. The true Babesia-only rate is 24%; the 46% combined rate is closer to 50%. Without healthy controls from the same geographic region, it is impossible to determine if this exceeds background endemic seroprevalence (Moritz et al. 2014 reported 1–3% B. microti seroprevalence in US blood donors from non-endemic areas; 15–20% in endemic areas). The enrichment blood culture method is more sensitive than standard testing, but also more likely to detect transient or subclinical parasitemia.

| **Certainty** | **0.65** — well-powered, two cohorts, J Transl Med |

**Assessment:** Direct biochemical link to the Babesia → hemolysis → haptoglobin depletion pathway. This creates a plausible mechanism: Babesia-induced hemolysis depletes haptoglobin, impairing free hemoglobin clearance, increasing oxidative stress, and worsening ME/CFS symptoms.

## Limitations & Gaps

1. **No controlled Babesia–ME/CFS study exists** — the only study (Breitschwerdt 2025) lacks healthy controls
2. **Background seroprevalence unknown** — Babesia seroprevalence in general US population is 1–3% (non-endemic) to 15–20% (endemic). Without regional controls, 24% PCR positivity cannot be interpreted
3. **Enrichment culture debate** — BAPGM enrichment culture is sensitive but its clinical specificity is debated; some argue it detects environmental contamination or non-viable organisms
4. **Null result from 1996** — MacDonald found 0% Babesia seropositivity in CFS; the Breitschwerder group argues PCR is more sensitive, but this discrepancy requires reconciliation
5. **No independent replication** — All positive Babesia–ME/CFS results come from a single research group (Breitschwerdt/Maggi, NC State)
6. **Significant COIs** — Breitschwerdt and Maggi are co-founders of Galaxy Diagnostics, which markets the BAPGM enrichment culture test used in the studies
7. **No prospective or treatment studies** — No RCTs of anti-Babesia treatment in ME/CFS patients with Babesia DNA
8. **Babesia species identification unclear** — The Breitschwerdt paper does not specify which Babesia species were detected in most cases

## Recommendation for ch07 Revision

The existing text ("A targeted 2024–2026 search returned no new studies linking Babesia infection specifically to ME/CFS") should be updated to:
- Acknowledge the Breitschwerdt 2025 paper (24% Babesia PCR positivity, no controls)
- Note the 46% combined Babesia+Bartonella rate but explain the limitations
- Add the MacDonald 1996 null result for balance
- Retain cautious language given lack of controls and COI
- Add the Moezzi 2025 haptoglobin paper as indirect biochemical link
