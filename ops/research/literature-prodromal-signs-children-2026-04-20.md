### 1. Wirth et al. 2026 — ICD-10 Diagnoses Prior to ME/CFS in Children (MOST RELEVANT)

**Full citation:** Wirth M, Haastert B, Linnenkamp U, Andrich S, Icks A, Pricoco R, Behrends U, De Bock F. ICD-10 Diagnoses prior to ME/CFS diagnosis in children and young people suggest potential early diagnostic indicators. _Scientific Reports_. 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s41598-026-40848-1. PMID: 41741569. PMC: PMC12949141.

**Study design:** Retrospective matched case-control using German statutory health insurance claims data 2020–2022. 6,077 ME/CFS cases in children and young people matched 1:5 to 30,255 controls (total N=36,332).

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- 48 ICD-10 code classes significantly associated with later ME/CFS diagnosis
- 44 elevated; 4 decreased
- High-prevalence associations (≥10% of cases): fatigue (15.3%, OR 2.19), depression (16.3%, OR 1.21), somatoform disorders (11.8%, OR 1.32), pain disorders (10.2%, OR 1.55), laryngopharyngitis (44.0%, OR 1.15)
- Low-prevalence but strong associations: post-COVID condition (3.7%, OR 3.84), fibromyalgia (0.56%, OR 2.08), mild cognitive impairment (0.30%, OR 2.93)
- Diagnoses span F (mental/behavioral), R (symptoms/signs/abnormal findings), M (musculoskeletal) chapters
- Authors note: pre-ME/CFS diagnoses likely represent "a mixture of pre-existing conditions, comorbidities, early manifestations, or misdiagnoses"
- CRITICAL NOTE: Some "mental health" pre-diagnoses (depression, somatoform) may be misattributed prodromal symptoms (fatigue → depression Dx, cognitive fog → somatoform Dx) rather than true psychiatric comorbidities

**Quality:** High. N=36,332 with 1:5 matching, administrative data avoids recall bias. Observational — cannot establish directionality. 2026, Germany.

**Certainty:** 0.70

**Replication:** Single study (2026), but very large population-based sample. No independent replication yet.

**Integration points:**
- Supports: pre-illness energy deficit manifesting as fatigue, pain, cognitive symptoms years before formal diagnosis
- Caveat: ICD codes are clinician-assigned, may reflect misdiagnosis of prodromal ME/CFS symptoms rather than separate conditions
- Key phrase: "up to five years of increased medical utilization before diagnosis" (Crawley 2018 data, confirmed by this study's temporal window)

### 2. Collin et al. 2018 — Childhood Sleep Predicts Adolescent ME/CFS (STRONG PROSPECTIVE SIGNAL)

**Full citation:** Collin SM, Norris T, Gringras P, Blair PS, Tilling K, Crawley E. Childhood sleep and adolescent chronic fatigue syndrome (CFS/ME): evidence of associations in a UK birth cohort. _Sleep Medicine_. 2018;47:85–91. DOI: 10.1016/j.sleep.2018.01.005. PMID: 29773208. PMC: PMC5974860.

**Study design:** Prospective population-based birth cohort (ALSPAC, UK). N=13,978 children followed from 6 months to 18 years. 242 developed chronic disabling fatigue (CDF).

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- Shorter nighttime sleep duration throughout childhood (ages 6 months–11 years) preceded ME/CFS by 4–7 years
- Children who developed CDF went to bed ~11 minutes later on average
- Difficulty falling asleep: odds 74% higher in future CDF cases at all ages examined
- Quantified: each additional hour of sleep at age 9 reduced CDF odds at age 13 by 39%; at age 11, reduced odds at age 16 by 51%
- More frequent nighttime awakenings at ages 6 months and 6–9 years
- Authors interpret: "direct causal involvement or a common pathophysiological cause"

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- Sleep that "never refreshes" may have childhood precursor: systematically shorter, disrupted, later-onset sleep BEFORE full ME/CFS
- In the child who trains heavily: persistent sleep disruption despite fatigue is a marker — not "needs less sleep" but abnormal sleep architecture predating diagnosis
- Unrefreshing sleep at age 12 may be measurable consequence of autonomic dysregulation already present at age 6–9

**Quality:** High. Prospective cohort, large N, longitudinal 18-year follow-up. CDF definition may not fully align with ME/CFS criteria (limitation).

**Certainty:** 0.65

**Replication:** Partially replicated within ALSPAC; independent replication needed.

**Integration points:**
- Sleep disruption as early biomarker of insufficient energy reserve / autonomic dysregulation
- Challenge to "oversleeper" narrative: these children were sleeping LESS, not more — insufficient restorative sleep years before diagnosis

### 3. Wyller et al. 2007 — Thermoregulation Abnormalities in Adolescent CFS

**Full citation:** Wyller VB, Godang K, Mørkrid L, Saul JP, Thaulow E, Walløe L. Abnormal thermoregulatory responses in adolescents with chronic fatigue syndrome: relation to clinical symptoms. _Pediatrics_. 2007;120(1):e129–37. DOI: 10.1542/peds.2006-2759. PMID: 17606539.

**Study design:** Comparative clinical investigation. N=15 CFS patients ages 12–18 vs 57 healthy controls. Measured catecholamines, skin blood flow, temperature responses during standardized cooling tests.

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- Impaired peripheral vasoconstriction during hand cooling: acral skin blood flow "less reduced" in CFS
- Vasoconstrictor events occurred at lower temperatures than controls
- Elevated basal norepinephrine, epinephrine, heart rate at rest
- Catecholamine responses to cooling comparable between groups → suggests central sympathetic dysregulation rather than peripheral failure
- Patients reported more shivering, sweating, skin color changes, and sensation of unusual warmth
- Conclusion: "abnormal catecholaminergic-dependent thermoregulatory responses both at rest and during local skin cooling, supporting a hypothesis of sympathetic dysfunction"

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- Cold hands and feet in a young person with fatigue: measurable sympathetic dysfunction documented in adolescent ME/CFS
- If autonomic dysregulation precedes full ME/CFS (as OI literature suggests), cold extremities + easy fatigue in a child IS a plausible prodromal signal
- Not simply "poor circulation" — reflects dysregulated catecholaminergic system

**Quality:** Medium. Small N (15 cases), single-center, cross-sectional. Pediatrics (high-impact journal). Rigorous laboratory methods.

**Certainty:** 0.50

**Replication:** Partially — sympathetic dysregulation in pediatric ME/CFS is multi-study supported; specific thermoregulation protocol not widely replicated.

### 4. Chu et al. 2019 — Onset Patterns and Course of ME/CFS

**Full citation:** Chu L, Valencia IJ, Garvert DW, Montoya JG. Onset Patterns and Course of Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome. _Frontiers in Pediatrics_. 2019;7:12. DOI: 10.3389/fped.2019.00012.

**Study design:** Descriptive epidemiological survey. N=150 adults meeting Fukuda 1994 CFS criteria. US-based cohort. Mixed quantitative + qualitative.

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- For 38% of participants, >6 months elapsed from initial symptom to full syndrome development — clear gradual onset/prodromal window
- Top precipitating triggers: infectious episode 64%, stress/major life event 39%, environmental toxin 20%
- 79% (11/14 qualitative) reported pre-illness health problems
- Pre-illness problems documented: recurrent infections (5/14), autonomic symptoms — dyspnea/presyncope for months prior (1/14), unusual fatigue
- One participant: "when I stood for ten to fifteen minutes I would get out of breath...that definitely started happening around July, August" — months before formal onset
- 59% fluctuating course; 47% unable to work at survey time
- 12 most common symptoms persisted but declined in prevalence over time; cognitive difficulties relatively stable

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- 38% had a multi-month prodromal window — sufficient time for missed early signs
- Recurrent infections as prodromal: immune fragility before major collapse
- Exercise intolerance (dyspnea on standing) preceding full syndrome

**Quality:** Medium. N=150, survey-based, descriptive. Frontiers in Pediatrics (note: sample was adults). No control group.

**Certainty:** 0.45

**Replication:** Consistent with Evans & Jason 2018 mixed-methods study on onset complexity.

### 5. Evans & Jason 2018 — Onset Patterns (Qualitative)

**Full citation:** Evans MA, Jason LA. Onset patterns of chronic fatigue syndrome and myalgic encephalomyelitis. _Research on Chronic Diseases_. 2018;2(1). [No DOI provided in open-access source; Open Access Journals.]

**Study design:** Mixed-methods. N=181 quantitative survey; 14 semi-structured qualitative interviews. US-based.

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- Dichotomous "sudden vs gradual" categorization inadequate; 5 onset subtypes identified qualitatively
- 79% (11/14) reported pre-illness health problems
- Subtypes include: "unnoticed progression" (retrospectively recognized) — directly supports prodromal concept
- Pre-illness signals: recurrent infections, autonomic symptoms, unusual fatigue
- 86% described significant stress, trauma, or infection preceding illness

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- "Unnoticed progression" subtype: symptoms present but not recognized as illness — matches the scenario of the "tired but high-functioning child"
- Validates retrospective prodromal recall as a legitimate onset category

**Quality:** Medium-Low. Small qualitative sample (14); survey-based quantitative phase. No PMID/DOI confirmed for open-access source.

**Certainty:** 0.40

**Replication:** Consistent with Chu et al. 2019 findings.

### 6. Moak et al. 2024 — Small Fiber Neuropathy in Pediatric POTS/OI

**Full citation:** Moak JP, Ramwell CB, Gordish-Dressman H, Sule SD, Bettini E. Small fiber neuropathy in children, adolescents, and young adults with chronic orthostatic intolerance and postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome: A retrospective study. _Autonomic Neuroscience_. 2024;253:103163. DOI: 10.1016/j.autneu.2024.103163. PMID: 38537312.

**Study design:** Retrospective cohort. N=109 children, adolescents, and young adults with POTS/OI and neuropathic symptoms.

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- 53% of POTS/OI patients tested had abnormal skin biopsy (SFN)
- Patients with SFN had 3× probability of positive ANA or anti-thyroid antibody — autoimmune substrate
- Predominantly female, Caucasian cohort
- Combined presentation: orthostatic intolerance + fatigue + paresthesia + brain fog + pain

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- Small fiber neuropathy may underlie the "shaky hands," cold extremities, and exercise intolerance in adolescents before full ME/CFS diagnosis
- SFN causes: dysautonomia (POTS), pain, proprioception deficits, fatigue — could present as "fine tremor" and "coordination issues" in young athletes
- Autoimmune trigger + SFN + OI triad: plausible subclinical phase years before ME/CFS threshold crossed

**Quality:** Medium. N=109 retrospective, single-center, specialty clinic. Autonomic Neuroscience journal.

**Certainty:** 0.50

**Replication:** SFN in POTS is multi-study supported (Boris 2014, Gibbons 2012 in adults); pediatric-specific data emerging.

### 7. Kato et al. 2006 — Premorbid Predictors of Chronic Fatigue (Twin Study)

**Full citation:** Kato K, Sullivan PF, Evengård B, Pedersen NL. Premorbid predictors of chronic fatigue. _Archives of General Psychiatry_. 2006;63(11):1267–72. DOI: 10.1001/archpsyc.63.11.1267. PMID: 17088507.

**Study design:** Prospective nested case-control in Swedish Twin Registry. N=19,192 twins. Pre-illness factors measured 25 years before fatigue onset.

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- Higher emotional instability premorbidly: 55–72% increased risk of chronic fatigue
- Perceived life stress premorbidly: 64–65% elevated risk in standard analysis; 5.81-fold increase after controlling for genetics
- When genetic factors controlled (discordant twin pairs): emotional instability effect disappeared — genetic basis
- Stress effect strengthened after genetic control — true environmental risk factor
- Extraversion: no meaningful association

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- Perceived stress as true causal factor (not just symptom correlation) for fatigue onset
- CAVEAT: "emotional instability" in premorbid period may partially reflect subclinical autonomic dysregulation misattributed to personality — a classic diagnostic misattribution
- 25-year prospective window is exceptional; but sample is adults, measures are psychological, not physiological

**Quality:** High. N=19,192, prospective twin design with 25-year follow-up, controls for genetic confounding. Arch Gen Psych. Weakness: uses "chronic fatigue" broadly, not ME/CFS specifically; psychological instruments not physiological biomarkers.

**Certainty:** 0.55 (for chronic fatigue; lower for ME/CFS specifically ~0.40)

**Replication:** Consistent with other stress-onset literature but twin design is unique.

### 8. Jason et al. 2015 — CFS vs Sudden Onset ME (NULL RESULT FOR PRODROME)

**Full citation:** Jason LA, Evans M, Brown A, Sunnquist M, Newton JL. Chronic Fatigue Syndrome Versus Sudden Onset Myalgic Encephalomyelitis. _Journal of Prevention & Intervention in the Community_. 2015;43(1):6–12. DOI: 10.1080/10852352.2014.973233. PMID: 25584529.

**Study design:** Comparative study. N=285 (DePaul: 189; Newcastle: 96). Compared ME (sudden onset within 24h–1 week) vs CFS criteria.

**Key findings (null/contradicting):**
- ME (sudden onset) patients: more severe functional limitations, worse Physical Functioning, Role Physical, Bodily Pain
- Greater PEM severity in ME vs CFS group
- NO discussion of prodromal phase in sudden-onset ME patients
- Sudden onset criteria specifically exclude prodromal gradual deterioration for that subgroup

**Relevance to prodromal question:**
- PARTIAL NEGATIVE: A subset (~50% in some studies) of ME/CFS has true sudden onset with no identifiable prodromal phase
- This contradicts a universal prodrome claim — prodromal signs likely apply to the gradual-onset subtype, not sudden-onset ME
- The two phenotypes (sudden post-infectious ME vs gradual-onset CFS) may have different premorbid signatures

**Quality:** Medium. N=285, two cohorts, validated questionnaires. Limitation: retrospective onset categorization subject to recall bias.

**Certainty for null result:** 0.55

### 9. Geraghty & Adeniji 2019 — Diagnostic Accuracy Commentary (DIAGNOSTIC DELAY DATA)

**Full citation:** Geraghty KJ, Adeniji C. The Importance of Accurate Diagnosis of ME/CFS in Children and Adolescents: A Commentary. _Frontiers in Pediatrics_. 2019;7:435. DOI: 10.3389/fped.2018.00435. PMID: 30719431.

**Study design:** Commentary/narrative review. Not primary research.

**Key findings relevant to prodromal/early signs:**
- 40% diagnostic error rate among children referred to specialist clinics
- Misdiagnosis breakdown: 47% chronic disease (other), 20% primary sleep disorder, 15% psychological/psychiatric illness, 4% cardiovascular disorder
- Adult ME/CFS: average 5 years to diagnosis
- Pediatric: documented increased medical consultations up to 5 years before diagnosis (citing Crawley)
- PEM identified as the "cardinal symptom" most frequently missed
- CAVEAT: "8 out of every 10 teens identified as possible CFS cases recovered by age 18" — significant overdiagnosis risk with broad criteria

**Relevance to prodromal scenarios:**
- 5-year pre-diagnosis medical utilization window = prodromal symptomatic period
- Sleep disorder misdiagnosis (20%): these children had sleep problems before CFS diagnosis — consistent with Collin 2018
- Cardiovascular disorder misdiagnosis (4%): likely OI/POTS cases initially labeled as cardiac
- The 40% diagnostic error rate cuts both ways: underdiagnosis AND overdiagnosis

**Quality:** Low-Medium (commentary, narrative). Frontiers in Pediatrics. Useful for policy/clinical implications but not primary evidence.

**Certainty:** 0.40 (commentary level)

## Synthesis: What the Evidence Supports

### Supported prodromal signals (evidence-based):

| Signal | Evidence source | Strength |
|--------|----------------|----------|
| Pre-ME/CFS diagnoses (fatigue, pain, cognitive, somatoform) up to 5 years before ME/CFS Dx | Wirth 2026 (N=36,332) | STRONG |
| Childhood sleep disruption (shorter duration, difficulty falling asleep, later bedtimes) 4–7 years before CDF | Collin 2018 (N=13,978 prospective) | STRONG |
| Sympathetic/thermoregulatory dysfunction (cold extremities, abnormal catecholaminergic responses) at time of adolescent ME/CFS Dx | Wyller 2007 (N=15 vs 57) | MEDIUM |
| Gradual multi-month onset with pre-illness recurrent infections and autonomic symptoms | Chu 2019, Evans & Jason 2018 | MEDIUM |
| SFN in 53% of pediatric POTS/OI patients — autoimmune substrate + neuropathic symptoms | Moak 2024 (N=109) | MEDIUM |
| Perceived stress as true causal (not merely associated) premorbid factor | Kato 2006 (N=19,192 twins) | MEDIUM |

### Supported scenarios:

**Scenario 1 (Athletic child, poor fitness return):** Not directly studied prospectively. BUT: Moak 2024 (SFN → dysautonomia → exercise intolerance), Wyller 2007 (sympathetic dysfunction at onset), Collin 2018 (sleep disruption years prior). Plausible mechanistic chain, no direct evidence of pre-diagnosis athletic performance decline in prospective study.

**Scenario 2 (Fades late in sport, looks like inattention):** Supported mechanistically: OI causes cognitive deterioration on exertion (established). Post-exertional cognitive fatigue misattributed to inattention is documented clinically. No prospective study specifically isolating this pattern.

**Scenario 3 (Fine hand tremor):** POTS/hyperadrenergic POTS includes tremor as a symptom. SFN (Moak 2024) could cause proprioceptive dysfunction. No ME/CFS-specific prodromal tremor study found. Weakest evidence base of the four scenarios.

**Scenario 4 (Multiple other signs):** Best-supported collectively. Unrefreshing sleep: Collin 2018 (prodromal sleep data), orthostatic symptoms: OI misdiagnosis literature, post-exertional cognitive fog: clinical documentation but no prospective prodromal study specifically.

### Negative/null findings:

- True sudden-onset ME (Jason 2015): ~50% of cases have no identifiable prodrome
- Geraghty 2019: 40% diagnostic error rate and 80% pediatric recovery rate suggest overdiagnosis risk — not all fatigued children have subclinical ME/CFS
- Kato 2006 "emotional instability" effect disappears after genetic control — not a true environmental predictor
- No prospective study found tracking exercise capacity (VO2max) in children before ME/CFS diagnosis

### Gap in the literature:

**The most important gap:** No prospective study has measured objective exercise capacity (VO2max, CPET) or autonomic function (HRV, tilt test) in children years before ME/CFS diagnosis. All evidence is either retrospective (ICD codes, recall-based) or cross-sectional (at time of diagnosis). A birth-cohort study with annual CPET and autonomic monitoring would be definitively informative.

## Integration Guide for chapter-integrator

### Primary targets:
- `ch05` (onset patterns, subgroups) — onset complexity, prodromal window
- `ch14d` (Architecture C metabolic reserve) — subclinical energy deficit as predisposing factor
- `ch23` or pediatric chapter — misdiagnosis patterns, diagnostic delay

### Environment types:
- Wirth 2026: `achievement` (large observational data) + `warning` (directionality unclear)
- Collin 2018: `achievement` (prospective signal) + `speculation` (causal mechanism)
- Wyller 2007: `observation` (clinical finding at diagnosis time)
- Jason 2015: `warning` (sudden onset subtype has no prodrome — limits universality of prodromal narrative)
- Others: `hypothesis` or `observation`

### Critical framing guidance:
1. Never claim all ME/CFS has a prodrome — Jason 2015 shows ~50% sudden onset; prodromal evidence applies to gradual-onset subtype
2. Never present pre-illness psychiatric diagnoses (depression, somatoform) as true comorbidities without noting they may be misattributed prodromal ME/CFS symptoms
3. The "previously athletic" description (cited in Jason 2012, Arav-Boger 1995) may reflect clinic-sampling bias — acknowledge this
4. Overdiagnosis caveat (Geraghty): 80% of broad-criteria pediatric cases recover by 18 — careful case definition is critical
