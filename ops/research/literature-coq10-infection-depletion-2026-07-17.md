## Hypothesis Assessment

**Does the evidence support infection-driven CoQ10 consumption as a causal driver of CoQ10 deficiency in ME/CFS?**

### Summary
The evidence is **SUPPORTIVE BUT INDIRECT** — the biochemical framework is solid, and there are isolated mechanistic links, but the specific causal chain (persistent infection → sustained ROS → accelerated CoQ10 consumption → deficiency) has NOT been directly tested as a unified hypothesis in ME/CFS.

### What IS established
1. CoQ10 is consumed as a sacrificial antioxidant when neutralizing lipid peroxyl radicals (Morris2013CoQ10) ✓
2. CoQ10 is lower in ME/CFS plasma (Maes2009CoQ10) ✓
3. Lipid peroxidation is elevated in ME/CFS (Maes2014OSEs, Maes2021Reification) ✓
4. Viral reactivation (HHV-6) causes mitochondrial fragmentation and increased ROS (Schreiner2020HHV6Mito) ✓
5. Post-viral fatigue (Long COVID) shows infection-severity-dependent antioxidant depletion (Al-Hakeim2023LongCOVIDOx) ✓

### What is MISSING (research gaps)
1. **No direct measurement** of CoQ10 consumption rate in ME/CFS vs controls
2. **No correlation** between pathogen load markers (EBV DNA, HHV-6 RNA) and CoQ10 levels
3. **No intervention** showing that antiviral treatment restores CoQ10
4. **No tissue CoQ10** measurements (all plasma)
5. **No genetic analysis** of COQ biosynthesis genes in ME/CFS to rule out primary deficiency
6. **No longitudinal study** tracking CoQ10 from acute infection through chronic illness

### Competing explanations not ruled out
- Primary CoQ10 deficiency from COQ gene polymorphisms (Laredj2014CoQBiosynth) — untested in ME/CFS
- Statin-induced CoQ10 depletion — relevant if ME/CFS patients take statins
- Downregulation of CoQ10 biosynthesis from general mitochondrial biogenesis defect
- Secondary deficiency from malnutrition/GI malabsorption

### Verdict
**HYPOTHESIS IS PLAUSIBLE BUT UNPROVEN.** The biochemical logic is sound (sustained ROS → CoQ10 consumption → deficiency). The observational evidence is consistent (CoQ10 is low, ROS/lipid peroxidation are high, viral reactivation occurs). But the causal arrow has not been demonstrated — CoQ10 deficiency could equally be a consequence of impaired biosynthesis, mitochondrial biogenesis defects, or secondary factors. A dedicated study measuring CoQ10 consumption kinetics, pathogen biomarkers, and biosynthesis gene expression in the same ME/CFS patients is needed.
