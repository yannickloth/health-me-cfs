# Literature Summary: Exercise Recovery and PEM Duration in ME/CFS

## Overview

This literature review focuses on:
1. PEM recovery duration and quantification
2. CPET protocols and limitations in ME/CFS
3. Severity vs recovery time relationships
4. Home-based exercise testing alternatives
5. Symptom severity scales for PEM
6. Null results and contradictory evidence

---

## Key Findings

### 1. 2-Day CPET: Consistent Evidence for Impaired Recovery

**High-certainty finding (n=84 ME/CFS, n=71 controls):**
- **Keller2024CPET** (PMID: 38965566): Largest 2-day CPET study to date confirms ME/CFS patients fail to reproduce CPET-1 measures during CPET-2, with significant declines in work, VO2, HR, O2 pulse at peak exertion. Intraclass correlations show greater stability in controls vs ME/CFS. Worsening impairment status on CPET-2 translates to tangible reductions in physiological function.

**Medium-certainty confirmations (n=25-82):**
- **vanCampen2020SeverityCPET** (PMID: 32629923): 82 female ME/CFS patients (31 mild, 31 moderate, 20 severe) - all severity groups showed significant CPET-2 declines. Peak workload decrease largest in severe group (-19% ± 11%).
- **vanCampen2021FemaleCPET** (PMID: 34198913): 50 ME/CFS vs 51 ICF females - ME/CFS showed deterioration on CPET2, ICF showed improvement (similar to healthy controls).
- **vanCampen2021MaleCPET** (PMID: 34198946): 26 ME/CFS vs 25 ICF males - same pattern as females.

**Meta-analysis:**
- **Lim2020CPETMeta** (PMID: 33327624): Confirms 2-day CPET can objectively assess PEM, with workload at ventilatory threshold showing highly significant differences between patients and controls on day 2.

**Historical validation:**
- **Snell2013CPET** (PMID: 23813081): 51 CFS vs 10 controls - 95.1% classification accuracy using CPET-2 parameters. Single test insufficient to demonstrate impairment.

**Conclusion:** 2-day CPET is the most validated objective measure of PEM in ME/CFS, with consistent findings across multiple studies and severity levels.

---

### 2. PEM Recovery Duration: Limited Direct Evidence

**Key gap identified:** No studies directly quantify PEM recovery time beyond 24h. All 2-day CPET studies use 24h interval between tests, which may be insufficient for full recovery.

**Indirect evidence:**
- **Moore2023recovery** (PMID: 36984572): Investigates recovery from exercise (existing in references.bib, certainty 0.65). Sample size and methodology not fully specified in available abstract.
- **Twomey2020PEMCancer** (PMID: 32105793): In chronic cancer-related fatigue, 33% met PEM criteria including recovery duration >24h. Suggests >24h recovery may be characteristic of PEM across conditions.

**Research gap:** No studies systematically measure recovery time to baseline in ME/CFS. Most use 24h as standard interval without validating it represents full recovery.

---

### 3. Severity vs Recovery Time

**Direct evidence:**
- **vanCampen2020SeverityCPET** (PMID: 32629923): All severity groups (mild, moderate, severe) showed CPET-2 declines. Peak workload decrease magnitude: severe > moderate > mild (significant difference mild vs severe, p=0.019). However, all groups showed similar patterns of impairment.

**Indirect evidence:**
- **Cotler2020PEMRespiration** (PMID: 36313239): PEM (measured by DSQ-PEM) predicted rapid respiratory rates in n=216 ME/CFS patients. Suggests PEM severity correlates with physiological abnormalities.

**Tomaskovic2026CPET** (PMID: 42043742): In 92 PCC patients (49% meeting ME/CFS criteria), ME/CFS patients showed lower VO2peak (13.0 vs 15.4 mL/min/kg) and PPO (0.9 vs 1.1 W/kg) vs non-ME/CFS. VO2peak and PPO correlated with Bell-Score (subjective disability).

**Conclusion:** Severity influences absolute exercise capacity but does not eliminate 2-day CPET impairment pattern. Even mild ME/CFS shows CPET-2 declines.

---

### 4. CPET Limitations and Challenges

**Sample bias:**
- All CPET studies exclude patients unable to complete maximal effort or 2-day protocol.
- **Snell2013CPET** explicitly excludes those unable to meet maximal effort criteria.
- This likely underestimates impairment in severe ME/CFS.

**Single-center bias:**
- Most studies from limited number of research centers (Stichting Cardiozorg, Cornell/Ithaca, etc.).
- **Keller2024CPET** and **vanCampen** series from same research networks.

**Comparison condition issues:**
- Some use sedentary controls, others use idiopathic chronic fatigue (ICF).
- ICF patients showed CPET-2 improvement (similar to healthy controls), suggesting 2-day CPET discriminates ME/CFS from general fatigue.

**24h interval assumption:**
- No validation that 24h represents full recovery time.
- May underestimate PEM duration if recovery takes >24h.

### 5. Home-Based Exercise Testing Alternatives

**Direct searches for ACHTSAM, FUNCAP:**
- FUNCAP (Functional Capacity Assessment) is a questionnaire, not exercise test (Sommerfelt2024FUNCAP, PMID: 38930014).
- No studies found on ACHTSAM protocol for ME/CFS.

**Indirect evidence for home-based protocols:**
- **Tomaskovic2026CPET** used DSQ-PEM (DePaul Symptom Questionnaire) as home-based PEM assessment.
- **Cotler2020PEMRespiration** used resting respiratory rate measurement as potential objective PEM metric.

**Gap:** No validated home-based exercise testing protocols found. All objective testing requires laboratory CPET.

---

### 6. Symptom Severity Scales for PEM

**Validated scales:**
- **DSQ-PEM (DePaul Symptom Questionnaire - Post-Exertional Malaise):**
  - Used in **Tomaskovic2026CPET** (79% screened positive)
  - Used in **Twomey2020PEMCancer** (defined PEM as: moderate-very severe symptoms ≥50% of time + worsening after minimal effort + recovery >24h)
  - Used in **Cotler2020PEMRespiration** (PEM predicted tachypnea)

- **Bell Disability Scale:**
  - Used in **Tomaskovic2026CPET** (correlated with VO2peak and PPO)
  - Bell-Score ≤30 (51% of patients) associated with objective work disability

- **MFI (Multidimensional Fatigue Inventory), Chalder Fatigue Scale:**
  - Mentioned in various studies but not primary focus

**Gap:** No studies correlate scale scores with objective recovery time measurements.

### 7. Null Results and Contradictory Evidence

**Long COVID vs ME/CFS:**
- **Gattoni2025LongCOVIDCPET** (PMID: 39490617): 15 Long COVID patients (80% with PEM symptoms) showed NO differences between day 1 and day 2 CPET. Suggests PEM in Long COVID may have different pathophysiology than ME/CFS.

**Cardiac function:**
- **Iversen2020CardiacNull** (PMID: 33081294): 16 ME/CFS vs 10 controls - NO differences in echocardiographic cardiac dimensions or function. Challenges cardiac dysfunction hypothesis for exercise intolerance.

**Sex differences:**
- **vanCampen2021MaleCPET** and **vanCampen2021FemaleCPET**: Both showed identical patterns - ME/CFS deteriorates on CPET2, ICF improves. No sex differences in CPET response.

**Cancer-related fatigue:**
- **Twomey2020PEMCancer**: Only 33% of chronic cancer-related fatigue patients met PEM criteria. Suggests PEM is not universal across all fatigue conditions.

### 8. Novel Mechanistic Insights

**Epigenetic changes:**
- **Sharma2025EpigeneticPEM** (PMID: 40943482): 5 ME/CFS patients showed temporally dynamic DNA methylation changes at 0h, 24h, 48h after CPET. 98% of differentially methylated fragments were ME/CFS-specific. Early DMFs (0-24h) and late DMFs (24-48h) suggest ongoing molecular recovery beyond 24h.

**Extracellular vesicles:**
- **Giloteaux2024EVExercise** (PMID: 38173127): 18 ME/CFS vs 17 controls - exercise affects EV proteome differently in ME/CFS. Changes correlated with symptom severity. Involved pathways: coagulation, muscle contraction, immune system, brain signaling.

**Brain activation:**
- **Baraniuk2022fMRIExercise** (PMID: 35053821): 36 ME/CFS vs 78 GWI vs 31 controls - exercise caused increased activation in ME/CFS but decreased activation in GWI in midbrain nuclei (periaqueductal gray, raphe, locus coeruleus). Opposite patterns suggest different pathophysiology.

**PEM pathophysiology model:**
- **Jin2026PEMMechanism** (PMID: 42051540): Integrative review proposes "metabolism-immune-neuro" interaction model: mitochondrial dysfunction → ROS/mtDNA as DAMPs → NLRP3 inflammasome → IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α → neuroinflammation → interoceptive network dysfunction → PEM.

**Skeletal muscle:**
- **Fanò-Illic2026SkeletalMuscle** (PMID: 42000527): Review emphasizes skeletal muscle involvement: oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, impaired Ca2+ handling, altered signaling. ME/CFS as skeletal muscle-metabolic disorder.

**Thalamic connectivity:**
- **Wu2026ThalamusFatigue** (PMID: 42021621): 100 CFS vs 100 controls - thalamic-surface FC alterations associated with fatigue. Tai Chi exercise showed FC changes correlated with fatigue improvement. Potential non-invasive brain stimulation target.

### 9. Exercise Efficacy Meta-Analysis

**Zhao2026ExerciseMeta** (PMID: 42000527): 17 RCTs (n=1944)
- Exercise significantly improved: fatigue (SMD=0.85), sleep (SMD=0.42), QoL (SMD=0.99), depression (SMD=0.56), anxiety (SMD=0.14)
- NO significant improvement in: cardiopulmonary function (SMD=0.13), functional capacity (SMD=0.09)
- **Critical limitation:** All outcomes patient-reported, no objective measures. High heterogeneity (I²=85.3% for fatigue).
- **Blinding bias:** Subjective outcomes susceptible to blinding bias (Hrobjartsson2014: SMD 0.56 exaggeration).

**Conclusion:** Exercise may improve subjective symptoms but does not restore objective cardiopulmonary function. Structural flaw in exercise RCTs - no blinding possible for exercise interventions.

---

### 10. Case Study: Identical Twins Discordant for ME/CFS

**Giloteaux2016Twins** (PMID: 27721367):
- 34-year-old monozygotic twins: WELL vs ILL
- ILL showed lower VO2peak and peak workload on both CPETs
- ILL had abnormal 13% reduction in VAT on CPET2 vs WELL (normal reproducibility)
- ILL showed higher post-exercise increases in resistin, sCD40L, sFasL (immune activation)
- ILL had lower gut microbiome diversity, fewer beneficial bacteria (Faecalibacterium, Bifidobacterium), expansion of bacteriophages
- **Significance:** Controls for genetics, age, early environment - differences due to ME/CFS pathology or environmental triggers.

## Research Gaps Identified

### Critical Gaps

1. **PEM recovery duration quantification:**
   - No studies measure time to full recovery after exertion
   - 24h interval used in all 2-day CPET studies without validation
   - Need systematic studies with multiple timepoints (24h, 48h, 72h, 1 week)

2. **Home-based exercise testing:**
   - No validated home-based protocols (ACHTSAM not found in literature)
   - All objective testing requires laboratory CPET
   - Need alternatives for bedbound/severe patients

3. **Severity-specific recovery:**
   - Do mild/moderate/severe patients have different recovery times?
   - vanCampen2020 shows severity affects exercise capacity but not CPET-2 pattern

4. **Longitudinal recovery trajectories:**
   - Only Sharma2025EpigeneticPEM provides 48h data
   - No studies beyond 48h

5. **Biomarkers of recovery:**
   - Epigenetic markers (Sharma2025), EV proteome (Giloteaux2024) promising
   - Need validation in larger cohorts

### Secondary Gaps

6. **Sex differences in recovery:**
   - vanCampen2021 shows no sex differences in CPET response
   - But recovery time may differ?

7. **Age effects on recovery:**
   - No studies examine age vs recovery relationship

8. **Comorbidities:**
   - How do POTS, OI, MCAS affect recovery?

9. **Treatment effects on recovery:**
   - Do any treatments accelerate recovery?
   - Zhao2026ExerciseMeta shows exercise improves symptoms but not objective function

10. **Comparison with other PEM conditions:**
    - Long COVID shows different 2-day CPET pattern (Gattoni2025 - null)
    - Cancer-related fatigue shows lower PEM prevalence (Twomey2020)
    - Need systematic cross-condition comparisons
