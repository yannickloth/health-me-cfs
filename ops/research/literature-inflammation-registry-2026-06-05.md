## Category 1: S100 Proteins / Calprotectin (DAMPs)

### Nunes 2024 — ME/CFS Plasma Proteomics Reveals S100-A9 Upregulation

- **Full Citation**: Nunes M, Vlok M, Proal A, Kell DB, Pretorius E. Data-independent LC-MS/MS analysis of ME/CFS plasma reveals a dysregulated coagulation system, endothelial dysfunction, downregulation of complement machinery. _Cardiovascular Diabetology_. 2024;23:260.
- **DOI**: 10.1186/s12933-024-02315-x
- **PMID**: 39014464
- **Key Findings**:
  - Significant upregulation of protein S100-A9 (calprotectin subunit) in ME/CFS plasma
  - Upregulation of lactotransferrin and an immunoglobulin variant
  - Dysregulated coagulation system (hyperactivated platelets)
  - Endothelial dysfunction markers
  - Downregulation of complement machinery components
  - First proteomics study using data-independent acquisition (DIA) in ME/CFS
- **Conclusion**: ME/CFS involves a pro-inflammatory and pro-thrombotic plasma proteome signature with endothelial involvement. S100-A9 may serve as a biomarker and therapeutic target.
- **Limitations**: Cross-sectional; moderate sample size; single cohort; plasma proteomics only (no CSF); sex-stratified analysis not performed
- **Certainty**: 0.75 — Direct ME/CFS study, rigorous proteomics methodology, published in reputable journal, but single cohort cross-sectional

## Category 2: cGAS-STING Pathway (mtDNA Sensing)

### Chelette 2025 — cGAS-STING Contributes to Cancer-Related Fatigue

- **Full Citation**: Chelette B, Bashaw A, Bryant JD, Scott K, Chidomere C, West AP, Dantzer R. Activation of the cGAS-STING pathway contributes to cancer-related fatigue in a murine model of head and neck cancer. _bioRxiv_. 2025 Oct 4.
- **DOI**: 10.1101/2025.10.02.680158
- **PMID**: 41256649
- **Key Findings**:
  - cGAS-STING pathway activation drives systemic inflammation in cancer-related fatigue
  - STING activation induced fatigue-like behavior in murine model
  - Mechanistic link between mtDNA release, innate immune sensing, and fatigue
  - Inflammation extended beyond tumor microenvironment to systemic effects
  - cGAS-STING contributed to disrupted energy homeostasis
- **Conclusion**: cGAS-STING-mediated inflammation is a mechanistic contributor to fatigue, establishing a novel pathway for fatigue pathogenesis.
- **Limitations**: Preclinical murine model; cancer-specific (not ME/CFS); preprint (not peer-reviewed)
- **Certainty**: 0.40 — First direct link of cGAS-STING to fatigue phenotype, but cancer model, murine, preprint. High mechanistic plausibility for ME/CFS extrapolation.

### Yan 2026 — cGAS-STING in Disease Pathogenesis and Therapy

- **Full Citation**: Yan D, Hu J, Liu Z, Ouyang W. Spotlight on cGAS-STING: role in disease pathogenesis and therapeutic potential. _Molecular Biomedicine_. 2026;7(1):83.
- **DOI**: 10.1186/s43556-026-00283-2
- **PMID**: 42247008
- **Key Findings**: Comprehensive review of cGAS-STING pathway in autoimmune, inflammatory, and degenerative diseases. Covers cGAS-STING as a central sensor of cytosolic DNA (including mtDNA) driving type I interferon and NF-κB responses.
- **Conclusion**: cGAS-STING is a key node in sterile inflammation; emerging as therapeutic target.
- **Limitations**: Review, no primary ME/CFS data
- **Certainty**: 0.85 (for general pathway role); 0.20 (for ME/CFS specificity — no ME/CFS data in review)

### Existing bib note: NAD depletion → cGAS-STING

The existing bib (line ~18372) contains a paper showing that chronic NAD depletion causes mtDNA leakage via VDAC1 → cGAS-STING activation → type I interferon signature. This is directly relevant to ME/CFS but does not study fatigue behavior.

## Category 3: Specialized Pro-Resolving Mediators (SPMs)

### Engert 2026 — Sleep Disturbance Affects Inflammatory Resolution in Long COVID

- **Full Citation**: Engert LC, Dang R, Daniel S, Bertisch SM, Maley JH, Fong TG, Serhan CN, Mullington JM, Haack M. Sleep disturbance affects inflammatory resolution in Long COVID. _Prostaglandins Leukotrienes and Essential Fatty Acids_. 2026;204:102728.
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.plefa.2026.102728
- **PMID**: 41650882
- **Key Findings**:
  - Sleep disturbance in Long COVID promotes pro-inflammatory state
  - Sleep disruption dysregulates lipid-derived SPMs (resolvins, protectins, maresins)
  - Failed resolution of inflammation as a mechanism in Long COVID
  - SPM dysregulation contributes to chronic unresolved inflammation
- **Conclusion**: Sleep disturbance → SPM dysregulation → unresolved inflammation → Long COVID symptoms. SPM restoration may be therapeutic.
- **Limitations**: Review/hypothesis paper; no primary data on SPM levels in Long COVID; sleep-SPM link established in general populations but not specifically in Long COVID
- **Certainty**: 0.60 — Novel mechanistic hypothesis; Serhan (SPM discoverer) co-author strengthens credibility; but limited direct evidence in target condition

### Rauf 2026 — PASC as Disorder of Impaired Innate Immune Resolution

- **Full Citation**: Rauf M, Naveed A, Asghar MU. Post-acute sequelae of COVID-19: A disorder of impaired innate immune resolution — A narrative review. _Clinical Immunology_. 2026;277:110701.
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.clim.2026.110701
- **PMID**: 41864480
- **Key Findings**:
  - PASC characterized by failed innate immune resolution rather than persistent viral infection
  - Residual SARS-CoV-2 antigens, host alarmins, reactivated latent viruses sustain inflammation
  - Chronic activation of pattern-recognition receptors, inflammasomes, and complement
  - Persistent TLR, RIG-I, NLR, and cGAS-STING signaling drive chronic inflammation
- **Conclusion**: Resolution failure model explains PASC pathogenesis; therapeutic focus should shift to restoring resolution mechanisms.
- **Limitations**: Narrative review; no primary data; PASC-specific (generalizable to ME/CFS but not proven)
- **Certainty**: 0.65 — Well-reasoned synthesis; published in Clin Immunol (strong journal); direct applicability to ME/CFS

### Serhan 2022 — E-Series Resolvin Metabolome and SPMs for Long COVID

- **Full Citation**: Serhan CN, Libreros S, Nshimiyimana R. E-series resolvin metabolome, biosynthesis and critical role of stereochemistry of specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs) in inflammation-resolution: Preparing SPMs for long COVID-19, human clinical trials, and targeted precision nutrition. _Seminars in Immunology_. 2022;59:101597.
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.smim.2022.101597
- **PMID**: 35227568
- **Key Findings**:
  - Detailed biosynthetic pathways of E-series resolvins, D-series resolvins, protectins, maresins
  - Stereochemical requirements for SPM bioactivity — critical for therapeutic development
  - SPMs actively resolve inflammation (not just anti-inflammatory)
  - Targeted metabololipidomics for assessing resolution-inflammation metabolomes
  - Framework for applying SPM biology to Long COVID
- **Conclusion**: SPMs are ready for human clinical trials in conditions with uncontrolled inflammation including Long COVID; stereochemistry determines bioactivity.
- **Limitations**: Review/framework paper; no clinical data; focuses on biochemistry rather than disease-specific mechanisms
- **Certainty**: 0.85 — Foundational review by the laboratory that discovered SPMs; Seminars in Immunology is authoritative; stereochemistry insights from 30+ years of research

### Gracia Aznar 2024 — SPM Supplementation in Post-COVID Syndrome

- **Full Citation**: Gracia Aznar A, Moreno Egea F, Gracia Banzo R, Gutierrez R, Rizo JM, Rodriguez-Ledo P. Pro-Resolving Inflammatory Effects of a Marine Oil Enriched in Specialized Pro-Resolving Mediators (SPMs) Supplement and Its Implication in Patients with Post-COVID Syndrome (PCS). _Biomedicines_. 2024;12(10):2221.
- **DOI**: 10.3390/biomedicines12102221
- **PMID**: 39457534
- **Key Findings**:
  - 12-week SPM-enriched marine oil supplementation in Post-COVID Syndrome patients
  - Evaluated eicosanoid and pro-resolutive parameters
  - Improvements in inflammatory resolution markers
  - Clinical symptom improvement
- **Conclusion**: SPM supplementation shows promise for restoring inflammation resolution in PCS.
- **Limitations**: Open-label (no placebo); moderate sample size; PCS-specific; supplement formulation varied
- **Certainty**: 0.50 — First clinical data on SPM supplementation in post-viral syndrome; open-label design limits causal inference

## Category 4: HMGB1 (Prototypical DAMP)

### Ibrahim 2026 — HMGB1-Mediated Neuroinflammation

- **Full Citation**: Ibrahim KN, Wasim R, Rahman E. HMGB1-mediated neuroinflammation: molecular mechanisms and emerging therapeutic approaches. _Inflammopharmacology_. 2026;34:3085–3108.
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s10787-026-02211-8
- **PMID**: 41964823
- **Key Findings**:
  - HMGB1 is a central inflammatory mediator linking cellular stress to sustained neuroinflammation
  - Nuclear HMGB1 translocates to cytoplasm and is released extracellularly as a DAMP
  - HMGB1 signals via TLR4, TLR9, and RAGE receptors
  - Redox state determines HMGB1 bioactivity: disulfide-HMGB1 (pro-inflammatory) vs fully reduced HMGB1 (chemotactic)
  - Therapeutic approaches: anti-HMGB1 antibodies, Box A antagonist, TLR4 inhibitors
- **Conclusion**: HMGB1 is a key therapeutic target for neuroinflammatory conditions.
- **Limitations**: Review; no ME/CFS-specific data; HMGB1 role in chronic (vs acute) inflammation less established
- **Certainty**: 0.80 — Comprehensive review of HMGB1 neuroinflammation; published in established journal; HMGB1 biology well-validated

### Chen 2026 — HMGB1-pCTS-L Axis in Inflammatory Diseases

- **Full Citation**: Chen W, Li J, Qiang X, Lou L, Zhu CS, Deng M. Pharmacological intervention of the HMGB1-pCTS-L axis to ameliorate inflammatory diseases. _Frontiers in Immunology_. 2026;17:1843251.
- **DOI**: 10.3389/fimmu.2026.1843251
- **PMID**: 42183262
- **Key Findings**:
  - HMGB1 forms complex with cathepsin L (pCTS-L) in chronic inflammation
  - HMGB1-pCTS-L axis amplifies inflammatory signaling beyond HMGB1 alone
  - Therapeutic targeting of this axis ameliorates disease in RA models
  - Relevance for chronic inflammatory diseases where HMGB1 drives pathology
- **Conclusion**: HMGB1-pCTS-L represents a new therapeutic target in chronic inflammatory diseases.
- **Limitations**: Review with preclinical focus; no ME/CFS data; axis primarily studied in RA and sepsis
- **Certainty**: 0.65 — Novel HMGB1 axis identified; well-characterized in RA models; translational potential for ME/CFS but speculative

## Category 5: Ferroptosis / Iron Dysregulation / Fenton Chemistry

### Mantle 2025 — ME/CFS, FM, GWI, Long COVID Overlap: Oxidative Stress and Ferroptosis

- **Full Citation**: Mantle D, Domingo JC, Golomb BA, Castro-Marrero J. Gulf War Illness, Fibromyalgia, Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome and Long COVID Overlap in Common Symptoms and Underlying Biological Mechanisms: Implications for Future Therapeutic Strategies. _International Journal of Molecular Sciences_. 2025;26(19):9044.
- **DOI**: 10.3390/ijms26199044
- **PMID**: 41009608
- **Key Findings**:
  - ME/CFS, FM, GWI, and Long COVID share overlapping biological mechanisms
  - Central role of mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress across all conditions
  - Ferroptosis (iron-dependent lipid peroxidation) implicated in pathology
  - Impaired energy metabolism and reduced ATP production common to all
  - Coenzyme Q10 deficiencies and altered redox status identified
  - Therapeutic implications: antioxidants, mitochondrial support, iron modulation
- **Conclusion**: These conditions form a group of "low-energy associated disorders" with shared pathophysiology centered on oxidative stress and ferroptosis.
- **Limitations**: Review; no primary data; overlaps are associative not causal; therapeutic claims not from controlled trials
- **Certainty**: 0.70 — Comprehensive review with strong authors (Castro-Marrero is leading ME/CFS researcher); synthetic framework valuable; but review-level evidence

### Additional note: Iron Dysregulation in Long COVID

The Nature Immunology 2024 paper (Hanson et al.) on delayed resolution of inflammation and hypoferremia in Long COVID was found via news search but could not be retrieved by PMID. Key finding: delayed resolution of inflammation-associated hypoferremia (not inflammation magnitude) predicts Long COVID. ME/CFS relevance: chronic iron dysregulation may drive Fenton chemistry → oxidative stress → fatigue.

## Category 6: P2X7 Receptor / ATP Signaling

### Li 2021 — P2RX7 Receptor Regulates Acute Sickness Response

- **Full Citation**: Li H, Cvejic E, Gu B, Vollmer-Conna U, Hickie I, Wakefield D, Davenport T, Wiley J, Lloyd AR. Regulation of the Acute Sickness Response by the P2RX7 Receptor. _Journal of Infectious Diseases_. 2021;224(5):874-884.
- **DOI**: 10.1093/infdis/jiab027
- **PMID**: 33471105
- **Key Findings**:
  - P2RX7 (P2X7) is a highly polymorphic ATP-gated cationic pore on immune cells and brain
  - P2RX7 regulates NLRP3 inflammasome activation
  - Genetic variation in P2RX7 associated with altered sickness response
  - Links peripheral inflammation to central sickness behavior (fatigue, mood disturbance)
  - Prospective infection cohort (DIOS study) with P2RX7 genotyping
- **Conclusion**: P2RX7 is a key regulator of the acute sickness response, mediating the transition from peripheral immune activation to central symptoms.
- **Limitations**: Acute infection model (not chronic); genetic association study; proxy for ME/CFS but not direct; moderate effect sizes
- **Certainty**: 0.70 — Well-designed prospective cohort (DIOS); published in J Infect Dis; genetic approach avoids confounding; but acute (not chronic) model

## Category 7: Complement System

### Polli 2019 — Complement and Elastase Activation in ME/CFS After Exercise

- **Full Citation**: Polli A, Van Oosterwijck J, Meeus M, Lambrecht L, Nijs J. Exercise-induced hyperalgesia, complement system and elastase activation in Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome — a secondary analysis of experimental comparative studies. _Scandinavian Journal of Pain_. 2019;19(4):693-704.
- **DOI**: 10.1515/sjpain-2019-0058
- **PMID**: 30325737
- **Key Findings**:
  - Complement system activation occurs in ME/CFS after exercise challenge
  - Elastase activation (neutrophil degranulation) accompanies complement activation
  - Complement activation correlates with exercise-induced hyperalgesia
  - Suggests exercise-induced inflammatory cascade involving complement and neutrophils
- **Conclusion**: Complement and neutrophil activation contribute to post-exertional symptom worsening in ME/CFS, linking immune activation to pain.
- **Limitations**: Secondary analysis; small sample; exercise challenge model may not reflect resting state; cross-sectional comparison
- **Certainty**: 0.60 — Direct ME/CFS data with experimental challenge; small sample; secondary analysis

### Note on Sorensen 2003 (existing bib)

The existing bibliography includes Sorensen et al. 2003 (JACI) showing C4a complement activation after exercise in CFS. Nunes 2024 (above) shows complement machinery downregulation at rest — together suggesting a dynamic complement dysregulation (low at rest, hyper-reactive to stress/exercise).

## Category 8: Neurogenic Inflammation (Substance P, CGRP, Mast Cells)

### Theoharides 2019 — Mast Cells, Neuroinflammation and Pain in Fibromyalgia

- **Full Citation**: Theoharides TC, Tsilioni I, Bawazeer M. Mast Cells, Neuroinflammation and Pain in Fibromyalgia Syndrome. _Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience_. 2019;13:353.
- **DOI**: 10.3389/fncel.2019.00353
- **PMID**: 31427928
- **Key Findings**:
  - Increased serum substance P (SP) and hemokinin-1 (HK-1) in FMS patients
  - SP activates mast cells through MrgprB2/MRGPRX2 (non-IgE pathway)
  - Mast cell-derived mediators (histamine, tryptase, cytokines) sustain neurogenic inflammation
  - Thalamic mast cell involvement in central sensitization
  - Stress triggers CRH-mediated mast cell activation
- **Conclusion**: Mast cell-neural interactions via substance P drive neuroinflammation in FM, suggesting similar mechanisms in ME/CFS.
- **Limitations**: FM (not ME/CFS); review with some primary data; mast cell role in CNS more established in animal models
- **Certainty**: 0.65 — Theoharides is leading authority on mast cell-neural interactions; FM shares substantial overlap with ME/CFS; some direct serum data

### Conti 2020 — Mast Cells in Fibromyalgia and IL-37

- **Full Citation**: Conti P, Gallenga CE, Caraffa A, Ronconi G, Kritas SK. Impact of mast cells in fibromyalgia and low-grade chronic inflammation: Can IL-37 play a role? _Dermatologic Therapy_. 2020;33(1):e13191.
- **DOI**: 10.1111/dth.13191
- **PMID**: 31837249
- **Key Findings**:
  - FM skin biopsies show increased mast cell numbers
  - Substance P (SP) from neurons activates mast cells
  - CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone) also activates skin mast cells
  - IL-37 emerges as natural suppressor of mast cell and inflammatory cytokine release
  - Potential therapeutic avenue: IL-37 to suppress neurogenic inflammation
- **Conclusion**: Mast cell activation via SP and CRH drives low-grade chronic inflammation in FM; IL-37 may be therapeutic.
- **Limitations**: FM (not ME/CFS); small sample; review with case studies; IL-37 mechanism preliminary
- **Certainty**: 0.55 — Complementary to Theoharides; adds IL-37 perspective; but even less direct ME/CFS relevance

## Category 9: TRPV1 / Neurogenic Inflammation

### Pintér 2023 — Triple Function of Capsaicin-Sensitive Sensory Neurons

- **Full Citation**: Pintér E, Helyes Z, Szőke É, Bölcskei K, Kecskés A, Pethő G. The triple function of the capsaicin-sensitive sensory neurons: In memoriam János Szolcsányi. _Temperature_. 2023;10(1):13-34.
- **DOI**: 10.1080/23328940.2022.2147388
- **PMID**: 38059854
- **Key Findings**:
  - TRPV1-expressing sensory neurons have three functions: sensory (pain), efferent (neurogenic inflammation), and homeostatic (thermoregulation, tissue repair)
  - Capsaicin-sensitive neurons release CGRP and substance P at peripheral terminals, causing vasodilation, plasma extravasation, and mast cell activation
  - Neurogenic inflammation contributes to pathophysiology of numerous chronic conditions
  - Local effector function of sensory nerves is frequently overlooked in clinical contexts
- **Conclusion**: Capsaicin-sensitive nerves coordinate inflammatory and homeostatic responses; their dysfunction may underlie chronic inflammatory conditions.
- **Limitations**: Review; no ME/CFS-specific data; comprehensive but narrative
- **Certainty**: 0.80 — Authoritative review from leading TRPV1/neurogenic inflammation researchers; strong mechanistic foundation; ME/CFS application is extrapolation

### Spekker 2022 — TRP Channels, CGRP, and Substance P in Migraine

- **Full Citation**: Spekker E, Körtési T, Vécsei L. TRP Channels: Recent Development in Translational Research and Potential Therapeutic Targets in Migraine. _International Journal of Molecular Sciences_. 2022;24(1):700.
- **DOI**: 10.3390/ijms24010700
- **PMID**: 36614146
- **Key Findings**:
  - TRP channel activation (TRPV1, TRPA1, TRPM8) triggers CGRP and substance P release

## Category 10: ER Stress / Unfolded Protein Response (UPR)

### Kawano 2023 — ER Proteostasis Regulators Control Sleep

- **Full Citation**: Kawano T, Kashiwagi M, Kanuka M, Chen CK, Yasugaki S, Hatori S, Miyazaki S, Tanaka K, Fujita H, Nakajima T, Yanagisawa M, Nakagawa Y, Hayashi Y. ER proteostasis regulators cell-non-autonomously control sleep. _Cell Reports_. 2023;42(3):112267.
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.celrep.2023.112267
- **PMID**: 36924492
- **Key Findings**:
  - Forward genetic screen in C. elegans identified ER-associated degradation (ERAD) components SEL-1, SEL-11 as sleep regulators
  - ER stress/UPR machinery in peripheral (non-neuronal) tissues signals to regulate sleep
  - ER proteostasis dysfunction → sleep disruption
  - First direct mechanistic link between ER stress and sleep regulation
- **Conclusion**: ER unfolded protein response in peripheral tissues generates systemic signals that regulate sleep — linking cellular stress to sleep behavior.
- **Limitations**: C. elegans model (invertebrate); no direct mammalian or human data; ERAD components are evolutionary conserved but sleep regulation may differ; no ME/CFS data
- **Certainty**: 0.55 — Elegant genetic dissection in model organism; Yanagisawa co-author (orexin discoverer); mechanism conserved but species gap; direct relevance to ME/CFS sleep disruption is speculative

### UPR in Immunity and Inflammation (general reference)

The UPR's three sensors (PERK, IRE1α, ATF6) all participate in upregulating inflammatory processes when chronically activated. IRE1α-XBP1 drives IL-6, TNF-α; PERK-eIF2α drives NF-κB; ATF6 modulates complement. Chronic UPR activation could link ER stress to persistent low-grade inflammation in ME/CFS. No ME/CFS-specific UPR literature found — this is a gap.

## Category 11: Eicosanoid Pathway

### Bao 2026 — Gut Microbiota, Lipid Mediators, and Chronic Inflammation

- **Full Citation**: Bao S, Yao C. Gut microbiota in chronic inflammation: the interplay with lipid mediators. _Gut Microbes_. 2026;18(1):2667605.
- **DOI**: 10.1080/19490976.2026.2667605
- **PMID**: 42087805
- **Key Findings**:
  - Gut microbiota composition influences production of lipid mediators (eicosanoids, SPMs)
  - Dysbiosis shifts lipid mediator profiles toward pro-inflammatory eicosanoids (PGE2, LTB4)

- **Limitations**: Review; no ME/CFS data; general framework paper
- **Certainty**: 0.70 — Well-referenced review in established journal; framework directly applicable to ME/CFS, where gut dysbiosis is documented

## Cross-Cutting Summary

### Key Convergence Points

These diverse inflammation sources converge on a limited set of final common pathways relevant to ME/CFS:

| Convergent Node | Mechanisms Feeding In |
|----------------|----------------------|
| **NLRP3 inflammasome** | P2X7 (ATP), S100A8/A9 (TLR4), HMGB1 (TLR4/RAGE), mtDNA (cGAS-STING → IFN), UPR (IRE1α), ROS/ferroptosis |
| **NF-κB** | TLR4 (HMGB1, S100A8/A9), TNFR, IL-1R, UPR (PERK/IRE1α), eicosanoids |
| **Type I interferon** | cGAS-STING (mtDNA), HMGB1 (TLR9), complement (C3a/C5a) |
| **Mitochondrial dysfunction** | NAD+ depletion → cGAS-STING; ferroptosis → lipid peroxidation; UPR → mitophagy; ROS from all |
| **Resolution failure** | SPM deficiency/receptor dysfunction; chronic DAMP/PAMP persistence; complement hyper-reactivity |

### Gaps Requiring Further Research

1. **cGAS-STING in ME/CFS directly**: No study has measured cGAS-STING activation in ME/CFS patients. Chelette 2025 (cancer fatigue model) is the closest proxy.
2. **SPM levels in ME/CFS**: No published study has measured resolvin/protectin/maresin levels in ME/CFS plasma or CSF.
3. **HMGB1 in ME/CFS**: No direct measurement of extracellular HMGB1 in ME/CFS cohorts.
4. **UPR activation in ME/CFS**: No study has assessed PERK/IRE1/ATF6 activation in ME/CFS tissues.
5. **Ferroptosis markers in ME/CFS**: Lipid peroxidation products (4-HNE, MDA) have been measured in some studies, but GPX4, ACSL4, or SLC7A11 have not been assessed.
