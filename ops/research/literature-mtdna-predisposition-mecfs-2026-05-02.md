# Literature Review: mtDNA Variants as Inherited Predisposition Threshold for ME/CFS
## with ADHD Comorbidity as Shared Mitochondrial Substrate Expression

**Date:** 2026-05-02
**Research stream:** `mitochondrial-predisposition`
**Status:** Papers added to bib and appendix-h; integration guide for chapter placement pending

---

## Topic Brief — Conceptual Origin

This topic arose from the following reasoning chain (to be reflected in Phase 3 brainstorm and Phase 2/4 integration):

### Q1: Do mitochondrial weaknesses come from the mother?
Yes — mtDNA is maternally inherited almost exclusively (sperm mitochondria degraded post-fertilization via autophagy). All children of an affected mother inherit her mtDNA. Heteroplasmy (mixed normal/mutant mtDNA ratios) can cause severity variation between siblings. **Important caveat for this document:** mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS appears acquired (driven by chronic inflammation, oxidative stress, viral-triggered epigenetic changes), not germline mtDNA mutation causing ME/CFS directly. Inherited mtDNA variants may lower resilience, not cause disease.

### Q2: ADHD as a brain-energy deficit — could this come from mitochondria?
The connection is real and researched. ADHD cognitive symptoms (poor sustained attention, low motivation, initiation difficulty) correlate with dopamine/norepinephrine dysregulation in prefrontal cortex. PFC is metabolically expensive; dopamine signalling is tightly coupled to mitochondrial function. Evidence: reduced cerebral glucose metabolism in PFC (imaging), increased mtDNA copy number (×1.3 vs controls), haplogroup-specific risk/protection (K/U protective), reduced Complex V activity and respiratory capacity (cybrid models), NDUFAF2/UCP2 gene variants. **Causal direction unresolved:** does mitochondrial dysfunction cause ADHD, or does dysregulated dopamine signalling secondarily stress mitochondria?

### Q3: Could mitochondrial predisposition make ME/CFS easier to acquire?
The core hypothesis: inherited mtDNA variants reduce mitochondrial reserve capacity below the threshold a major stressor (viral infection, oxidative stress, immune activation) would otherwise not breach. This fits the ME/CFS two-hit/threshold model:
- Hit 1: pre-existing vulnerability (mitochondrial, immune, autonomic)
- Hit 2: trigger (EBV, SARS-CoV-2, trauma, surgery)

**Why mitochondrial predisposition is mechanistically plausible:**
- Explains why only a fraction of people exposed to the same trigger develop ME/CFS
- Explains maternal inheritance clustering sometimes observed in ME/CFS families (anecdotal)
- Connects to observed energy metabolism failure without requiring mtDNA to be primary cause
- ADHD comorbidity explained as shared substrate: low mitochondrial reserve → different phenotype depending on which system is most stressed (dopaminergic PFC in ADHD; post-exertional metabolic collapse in ME/CFS)

**What is missing:**
- No large mtDNA sequencing study testing onset-predisposition in ME/CFS (existing studies are null for onset, positive only for symptom severity)
- Heteroplasmy measurement in ME/CFS not systematically done
- Maternal vs paternal inheritance clustering in ME/CFS families not formally assessed
- No study testing mtDNA haplogroup × ADHD comorbidity × ME/CFS in single cohort

### Integration guidance from conceptual origin
- The **onset-predisposition claim must be flagged as speculation** with null evidence cited
- The **symptom-severity modulation claim** (haplogroup U/J/H effects) is better supported (~0.45)
- The **ADHD–ME/CFS shared mitochondrial substrate** is the most novel and least-studied angle
- The **maternal inheritance framing** is the most intuitive patient-facing explanation — but must be distinguished from established mitochondrial disease inheritance
- The **two-hit threshold model** should be the narrative frame; mitochondrial predisposition as one possible Hit 1

## Papers Found and Assessed

### 1. Billing-Ross et al. 2016 — mtDNA Variants Correlate with ME/CFS Symptoms
**Citation key:** `BillingRoss2016mtDNA`
**Journal:** Journal of Translational Medicine 14:19
**DOI:** 10.1186/s12967-016-0771-6 | PMID: 26791940
**Certainty:** 0.55

**Study:** Complete mitochondrial genome sequencing; 193 ME/CFS cases vs 196 controls (predominantly European descent).

**Key findings:**
- NULL for susceptibility: no association between mtDNA variants and ME/CFS onset
- Haplogroups J, U, H and 8 SNPs significantly associated with symptom severity and type
  - Haplogroup J: protective against joint pain metrics
  - Haplogroup U: less severe bloating
  - Haplogroup H: increased "feeling dead after exercise"
- Heteroplasmy levels low and comparable between groups

**Implications for predisposition model:** mtDNA does not predispose to ME/CFS onset but modulates symptom expression. The susceptibility threshold model must distinguish between onset-predisposition and symptom-severity modulation.

**Quality:** Peer-reviewed, adequate n, rigorous NGS methodology; symptom associations unreplicated.

---

### 2. Venter et al. 2019 — MtDNA Population Variation in ME/CFS: Two Cohorts
**Citation key:** `Venter2019mtDNA`
**Journal:** Scientific Reports 9:2914
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41598-019-39060-1 | PMID: 30814539
**Certainty:** 0.52

**Study:** Whole mitochondrial genome sequencing; UK cohort (118 patients, 64 controls) + South Africa cohort (143 patients, 98 controls).

**Key findings:**
- No clinically proven pathogenic mtDNA mutations in either cohort
- ME/CFS patients had significantly FEWER mildly deleterious variants than controls
  - UK: 55% patients vs 27% controls lacked such variants (p=0.0008)
  - SA: 56% patients vs 41% controls (p=0.03)
- Pattern replicated independently across two geographically distinct cohorts

**Implications:** The "damaged mtDNA = predisposition" model is NOT supported. Instead, an anomalous variant distribution (fewer deleterious variants) raises unexplained questions. This may reflect: (a) different selection pressures in ME/CFS populations, (b) threshold effects where even low-level mitochondrial stress accumulates disproportionately, or (c) nuclear-mitochondrial interaction dynamics.

**Quality:** Cross-cohort replication is a meaningful strength; interpretive uncertainty is high.

### 3. Dibble, McGrath, Ponting 2020 — Genetic Risk Factors of ME/CFS: Critical Review
**Citation key:** `Dibble2020genetics` (already in bib; appendix-h entry added)
**Journal:** Human Molecular Genetics 29(R1):R117–R124
**DOI:** 10.1093/hmg/ddaa169 | PMID: 32744306
**Certainty:** 0.62

**Key findings:**
- SNP-based heritability h² = 0.08 (UK Biobank; low confidence); one study claimed higher
- Clinically proven mtDNA variants do not commonly explain ME/CFS
- Corroborates Venter 2019: patients more likely to carry mtDNA lacking mildly deleterious variants
- Most candidate gene associations unreplicated; GWAS urgently needed

**Implications:** Establishes the evidential ceiling: as of 2020, no specific genetic (nuclear or mitochondrial) locus reliably predicts ME/CFS onset. Mitochondrial predisposition cannot be dismissed but lacks direct mechanistic evidence.

---

### 4. Albright et al. 2011 — Heritable Predisposition to CFS
**Citation key:** `Albright2011heritability`
**Journal:** BMC Neurology 11:62
**DOI:** 10.1186/1471-2377-11-62 | PMID: 21619629
**Certainty:** 0.62

**Study:** Utah Population Database (up to 15 generations), 811 CFS cases, ICD-9 ascertainment.

**Key findings:**
- First-degree relatives: RR = 2.70 (p = 0.001)
- Second-degree: RR = 2.34 (p = 0.008); third-degree: RR = 1.93 (p = 0.009)
- Excess relatedness persisted excluding close relatives — argues against shared-environment explanation
- Does NOT distinguish maternal vs paternal inheritance

**Implications for mitochondrial predisposition:** Establishes the heritable component requiring explanation. The maternal vs paternal breakdown — which would be diagnostic for mitochondrial inheritance — was not reported. This is a key gap in the literature.

### 5. Naviaux 2014 — Metabolic Features of the Cell Danger Response
**Citation key:** `Naviaux2014cdr` (already in bib + appendix-h)
**Journal:** Mitochondrion 16:7–17
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.mito.2013.08.006 | PMID: 23981537
**Certainty:** 0.50 (theoretical framework; well-cited)

**Relevant to predisposition question:**
- CDR proposed as the evolutionarily conserved metabolic response protecting cells from harm
- Post-threat recovery involves mitochondrial content changes that increase functional reserve capacity — implying pre-existing reserve capacity differences could affect CDR recovery
- Chronic diseases (including ME/CFS) proposed as persistent CDR states
- Does NOT explicitly propose mtDNA predisposition; however, reserve capacity as a pre-existing variable is implicit in the framework

**Assessment:** The CDR framework does not directly address inherited mtDNA predisposition. It describes a state-transition mechanism. Pre-existing mitochondrial reserve (which could be influenced by mtDNA haplogroup or copy number) would affect CDR recovery threshold, but this is an inference not stated by Naviaux.

### 6. Naviaux et al. 2016 — Metabolic Features of CFS (PNAS)
**Citation key:** `Naviaux2016metabolomics` (already in bib + appendix-h)
**Journal:** PNAS 113(37):E5472–E5480
**DOI:** 10.1073/pnas.1607571113 | PMID: 27573827
**Certainty:** 0.65

**Relevant to predisposition question:**
- ME/CFS identified as a hypometabolic dauer-like state
- 20 metabolic pathways affected; 80% of diagnostic metabolites decreased
- Mitochondrial metabolism explicitly one of the 20 pathways
- Does NOT propose mtDNA predisposition; characterises the disease state, not its genetic origins

### 7. Chang et al. 2020 — MtDNA Haplogroups and ADHD Risk (European Americans)
**Citation key:** `Chang2020haploADHD`
**Journal:** Translational Psychiatry 10:370
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41398-020-01064-1 | PMID: 33139694
**Certainty:** 0.65

**Study:** 2,076 ADHD cases vs 5,078 controls across three European cohorts; meta-analysis of haplogroup associations.

**Key findings:**
- Haplogroups K and U: protective against ADHD
- Haplogroup HHV*: increased ADHD risk
- Haplogroup U also seen in Billing-Ross 2016 as ME/CFS symptom modifier

**Cross-disease overlap:** Haplogroup U is protective in ADHD AND symptom-modifying in ME/CFS. This is the strongest single data point for a shared mitochondrial background between the two conditions.

### 8. Giannoulis et al. 2024 — Systematic Review of MtDNA Variation in ADHD
**Citation key:** `Giannoulis2024sysrevmtADHD`
**Journal:** European Child & Adolescent Psychiatry 33(6):1675–1685
**DOI:** 10.1007/s00787-022-02030-6 | PMID: 35796884
**Certainty:** 0.58

**Study:** Systematic review; 507 screened, 10 primary studies included.

**Key findings:**
- Increased mtDNA copy number, SNP associations, haplogroup effects documented across ADHD populations
- Both mtDNA and nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes implicated
- Field underdeveloped; methodology heterogeneous

**Assessment:** Establishes the evidential base for mitochondrial genetic variation in ADHD — a necessary (though not sufficient) foundation for the shared-substrate hypothesis.

---

### 9. Verma et al. 2016 — ADHD Suffers from Mitochondrial Dysfunction (Cybrid Study)
**Citation key:** `Verma2016ADHDcybrid`
**Journal:** BBA Clinical 6:153–158
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.bbacli.2016.10.003 | PMID: 27896136
**Certainty:** 0.30

**Study:** Cybrid cell lines from ADHD patient platelets; n=3 ADHD, 4 controls (ages 5–13).

**Key findings:**
- Lower cellular and mitochondrial respiration in ADHD cybrids
- Reduced ATPase6/8 transcripts; decreased Complex V activity
- Loss of mitochondrial membrane potential; elevated oxidative stress
- Defects transferable via patient mitochondria (not just nuclear environment)

**Important caveat:** n=3. The cybrid model is mechanistically informative but the sample size makes this low certainty. The finding that mitochondrial dysfunction is transferable via patient mitochondria is the conceptually significant result.

### 10. Öğütlü et al. 2022 — Mitochondrial Dysfunction in ADHD (Narrative Review)
**Citation key:** `Ogutlu2022ADHDmito`
**Journal:** Eurasian Journal of Medicine 54(Suppl 1):S187–S195
**DOI:** 10.5152/eurasianjmed.2022.22187 | PMID: 36655466
**Certainty:** 0.45

**Key synthesis points:**
- mtDNA copy number ~1.3x higher in ADHD vs controls (multiple studies)
- Haplogroup B4 increases ADHD risk; K and U reduce risk in European populations
- 10398 A/G polymorphism associated with ADHD in Korean cohort
- Mitochondrial apoptotic signalling: second strongest mechanism in ADHD (GWAS)
- Notes parallel with ME/CFS: reduced spare respiratory capacity, elevated oxidative stress

**Note on haplogroup overlap:** Haplogroup U protective in ADHD (Chang 2020) and symptom-modifying in ME/CFS (Billing-Ross 2016) — the review draws attention to this cross-disease overlap.

### 11. Almutairi et al. 2024 — Mitochondrial Dysfunction and Mitophagy in ADHD
**Citation key:** `Almutairi2024mitoadhd` (already in bib + appendix-h)
**Journal:** Saudi Pharmaceutical Journal 32(12):102212
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jsps.2024.102212 | PMC: 11664080

**Key findings (relevant to predisposition):**

- Mitochondrial organization gene variants reduce respiratory capacity

## Overall Synthesis

### What the Evidence Supports

**1. Heritable predisposition to ME/CFS exists (Albright 2011, Dibble 2020)**
RR ~2.7 for first-degree relatives in the best-powered study. The molecular substrate is unknown. Heritability could be nuclear, mitochondrial, or epigenetic.

**2. Inherited mtDNA variants do NOT predispose to ME/CFS onset (Billing-Ross 2016, Venter 2019, Dibble 2020)**
This is the consistent null finding across three independent studies. No clinically pathogenic mtDNA mutations explain ME/CFS.

**3. MtDNA haplogroups modulate ME/CFS symptom severity — not onset (Billing-Ross 2016)**
Haplogroups J, U, H associate with symptom clusters. This is a symptom-modifier effect, not a predisposition effect.

**4. An anomalous mtDNA variant distribution exists in ME/CFS (Venter 2019)**
Patients carry FEWER mildly deleterious variants. Mechanism unknown. This finding runs counter to simple "accumulated mitochondrial damage" models.

**5. MtDNA haplogroups affect ADHD risk in large samples (Chang 2020, Giannoulis 2024)**
Haplogroups K and U protective; HHV* increases risk. Evidenced in n>7,000 with three-cohort replication.

**6. ADHD shows mitochondrial bioenergetic impairment in experimental models (Verma 2016, Almutairi 2024, Öğütlü 2022)**
Complex V activity reduced, oxidative stress elevated, mitochondrial membrane potential impaired in ADHD cell models. Evidence base is preclinical with small n.

**7. Haplogroup U is a shared modifier across ADHD and ME/CFS**
This single overlap point (Chang 2020 × Billing-Ross 2016) is the strongest empirical anchor for a shared mitochondrial background. It is hypothesis-generating, not proof.

**8. Cell Danger Response framework (Naviaux 2014, 2016) does not explicitly propose mtDNA predisposition**
The CDR describes how mitochondria coordinate the response to threat. Pre-existing mitochondrial reserve capacity is implicitly relevant to CDR recovery threshold, but Naviaux does not claim mtDNA variants are predisposing.

### What the Evidence Contradicts

- A simple "damaged mtDNA → ME/CFS" model is contradicted by Billing-Ross 2016 and Venter 2019
- Classic mitochondrial disease mutations as ME/CFS cause: ruled out by multiple sequencing studies
- The claim that ME/CFS is "a mitochondrial disease" in the traditional genetic sense is not supported

### Evidence Gaps (Critical)

1. **Maternal vs paternal inheritance breakdown** not reported in Albright 2011 or other family studies. This is the single most important missing data point for the mitochondrial predisposition hypothesis.
2. **No study has directly tested whether ME/CFS patients have reduced mitochondrial reserve capacity at baseline** (before onset), which would be the mechanistic prediction of the threshold model.
3. **No study has tested mtDNA haplogroup × ADHD comorbidity × ME/CFS in a single cohort.**
4. The "fewer mildly deleterious variants in ME/CFS patients" finding (Venter 2019) has not been mechanistically explained.

### Certainty Assessment for the Composite Hypothesis

| Sub-claim | Evidence | Certainty |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| ME/CFS has a heritable component | Albright 2011, Dibble 2020 | 0.62 |
| Inherited mtDNA predisposes to ME/CFS onset | Billing-Ross 2016, Venter 2019 | 0.10 (null) |
| MtDNA haplogroups modulate ME/CFS symptoms | Billing-Ross 2016 | 0.45 |
| ADHD has mitochondrial genetic vulnerability | Chang 2020, Giannoulis 2024 | 0.58 |
| ADHD and ME/CFS share mitochondrial genetic substrate | Haplogroup U overlap only | 0.20 |
| CDR predisposition via mtDNA | Naviaux 2014 (implicit only) | 0.15 |
| Maternal inheritance clustering in ME/CFS | No direct evidence found | 0.10 |

### Framing Recommendation for Integration

The mtDNA predisposition hypothesis should be framed as a **speculation** (certainty < 0.45) in the document, not a hypothesis. The strongest supportable claim is:

> MtDNA haplogroups modulate symptom severity in ME/CFS without conferring susceptibility to onset. The same haplogroup (U) is protective against ADHD, raising the possibility of a shared mitochondrial energy background. Whether reduced mitochondrial reserve capacity — potentially shaped by haplogroup — constitutes a two-hit predisposition threshold for ME/CFS remains untested. Maternal inheritance clustering data needed to evaluate the mitochondrial inheritance pathway.
