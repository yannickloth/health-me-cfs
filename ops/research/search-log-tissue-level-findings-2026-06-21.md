## Per-Domain Results

### 1. Gut sealing (larazotide / AT-1001)

| PubMed query | Results | Notes |
|--------------|---------|-------|
| `larazotide OR AT-1001 AND (leaky gut) AND (COVID/ME/CFS)` | 0 | No matches — larazotide never studied in COVID or ME/CFS |
| `larazotide AND (tight junction OR intestinal permeability)` | 50 | Mostly celiac disease (Phase 2/3), pancreatitis, MIS-C |
| `larazotide adverse effects OR safety OR negative OR null` | 22 | Safety established in celiac trials; no serious AEs in Phase 3 |

**Key papers:**
- **Yonker et al. 2022** — "Zonulin Antagonist, Larazotide (AT1001), As an Adjuvant Treatment for Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome in Children: A Case Series" — *Crit Care Explorations*. PMID: 35211683. **N=3**. Larazotide used in MIS-C based on hypothesis of zonulin-mediated gut barrier breach of SARS-CoV-2. Clinical improvement post-larazotide. **Low certainty (n=3, case series).** No larazotide studies in ME/CFS found anywhere.
- **Veres-Székely et al. 2023** — "Zonulin as a Potential Therapeutic Target in Microbiota-Gut-Brain Axis Disorders" — *IJMS*. PMID: 37108711. Review. Zonulin as therapeutic target; mentions larazotide. Relevant to chronic fatigue context theoretically but no direct data.

**Evidence gap:** No larazotide trials in ME/CFS or LC. The MIS-C application (Yonker 2022) is the closest bridge. Zonulin hypothesis remains untested in post-infectious fatigue.

### 2. Gut healing (maraviroc + pravastatin/atorvastatin)

| Query | Results | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| `maraviroc pravastatin long COVID` | 4 | Patterson 2023 case series; Treadwell 2025 scoping review |
| `CCR5 antagonist AND (chronic fatigue OR ME/CFS)` | 1 | 33681680 |
| `statins AND long COVID AND cardiovascular` | 39 | Broader statin literature |

**Key papers:**
- **Patterson et al. 2023** — "Maraviroc and pravastatin as a therapeutic option to treat long COVID/PASC" — *Front Med*. PMID: 36844201. **ALREADY IN BIB** (long-covid.bib). N=~100 case series. CCR5 antagonist + statin targeting monocyte/macrophage persistence. **Medium certainty** (case series, no control, corrigendum on COI).
- **Treadwell et al. 2025** — "Treatments for Long COVID autonomic dysfunction: a scoping review" — *Clin Auton Res*. PMID: 39658729. Mentions maraviroc among treatments. No new data.
- **Risner et al. 2022** — Maraviroc inhibits SARS-CoV-2 multiplication in vitro (bioRxiv). Preclinical only.

**Evidence gap:** Maraviroc+pravastatin limited to uncontrolled case series. No RCTs. Replication needed.

### 3. Lymph node B-cell dysfunction (Locci)

| Query | Results | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| `lymph node germinal center SARS-CoV-2 long COVID` | 138 | Rich literature on GC responses to vaccination |
| `"Michela Locci" AND lymph node AND COVID` | 5 | Locci is co-author on key GC/FNA papers |
| `germinal center dysfunction AND chronic fatigue` | 3 | None directly in ME/CFS |

**Key papers:**
- **Lederer et al. 2022** — "Germinal center responses to SARS-CoV-2 mRNA vaccines in healthy and immunocompromised individuals" — *Cell*. PMID: 35202565. **N=54** LN FNA biopsies. GC responses impaired in immunocompromised. Locci is co-author. Establishes LN FNA method for GC analysis in longitudinal, which could be applied to post-infectious syndromes.
- **Bettini et al. 2022** (STAR Protocols) — Detailed protocol for LN FNA + spectral flow. Locci is senior/last author. PMID: 36595931.
- **Alameh et al. 2021** — *Immunity*. LN GC responses to LNP-mRNA vaccines. PMID: 34852217.

**Evidence gap:** No direct studies of lymph node GC function in ME/CFS or LC patients. All studies focus on vaccine response in healthy or immunocompromised, not post-infectious fatigue. Major gap for translation.

### 4. Arterial plaque inflammation (Giannerelli)

| Query | Results | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| `arterial plaque SARS-CoV-2 macrophage clearance` | 457 | Large literature |
| `atherosclerosis long COVID` | 16 focused on LC | Eberhardt 2023 key finding |
| `coronavirus plaque gene expression dysregulation` | 5 | Limited |
| `cardiovascular inflammation ME/CFS` | 69 (mostly nonspecific) | |

**Key papers:**
- **Eberhardt et al. 2023** — "SARS-CoV-2 infection triggers pro-atherogenic inflammatory responses in human coronary vessels" — *Nature Cardiovascular Research*. PMID: 38076343. DOI: 10.1038/s44161-023-00336-5. **Direct tissue examination:** human coronary specimens from COVID-19 patients (autopsy/surgery). SARS-CoV-2 Spike protein detected in coronary plaque macrophages. Pro-inflammatory gene activation in coronary arteries. **Medium-High certainty.** Existence proof that SARS-CoV-2 directly affects arterial plaque.
- **Mátyás et al. 2023** — "Elevated FAI Index of Pericoronary Inflammation on Coronary CT Identifies Increased Risk of Coronary Plaque Vulnerability after COVID-19 Infection" — *IJMS*. PMID: 37108558. N=128. Imaging study showing increased pericoronary inflammation post-COVID.
- **Blagova et al. 2022** — "Chronic biopsy proven post-COVID myoendocarditis with SARS-Cov-2 persistence and high level of antiheart antibodies" — *Clin Cardiol*. PMID: 35855554. Endomyocardial biopsy-proven inflammation + viral persistence. **Direct tissue examination.** N=14. Virus detected in myocardium months post-infection. **Medium certainty** (small n, single center).
- **Zanini et al. 2024** — "Vascular Long COVID: A New Vessel Disease?" — *Angiology*. PMID: 36652923. Review.

**Evidence gap:** Eberhardt is the strongest paper (ex vivo human tissue). No studies link arterial plaque inflammation to ME/CFS specifically. The pro-atherogenic shift following COVID is established for cardiovascular outcomes but unexplored for fatigue/PEM mechanisms.

### 5. Retinal neuroinflammation (Miller)

| Query | Results | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| `retina neuroinflammation long COVID amyloid` | 14 | Miller 2025 most relevant |
| `"Sean Miller" Yale retina COVID` | 1 | Miller SJ 2025 |
| `retinal biomarker neurodegenerative chronic fatigue` | 1 (Schlick) | |

**Key papers:**
- **Miller et al. 2025** — "SARS-CoV-2 induces Alzheimer's disease-related amyloid-β pathology in ex vivo human retinal explants and retinal organoids" — *Science Advances*. PMID: 40614201. PMC12227045. DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.ads5006. **Direct tissue examination:** Human retinal explants + iPSC-derived retinal organoids infected with SARS-CoV-2 produce Aβ pathology. Retinal amyloid as a potential biomarker. **Medium-High certainty** (ex vivo human tissue, well-regarded journal).
- **Schlick et al. 2022** — "Post-COVID-19 Syndrome: Retinal Microcirculation as a Potential Marker for Chronic Fatigue" — *IJMS*. PMID: 36430175. N=40. Retinal microcirculation (OCT-A) correlated with chronic fatigue severity in PCS. **Direct retinal imaging.**
- **Cañadas et al. 2023** — "Neuroinflammatory Findings of Corneal Confocal Microscopy in Long COVID-19 Patients, 2 Years after Acute SARS-CoV-2 Infection" — *Diagnostics*. PMID: 37892009. N=50. Corneal small nerve fiber loss and immune cell infiltration in LC. **Direct tissue-level imaging of nerve inflammation.**

**Evidence gap:** Retinal Aβ as biomarker for brain amyloid in LC is promising but not yet validated in ME/CFS. Corneal confocal microscopy shows neuroinflammation but not linked to fatigue severity.

### 7. Brainstem / vagus nerve (VanElzakker)

| Query | Results | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| `brainstem inflammation PET-MRI long COVID` | 1 (Visser 2026) | |
| `"VanElzakker" brainstem COVID` | 4 | VanElzakker 2024 key paper |
| `vagus nerve hypothesis ME/CFS` | 18 | Rich theoretical literature |
| `TSPO PET brainstem chronic fatigue` | 1 (VanElzakker 2019 review) | |

**Key papers:**
- **VanElzakker et al. 2024** — "Neuroinflammation in post-acute sequelae of COVID-19 (PASC) as assessed by [11C]PBR28 PET" — *Brain, Behavior, and Immunity*. PMID: 38642615. **Direct neuroimaging of neuroinflammation in LC.** TSPO PET in PASC patients. Brainstem and other regions show increased TSPO signal. This is the PUBLISHED version (original bioRxiv 2023, PMID 37905031). **Medium certainty** (N=~20, first study of its kind in PASC).
- **Visser et al. 2026** — "Decreased functional connectivity in post-COVID syndrome patients with high neuroinflammatory activity" — *NeuroImage*. PMID: 42034234. N=~50. Links TSPO PET neuroinflammation to resting-state fMRI connectivity. Adds mechanistic depth to neuroinflammation findings.
- **Proal & VanElzakker 2021** — "Long COVID or Post-acute Sequelae of COVID-19 (PASC): An Overview of Biological Factors" — *Front Microbiol*. PMID: 34248921. Overview paper.

**Evidence gap:** VanElzakker 2024 is the most directly relevant paper in the entire literature search — it uses PET-MRI to demonstrate neuroinflammation in PASC, validating the direct tissue examination thesis. However, no ME/CFS-specific brainstem PET study has been published (the PolyBio project is ongoing, only registered as VanElzakker2024NeuroinflammationProject in bib).

## Evidence Gaps Identified

1. **Larazotide in ME/CFS or LC:** Zero studies. Only bridge is Yonker 2022 in pediatric MIS-C (n=3).
2. **Lymph node GC in post-infectious fatigue:** Zero studies. LN FNA method (Locci/Lederer) validated in vaccine response but never applied to ME/CFS or LC.
3. **Murakami CCI/CSF COVID papers:** Not found in PubMed. CCI-COVID link entirely unexplored in published literature.
4. **Retinal Aβ in ME/CFS:** Miller 2025 in retinal explants/organoids suggests mechanism but no patient studies measuring retinal amyloid in ME/CFS or LC.
5. **Brainstem PET in ME/CFS:** VanElzakker 2024 in PASC is the closest — no pure ME/CFS brainstem PET published.
6. **Arterial plaque inflammation in ME/CFS:** Eberhardt 2023 shows SARS-CoV-2 effects on coronary plaque; no studies link this to PEM or fatigue.
7. **Microclot clinical trials:** Saleem 2026 reviews the field but no interventional trials linking microclot reduction to symptom improvement.

**Synthesis:** The direct tissue examination thesis is well-supported within each individual domain (retina, coronary, LN, brainstem) but the *integrative claim* — that examining the tissue directly reveals pathophysiology invisible in blood — is validated only by the collective weight of disparate studies, each of which is small. No single study spans multiple tissue compartments in the same patients. This is the key gap the "Into the Tissues We Go" symposium aims to address.
