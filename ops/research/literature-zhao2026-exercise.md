# Literature Summary: Zhao 2026 Exercise Meta-Analysis — Critical Support Papers

**Purpose:** Support integration of Zhao et al. 2026 into ch24-controversies.typ and ch17-lifestyle-interventions.typ.
**Stance:** Paper documents subjective-only improvements (consistent with blinding bias) used to endorse exercise in a heterogeneous, non-PEM population.

---

## Paper 1: Zhao et al. 2026 (INDEX PAPER)

**Full Citation:** Zhao L, Gou B, Zhang M. The efficacy of exercise in patients with myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Journal of Psychosomatic Research*. 2026. doi:10.1016/j.jpsychores.2026.001613

**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jpsychores.2026.001613
**PROSPERO:** CRD42024618649
**ScienceDirect:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0022399926001613

**Study Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis; 17 RCTs; n=1,944; databases: MEDLINE, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane, CINAHL Ultimate, SPORTDiscus; RoB 2.0 tool applied.

**Geographic distribution:** 5 Asia, 9 Europe, 2 Oceania, 1 North America.

**Diagnostic criteria used:** Oxford (Sharpe 1991), Fukuda (1994), Reeves (2005) — none requiring PEM as a mandatory criterion.

**Interventions mixed:** aerobic exercise, yoga, qigong, tai chi — all pooled.

**Key Findings:**
- Exercise improves fatigue (SMD=0.85), sleep, QoL, depression, anxiety (all subjective)
- No improvement in cardiopulmonary function or objective functional capacity
- Authors acknowledge "prevalent high risk of bias across included studies"
- Authors dismiss bias: "methodological constraints represent inherent challenges of the field rather than fatal flaws"
- Authors propose "physiological ceiling" to explain subjective-only improvements

**Critical analysis points:**
1. Subjective-only improvements + RoB 2.0 high risk = precisely the BRANDO/Hróbjartsson prediction
2. Oxford/Fukuda without mandatory PEM: sample is heterogeneous, likely contains non-ME/CFS patients
3. Pooling aerobic exercise with yoga/qigong/tai chi conflates mechanistically different interventions
4. "Physiological ceiling" is a post-hoc explanation; simpler explanation is reporting bias
5. Journal of Psychosomatic Research: peer-reviewed but historically sympathetic to psychosomatic framing of ME/CFS

**Quality:** Medium (peer-reviewed, large n=1944, but acknowledged high RoB, problematic inclusion criteria)
**Certainty for claims:** 0.35 — High risk of bias throughout; subjective-only improvements in non-PEM population

## Paper 2: Hróbjartsson et al. 2014

**Full Citation:** Hróbjartsson A, Emanuelsson F, Thomsen ASS, Hilden J, Brorson S. Bias due to lack of patient blinding in clinical trials. A systematic review of trials randomizing patients to blind and nonblind sub-studies. *International Journal of Epidemiology*. 2014;43(4):1272–83.

**DOI:** 10.1093/ije/dyu115
**PMID:** 24881045
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24881045/

**Study Design:** Systematic review of 12 trials (n=3,869) using a unique internal design: each trial randomized patients to both blinded and unblinded sub-groups simultaneously, allowing direct within-trial comparison of blinding effects.

**Key Findings:**
- Unblinded patients exaggerated effect size by **mean SMD 0.56** (95% CI −0.71 to −0.41) for patient-reported outcomes
- Acupuncture trials: larger bias (−0.63); non-acupuncture trials: minimal bias (−0.17)
- Observer-reported outcomes: **no comparable exaggeration** — bias operates through patient perception
- Lack of blinding also increased control-group attrition (ratio 1.79) and co-interventions (ratio 1.55)

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- Zhao's fatigue SMD=0.85 is within the range that blinding bias alone (0.56) could explain
- All Zhao outcomes are patient-reported — the exact domain where Hróbjartsson found maximum bias
- Zhao's finding of NO objective improvement is the predicted signature of blinding bias per this paper

**Quality:** High — internal design eliminates confounders; gold-standard methodology for blinding bias quantification.
**Certainty:** 0.85
**Journal impact:** International Journal of Epidemiology (high impact)

**BibTeX key:** `Hrobjartsson2014blinding`

## Paper 3: Savović et al. 2012

**Full Citation:** Savović J, Jones HE, Altman DG, Harris RJ, Jüni P, Pildal J, Als-Nielsen B, Balk EM, Gluud C, Gluud LL, Ioannidis JPA, Schulz KF, Beynon R, Welton NJ, Wood L, Moher D, Deeks JJ, Sterne JAC. Influence of reported study design characteristics on intervention effect estimates from randomized, controlled trials. *Annals of Internal Medicine*. 2012;157(6):429–38.

**DOI:** 10.7326/0003-4819-157-6-201209180-00537
**PMID:** 22945832
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22945832/

**Study Design:** Meta-epidemiological analysis; 1,973 RCTs within 234 meta-analyses; combined analysis of 7 meta-epidemiological datasets (the BRANDO collaboration).

**Key Findings:**
- Trials lacking double-blinding: **average 13% exaggeration** of intervention effects (ROR 0.87)
- Effect was **driven entirely by trials with subjective outcomes** — "little evidence of bias in trials with objective and mortality outcomes"
- Subjective-outcome trials without blinding: ROR ~0.77 (23% exaggeration estimate)
- Provides the meta-epidemiological foundation for predicting that unblinded subjective-outcome trials overestimate effects

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- This is the primary BRANDO project paper cited in the mecfsscience.org blinding article
- Directly applicable: Zhao's trials are unblinded + all primary outcomes are subjective
- The 13% average exaggeration figure is a *floor* — higher for behavioral interventions with non-blind patient report
- "Clinical and policy decisions should not be based on trials in which blinding is not feasible and outcome measures are subjectively assessed" (BRANDO conclusion)

**Quality:** High — large multi-dataset meta-epidemiological synthesis; Annals of Internal Medicine.
**Certainty:** 0.85
**BibTeX key:** `Savovic2012blinding`

## Paper 4: Pitre et al. 2023

**Full Citation:** Pitre T, Kirsh S, Jassal T, Anderson M, Padoan A, Xiang A, Mah J, Zeraatkar D. The impact of blinding on trial results: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Cochrane Evidence Synthesis and Methods*. 2023;1(4):e12015.

**DOI:** 10.1002/cesm.12015
**PMID:** 40475370
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40475370/

**Study Design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis; searched MEDLINE, EMBASE, Cochrane, JBI, Web of Science; compared blinded vs. unblinded trials.

**Key Findings:**
- For continuous outcomes: unblinded patient/provider trials — low certainty evidence of overestimation
- For continuous outcomes: unblinded outcome assessors — moderate certainty evidence of overestimation
- Findings consistent with Savović 2012 and Hróbjartsson 2014 — blinding matters, especially for non-objective outcomes
- Authors note modest average effect magnitude with low certainty

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- Most recent Cochrane-affiliated update on blinding effects; reinforces the two earlier landmark papers
- Appropriate to cite as confirming evidence that the blinding bias concern is current and active in the literature

**Quality:** High — Cochrane Evidence Synthesis and Methods.
**Certainty:** 0.80
**BibTeX key:** `Pitre2023blindingImpact`

## Paper 5: Wormgoor & Rodenburg 2021 (already in references.bib as Wormgoor2021physio)

**Full Citation:** Wormgoor MEA, Rodenburg SC. The evidence base for physiotherapy in myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome when considering post-exertional malaise: a systematic review and narrative synthesis. *Journal of Translational Medicine*. 2021;19(1):1–22.

**DOI:** 10.1186/s12967-020-02683-4
**PMID:** 33397399
**BibTeX key:** `Wormgoor2021physio` — **ALREADY IN references.bib**

**Key Findings (relevant to Zhao 2026 critique):**
- 18 RCTs analyzed; classified by PEM-strictness of diagnostic criteria:
  - CF (PEM not mentioned): 3 RCTs
  - CFS (PEM optional): 14 RCTs
  - ME (PEM required): 1 RCT
- Treatment effects **disappeared with narrower PEM-inclusive criteria**
- Treatment effects disappeared with **increasing objectivity of outcome measures**
- Treatment effects disappeared with **longer follow-up**
- Conclusion: "no scientific evidence for effective physiotherapy for ME patients"
- "Applying treatment that seems effective for CF or CFS patients may have adverse consequences for ME patients"

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- Directly demonstrates that including Oxford/Fukuda-without-PEM criteria dilutes the ME population
- Wormgoor found that PEM-criterion-stratified analysis makes exercise effects disappear — Zhao does not perform this stratification despite similar criteria mix
- The "disappearing with objectivity" finding mirrors Zhao's subjective-only result

**Quality:** High — systematic review, Journal of Translational Medicine.
**Certainty:** 0.80
**Status:** Already cited; confirm it is cited in ch24 or integration guide should reference it.

## Paper 6: Vink & Vink-Niese 2018

**Full Citation:** Vink M, Vink-Niese A. Graded exercise therapy for myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome is not effective and unsafe. Re-analysis of a Cochrane review. *Health Psychol Open*. 2018;5(2):2055102918805187.

**DOI:** 10.1177/2055102918805187
**PMID:** 30305916
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30305916/

**Study Design:** Re-analysis of the 2017 Cochrane exercise review for ME/CFS; focused on objective outcomes reported in the underlying trials.

**Key Findings:**
- Objective outcomes in Cochrane-included GET trials showed **no effectiveness**
- Cochrane review did not adequately report harms; safety cannot be assumed
- Cochrane reviewers focused on subjective outcomes and ignored contradictory objective data
- Patient surveys indicate GET is detrimental to health of ≥50% of patients

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- Establishes precedent that even the headline Cochrane exercise review, when re-analyzed for objective outcomes, shows no effect
- Zhao 2026 repeats this pattern on a larger international dataset — subjective improvement, no objective improvement
- Patient harm data is relevant when considering whether "no objective harm" from exercise means "no harm"

**Quality:** Medium — re-analysis; Health Psychol Open (peer-reviewed but lower impact than primary sources)
**Certainty:** 0.70
**BibTeX key:** `VinkNiese2018getCochrane`

## Paper 7: Vink & Vink-Niese 2020

**Full Citation:** Vink M, Vink-Niese F. Graded exercise therapy does not restore the ability to work in ME/CFS — Rethinking of a Cochrane review. *Work*. 2020;66(2):283–308.

**DOI:** 10.3233/WOR-203174
**PMID:** 32568149
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32568149/

**Study Design:** Critical rethinking of the amended Cochrane exercise review; focus on work capacity (functional outcome) as an objective endpoint.

**Key Findings:**
- GET does not restore ability to work despite subjective fatigue improvement claims
- Objective outcomes showed GET is not effective
- "Unreliability of subjective outcomes in non-blinded trials" is a central methodological concern
- Study design flaws documented: poorly matched controls, outcome switching, p-hacking
- GET "detrimental to health of ≥50% of patients" per patient surveys

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- Ability to work is a critical real-world functional outcome — if exercise doesn't restore function, subjective scale improvements are clinically meaningless
- Zhao explicitly does not examine work capacity or real-world function
- The pattern of "subjective improvement without functional restoration" seen in Cochrane data is precisely what Zhao replicates

**Quality:** Medium — critical re-analysis; Work journal (IOS Press, peer-reviewed).
**Certainty:** 0.65
**BibTeX key:** `VinkNiese2020workCFS`
**Note:** `Vink2020CochraneWork` variant already listed in appendix-h (line 2560 in appendix-h). Confirm citation key.

## Paper 8: Vink & Vink-Niese 2022

**Full Citation:** Vink M, Vink-Niese A. The Updated NICE Guidance Exposed the Serious Flaws in CBT and Graded Exercise Therapy Trials for ME/CFS. *Healthcare (Basel)*. 2022;10(5):898.

**DOI:** 10.3390/healthcare10050898
**PMID:** 35628033
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9141828/

**Study Design:** Critical review analyzing how the 2021 NICE guideline reversal on GET/CBT was justified by the same methodological flaws documented in earlier re-analyses.

**Key Findings:**
- NICE 2021 reversal was driven by recognition that non-blinded trials with subjective outcomes are unreliable
- In the PACE trial, "20% of participants whose physical functioning improved subjectively had deteriorated objectively" — direct quantification of subjective/objective discordance
- Oxford criteria artificially inflate treatment efficacy by including patients with self-limiting illness
- Endpoint changes created overlapping entry/recovery criteria, inflating recovery rates
- Lack of blinding → average 13% exaggeration (cites Savović 2012)

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- The Oxford-criteria problem Zhao inherits is documented here
- The PACE subjective/objective discordance (20% improved subjectively but deteriorated objectively) provides a concrete example of the same pattern Zhao shows at meta-analysis level
- Serves as a bridge paper: connects PACE-era critique to the BRANDO/Savović blinding framework

**Quality:** Medium-High — peer-reviewed; Healthcare (MDPI); moderate impact.
**Certainty:** 0.75
**BibTeX key:** `VinkNiese2022NICE`

## Paper 9: Larun et al. 2024 (Cochrane, updated)

**Full Citation:** Larun L, Brurberg KG, Odgaard-Jensen J, Price JR. Exercise therapy for chronic fatigue syndrome. *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews*. 2024;12(12):CD003200.

**DOI:** 10.1002/14651858.CD003200.pub9
**PMID:** 39697147
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39697147/

**Study Design:** Cochrane systematic review (updated December 2024); compared exercise therapy vs. passive controls in CFS.

**Key Findings:**
- "The intervention does not allow blinding of participants or staff delivering exercise-based interventions"
- All included studies rated **high risk of performance and detection bias** (all measures self-reported)
- Despite acknowledging high RoB: review concludes exercise "probably reduces fatigue at end of treatment"
- Long-term effects uncertain
- Review abandoned update initiative in Dec 2024 despite documented methodological deficiencies

**Relevance to Zhao 2026:**
- Zhao 2026 replicates the same acknowledged-but-discounted high RoB pattern as Larun 2024
- Larun 2024 applies the same logic as Zhao: acknowledge bias, then conclude effectiveness anyway
- Both reviews fail to stratify by diagnostic criteria stringency (PEM-requiring vs. not)
- Larun 2024 is under active criticism for being outdated and misleading; citing it alongside Zhao 2026 shows this is a pattern, not an isolated flaw

**Quality:** High (Cochrane) — but with significant acknowledged limitations and ongoing controversy.
**Certainty for exercise claims:** 0.40 given acknowledged high RoB and diagnostic criteria issues.

**Quality:** Medium — iScience (Cell Press, peer-reviewed, open-access).

## Summary for Integration

### The Critical Chain of Evidence

```
Savović 2012 (BRANDO) → Unblinded + subjective outcomes → 13-23% exaggeration
    ↓
Hróbjartsson 2014 → SMD 0.56 exaggeration from lack of patient blinding (patient-reported outcomes specifically)
    ↓
Pitre 2023 (Cochrane) → Confirms blinding affects trial results; moderate certainty for outcome assessors
    ↓
Wormgoor 2021 → ME/CFS-specific: effect disappears with PEM criteria + objective measures
    ↓
Vink 2018, 2020, 2022 → GET Cochrane trials: objective outcomes null; subjective gains no functional meaning
    ↓
Larun 2024 → Cochrane: acknowledges high RoB throughout, yet endorses exercise
    ↓
Zhao 2026 (INDEX) → Same pattern: 17 RCTs, all high RoB, subjective improvement SMD=0.85, no objective improvement
    ↓
Wei 2025, Liao 2025 → Pattern is now replicated across multiple contemporaneous meta-analyses
```

### Key Argument Points for ch24

1. **Zhao's SMD=0.85 for fatigue is within the Hróbjartsson bias range** (mean 0.56, CI −0.71 to −0.41 for patient-reported outcomes). The "improvement" could entirely reflect unblinding artifact.

2. **The subjective/objective split is the predicted signature of blinding bias** per Savović 2012: "little evidence of bias in trials with objective outcomes." Zhao shows this pattern perfectly.

3. **The "physiological ceiling" explanation is post-hoc and less parsimonious** than the blinding bias explanation, which is empirically grounded in 1,973 trials (Savović) and 3,869 patients (Hróbjartsson).

4. **Diagnostic criteria problem**: Zhao includes Oxford (no PEM required), Fukuda (PEM optional), and Reeves 2005 (expanded Fukuda, also lacks mandatory PEM). Wormgoor 2021 showed treatment effects disappear when PEM-requiring criteria are used. Zhao does not perform this sensitivity analysis.

5. **Intervention heterogeneity**: Pooling aerobic exercise, yoga, qigong, tai chi obscures mechanisms. These interventions have different safety profiles in ME/CFS — aerobic exercise with PEM patients risks harm.

6. **Zhao's own RoB 2.0 assessment found "prevalent high risk of bias"** — the authors thus acknowledge the core methodological problem while dismissing its implications. This self-contradiction weakens their conclusions.

### Hróbjartsson et al. 2014

```typst
/ *Full Citation:*: Hróbjartsson A, Emanuelsson F, Thomsen ASS, Hilden J, Brorson S. Bias due to lack of patient blinding in clinical trials. A systematic review of trials randomizing patients to blind and nonblind sub-studies. _International Journal of Epidemiology_. 2014;43(4):1272--83. @Hrobjartsson2014blinding

/ *Key Findings:*: 12 trials (n=3,869) using internal blinded/unblinded sub-study design. Unblinded patients exaggerated treatment effects by mean SMD 0.56 for patient-reported outcomes (95% CI −0.71 to −0.41). No exaggeration found for observer-reported outcomes. Acupuncture trials showed larger bias (−0.63); non-acupuncture trials showed minimal bias (−0.17). Lack of blinding also increased control-group attrition (ratio 1.79) and co-interventions (ratio 1.55).

/ *Relevance:*: Provides the strongest empirical quantification of patient-blinding bias specifically for patient-reported outcomes. When exercise trial meta-analyses like Zhao 2026 report fatigue SMD ≈ 0.85 in unblinded trials, the Hróbjartsson finding (mean SMD bias 0.56) means the apparent effect could be almost entirely explained by unblinding artifact. Directly relevant to all unblinded behavioral intervention trials in ME/CFS.

/ *Certainty Assessment:*:
  - *Quality:* High — internal sub-study design eliminates confounders; gold-standard methodology
  - *Sample:* n=3,869 across 12 trials
  - *Replication:* Consistent with Savović 2012 and Pitre 2023; core BRANDO findings replicated
  - *Limitations:* Acupuncture-heavy sample (10/12 trials); non-acupuncture bias estimate has wide CI
```

### Savović et al. 2012

```typst
/ *Full Citation:*: Savović J, Jones HE, Altman DG, et al. Influence of reported study design characteristics on intervention effect estimates from randomized, controlled trials. _Annals of Internal Medicine_. 2012;157(6):429--38. @Savovic2012blinding

/ *Key Findings:*: Meta-epidemiological analysis (BRANDO collaboration) of 1,973 RCTs within 234 meta-analyses. Lack of double-blinding → average 13% exaggeration of intervention effects (ROR 0.87). Bias was driven entirely by trials with subjective outcomes; little to no bias in objective or mortality outcome trials. Subjective outcome trials without blinding showed ~23% exaggeration (ROR ~0.77). Conclusion: "clinical and policy decisions should not be based on trials in which blinding is not feasible and outcome measures are subjectively assessed."

/ *Relevance:*: Primary BRANDO project publication. Establishes the general principle that unblinded exercise/behavioral intervention trials measuring subjective outcomes systematically overestimate effects. Applies directly to Zhao 2026 (17 unblinded RCTs, all primary outcomes subjective). The BRANDO conclusion — that such trials should not drive clinical decisions — directly undermines using Zhao 2026 to recommend exercise in ME/CFS.

/ *Certainty Assessment:*:
  - *Quality:* High — Annals of Internal Medicine; 7-dataset combined meta-epidemiological analysis
  - *Sample:* 1,973 RCTs across 234 meta-analyses
  - *Replication:* Foundational; confirmed by Hróbjartsson 2014 and Pitre 2023
  - *Limitations:* Aggregate ROR estimate; individual trial variation is substantial
```

### Vink & Vink-Niese 2022
