# Literature Summary: Microglial Mitochondrial Dysregulation from Direct Borrelia/Bartonella Infection

**Date:** 2026-07-17
**Research Stream:** microglial-mitochondrial-borrelia
**Scope:** Does Borrelia burgdorferi and/or Bartonella species directly infect microglia, causing mitochondrial dysregulation that could contribute to neuroinflammatory and metabolic features of ME/CFS?

---

## Executive Summary

Borrelia burgdorferi directly and potently activates microglia via TLR1/TLR2, inducing proinflammatory cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β), oxidative stress, and neuronal apoptosis in a microglia-dependent manner. Bartonella henselae also infects microglia in vitro but evidence is limited to one older study. No studies directly quantify mitochondrial function in microglia after Borrelia/Bartonella infection. However, indirect evidence is strong: (1) Bb triggers oxidative stress pathways with mitochondrial implications in non-microglial cells; (2) Bb outer membrane vesicles induce ROS in neuronal cell lines; (3) PET imaging in PTLDS patients demonstrates glial activation in vivo; (4) non-viable Bb remnants suffice to sustain neuroinflammation; (5) ME/CFS exosome-associated mtDNA activates human microglia to release IL-1β — providing a conceptual bridge where pathogen-triggered microglial mitochondrial dysfunction could drive feed-forward neuroinflammation.

**Overall certainty: 0.55** — The microglial activation axis is well-established (0.65–0.75). The mitochondrial dysregulation axis in microglia specifically is unstudied (0.00). The indirect pathway (Bb→microglia→ROS→mitochondrial damage→mtDNA release→sustained microglial activation) is mechanistically plausible and partially supported by separate lines of evidence but has not been tested as an integrated hypothesis.

## Per-Paper Detail

### 1. Myers2009Microglia

- **Citation:** Myers TA, Kaushal D, Philipp MT. Microglia are mediators of Borrelia burgdorferi-induced apoptosis in SH-SY5Y neuronal cells. *PLoS Pathogens*. 2009;5(11):e1000659.
- **PMID:** 19911057
- **DOI:** 10.1371/journal.ppat.1000659
- **Journal:** PLoS Pathogens (IF ~6.7)
- **Design:** In vitro co-culture: primary rhesus microglia + SH-SY5Y human neuroblastoma cells + live Bb
- **Sample:** Primary microglial cultures from rhesus macaques; SH-SY5Y cell line
- **Key findings:**
  - Neurons alone + Bb → no apoptosis
  - Microglia + Bb → robust inflammatory cytokine/chemokine expression
  - Neurons + microglia + Bb → neuronal apoptosis via caspase-3 activation
  - Microglial conditioned medium from Bb exposure was sufficient to induce apoptosis (soluble mediators)
  - TNF-α and IL-1β identified as key mediators
- **Conclusion:** Microglia are necessary intermediaries for Bb-induced neuronal injury. Bb does not directly kill neurons — the microglial inflammatory response is required.
- **Limitations:** In vitro; rhesus not human microglia; cell line neurons; no mitochondrial readouts
- **Gap analysis:** Does not examine mitochondrial function in microglia. Does not test whether microglial mitochondrial dysfunction mediates the neurotoxic response.
- **Relevance:** HIGH — establishes microglia as essential effector cells in neuroborreliosis
- **Certainty:** 0.70 — well-powered in vitro study, high-impact journal, replicated conceptually

### 2. Kuhlow2005MicrogliaPhag

- **Citation:** Kuhlow CJ, Garcia-Monco JC, Coleman JL, Benach JL. Murine microglia are effective phagocytes for Borrelia burgdorferi. *Journal of Neuroimmunology*. 2005;168(1-2):183-7.
- **PMID:** 16125249
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.jneuroim.2005.06.030
- **Design:** In vitro murine microglial phagocytosis assay
- **Key findings:**
  - Microglia rapidly phagocytose live B. burgdorferi
  - Phagocytosis triggers proinflammatory cytokine production
  - Microglial uptake may be a rapid clearance mechanism explaining scarcity of spirochetes in CNS
- **Conclusion:** Microglia are first-line defenders against Bb in CNS; their activation is immediate
- **Limitations:** Murine cells; no human confirmation; no mitochondrial assays
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — solid basic immunology, Journal of Neuroimmunology

### 3. Parthasarathy2015MicrogliaTLR

- **Citation:** Parthasarathy G, Philipp MT. Inflammatory mediator release from primary rhesus microglia in response to Borrelia burgdorferi results from the activation of several receptors and pathways. *Journal of Neuroinflammation*. 2015;12:60.
- **PMID:** 25889406
- **DOI:** 10.1186/s12974-015-0274-z
- **Design:** In vitro rhesus microglia + live Bb; TLR blocking antibodies; signaling pathway inhibitors
- **Key findings:**
  - Bb activates microglia through multiple TLRs (TLR1, TLR2, TLR5) — not a single receptor
  - MyD88-dependent and TRIF-dependent pathways both engaged
  - NF-κB and MAPK pathways activated
  - TNF-α, IL-6, IL-12, CCL2, CXCL10 all upregulated
  - Blocking individual TLRs only partially reduced cytokine output (redundancy)
- **Conclusion:** Microglial inflammatory response to Bb is multi-receptor, multi-pathway — makes single-target therapeutic intervention difficult
- **Limitations:** In vitro; rhesus cells; no mitochondrial readouts
- **Certainty:** 0.70 — detailed mechanistic study, good journal, Tulane group

### 4. CassianiIngoni2006MicrogliaTLR

- **Citation:** Cassiani-Ingoni R, et al. Borrelia burgdorferi Induces TLR1 and TLR2 in human microglia and peripheral blood monocytes but differentially regulates HLA-class II expression. *Journal of Neuropathology and Experimental Neurology*. 2006;65(6):540-8.
- **PMID:** 16783164
- **DOI:** 10.1097/00005072-200606000-00002
- **Design:** In vitro human fetal microglia, astrocytes, neurons, glial progenitors + Bb; oligoarray gene expression
- **Key findings:**
  - Bb upregulates TLR1 and TLR2 in human microglia (confirmed in primary human cells — critical species translation)
  - Differential HLA class II regulation: microglia upregulate, PBMCs downregulate
  - Broad transcriptional response including 30+ immune-related genes
- **Conclusion:** Human microglia recognize Bb through TLR1/TLR2 and mount a distinct transcriptional program
- **Limitations:** Fetal tissue; in vitro; no mitochondrial gene expression data
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — human cells strengthen relevance; single time point

### 5. Parthasarathy2023FGFRmicroglia

- **Citation:** Parthasarathy G, Pattison MB, Midkiff CC. The FGF/FGFR system in the microglial neuroinflammation with Borrelia burgdorferi: likely intersectionality with other neurological conditions. *Journal of Neuroinflammation*. 2023;20(1):10.
- **PMID:** 36650549
- **DOI:** 10.1186/s12974-022-02681-x
- **Design:** In vitro rhesus microglia + Bb; RNA-seq; FGFR pathway analysis
- **Key findings:**
  - Bb upregulates FGF2 and FGFR1 in microglia
  - FGFR signaling contributes to inflammatory mediator production
  - FGF/FGFR system links neuroborreliosis to other neurological conditions (Alzheimer's, MS)
- **Conclusion:** Neuroborreliosis shares neuroinflammatory pathways with neurodegenerative diseases — FGF/FGFR as potential common mechanism
- **Limitations:** In vitro; rhesus; no mitochondrial analysis
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — J Neuroinflammation; RNA-seq adds depth; same group as Parthasarathy2015

### 6. Xu2023BmpAmicroglia

- **Citation:** Xu X, et al. A key protein from Borrelia burgdorferi could stimulate cytokines in human microglial cells and inhibitory effects of Cucurbitacin IIa. *IBRO Neuroscience Reports*. 2023;15:376-385.
- **PMID:** 38046885
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.ibneur.2023.11.004
- **Design:** In vitro HMC3 human microglial cell line + recombinant BmpA protein; cytokine chip + qPCR
- **Key findings:**
  - BmpA (Borrelia membrane protein A) alone is sufficient to induce cytokine/chemokine release from microglia
  - IL-6, IL-8, MCP-1, GRO, etc. upregulated
  - Cucurbitacin IIa (plant compound) inhibited cytokine production
- **Conclusion:** A single Bb protein can trigger microglial inflammatory response — intact spirochete not required
- **Limitations:** HMC3 immortalized cell line (not primary); recombinant protein only; no mitochondrial data
- **Certainty:** 0.45 — immortalized cell line reduces physiological relevance; single protein stimulation is artificial but informative

### 7. Akinlusi2025MicrogliaPolarization

- **Citation:** Akinlusi I, et al. Human microglia polarization following infection with the Lyme disease spirochete. *Journal of Investigative Medicine*. 2025;73(1):172-178.
- **PMID:** 39324305
- **DOI:** 10.1177/10815589241290206
- **Design:** In vitro HMC3 human microglia + Bb; M1/M2 polarization markers
- **Key findings:**
  - Bb infection shifts microglia toward M1 (proinflammatory) phenotype
  - Increased iNOS, TNF-α, IL-1β (M1 markers)
  - Decreased Arg-1, CD206 (M2 markers)
  - M1 polarization would be expected to shift metabolism toward aerobic glycolysis (Warburg-like) — though not measured
- **Conclusion:** Neuroborreliosis drives microglial M1 polarization, consistent with chronic neuroinflammation
- **Limitations:** HMC3 cell line; polarization marker panel limited; no metabolic/mitochondrial assays
- **Certainty:** 0.45 — recent (2025), but HMC3 line; polarization data matches expectation

### 8. Munana2001BartonellaMicroglia

- **Citation:** Munana KR, Vitek SM, Hegarty BC, Kordick DL, Breitschwerdt EB. Infection of fetal feline brain cells in culture with Bartonella henselae. *Infection and Immunity*. 2001;69(1):564-9.
- **PMID:** 11119554
- **DOI:** 10.1128/IAI.69.1.564-569.2001
- **Design:** In vitro feline fetal microglial- and astrocyte-enriched cultures + B. henselae
- **Key findings:**
  - B. henselae infects and survives in feline microglia and astrocytes
  - Infection was non-cytolytic — bacteria persist intracellularly without immediate cell death
  - Establishes that Bartonella can directly invade CNS glial cells
- **Conclusion:** Bartonella henselae can directly infect microglia — mechanism for CNS involvement in cat-scratch disease
- **Limitations:** Feline cells, not human; one study only; 2001 (no follow-up in human microglia); no mitochondrial data; no cytokine readouts
- **Certainty:** 0.55 — single study, good journal (Infect Immun), but species limitation and age

### 9. Coughlin2018PTLDSglialPET

- **Citation:** Coughlin JM, et al. Imaging glial activation in patients with post-treatment Lyme disease symptoms: a pilot study using [11C]DPA-713 PET. *Journal of Neuroinflammation*. 2018;15(1):346.
- **PMID:** 30567544
- **DOI:** 10.1186/s12974-018-1381-4
- **Design:** Human PET imaging study; [11C]DPA-713 TSPO ligand to quantify glial activation; PTLDS patients vs healthy controls
- **Sample:** 12 PTLDS patients, 19 healthy controls
- **Key findings:**
  - Increased TSPO binding in PTLDS patients vs controls — evidence of glial activation (microglia and/or astrocytes)
  - Correlation with symptom severity (cognitive complaints, pain, fatigue)
  - Effect persisted years after antibiotic treatment
- **Conclusion:** Glial activation is present in PTLDS patients and may contribute to persistent symptoms — first in vivo human evidence
- **Limitations:** Small sample (n=12); TSPO cannot distinguish microglia from astrocytes; no pre-treatment baseline; single time point; TSPO binding affected by rs6971 polymorphism
- **Certainty:** 0.60 — human in vivo data is valuable; small pilot; needs replication

### 10. Parthasarathy2022NonviableBb

- **Citation:** Parthasarathy G, Gadila SKG. Neuropathogenicity of non-viable Borrelia burgdorferi ex vivo. *Scientific Reports*. 2022;12(1):688.
- **PMID:** 35027599
- **DOI:** 10.1038/s41598-021-03837-0
- **Design:** Ex vivo study comparing viable vs non-viable (antibiotic-killed) Bb effects on microglia
- **Key findings:**
  - Non-viable Bb remnants are still capable of activating microglia and inducing neuroinflammation
  - Antibiotic treatment does not eliminate the inflammatory stimulus
  - Implications for PTLDS: persistent symptoms may be driven by bacterial debris, not live infection
- **Conclusion:** Microbial debris suffices for sustained microglial activation — explains post-antibiotic neuroinflammation
- **Limitations:** Ex vivo; mechanisms of debris recognition not fully characterized; no mitochondrial analysis
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — Sci Rep; conceptually important for PTLDS/ME/CFS overlap

### 11. Peacock2015LymeOxidativeStress

- **Citation:** Peacock BN, et al. New insights into Lyme disease. *Redox Biology*. 2015;5:66-70.
- **PMID:** 25838067
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.redox.2015.03.002
- **Design:** Review + original data on oxidative stress mechanisms in Bb infection
- **Key findings:**
  - Bb infection induces oxidative stress in multiple cell types
  - Lipid peroxidation and protein carbonylation detected
  - Host antioxidant defenses overwhelmed
  - Mitochondria are primary targets of Bb-induced oxidative damage (by inference from broader data)
- **Conclusion:** Oxidative stress is a core pathological mechanism in Lyme disease — mitochondrial involvement likely
- **Limitations:** Predominantly review; limited original mitochondrial data; not microglia-specific
- **Certainty:** 0.50 — Redox Biology; useful for the oxidative stress→mitochondrial damage link

### 14. Tsilioni2022ExosomemtDNAmicroglia

- **Citation:** Tsilioni I, Natelson B, Theoharides TC. Exosome-associated mitochondrial DNA from patients with myalgic encephalomyelitis/chronic fatigue syndrome stimulates human microglia to release IL-1β. *European Journal of Neuroscience*. 2022;56(10):5784-5794.
- **PMID:** 36153118
- **DOI:** 10.1111/ejn.15828
- **Design:** In vitro HMC3 human microglia + serum exosomes from ME/CFS patients; mtDNA quantification
- **Key findings:**
  - ME/CFS patient serum exosomes contain elevated mtDNA (vs healthy controls), but only after exercise challenge
  - ME/CFS exosomes stimulate HMC3 microglia to release IL-1β

- **Limitations:** HMC3 cell line; small n (ME/CFS = ~20, controls = ~10); no subtyping of patients; post-exercise only
- **Certainty:** 0.55 — conceptual bridge paper between mitochondrial dysfunction and microglial activation in ME/CFS; needs replication

## Synthesis: The Case for Microglial Mitochondrial Dysregulation from Borrelia/Bartonella

### Established (Certainty ≥ 0.60)
1. **Bb directly activates microglia** via TLR1/TLR2/MyD88 → NF-κB → TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, chemokines (6 independent studies, human and primate cells)
2. **Microglia are necessary** for Bb-induced neuronal apoptosis — Bb alone does not kill neurons
3. **Non-viable Bb debris suffices** to sustain microglial activation → explains post-antibiotic neuroinflammation
4. **In vivo glial activation** confirmed in PTLDS patients via TSPO-PET

### Plausible but Unconfirmed (Certainty 0.30–0.55)
5. **B. henselae infects microglia** — single 2001 feline study; no human microglia studies found
6. **Bb induces oxidative stress** in non-microglial cells — inferred but not measured in microglia
7. **Bb OMVs induce ROS** in neuronal cells — same mechanism likely operates in microglia
8. **ME/CFS mtDNA activates microglia** — establishes the downstream effector mechanism

### Gap (Certainty 0.00)
9. **No study has measured mitochondrial function** (respiration, membrane potential, mtDNA integrity, mitophagy) in microglia after Borrelia or Bartonella exposure

### Proposed Hypothesis Chain
Bb/Bartonella → TLR1/TLR2 activation → microglial M1 polarization → iNOS/NO → mitochondrial complex inhibition → mtDNA release → NLRP3/cGAS-STING → sustained IL-1β/IFN → feed-forward neuroinflammation → ME/CFS neurologic symptoms

The Tsilioni2022 paper provides the terminal step (mtDNA→microglia IL-1β). Multiple papers provide the initial steps. The middle steps (microglial mitochondrial dysfunction from bacterial infection) remain untested.
