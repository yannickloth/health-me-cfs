## Evidence Landscape

### Strong
- None. No high-quality, adequately powered study has directly examined RF prevalence or significance in ME/CFS.

### Weak
- **Hashimoto 1992:** Only direct RF measurement in CFS cohort. 11.8% RF+ (6/51). CDC 1988 criteria, small n, Japanese population only. Certainty: 0.25.
- **Rasmussen 1994:** No RF elevation in 21 CFS patients. Small n, underpowered. Certainty: 0.30.
- **Liñán Ponce 2024:** FM with elevated RF associated with poor treatment response. High FM/ME/CFS overlap but not ME/CFS-specific. Certainty: 0.55.
- **Kerr 2002:** Post-B19 infection cohort developing CFS; RF part of broader autoAb panel. Certainty: 0.40.

### Missing
- No study has examined RF prevalence in a large, well-characterized ME/CFS cohort using modern diagnostic criteria (IOM 2015, NICE 2021, Canadian Consensus 2003).
- No study has compared RF positivity rates in ME/CFS vs healthy controls vs autoimmune disease controls.
- No study has examined whether RF+ ME/CFS patients represent a distinct clinical subgroup.
- RF has not been included in any modern autoantibody profiling panel for ME/CFS (e.g., Germain 2025 used REAP + Luminex panels that did not include RF).
- No study has examined RF isotypes (IgM-RF, IgA-RF, IgG-RF) in ME/CFS.
- No study has examined RF in relation to ME/CFS severity, PEM, or autonomic dysfunction.

### Null
- **Rasmussen 1994:** No significant autoantibody differences in CFS vs controls. Very small n (21).
- **Germain 2025:** Complete null for autoantibody profiling in ME/CFS (n=172). RF not specifically measured but provides context.
- **Batham 2024:** Systematic review concludes insufficient evidence for ME/CFS as autoimmune disease.

## Contradictions
- No direct contradictions found because the literature is too sparse for replication.
- Indirect tension: Hashimoto 1992 suggests possible RF elevation (11.8% positive in CFS), while Rasmussen 1994 found no autoantibody abnormalities. Different eras (1992 vs 1994), different criteria (CDC 1988 vs CDC 1994?), different populations (Japan vs Denmark), both very small n.

## Clinical Relevance Assessment
- **Clinical relevance:** LOW. RF testing is not indicated in routine ME/CFS workup except when signs/symptoms suggest comorbid autoimmune disease (RA, Sjögren's, SLE, HCV).
- **Subset applicability:** Unknown. No data on which ME/CFS patients might be RF+.
- **Mechanistic relevance:** LOW. No evidence that RF itself is pathogenic in ME/CFS. RF could be: (a) an epiphenomenon of general immune dysregulation, (b) a marker of comorbid autoimmune disease, (c) a marker of chronic infection (HCV, EBV), or (d) relevant only in specific subsets.
- **Biomarker potential:** LOW. Without prevalence data, no basis for RF as a diagnostic or stratifying biomarker.
