## Papers Already in Bib (Verified — All 3 Present and Correct)

### 1. Laborde2022SlowBreathingHRV — Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104711
- **Source:** *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 2022, 138:104711
- **Design:** Systematic review + meta-analysis of 15 studies
- **Key findings:** Voluntary slow breathing (VSB) consistently increases vagally-mediated HRV (RMSSD) and reduces HR during and after sessions. Effects most pronounced at ~6 breaths/min (resonance frequency). Modest effect sizes: g=0.31–0.46 for RMSSD.
- **Mechanism:** Baroreflex entrainment + pulmonary stretch receptor vagal afferent activation
- **Quality:** High. 231 citations (Crossref). Comprehensive meta-analysis. 15 studies included.
- **Limitations:** Publication bias possible; most studies small; acute effects measured more than chronic.
- **Certainty:** 0.80

### 2. Lehrer2014ResonanceBreathing — Theoretical Foundation
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fpsyg.2014.00756
- **Source:** *Frontiers in Psychology*, 2014, 5:756
- **Key findings:** Established the resonance frequency model of HRV biofeedback. Breathing at ~6 breaths/min (0.1 Hz) maximally entrains the baroreflex, producing the largest HRV oscillations. Baroreflex resetting within a single session.
- **Quality:** High. Foundational theoretical paper by Paul Lehrer (leading HRV-BF researcher).
- **Certainty:** 0.85

### 3. Shao2024SlowBreathingMeta — Cardiovascular + Emotion Meta-Analysis
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s12671-023-02268-w
- **Source:** *Mindfulness*, 2024, 15:1–18
- **Key findings:** Meta-analysis confirming slow-paced breathing improves cardiovascular function (HRV, BP) and emotional regulation. Extends Laborde findings to emotion/stress.
- **Quality:** Medium-High. Well-conducted meta-analysis.
- **Certainty:** 0.75

## NEW Papers Added to Bib (7 Total)

### 4. Zaccaro2018BreathControl — Systematic Review: Psycho-Physiological Correlates of Slow Breathing
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fnhum.2018.00353
- **Source:** *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 2018, 12:353
- **Key findings:** Slow breathing (<10 breaths/min) enhances parasympathetic activity, increases HRV, improves cognitive performance, and facilitates emotional regulation. Specific mechanisms: pulmonary stretch receptors → vagal afferent → NTS → modulation of amygdala, prefrontal cortex, and insula. EEG evidence shows increased alpha and theta oscillations relevant to sleep onset.
- **Quality:** Medium-High. 28 studies, well-cited (300+). Certainty: 0.75

### 5. Gerritsen2018BreathOfLife — Respiratory Vagal Stimulation Model
- **DOI:** 10.3389/fnhum.2018.00397
- **Source:** *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 2018, 12:397
- **Key findings:** Proposes the Respiratory Vagal Stimulation (RVS) model. NTS receives convergent input from pulmonary stretch receptors AND baroreceptors. At resonance frequency (0.1 Hz), the respiratory and baroreflex oscillations synchronize → maximal vagal outflow. Explains meditation/pranayama/HRV-BF effects.
- **Quality:** Medium-High. Well-reasoned theoretical framework. Certainty: 0.70

### 6. Kromenacker2018VagalMediation — Vagal Mediation of LF-HRV During Slow Yogic Breathing
- **DOI:** 10.1097/PSY.0000000000000603
- **Source:** *Psychosomatic Medicine*, 2018, 80(6):581-587
- **Key findings:** Pharmacological blockade study (glycopyrrolate — vagal blockade). LF-HRV during slow yogic breathing (6 breaths/min) is VAGALLY-MEDIATED. Vagal blockade nearly eliminated LF-HRV. **Gold-standard demonstration that LF-HRV during resonance breathing reflects vagal tone, not sympathetic activation.**
- **Quality:** High. Pharmacological blockade is gold standard. Certainty: 0.85

### 7. Laborde2019SlowBreathingSleep — 30-Day SPB Intervention for Sleep Quality
- **DOI:** 10.3390/jcm8020193
- **Source:** *Journal of Clinical Medicine*, 2019, 8(2):193
- **Key findings:** RCT — 30-day SPB (6 breaths/min, 20 min/day) vs social media control (n=60). SPB group showed significant improvement in subjective sleep quality (PSQI), increased vagally-mediated HRV at rest POST-intervention (training effect). HRV changes correlated with sleep quality improvements.
- **Quality:** Medium-High. RCT design. Certainty: 0.68

### 8. Sakakibara2020PacedBreathingBaroreflex — Paced Breathing at LF Peak Improves Baroreflex Sensitivity
- **DOI:** 10.1007/s10484-019-09453-z
- **Source:** *Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback*, 2020, 45(1):31-40
- **Key findings:** Paced breathing at individual LF peak (~6 breaths/min) significantly increased baroreflex sensitivity (BRS) and HRV vs spontaneous breathing. Directly demonstrates the BRS mechanism linking SPB to vagal enhancement.
- **Quality:** Medium. Direct BRS measurement. Certainty: 0.68

### 9. SevozCouche2022HRVCoherenceResonance — HRV Coherence Meets Resonance
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.neubiorev.2022.104576
- **Source:** *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 2022, 135:104576
- **Key findings:** Integrative review of HRV coherence (RSA) and resonance (baroreflex) mechanisms. At resonance frequency the two synchronize producing maximal HRV. NTS identified as the convergence point for pulmonary stretch and baroreceptor afferents.
- **Quality:** Medium-High. Companion to Laborde2022. Certainty: 0.75

### 10. Jayawardena2020PranayamaAutonomic — Pranayama: Systematic Review of Autonomic Outcomes
- **DOI:** 10.4103/ijoy.IJOY_37_19
- **Source:** *International Journal of Yoga*, 2020, 13(2):99-114
- **Key findings:** Systematic review of 19 studies. Slow pranayama techniques (Nadi Shodhana, Bhramari, Ujjayi) significantly increase HRV and parasympathetic activity, reduce BP, improve HR recovery. Longer interventions (≥8 weeks) produce larger effects.
- **Quality:** Medium. Heterogeneous study quality. Certainty: 0.65

## CRITICAL Paper: Ryabkova2024ME/CFS — HRV and Baroreflex Failure in ME/CFS (CONTRAST/NULL RESULT)
- **DOI:** 10.3390/pathophysiology31010001
- **Source:** *Pathophysiology*, 2024, 31(1):1-17
- **Design:** Cross-sectional with spiroarteriocardiorhythmography
- **Sample:** n=34 ME/CFS, n=29 PCC, n=32 healthy controls
- **Key findings:** At rest: HRV (TP, LF, HF) and baroreflex sensitivity significantly lower in ME/CFS vs controls. During SLOW BREATHING (6 breaths/min): HRV normalized in PCC but **NOT** in ME/CFS patients. Fatigue correlated with HRV/baroreflex indices, NOT with depression/anxiety scores.
- **CRITICAL relevance:** ME/CFS patients have baroreflex impairment that does NOT normalize during single-session SPB the way PCC does. Suggests entrenched autonomic deficit requiring longer SPB training.
- **Already in bib** but as inline citation in Appendix H, not as `@article` entry. Add separate bib entry if citing in main paper.

## Critical Gaps in Literature

1. **No RCT of SPB in ME/CFS** — zero direct evidence for SPB efficacy in ME/CFS
2. **No SPB + sleep study in any chronic illness population** — all sleep data from healthy subjects
3. **No SPB + HRV training study in dysautonomia** — except single-session studies
4. **4-7-8 breathing has ZERO peer-reviewed evidence** — only popular press/anecdotal
5. **Optimal dosing unknown for patient populations** — frequency, duration, session length
6. **Long-term adherence data lacking** — no studies >30 days in any population
7. **No mechanistic SPB studies in β2AR/M3R antibody-positive ME/CFS** despite vagal-autonomic connection
8. **No adverse event monitoring for SPB in severe ME/CFS** — concern: could excessive vagal activation worsen orthostatic intolerance in some patients?
