# Literature Summary — Vagal Cholinergic Gastric Denervation in Long COVID & Relevance to ME/CFS

**Date:** 2026-07-10  
**Topic slug:** vagal-gastric-denervation-longcovid  
**Seed paper:** Acanfora et al. 2026 — IJID

---

## Overview

This research stream investigates structural vagal/parasympathetic denervation in Long COVID and its implications for ME/CFS. The seed paper (Acanfora 2026) reports the first *in vivo* morphological evidence of selective cholinergic denervation of the gastric mucosa in Long COVID patients, using PGP 9.5 and VIP immunohistochemistry on gastric biopsies with 3D Neurolucida 360 quantification. This finding is contextualised by convergent evidence from three independent lines: (1) direct vagus nerve pathology (Woo 2023 — postmortem vagus inflammation; Lladós 2024 — vagus ultrasound thickening), (2) the established cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway literature (Tracey 2002; Bonaz 2018), (3) small fibre neuropathy evidence in ME/CFS (Oaklander/Joseph 2021; Azcue 2023).

---

## Paper-by-Paper Summary

### 1. Acanfora et al. 2026 — Vagal Cholinergic Denervation (SEED)
- **Design:** Single-centre case-control, n=12 Long COVID vs 8 dyspeptic controls
- **Key result:** Gastric mucosal innervation significantly reduced (PGP 9.5 and VIP); IENF (skin) NOT reduced → selective visceral cholinergic denervation
- **Correlations:** Nerve density ↔ HRV LF/HF (R=0.50), NT-proBNP (R=0.52), D-dimer (R=0.61)
- **Certainty:** 0.60 — novel first-in-kind study, small n, controls were dyspeptic
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Direct structural evidence of vagal cholinergic denervation in post-viral illness; CAP disruption mechanism directly applicable

### 2. Woo et al. 2023 — Vagus Nerve Inflammation (Acta Neuropathol)
- **Design:** Postmortem histopathology + RNAseq; clinical cohort n=323
- **Key result:** SARS-CoV-2 RNA + monocyte infiltration in vagus nerves; transcriptomic inflammation correlated with viral load
- **Certainty:** 0.75 — high-impact journal, histopathology gold standard
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Provides proximal mechanism (viral vagus infection → inflammation → denervation) for the structural deficit Acanfora found; validates VanElzakker 2013 hypothesis

### 3. Lladós et al. 2024 — Vagus Nerve Dysfunction Post-COVID (CMI)
- **Design:** Cross-sectional pilot n=30 PCC + 14 recovered + 16 never-infected; neck ultrasound
- **Key result:** Vagus nerve thickening; reduced GI peristalsis; phrenic nerve involvement
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — pilot, modest sample, but controlled design
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Functional + structural imaging evidence of vagus nerve abnormality in Long COVID; complements histology (Woo) and mucosal biopsy (Acanfora)

### 4. Tracey 2002 — The Inflammatory Reflex (Nature)
- **Design:** Foundational review
- **Key result:** Discovery of the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (CAP): vagus → α7nAChR → macrophage TNF inhibition
- **Certainty:** 0.85 — Nature, >3800 citations, extensively replicated
- **ME/CFS relevance:** The mechanistic bridge: structural vagal denervation (Acanfora) → CAP disruption → sustained pro-inflammatory state → ME/CFS symptoms

### 5. Bonaz et al. 2018 — Vagus Nerve at Microbiota-Gut-Brain Axis (Front Neurosci)
- **Design:** Comprehensive review
- **Key result:** Vagus = 80% afferent, 20% efferent; dual anti-inflammatory (HPA + CAP); VNS as anti-TNF therapy
- **Certainty:** 0.70 — well-consolidated review
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Detailed mechanistic framework; gut-brain axis implications for GI symptoms in ME/CFS; VNS therapeutic rationale

### 6. VanElzakker 2013 — Vagus Nerve Infection Hypothesis (Med Hypotheses)
- **Design:** Hypothesis/theory paper
- **Key result:** Proposes ME/CFS from pathogen-infected vagal ganglia → exaggerated sickness behaviour
- **Certainty:** 0.40 — hypothesis only, low-tier journal — but remarkably predictive
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Correctly anticipated Woo 2023 (viral vagus inflammation) and Acanfora 2026 (structural vagal denervation) by a decade. Framework for understanding post-viral vagal pathology.

### 7. Oaklander (Joseph) et al. 2021 — PGP 9.5 Skin Biopsy SFN in ME/CFS (Chest)
- **Design:** n=160 ME/CFS, iCPET + PGP 9.5 skin biopsy
- **Key result:** 31% SFN by skin biopsy (same PGP 9.5 marker); two types of neurovascular dysregulation
- **Certainty:** 0.70 — large study, Chest journal, objective measures
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Critical comparator: Acanfora found NO skin denervation in Long COVID, while Oaklander finds ~31% SFN in ME/CFS. This suggests Long COVID vagal denervation may be *selectively visceral* (sparing somatic fibres), whereas ME/CFS involves more generalised SFN. Differentiates the two conditions while confirming shared small fibre vulnerability.

### 8. Azcue et al. 2023 — Dysautonomia and SFN in Post-COVID and ME/CFS (J Transl Med)
- **Design:** Cross-sectional QST + tilt testing
- **Key result:** C-fibre denervation in ME/CFS; 31% POTS; 34% non-length-dependent SFN pattern
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — objective neurophysiological measures
- **ME/CFS relevance:** Non-length-dependent SFN pattern suggests systemic/central origin, consistent with vagal nuclei involvement. Links dysautonomia and SFN across both conditions.

## Thematic Synthesis

| Theme | Supporting Papers | Key Insight |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| Structural vagal pathology | Acanfora 2026, Woo 2023, Lladós 2024 | Triangulating evidence: mucosal denervation, nerve inflammation, ultrasound thickening |
| CAP disruption | Tracey 2002, Bonaz 2018, Acanfora 2026 | Vagal denervation → loss of α7nAChR anti-inflammatory signalling → sustained pro-inflammation |
| Vagal infection mechanism | VanElzakker 2013, Woo 2023 | SARS-CoV-2 infects vagus → inflammation → structural damage (predictive hypothesis confirmed) |
| SFN in ME/CFS vs Long COVID | Oaklander 2021, Azcue 2023, Acanfora 2026 | ME/CFS = somatic + visceral SFN; Long COVID (Acanfora) = selective visceral, sparing skin. May differentiate the conditions or reflect different disease stages |
| VNS therapeutic relevance | Tracey 2002, Bonaz 2018 | If vagal structural damage underlies dysautonomia, VNS (especially early) might prevent or reverse CAP disruption |

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **No null/negative studies found** explicitly contradicting vagal denervation or finding normal vagal structure in Long COVID/ME/CFS. This may reflect publication bias.
2. **No autoantibody papers specifically targeting vagal structures** verified through this search. The GPCR autoantibody literature exists (Loebel 2016, Scheibenbogen 2018) but was already integrated through other research streams.
3. **No ME/CFS-specific vagal denervation studies exist** — only Long COVID evidence. Extrapolation to ME/CFS is indirect but supported by shared pathophysiology.
4. **No longitudinal studies** tracking whether vagal denervation improves or worsens over time in Long COVID.
5. **No functional deconditioning alternative hypothesis** papers specifically addressing vagal dysfunction as a confound — though the broader deconditioning literature exists.
