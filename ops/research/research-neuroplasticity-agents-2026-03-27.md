# Neuroplasticity Agents: Lithium, Valproate, AEDs, and Thyroid Hormones

**Research date:** 2026-03-27
**Purpose:** Evaluate brain rewiring / neuroplasticity potential for ME/CFS context

---

## Executive Summary

Four classes of neuroplasticity-modifying agents were researched for their ability to rewire brain circuits, reverse central sensitization, and restore cognitive function — with specific attention to ME/CFS relevance.

### Ranking by Brain Rewiring Potential

| Agent | Rewiring mechanism | Cognitive profile | ME/CFS fit | Overall |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lithium** | GSK-3beta -> BDNF, neurogenesis, grey matter +2.6% | Bidirectional (dose-dependent) | High (Long COVID trial exists) | Strongest evidence |
| **Valproate (Depakine)** | HDAC inhibition -> critical period reopening, epigenetic reprogramming | Negative (working memory, VIRCD risk) | Moderate (cognitive trade-off) | Unique mechanism |
| **T3 (triiodothyronine)** | Myelination, mitochondrial biogenesis, dendritic remodeling | Restores hypothyroid deficits | Very high (documented low T3 in ME/CFS) | Addresses root cause |
| **Lamotrigine** | Glutamate reduction, BDNF, Bcl-2 | Cognitive-sparing / enhancing | High | Best tolerability |
| **Levetiracetam** | SV2A -> anti-kindling, anti-neuroinflammation | Clean cognitive profile | High (anti-inflammatory) | Disease-modifying |
| **Gabapentinoids** | Anti-synaptogenesis (alpha2delta-1/thrombospondin) | Moderate impairment | Moderate (fibromyalgia overlap) | Targets sensitization |
| **Lithium + Valproate** | Synergistic GSK-3beta inhibition (70% vs 40%/20% alone) | Combined side effects | Theoretical | Synergy documented |

## 1. LITHIUM

### Key Brain Rewiring Mechanisms

**Primary target: GSK-3beta inhibition** (Ki: 1-2 mM, within therapeutic range 0.6-1.2 mM)

- **Grey matter increase**: +2.56% peak at 10-12 weeks; +10-15% in cingulate gyrus; even healthy volunteers show DLPFC increases after 4 weeks
- **BDNF upregulation**: +10-28% at *subtherapeutic* concentrations (0.02-0.2 mM); epigenetic mechanism via BDNF exon IV promoter hypomethylation
- **Neurogenesis**: Wnt/beta-catenin activation promotes hippocampal neurogenesis
- **Dendritic remodeling**: Blocks stress-induced atrophy in CA3; increases dendritic branching and LTP
- **Autophagy**: mTOR-independent pathway via IMPase inhibition -> IP3 depletion; clears huntingtin, alpha-synuclein aggregates; additive with rapamycin
- **Anti-inflammatory**: TLR4 suppression, NLRP3 inflammasome reduction, NF-kappaB inhibition, 1.9-fold increase in pro-resolvin 17-OH-DHA

**Unique among mood stabilizers**: Only lithium (not valproate, not antipsychotics) produces measurable grey matter increases.

### Cognitive Effects

- **Therapeutic**: After mood stabilization, 30.7% showed reliable cognitive improvement; verbal learning, delayed recall, executive function all improved
- **Adverse**: Doses >1g/day may cause cognitive slowing; subjective "dulling" common but often not confirmed objectively
- **Dose-dependent**: First intervention for lithium cognitive complaints is to lower the dose
- **Dementia protection**: RR 0.59 for Alzheimer's, RR 0.66 for all-cause dementia (2024 meta-analysis)

### Low-Dose / Microdose Evidence

- **300 micrograms/day** (lithium microdose) stabilized MMSE in Alzheimer's patients for 15 months while placebo declined from ~20 to ~14
- **Lithium orotate** (supplement, 5-10 mg elemental): crosses BBB more efficiently via OATP1A2
- **Long COVID trial (JAMA Network Open 2024)**: 10-15 mg/day ineffective; 40-45 mg/day (serum 0.18-0.49 mEq/L) showed improvements in cognitive dysfunction and fatigue
- **Nature 2025**: Lithium was the only metal significantly reduced in brains of people with mild cognitive impairment

### ME/CFS Relevance

- Directly addresses: neuroinflammation (microglial suppression), brain fog (BDNF/synaptogenesis), glutamate excitotoxicity, autophagy dysfunction
- Long COVID trial provides dose-ranging data: **40-45 mg/day** appears to be the minimum effective dose
- Lithium orotate available OTC as supplement
- Historical ME/CFS trials were negative but used standard psychiatric dosing; modern low-dose approach may warrant re-examination

### Key Sources

- Chiu et al. 2013, Int J Neuropsychopharmacol (PMC3565922) — mood stabilizer neuroprotection review
- 2025/2026 systematic review, Nature (115 studies) — frontolimbic circuitry restoration
- Guttuso et al. 2024, JAMA Network Open — lithium in Long COVID
- Nature 2025 — lithium brain depletion in MCI
- Leng et al. 2008, J Neurosci (PMC5241911) — lithium + valproate synergy

## 2. VALPROATE (DEPAKINE / VPA)

### Key Brain Rewiring Mechanisms

**Primary target: HDAC inhibition** (class I-preferring; IC50 ~0.4 mM for HDAC1, within therapeutic range)

- **Critical period reopening**: RCT showed VPA (500-1000 mg/day) enabled adult learning of absolute pitch — normally only acquirable in early childhood (Gervain et al. 2013, Front Syst Neurosci)
- **Epigenetic reprogramming**: Alters expression of >1,300 genes (726 up, 577 down); induces DNA demethylation; remodels retrotransposon elements at enhancer regions
- **BDNF upregulation**: Via HDAC inhibition + ERK/CREB activation; enhances extracellular BDNF in astrocytes
- **Anti-kindling**: True antiepileptogenic effect (not just seizure suppression); prevents morphine and methylphenidate sensitization — general anti-sensitization mechanism
- **Neuroprotection**: Upregulates Bcl-2, suppresses caspase-3, induces HSP70; protects against oxidative stress and TBI

**The critical period model**: VPA creates a transient window of heightened epigenetic plasticity during which neural circuits can be reshaped by experience/training — analogous to developmental critical periods but pharmacologically induced in adulthood. Combined with targeted training/therapy, VPA could enable circuit-level remodeling normally impossible after critical periods close.

### Cognitive Effects

- **Negative**: Working memory impairment, processing speed reduction at therapeutic doses
- **VIRCD (valproate-induced reversible cognitive decline)**: Dementia-like syndrome after mean 6.87 years; completely reversible on withdrawal
- **Paradox**: Promotes Wnt-driven neurogenesis in some contexts but inhibits hippocampal cell proliferation in others
- **Teratogenicity**: Severe — contraindicated in pregnancy (HR 2.4-4.48 for intellectual disability, OR 1.70-4.38 for autism)

### ME/CFS Relevance

- **Central sensitization**: HDAC inhibitors shift microglia from M1 to M2 phenotype, restore glutamate transporter expression
- **Anti-kindling**: General anti-sensitization could counteract progressive neural sensitization in ME/CFS
- **Epigenetic resetting**: Could theoretically reverse persistent post-infectious epigenetic changes
- **Major concern**: Cognitive side effects (working memory, processing speed) would compound existing ME/CFS brain fog
- No direct ME/CFS studies

### Key Sources

- Gervain et al. 2013, Front Syst Neurosci (PMC3848041) — critical period reopening (RCT)
- Gottlicher et al. 2001, EMBO J (PMC125788) — VPA as HDAC inhibitor
- Guo et al. 2015, Epigenetics & Chromatin — retrotransposon remodeling
- Zhang et al. 2024, CNS Neurosci Ther (PMC11077000) — HDAC in neuropathic pain
- Armon et al. 2023, Seizure — VIRCD review

## 3. T3 / T4 (THYROID HORMONES)

### Key Brain Rewiring Mechanisms

**T3 is the active hormone in the brain; T4 is a pro-hormone converted locally by DIO2 in astrocytes**

- **Myelination**: T3 is THE primary signal driving oligodendrocyte differentiation; promotes remyelination in demyelination models; Phase I MS trial showed favorable safety
- **Dendritic remodeling**: 14-day T3 treatment increases dendritic spine density including mushroom-type (mature) spines; upregulates GluR2 (AMPA receptor)
- **Mitochondrial biogenesis**: T3 upregulates PGC-1alpha, increases Complex IV activity, stimulates OXPHOS within minutes (non-genomic)
- **Anti-neuroinflammation**: Reverses microglial activation (Iba-1), reduces TNF-alpha/IL-6/IL-1beta in hippocampus; promotes mitophagy via PINK1-Parkin
- **BDNF regulation**: T3 regulates BDNF expression (promoter-specific, region-specific); hypothyroidism -> low BDNF -> loss of PI3K-Akt inhibition -> M1 microglial polarization -> neuroinflammation (vicious cycle)
- **Active gene repression**: Unliganded thyroid receptors (without T3) don't just fail to activate — they actively REPRESS target genes via corepressor recruitment. Hypothyroidism = active suppression.
- **Functional recovery**: In stroke models, T3-treated mice: 73% achieving high functional scores vs 9% vehicle-treated, without changes in infarct volume (plasticity enhancement, not tissue preservation)

### The DIO2 Thr92Ala Polymorphism

- Present in ~50% of global population
- Causes ER stress and aberrant DIO2 accumulation in trans-Golgi -> reduced local T3 production
- Carrier mice: impaired short-term memory, reduced activity, increased sleep
- Carriers respond better to T4+T3 combination therapy
- Associated with bipolar disorder, Alzheimer's, and post-COVID depression

### ME/CFS-Specific Evidence (CRITICAL)

**Documented thyroid dysfunction (Ruiter et al. 2018, Frontiers in Endocrinology):**
- 98 CFS patients vs 99 controls:
  - Free T3: significantly lower (p=0.047); 16% below reference vs 7% controls (OR 2.56)
  - Total T3: reduced 12.5% (p<0.001)
  - Total T4: decreased 11.9% (p<0.001)
  - Reverse T3: increased 13.3% (p<0.001)
  - **TSH: NO difference (p=0.527)** — standard testing misses this entirely
  - SPINA-GD (deiodinase activity): 14.4% reduction (p<0.001)
  - Urinary iodine: 27.6% reduction (p<0.001)

**Proposed vicious cycle:**
Inflammation -> cytokines downregulate DIO1/DIO2, upregulate DIO3 -> low T3 / high rT3 -> impaired mitochondrial function -> reduced ATP -> impaired immune regulation -> more inflammation

**Selenoprotein P autoantibodies** (2023): identified in CFS, suggesting selenium transport impairment and acquired thyroid hormone resistance at tissue level

**Brain fog overlap**: ME/CFS brain fog symptoms closely mirror hypothyroid cognitive impairment: memory deficits, executive dysfunction, processing speed reduction, word-finding difficulty

### T3 Augmentation in Psychiatry (Relevant Dosing Data)

- STAR*D trial: ~23% response, ~25% remission; better tolerated than lithium augmentation
- T3 37.5 mcg vs T4: 53% response vs 19% (p=0.026)
- Enhancement dose: 25-40 mcg/day; augmentation dose: 25-50 mcg/day
- All responders in one SSRI augmentation study were female

### Euthyroid Sick Syndrome / Low T3 Syndrome

- ME/CFS thyroid pattern resembles "mild non-thyroidal illness syndrome"
- Standard endocrine guidance says don't treat (it's "adaptive")
- Counterargument: if self-perpetuating, breaking the cycle with T3 may be therapeutic
- **No RCTs exist for T3 in ME/CFS**

### Hashimoto's: Independent Brain Effects

- Euthyroid Hashimoto's mice showed learning/memory deficits despite normal hormones
- Anti-TPO antibodies bind cerebellar cells expressing GFAP (astrocyte marker)
- Hashimoto's encephalopathy: seizures, cognitive deterioration, neuropsychiatric symptoms — often with normal thyroid function

### Key Sources

- Ruiter et al. 2018, Frontiers in Endocrinology (PMC5869352) — ME/CFS thyroid dysfunction
- Gothie et al. 2020, Acta Neuropathologica Communications (PMC6925884) — adult neuroplasticity
- Bianco & Kim 2018, JCI (PMC6307957) — DIO2 polymorphism
- Science Advances 2024 (PMC10942107) — brain hypothyroidism silences microglia in AD
- Selenoprotein P autoantibodies 2023, Redox Biology

## 4. OTHER AEDs — COMPARATIVE SUMMARY

### Lamotrigine (Lamictal) — Best Cognitive Profile

- **Mechanism**: Na+ channel block -> reduced presynaptic glutamate release
- **Neuroplasticity**: BDNF upregulation, Bcl-2 induction, LTP/LTD modulation, dendritic spine increase
- **Cognitive**: Sparing or ENHANCING (enhanced working memory and verbal memory in pediatric bipolar)
- **Anti-kindling**: Moderate (via glutamate reduction)
- **ME/CFS fit**: HIGH — neuroprotective without cognitive penalty; first-line for bipolar depression
- **Key advantage**: "Mirror image of lithium" — prevents depression > mania

### Levetiracetam (Keppra) — Strongest Anti-Kindling + Anti-Inflammatory

- **Mechanism**: SV2A (synaptic vesicle protein 2A) binding — unique among all AEDs
- **Anti-kindling**: STRONGEST evidence — prevents kindling AND effects persist after discontinuation (true disease modification)
- **Anti-inflammatory**: Superior to valproate and carbamazepine for microglial suppression; reduces TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, IL-6, COX-2, NF-kappaB
- **Cognitive**: Clean / sparing
- **ME/CFS fit**: HIGH — combination of anti-neuroinflammation + anti-kindling + cognitive sparing
- **Key concern**: Behavioral side effects ("Keppra rage") in 13-17% — irritability, agitation, hostility (reversible)

### Gabapentin / Pregabalin — Targets Central Sensitization

- **Mechanism**: alpha2delta-1 subunit binding — blocks thrombospondin-mediated aberrant excitatory synaptogenesis
- **Anti-synaptogenic**: Prevents formation of new pathological excitatory connections (different from anti-kindling)
- **Cognitive**: Moderate impairment; concerning long-term dementia risk signals (29-40% increased risk at 6-12+ prescriptions)
- **ME/CFS**: Most directly studied — pregabalin FDA-approved for fibromyalgia; gabapentin survey (119 ME/CFS patients): 34 moderate/major improvement, 44 no effect, 39 side effects
- **Key concern**: Cognitive impairment + long-term dementia signals in a population already cognitively impaired

### Carbamazepine (Tegretol) — Classic Anti-Kindling

- **Mechanism**: Na+ channel (use-dependent)
- **Anti-kindling**: Strong (classic agent, especially withdrawal-induced)
- **Anti-inflammatory**: NO 77% reduction, PGE2 88% reduction in glial cells
- **Cognitive**: Moderate impairment; reduces hippocampal sharp wave-ripples by 40-65% (bad for memory consolidation)
- **ME/CFS fit**: LOW — cognitive burden outweighs benefits

### Topiramate (Topamax / "Dopamax") — Avoid in ME/CFS

- **Mechanism**: 6 distinct mechanisms (broadest profile)
- **Cognitive**: SEVERE impairment — 10% treatment-emergent cognitive events; word-finding, working memory, verbal fluency all impaired; nicknamed "Dopamax"
- **ME/CFS fit**: VERY LOW — would devastate already-impaired cognition
- **Only use case**: Comorbid migraine with weight management needs

### Oxcarbazepine (Trileptal) — Better-Tolerated Carbamazepine

- **Anti-inflammatory**: Microglia inhibition, Nrf2 pathway activation
- **Cognitive**: Comparable to carbamazepine but slightly better; fewer drug interactions
- **ME/CFS fit**: MODERATE — cleaner than carbamazepine if Na+ channel blocker needed

## 5. CROSS-CUTTING THEMES

### The Central Sensitization -> Rewiring Hypothesis in ME/CFS

ME/CFS involves multiple overlapping sensitization mechanisms:

1. **Microglial activation** -> chronic neuroinflammation
2. **Aberrant synaptogenesis** -> pathological excitatory connections (thrombospondin-driven)
3. **Glutamate excitotoxicity** -> neuronal damage
4. **Kindling** -> progressive lowering of symptom thresholds
5. **Epigenetic locking** -> maladaptive gene expression patterns
6. **Low T3** -> active gene repression + demyelination + mitochondrial failure

No single agent addresses all 6. A combination approach might target:

| Mechanism | Best agent(s) |
|---|---|
| Microglial activation | Levetiracetam, lithium, T3 |
| Aberrant synaptogenesis | Gabapentinoids |
| Glutamate excitotoxicity | Lamotrigine, lithium |
| Anti-kindling | Levetiracetam (strongest), lithium |
| Epigenetic reprogramming | Valproate (unique), lithium (partial) |
| Low T3 / demyelination | T3 supplementation |
| BDNF restoration | Lithium, lamotrigine, T3 |
| Autophagy / cellular cleanup | Lithium |

### Suppression vs. True Rewiring

Critical distinction:
- **Suppression**: Symptoms controlled while on drug; return when stopped
- **True circuit modification**: Persistent change even after drug withdrawal

Evidence for true rewiring:
- **Levetiracetam**: Anti-kindling effects persist after discontinuation
- **Valproate**: Critical period reopening (Gervain et al.) — learned abilities persist
- **Lithium**: Grey matter volume increases maintained; dementia risk reduction long-term
- **T3**: Myelination changes are structural and persist

### The Lithium + T3 Combination Hypothesis

Both agents address complementary ME/CFS mechanisms:
- **Lithium**: GSK-3beta inhibition, BDNF, autophagy, anti-inflammatory, grey matter
- **T3**: Myelination, mitochondrial biogenesis, deiodinase bypass, anti-neuroinflammation

Lithium's known effect of inhibiting thyroid function is a concern but also a design consideration — monitoring and T3 co-supplementation could be complementary rather than contradictory.

### Lithium + Valproate Synergy (Documented)

- Co-treatment produces complete blockade of glutamate-induced neuronal death
- ~70% GSK-3 inhibition (vs 40% lithium alone, 20% VPA alone)
- ~90% tau phosphorylation reduction
- Combined treatment in ALS mice delayed disease onset and prolonged survival
- BUT: VPA's cognitive side effects are a significant concern for ME/CFS

## 6. PRACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR ME/CFS

### Most Promising Agents (Ranked)

1. **T3 (triiodothyronine)** — Addresses documented pathology (low T3 syndrome); no RCTs but strongest mechanistic rationale; requires monitoring
2. **Lithium (low-dose, 40-45 mg/day)** — Long COVID trial data; neuroprotective at sub-therapeutic doses; lithium orotate available OTC
3. **Lamotrigine** — Cognitive-sparing neuroprotection; first-line for bipolar depression; no ME/CFS trials but strong theoretical fit
4. **Levetiracetam** — Best anti-kindling + anti-inflammatory combination; clean cognitive profile; behavioral side effects manageable
5. **Pregabalin** — FDA-approved for fibromyalgia; targets central sensitization; cognitive and dependency concerns

### Agents to Avoid in ME/CFS

1. **Topiramate** — Cognitive devastation
2. **High-dose valproate** — Working memory impairment + VIRCD risk
3. **Carbamazepine** — Cognitive burden from SWR reduction

### Monitoring Requirements

| Agent | Key monitoring |
|---|---|
| Lithium (even low-dose) | Serum lithium, TSH, creatinine |
| Valproate | LFTs, CBC, ammonia, drug level |
| T3 | Free T3, free T4, TSH, heart rate, bone density (long-term) |
| Lamotrigine | Rash monitoring (slow titration required for SJS prevention) |
| Levetiracetam | Behavioral monitoring |
| Gabapentinoids | Cognitive monitoring, dependency risk |

### Key Gaps / No RCTs Exist For

- T3 supplementation in ME/CFS
- Lithium (any dose) in ME/CFS (Long COVID trial is closest)
- Lamotrigine in ME/CFS
- Levetiracetam in ME/CFS
- Any combination approaches
