### 1. Blitshteyn, Doherty, Steinman 2026 — PRIMARY PAPER
**BibTeX key:** `Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune`
**Journal:** ImmunoTargets and Therapy (2026; 15:1–10)
**DOI:** 10.2147/ITT.S581262
**Certainty:** 0.85

**Design:** Narrative review framing POTS, ME/CFS, and Long COVID as neuroimmune disorders. 74 references.

**Key findings:**
- Argues these three conditions share common pathophysiologic mechanisms: autonomic dysfunction, immune dysregulation, autoimmunity, mitochondrial dysfunction, cerebral hypoperfusion, and neuroinflammation
- Proposes inclusion within neuroimmunology subspecialty for curriculum, training, and clinical care
- POTS and ME/CFS identified as most common phenotypes of Long COVID
- GPCR autoantibodies (adrenergic α1, β1/β2, muscarinic M2/M4) as shared autoimmune biomarker across all three conditions
- Vagus nerve / cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway dysfunction as common mechanism
- Dorsolateral inferior medulla neuroinflammation as possible CNS localization
- Immunomodulatory treatment overlap: IVIG, immunoadsorption, rituximab across conditions
- Autonomic ganglia as autoimmune targets (ganglionic AChR antibodies)
- Molecular mimicry triggered by SARS-CoV-2, EBV, and other pathogens

**Significance for our paper:** Provides the overarching "neuroimmune" framing that our document can adopt as an integrating theme across immune (ch07), neurological (ch08), POTS (ch10), and cross-disease (ch14d) chapters.

**Limitations:** Narrative review (not systematic); limited original data; authors have advocacy positions; some refs from pre-COVID era that may not generalize.

### 4. Gunning et al. 2019 — GPCR Autoantibodies in POTS
**BibTeX key:** `Gunning2019GPCRPOTS`
**Journal:** Journal of the American Heart Association (2019; 8:e013602)
**PMID:** 31495251
**DOI:** 10.1161/JAHA.119.013602
**Certainty:** 0.65

**Design:** Case-control. n=55 POTS patients + controls. ELISA for 9 GPCR autoantibody subtypes.

**Key findings:**
- 89% of POTS patients had elevated α1-adrenergic receptor autoantibodies
- 53% elevated M4 muscarinic receptor autoantibodies
- Elevation of muscarinic autoantibodies dependent on α1-adrenergic elevation
- 5/55 POTS patients had no autoantibody elevation (subgroup)
- Weak correlation of symptom severity with autoantibody levels
- Many patients reported viral-like symptoms preceding syncope

**Significance:** Foundational evidence for GPCR autoantibodies as biomarker in POTS; cited in Blitshteyn 2026 as key reference.

**Limitations:** Single-center; ELISA method (no functional confirmation); predominantly young females; no longitudinal follow-up.

### 5. Kharraziha et al. 2020 — GPCR Activity and Orthostatic Symptom Severity
**BibTeX key:** `Kharraziha2020GPCRseverity**
**Journal:** Journal of the American Heart Association (2020; 9:e015989)
**PMID:** 32750291
**DOI:** 10.1161/JAHA.120.015989
**Certainty:** 0.60

**Design:** Cross-sectional. n=65 POTS patients + controls. Functional GPCR antibody bioassay.

**Key findings:**
- Serum activity against GPCRs correlated with orthostatic symptom severity
- Supports functional (not just binding) role of GPCR autoantibodies
- Replicates in Swedish population (Lund cohort) the findings from Gunning et al.

**Significance:** Independent Swedish replication of GPCR autoantibody findings. Functional assay provides stronger evidence than ELISA alone.

**Limitations:** Cross-sectional; moderate sample size; single-center.

### 6. Fedorowski et al. 2017 — Antiadrenergic Autoimmunity in POTS
**BibTeX key:** `Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic`
**Journal:** Europace (2017; 19(7):1211–1219)
**PMID:** 27702852
**DOI:** 10.1093/europace/euw154
**Certainty:** 0.70

**Design:** Case-control with functional cell-based assays. n=17 POTS, 7 VVS, 11 controls. IgG functional analysis + rat arteriole contractility.

**Key findings:**
- IgG from POTS patients activated α1AR and β1/β2AR significantly more than controls
- 8/17 activated α1AR; 11/17 activated β1AR; 12/17 activated β2AR
- Pharmacological blockade suppressed IgG-induced activation
- POTS IgG contracted rat cremaster arterioles (reversed by α1AR blockade)
- Upright HR correlated with β1AR and α1AR IgG activity
- Allosteric shifts in receptor responsiveness proposed as mechanism

**Significance:** Strongest functional evidence for POTS IgG pathogenicity. Multi-center (Lund/Oklahoma). Directly supports autoimmune hypothesis.

**Limitations:** Small n=17 POTS; in vitro and animal model; single-sex not reported.

### 7. Li et al. 2014 — Autoimmune Basis for POTS (Oklahoma Group Landmark)
**BibTeX key:** `Li2014AutoimmunePOTS`
**Journal:** Journal of the American Heart Association (2014; 3:e000755)
**PMID:** 24572257
**DOI:** 10.1161/JAHA.113.000755
**Certainty:** 0.65

**Design:** Case-control. n=31 POTS patients + 24 controls. Cell-based functional assays + ELISA.

**Key findings:**
- Anti-β1/β2 adrenergic and anti-M2 muscarinic receptor autoantibodies in POTS
- Functional effects: activation of receptors in transfected cells
- First systematic demonstration of autoimmune basis in POTS

**Significance:** Landmark study that opened the field; widely cited (required for Blitshteyn 2026 reference list).

**Limitations:** Moderate sample; subsequent replication has been partial; some null results.

### 8. Li et al. 2019 — Adrenergic Autoantibody-Induced POTS in Rabbits

**Journal:** Journal of the American Heart Association (2019; 8:e013006)
**PMID:** 31547749
**DOI:** 10.1161/JAHA.119.013006
**Certainty:** 0.55

**Design:** Passive transfer animal model. Purified autoantibodies injected into rabbits.

**Key findings:**
- Adrenergic autoantibodies from POTS patients induced tachycardia in rabbits
- Orthostatic HR changes reproduced in animal model

**Significance:** Causal evidence from animal model. Cited in Blitshteyn 2026 as mechanistic evidence.

**Limitations:** Animal model (rabbit); small sample; may not fully recapitulate human POTS; single group.

### 9. Wallukat et al. 2021 — Functional GPCR Autoantibodies in Long COVID
**BibTeX key:** `Wallukat2021GPCRLongCOVID`
**Journal:** Journal of Translational Autoimmunity (2021; 4:100100)
**PMID:** 33880442
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jtauto.2021.100100
**Certainty:** 0.70

**Design:** Cross-sectional. n=75 Long COVID patients + controls. Bioassay for functional GPCR autoantibodies.

**Key findings:**
- Functional GPCR autoantibodies found in Long COVID: β2-adrenergic, M2 muscarinic, AT1 receptor
- Autoantibodies showed agonistic activity in neonatal rat cardiomyocyte bioassay
- Pattern different from healthy controls and disease controls

**Significance:** Landmark Long COVID autoimmunity paper. Bridges POTS autoimmunity to Long COVID. Cited in Blitshteyn 2026 as key reference for LC autoimmunity.

**Limitations:** Cross-sectional; no correlation with specific symptoms; bioassay complex.

### 10. Hohberger et al. 2021 — BC 007 Neutralization of GPCR Autoantibodies in Long COVID
**BibTeX key:** `Hohberger2021NeutralizationGPCR`
**Journal:** Frontiers in Medicine (2021; 8:754667)
**PMID:** 34869451
**DOI:** 10.3389/fmed.2021.754667
**Certainty:** 0.45

**Design:** Case report. Single patient with Long COVID/CFS treated with BC 007 (DNA aptamer neutralizing GPCR autoantibodies).

**Key findings:**
- Single BC 007 infusion functionally inactivated GPCR autoantibodies within 48h
- Fatigue symptoms improved; capillary microcirculation improved (retinal OCT-A)
- Effects sustained for 4-week observation period
- Taste restored

**Significance:** Proof-of-concept for therapeutic targeting of GPCR autoantibodies. Cited in Blitshteyn 2026 as immunomodulatory treatment evidence.

**Limitations:** n=1 case report; strong commercial conflict of interest (Berlin Cures developed BC 007); no control; short follow-up; not replicated.

## Cross-Paper Synthesis

**Core thesis (convergent evidence):**
1. POTS, ME/CFS, and Long COVID share overlapping neuroimmune pathophysiology (Blitshteyn 2026; Moen 2025; El-Rhermoul 2023)
2. GPCR autoantibodies (α1, β1/β2, M2/M4) found across all three conditions (Gunning 2019; Fedorowski 2017; Wallukat 2021; Kharraziha 2020; Li 2014)
3. Functional evidence for IgG pathogenicity: cell-based activation, animal model passive transfer (Fedorowski 2017; Li 2019)
4. Brainstem neuroinflammation as shared CNS substrate (Blitshteyn 2025; Wagoner 2019)
5. Neuroimmune axis via vagus nerve / cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway
6. Genetic predisposition via HLA associations (Shin 2019)
7. Clinical autoimmune comorbidity elevated across all three (Blitshteyn 2015)
8. Immunomodulatory treatments show benefit (case series; RCTs pending)
9. All three conditions can be triggered by identical pathogens (SARS-CoV-2, EBV, other viruses) → shared post-infectious autoimmune mechanism

**Key open questions:**
- GPCR autoantibody detection not standardized across labs
- Causal role vs. epiphenomenon debated for some antibody types
- Germain 2025 null result (ME/CFS n=172) found NO GPCR autoantibody signal using Luminex/REAP — may reflect assay sensitivity
- Large-scale RCTs for immunomodulation not yet completed
- Whether brainstem neuroinflammation is primary or secondary
- Subgroup stratification unclear

**Null/negative findings in this space:**
- Li 2014 (n=31 POTS): 5/31 POTS patients did not have GPCR autoantibodies (15% null subgroup)
- Gunning 2019 (n=55): 5/55 POTS patients had no elevation of any autoantibody (9% null subgroup)
- No dedicated "POTS autoantibody negative replication" study found (query returned zero results)
- No "POTS small fiber neuropathy autoantibody" link study found (query returned zero results)
- Germain 2025: null for GPCR autoantibodies in ME/CFS using different assay (Luminex vs. ELISA)

**Certainty assessment across the cluster:**
- Autoimmunity in POTS: moderate-high (0.65–0.70) — multiple independent groups, functional validation, animal model
- Autoimmunity in ME/CFS: moderate (0.50–0.60) — less consistent, Germain null result
- Autoimmunity in Long COVID: moderate (0.60–0.70) — Wallukat 2021 strong but cross-sectional
- Brainstem neuroinflammation: low-moderate (0.45–0.60) — limited direct evidence
- Clinical neuroimmune overlap between the three: high (0.80) — epidemiological + clinical convergence
