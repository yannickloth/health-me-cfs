# Literature Summary: Oral Micronized Progesterone as Therapeutic Agent in ME/CFS
**Date:** 2026-04-25
**Research stream:** `progesterone-mecfs`

---

## Hypothesis

Oral micronized progesterone (Prometrium/Utrogestan, 100--200 mg/day) may replicate the
pregnancy-associated ME/CFS improvement observed in ~30% of patients (Schacterle &
Komaroff 2004) via three mechanisms:

1. **Th2 immunoshift / Treg elevation** — via progesterone-induced blocking factor (PIBF)
2. **Allopregnanolone production** — GABA-A potentiation leading to HPA calming, sleep
   architecture improvement, and autonomic stabilisation
3. **Anti-inflammatory signalling** — via progesterone receptor (PR) and glucocorticoid
   receptor (GR) cross-activation

Importantly: ME/CFS is NOT a progesterone-deficiency state (Gur 2004, Cevik 2004 — see
null results below). The rationale is pharmacological/supraphysiological, not replacement.

## Papers Found and Added to bib

### 1. MeltzerBrody2018brexanolone
- **PMID:** 30177236
- **Journal:** Lancet (2018) — high-impact peer-reviewed
- **Design:** Two phase 3 double-blind RCTs; n=246 combined
- **Certainty:** 0.82
- **Relevance:** Proof-of-concept for allopregnanolone route. Brexanolone (synthetic IV
  allopregnanolone) produces rapid, clinically significant effects on a neuroendocrine
  disorder via GABA-A potentiation. The mechanism chain
  oral progesterone → allopregnanolone → GABA-A agonism is pharmacologically plausible
  based on this trial.
- **Key caveat:** IV not oral; PPD is not ME/CFS; 30-day follow-up only.

### 2. MeltzerBrodyKanes2020allopregnanolone
- **PMID:** 32435663
- **Journal:** Neurobiology of Stress (2020)
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Certainty:** 0.52
- **Relevance:** Documents allopregnanolone's HPA-calming and anti-inflammatory effects.
  HPA axis dysregulation (hypocortisolism, blunted ACTH response) is a core ME/CFS
  feature; allopregnanolone's suppression of CRH/ACTH may address this.
- **Key addition:** Brexanolone inhibits inflammatory signalling post-infusion — raises
  possibility of immune benefit beyond neurosteroid/GABA effects.

### 3. SzekeresBartho2019progestogens
- **PMID:** 31345741
- **Journal:** Best Practice & Research Clinical Obstetrics & Gynaecology (2019)
- **Design:** Authoritative review by leading PIBF researcher
- **Certainty:** 0.58
- **Relevance:** Core mechanistic paper for the immune hypothesis. PIBF mediates:
  (a) Th2 cytokine increase (IL-4, IL-6, IL-10), (b) NK cell degranulation inhibition.
  The ME/CFS connection: elevated Th1 and NK hyperactivity are documented features;
  PIBF-mediated reversal would be expected to reduce immune-driven symptoms.
- **Key nuance:** Natural progesterone and dydrogesterone induce PIBF; MPA does not.
  Therefore micronized progesterone specifically is required, not synthetic progestins.

### 4. Raghupathy2009PIBF
- **PMID:** 19371956
- **Journal:** Journal of Reproductive Immunology (2009)
- **Design:** In vitro, n=72 women (4 groups)
- **Certainty:** 0.55
- **Relevance:** Experimental evidence for PIBF-driven Th2 shift. IL-4, IL-6, IL-10
  significantly increased by PIBF in pregnancy groups. Effect absent in non-pregnant
  controls — raises the unresolved question whether ME/CFS patients would respond.
- **Key concern:** Non-pregnant controls unresponsive; mechanism may require pregnancy-
  associated immune sensitisation.

### 5. Hierweger2019progesteroneT
- **PMID:** 30604567
- **Journal:** American Journal of Reproductive Immunology (2019)
- **Design:** In vitro murine study
- **Certainty:** 0.38
- **Relevance:** Second mechanism for Treg enrichment: progesterone cross-activates
  glucocorticoid receptor (GR), selectively inducing effector T-cell apoptosis while
  sparing Tregs. This is pharmacologically important: at supraphysiological doses
  (as with 100--200 mg oral micronized progesterone), GR cross-activation may provide
  immunosuppression independent of PIBF/PR pathway.
- **Key concern:** Murine in vitro; requires pharmacological concentrations.

### 6. AndreenBackstrom2006pharmacokinetics
- **PMID:** 16406399
- **Journal:** Maturitas (2006)
- **Design:** Open-label PK study, n=8 postmenopausal women
- **Certainty:** 0.42
- **Relevance:** Pharmacokinetic foundation for the hypothesis. Oral 20 mg progesterone
  twice daily produces allopregnanolone at luteal-phase equivalent concentrations.
  Confirms the prodrug mechanism of oral micronized progesterone. Clinical doses of
  100--200 mg would produce 5--10x higher allopregnanolone levels.
- **Key concern:** Only 20 mg studied; n=8 postmenopausal only; extrapolation to clinical
  doses has uncertainty.

### 7. Zhao2012continuousProgesterone
- **PMID:** 22393359
- **Journal:** PLoS One (2012)
- **Design:** Animal study (ovariectomized rats)
- **Certainty:** 0.38
- **Relevance:** Critical safety and efficacy concern. Continuous progesterone exposure
  downregulates progesterone receptors and abolishes neuroprotective gene expression
  profiles, mimicking hormone-depleted conditions. Cyclic progesterone preserved
  benefits. Implies that continuous daily oral progesterone may lose efficacy over time;
  cyclic dosing is pharmacologically preferable.
- **Key concern:** Rodent only; no immune endpoints; translational relevance uncertain.

### 8. AndreenBackstrom2009negMood
- **PMID:** 19272715
- **Journal:** Psychoneuroendocrinology (2009)
- **Design:** Review and human experimental analysis
- **Certainty:** 0.45
- **Relevance:** Core safety signal. Low allopregnanolone concentrations (as may occur
  with low-dose or poorly-absorbed oral progesterone) paradoxically worsen anxiety via
  altered GABA-A receptor subunit composition. Women with PMDD are particularly
  vulnerable. This is the most important adverse effect concern for the ME/CFS
  hypothesis.
- **Clinical implication:** Patients must be titrated carefully; start low; monitor for
  paradoxical worsening.

### 9. Gur2004fibromyalgiaHormones — NULL RESULT
- **PMID:** 15142269
- **Journal:** Arthritis Research & Therapy (2004)
- **Design:** Cross-sectional, n=176 (46 controls, 68 fibromyalgia, 62 CFS)
- **Certainty:** 0.55
- **Relevance:** Null result: no progesterone differences in CFS. Supports pharmacological
  (not replacement) rationale. Also: lower cortisol confirms HPA hypocortisolism in CFS.

### 10. Cevik2004CFShormones — NULL RESULT (REPLICATION)
- **PMID:** 15588275
- **Journal:** BMC Musculoskeletal Disorders (2004)
- **Design:** Case-control, n=43 CFS + 35 controls; both menstrual phases sampled
- **Certainty:** 0.52
- **Relevance:** Replication of Gur 2004 null result. Importantly, luteal-phase
  progesterone also normal, ruling out luteal-phase deficiency. Two independent studies
  confirm baseline HPG axis intact.

## Evidence Gaps (Not Found)

1. **No RCT of progesterone in ME/CFS or fibromyalgia.** No trial retrieved. This is
   the most critical evidence gap. The hypothesis remains at the mechanistic/speculative
   stage.
2. **No oral progesterone + sleep architecture RCT.** The Friess-type progesterone +
   polysomnography studies were not retrievable in PubMed searches; the classic Friess
   et al. (1997) paper (Psychopharmacology 133:309) may pre-date indexing in these
   searches. This should be retrieved separately if sleep architecture effects need
   primary citation.
3. **No long-term safety data for continuous oral progesterone in immune-dysregulated
   populations.** Zhao 2012 (animal) raises receptor downregulation concern; no human
   equivalent.
4. **PIBF responsiveness in non-pregnant women with immune dysregulation.** The Raghupathy
   2009 null result in non-pregnant controls is the critical gap: unknown whether ME/CFS
   patients would produce sufficient PIBF in response to oral progesterone.
