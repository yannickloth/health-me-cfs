### 1. Zametkin1990 — Cerebral Glucose Metabolism in Adults with ADHD

**Authors:** Zametkin AJ, Nordahl TE, Gross M, King AC, Semple WE, Rumsey J, Hamburger S, Cohen RM
**Title:** Cerebral glucose metabolism in adults with hyperactivity of childhood onset
**Journal:** New England Journal of Medicine
**Year:** 1990
**Volume/Issue:** 323(20)
**Pages:** 1361–1366
**DOI:** 10.1056/NEJM199011153232001
**PMID:** 2233902
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2233902/

**Study design:** PET [18F]FDG imaging, n=25 hyperactive adults vs n=50 controls; auditory-attention task

**Key findings:**
- Global cerebral glucose metabolism 8.1% lower in ADHD adults than controls
- Largest reductions in premotor cortex and superior prefrontal cortex
- Significantly reduced in 30 of 60 brain regions examined
- Prefrontal hypometabolism concentrated in regions controlling attention and motor activity

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: High (NEJM landmark study, seminal PET methodology)
- Sample size: n=25 ADHD + n=50 controls
- Replication: Extensively replicated and extended over 30 years
- COI: No commercial conflicts; NIH-funded
- Limitations: Adults only; task-state PET; no resting-state measure; pre-modern ADHD diagnostic criteria

**Certainty rating:** 0.80 — High. Foundational PET study. Replicated conceptually by subsequent SPECT and fMRI work.

**Relevance to ME/CFS predisposition hypothesis:**
Establishes that ADHD brains operate with reduced prefrontal glucose metabolism at baseline — a metabolic reserve deficit that could lower the threshold at which infection-triggered neuroinflammation tips into sustained hypometabolism (ME/CFS phenotype).

### 2. Quadt2024 — Childhood Neurodivergent Traits, Inflammation and CDF (ALSPAC 2024)

**Authors:** Quadt L, Csecs J, Bond R, and colleagues
**Title:** Childhood neurodivergent traits, inflammation and chronic disabling fatigue in adolescence: a longitudinal case-control study
**Journal:** BMJ Open
**Year:** 2024
**Volume:** 14(7)
**Article:** e084203
**DOI:** 10.1136/bmjopen-2024-084203
**PMID:** 39038862
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39038862/

**Study design:** ALSPAC longitudinal case-control; children assessed ages 7 and 9 (n=8,036–8,115); self-report at age 18 (n=4,563); mediation analysis for IL-6

**Key findings:**
- ADHD traits at age 7–9 → OR=2.18 (95% CI 1.33–3.56, p=0.002) for chronic disabling fatigue at age 18
- Autism traits at age 7–9 → OR=1.78 (95% CI 1.17–2.72, p=0.004)
- IL-6 at age 9 mediated the relationship (indirect path significant, independent of depressive symptoms)
- Authors recommend transdiagnostic screening during childhood

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: High (large prospective birth cohort, mediation analysis, pre-registered)
- Sample size: n>4,500 at follow-up
- Replication: Replicates and extends Norris 2017 (same ALSPAC cohort, different timepoints)
- COI: Not apparent; UK academic funding
- Limitations: Screening instruments (not clinical diagnoses); fatigue outcomes rely on self-report; "chronic disabling fatigue" may not meet full ME/CFS criteria; IL-6 measured only at one timepoint

**Certainty rating:** 0.75 — High. Large sample, longitudinal, mediation analysis. Caution: chronic disabling fatigue outcome is not strictly ME/CFS.

**Relevance:** Strongest prospective evidence that neurodivergent traits predict adolescent fatigue, with IL-6 as a plausible mediating mechanism.

### 3. SaezFrancas2012adhdcfs — ADHD Prevalence in Adult CFS Patients

**Authors:** Sáez-Francàs N, Alegre J, Calvo N, and colleagues
**Title:** Attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder in chronic fatigue syndrome patients
**Journal:** Psychiatry Research
**Year:** 2012
**Volume:** 200(2-3)
**Pages:** 748–753
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.psychres.2012.04.041
**PMID:** 22648008
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22648008/

**Study design:** Cross-sectional; 158 adult CFS patients; structured ADHD assessment

**Key findings:**
- 29.7% (47/158) of CFS patients had childhood ADHD; 20.9% (33/158) persisted into adulthood
- CFS patients with comorbid adult ADHD: earlier CFS onset, more anxiety/depression, higher suicide risk
- Depressive symptoms and ADHD severity were independent significant predictors of fatigue intensity

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: Medium (single-centre cross-sectional; structured diagnostic assessment is strength)
- Sample size: n=158
- Replication: Partially replicated by other single-centre studies (similar prevalence range 21–30%)
- COI: Not apparent
- Limitations: Cross-sectional; recall bias for childhood ADHD; single specialist CFS clinic (selection bias); Oxford CFS criteria used

**Certainty rating:** 0.55 — Medium. Consistent with multiple similar studies but no large multicentre replication.

**Relevance:** Primary source for the ~30% childhood ADHD prevalence figure in CFS; note that bib entry `Rimes2015adhdcfs` (key misnamed) cites this or similar data.

### 4. Yokokura2021 — In Vivo D1R and Microglial Imaging in ADHD (PET)

**Authors:** Yokokura M, Takedera K, Kazumata K, and colleagues
**Title:** In vivo imaging of dopamine D1 receptor and activated microglia in attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder: a positron emission tomography study
**Journal:** Molecular Psychiatry
**Year:** 2021
**Volume:** 26(9)
**Pages:** 4958–4967
**DOI:** 10.1038/s41380-020-0784-1
**PMID:** 32439845
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32439845/

**Study design:** PET imaging (D1R tracer + activated microglia tracer); n=24 ADHD vs n=24 controls; drug-naive participants

**Key findings:**
- Decreased D1R availability in anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) in ADHD; correlated with hyperactivity severity
- Increased activated microglia in dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) and orbitofrontal cortex
- In ADHD only: positive correlation between D1R reduction and microglial activation
- Microglial activation in DLPFC associated with processing speed deficits and attention impairment

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: High (dual-tracer PET, drug-naive participants, matched controls)
- Sample size: n=24 per group (adequate for PET)
- Replication: Single study; convergent with other PET data on D2/D3 and DAT
- COI: No apparent conflicts; Japanese government-funded
- Limitations: n=24 per group; ADHD diagnosis in adults; cross-sectional; cannot determine causality

**Certainty rating:** 0.62 — Medium-High. Mechanistically important: co-imaging of dopaminergic deficit and neuroinflammation in same subjects. Replication needed.

**Relevance:** Directly bridges ADHD dopaminergic deficit and neuroinflammation in one study — connects the shared ME/CFS–ADHD pathway through both catecholamine depletion and microglial activation.

### 5. Moffitt2015adhdadult — Is Adult ADHD a Childhood-Onset Disorder? (Dunedin)

**Authors:** Moffitt TE, Houts R, Asherson P, Belsky DW, Corcoran DL, and colleagues
**Title:** Is adult ADHD a childhood-onset neurodevelopmental disorder? Evidence from a four-decade longitudinal cohort study
**Journal:** American Journal of Psychiatry
**Year:** 2015
**Volume:** 172(10)
**Pages:** 967–977
**DOI:** 10.1176/appi.ajp.2015.14101266
**PMID:** 25998281
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25998281/

**Study design:** Dunedin birth cohort, n=1,037, followed birth to age 38 (95% retention); childhood ADHD assessed by parents/teachers; adult ADHD assessed at age 38

**Key findings:**
- 90% of adult ADHD cases (age 38) had no childhood ADHD diagnosis
- Only 5% of childhood ADHD cases still met criteria at age 38
- Adult-onset group showed: no neuropsychological deficits in childhood or adulthood, no polygenic risk for childhood ADHD
- Childhood and adult ADHD are "virtually non-overlapping sets"
- Authors suggest adult ADHD may be a distinct condition

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: High (gold-standard longitudinal cohort, 95% retention, 40 years, multi-informant)
- Sample size: n=1,037 (whole population cohort)
- Replication: Challenged by other cohorts (some show higher continuity); ongoing controversy
- COI: No apparent conflicts; Medical Research Council, NZ HRC
- Limitations: Single birth cohort (NZ 1972-73); adult ADHD diagnosed only at age 38 (may miss peak onset); stimulant-treated childhood cases may show suppression of adult symptoms

**Certainty rating:** 0.70 — High within its methodology, but findings remain contested in the field.

**Relevance:** Critical for the predisposition hypothesis: if "adult ADHD" is biologically distinct from childhood ADHD, some cases may represent acquired catecholaminergic depletion — compatible with post-infectious ME/CFS trajectory. Also raises the concern that ME/CFS patients with apparent "adult-onset ADHD" may not have pre-existing neurodevelopmental risk.

### 6. Williams2025GCH1 — GCH1 Deficiency, BH4, and Mental Health

**Authors:** Williams GE, Hausman-Cohen S, Sotos M, Gutierrez E, Bilich C, Mueller FW, Jagshi S
**Title:** The role of GCH1 deficiency and tetrahydrobiopterin in mental health
**Journal:** International Journal of Molecular Sciences
**Year:** 2025
**Volume:** 26(16)
**Article:** 8030
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijms26168030
**PMID:** 40869351
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40869351/

**Study design:** Narrative review with case reports; genomic analysis of GCH1 variants; clinical series

**Key findings:**
- GCH1 rs841 homozygous variant associated with ADHD traits including impaired sustained attention and vigilance
- GCH1 is rate-limiting enzyme for BH4 synthesis; BH4 is essential cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase (dopamine/NE), tryptophan hydroxylase (serotonin), and nitric oxide synthases
- Five case reports: ADHD behaviour improved with low-dose BH4 supplementation (0.088–0.292 mg/kg/day)
- Partial BH4 deficiency may contribute to depression, ADHD, anxiety, PMDD, insomnia, autism traits

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: Low-Medium (narrative review + case series; not a clinical trial)
- Sample size: n=5 case reports for BH4 supplementation
- Replication: Consistent with separate GCH1 genetic studies but case series evidence is weak
- COI: Some authors affiliated with genomic testing companies; potential commercial bias
- Limitations: Narrative not systematic; case reports only for intervention; no placebo arm; genetic association ≠ causation

**Certainty rating:** 0.40 — Medium-Low. Mechanism is biologically sound; GCH1 genetic data is convergent; but clinical case evidence is anecdotal.

**Relevance:** Provides the mechanistic link: GCH1 variants → reduced BH4 → impaired catecholamine synthesis → ADHD phenotype. BH4 is already cited in ME/CFS (low BH4 in orthostatic intolerance). Shared BH4 bottleneck could explain why ADHD and ME/CFS co-occur.

### 7. Berthier2025cbfADHD — Cerebral Blood Flow in ADHD (Systematic Review)

**Authors:** Berthier J, Endomba FT, Lecendreux M, and colleagues
**Title:** Cerebral blood flow in attention deficit hyperactivity disorder: A systematic review
**Journal:** Neuroscience
**Year:** 2025
**Volume:** 567
**Pages:** 67–76
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.neuroscience.2024.11.075
**PMID:** 39631658 (confirmed)
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39631658/

**Study design:** Systematic review; 20 studies, n=1,652 ADHD participants + n=580 controls; SPECT, ASL, PET, and BOLD-derived measures

**Key findings:**
- Resting state: hypoperfusion in right orbitofrontal gyrus, temporal cortex, basal ganglia, putamen
- Concurrent hyperperfusion in frontal, postcentral, and occipital regions (inconsistent pattern)
- During cognitive tasks: hyperperfusion in frontal, temporal, cingulate, precuneus
- Methylphenidate normalises striatal, thalamic, and precentral blood flow

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: Medium-High (systematic review, but underlying studies use heterogeneous methods)
- Sample size: n=1,652 ADHD across 20 studies
- Replication: Consistent across studies for prefrontal/striatal hypoperfusion; specific region findings vary
- COI: Not apparent
- Limitations: High heterogeneity in imaging methods; most studies are small; medication status varies across studies

**Certainty rating:** 0.62 — Medium-High for the core finding (hypoperfusion in prefrontal/striatal regions); weaker for specific region claims.

**Relevance:** Converges with ME/CFS reduced cerebral blood flow data; ADHD baseline CBF deficit may represent shared vascular-metabolic vulnerability.

### 8. Almutairi2024mitoadhd — Mitochondrial Dysfunction and Mitophagy in ADHD

**Authors:** Almutairi MM, Althekair A, Almutairi F, Alatabani M, Alsaikhan A
**Title:** Mitochondrial dysfunction and mitophagy in ADHD: Cellular and molecular mechanisms
**Journal:** Saudi Pharmaceutical Journal
**Year:** 2024
**Volume:** 32(12)
**Article:** 102212
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.jsps.2024.102212
**PMC:** PMC11664080
**URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11664080/

**Study design:** Narrative review of cellular/molecular mechanisms

**Key findings:**
- Decreased mitochondrial membrane potential and impaired oxidative phosphorylation documented in ADHD
- Genetic variants in NDUFAF2 (Complex I assembly), UCP2, and mitochondrial organization genes (Prps18c, Letm1, Uqcrc2) correlated with reduced respiratory capacity
- Methylphenidate maintains mitochondrial homeostasis (increases Parkin, reduces ROS)
- Interaction between monoaminergic gene variants (DAT, DRD4, SERT, NET) and mitochondrial dysfunction

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: Low-Medium (narrative review; source quality is mixed preclinical/human)
- Sample size: Review (preclinical studies dominate)
- Replication: Convergent with other reviews but limited human studies
- COI: No apparent commercial conflicts; Saudi government-funded
- Limitations: Mostly animal models; mechanistic claims vary in evidence quality; no systematic methodology stated

**Certainty rating:** 0.35 — Low-Medium. Mechanism is plausible; human data is thin; serves as hypothesis-generating background.

**Relevance:** Establishes mitochondrial energy failure as a plausible ADHD mechanism — parallel to ME/CFS mitochondrial dysfunction literature.

### 9. Fanet2021BH4neuro — BH4 Pathway: From Metabolism to Neuropsychiatry

**Authors:** Fanet H, Capuron L, Castanon N, Calon F, Vancassel S
**Title:** Tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) pathway: from metabolism to neuropsychiatry
**Journal:** Current Neuropharmacology
**Year:** 2021
**Volume:** 19(5)
**Pages:** 591–609
**DOI:** 10.2174/1570159X18666200729103529
**PMID:** 32744952
**URL:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32744952/

**Study design:** Narrative review

**Key findings:**
- BH4 is cofactor for TH (dopamine/NE), TPH (serotonin), and all three NOS isoforms
- Inflammatory cytokines increase BH4 synthesis but paradoxically increase oxidation → net depletion of monoamines
- Reduced CSF BH4 associated with depression, Parkinson's, Alzheimer's, ADHD, autism (cited as association)
- BH4 has anti-inflammatory and anti-oxidant roles beyond neurotransmitter synthesis

**Quality assessment:**
- Study quality: Medium (narrative review; published in established journal)
- Sample size: Review
- Replication: Findings consistent with consensus on BH4 biochemistry
- COI: Not apparent
- Limitations: Does not specifically review ADHD clinical evidence; ADHD cited briefly

**Certainty rating:** 0.50 — Medium. Core biochemistry is well-established; neuropsychiatric associations are cited but not systematically reviewed.

**Relevance:** Mechanistic bridge: inflammation → BH4 oxidation → dopamine/NE depletion → both ADHD-like and ME/CFS symptoms. Also relevant to ME/CFS orthostatic intolerance BH4 papers already in bib.

## Negative / Null Results and Caveats

1. **Moffitt 2015 itself as a caveat:** If 90% of adult ADHD is not childhood-onset neurodevelopmental, then the "ADHD predisposes to ME/CFS" hypothesis must be stated carefully — it may apply to childhood-onset ADHD with documented metabolic reserve deficit, not to adult-onset ADHD broadly.

2. **No direct proof of causal chain:** No study has prospectively shown that pre-ADHD neurobiology directly causes ME/CFS. All evidence is epidemiological (association) or mechanistic inference. The Quadt 2024 mediation through IL-6 is the closest, but IL-6 is a general inflammatory marker.

3. **Alternative interpretation of comorbidity:** High ADHD prevalence in ME/CFS cohorts (30%) may partly reflect diagnostic confusion (cognitive symptoms of ME/CFS misattributed to ADHD) rather than pre-existing ADHD.

4. **Mitochondrial evidence in ADHD:** Almost entirely preclinical (animal models); human mitochondrial studies in ADHD are limited and heterogeneous.

5. **BH4 in ADHD:** GCH1 genetic association is weak (rs841 single SNP); BH4 supplementation evidence is anecdotal (n=5 case reports).
