## Paper 1: H2S Mechanism — Complex IV Inhibition

**Citation key:** `Nicholls2013sulfideCOX`
**Authors:** Nicholls P, Marshall DC, Cooper CE, Wilson MT
**Journal:** Biochemical Society Transactions, 2013; 41(5):1312–1316
**DOI:** 10.1042/BST20130070 | **PMID:** 24059525

**Key findings:**
- Mitochondrial cytochrome c oxidase inhibited by sulfide in a **biphasic** pattern: at least two successive inhibited enzyme states
- Primary target: binuclear center (CuB + heme a3) — same site as cyanide
- Inhibition is **partially reversible**: sulfide oxidation by the enzyme itself can recover activity
- At low H2S: functions as electron donor to ETC (substrate for Complex II via SQOR)
- At ~3–30× higher concentration: switches to Complex IV inhibitor
- IC50 for bacterial bo3 oxidase: ~1.1 μM — eukaryotic Complex IV similar range

**Quality:** High — peer-reviewed, mechanistic biochemistry, foundational reference
**Certainty:** 0.90 (well-established mechanism; concentrations in human gut uncertain)
**Sample size:** Enzyme kinetics (in vitro) — no human cohort

## Paper 2: H2S Bioenergetics — Dual Role at Mitochondria and Bacteria

**Citation key:** `Borisov2021h2sMito`
**Authors:** Borisov VB, Forte E
**Journal:** International Journal of Molecular Sciences, 2021; 22(23):12688
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijms222312688 | **PMID:** 34884491

**Key findings:**
- At low concentrations: H2S enhances FoF1-ATP synthase and lactate dehydrogenase via S-sulfhydration; stimulates ETC
- At higher concentrations: inhibits Complex IV — both cytochrome c oxidase (eukaryotic) and bo3 terminal oxidase (bacterial)
- Bacteria evade H2S toxicity via alternative terminal oxidases (cytochrome bd) not inhibited by sulfide — explains why H2S-producing gut bacteria survive their own toxin
- Dose-response: low μM = beneficial → mid μM = Complex IV inhibition → high μM = ATP collapse
- Cellular energy depletion confirmed as primary cytotoxic mechanism at high H2S

**Quality:** High — peer-reviewed review, comprehensive mechanistic coverage
**Certainty:** 0.85
**Sample size:** Review — no cohort

## Paper 3: SQOR — The Mitochondrial H2S Clearance Enzyme

**Citation key:** `Landry2020SQOR`
**Authors:** Landry AP, Ballou DP, Banerjee R
**Journal:** ChemBioChem, 2021 (epub 2020); 22(6):949–960
**DOI:** 10.1002/cbic.202000661 | **PMID:** 33080111

**Key findings:**
- SQOR (sulfide quinone oxidoreductase) is the committed step in mitochondrial H2S clearance
- Couples H2S oxidation to coenzyme Q10 reduction — directly links H2S detox to ETC
- Generates glutathione persulfide (GSSH) as reactive intermediate — feeds into ETHE1 → sulfite oxidase → sulfate pathway
- Unusually flexible active site: can accommodate multiple persulfide acceptors
- **Therapeutic relevance:** SQOR positioned as drug target for H2S-related pathology
- CoQ10 deficiency (relevant to ME/CFS hypotheses) directly impairs SQOR function

**Quality:** High — peer-reviewed, structural/kinetic characterization
**Certainty:** 0.85
**Sample size:** Biochemical (enzyme) — no cohort

## Paper 4: SQOR 2025 — Metabolic Rheostat Framework

**Citation key:** `Peng2025SQORrheostat`
**Authors:** Peng MH, Zhang KL, Ma ZW, Zhang HW, Guan SW, Yu HB
**Journal:** Frontiers in Cell and Developmental Biology, 2025; 13
**DOI:** 10.3389/fcell.2025.1685252 | **PMID:** not yet indexed (2025 epub)

**Key findings:**
- SQOR acts as "metabolic rheostat" — dynamically tunes sulfide flux and redox balance in real time
- Body maintains H2S in narrow steady-state window; SQOR is the primary set-point enzyme
- SQOR deficiency → "severe metabolic crises" (Leigh syndrome phenotype in mice)
- Disease associations: neurological (Leigh), cardiovascular (ischemia-reperfusion), cancer (ferroptosis), metabolic (diabetic nephropathy)
- When SQOR is overwhelmed by excess gut-derived H2S → spillover inhibits Complex IV
- **Critical connection to ME/CFS:** CoQ10 deficiency (documented in some ME/CFS studies) would impair SQOR — creating H2S accumulation even at normal gut production

**Quality:** Medium-High — 2025 review, comprehensive but recent
**Certainty:** 0.70
**Sample size:** Review

## Paper 5: Liver Portal Clearance of H2S

**Citation key:** `Norris2011liverH2S`
**Authors:** Norris EJ, Culberson CR, Narasimhan S, Clemens MG
**Journal:** Shock, 2011; 36(3):242–250
**DOI:** 10.1097/SHK.0b013e3182252ee7 | **PMID:** 21617578

**Key findings:**
- Liver is the primary organ for H2S clearance from portal circulation
- Isolated perfused liver clears H2S up to 200 μM infusion concentration with >97% efficiency when oxygenated
- Clearance is **oxygen-dependent**: removing O2 drops clearance from 97% to 23%
- H2S infusion increases NADH/NAD+ ratio → reduces hepatic oxygen availability (feedback loop)
- Septic animal livers maintained H2S-metabolizing capacity
- Major hepatic oxidation products: **sulfate** (primary) and thiosulfate (intermediate)
- **Implication for ME/CFS:** Any condition reducing hepatic oxygenation (orthostatic hypoperfusion, cardiac output reduction) could impair portal H2S clearance → increased systemic H2S spillover

**Quality:** High — experimental, isolated perfused liver model, quantified
**Certainty:** 0.80 (animal/ex vivo; human translation uncertain)
**Sample size:** Animal ex vivo — rat livers

## Paper 6: Sulfidogenic Bacteria in Human Disease (Review)

**Citation key:** `Pimenta2024sulfidogenic`
**Authors:** Pimenta AI, Bernardino RM, Pereira IAC
**Journal:** Advances in Microbial Physiology, 2024; 85:145–200
**DOI:** 10.1016/bs.ampbs.2024.04.003 | **PMID:** 39059820

**Key findings:**
- Desulfovibrio spp. and Bilophila wadsworthia are the principal sulfidogenic bacteria in human gut
- Western diet (high animal protein, fat, sugar) promotes their overgrowth via increased luminal sulfate and taurine availability
- B. wadsworthia specifically: demonstrates pathogenic properties — epithelial invasion, LPS-mediated inflammation, membrane vesicle production
- At high concentrations, H2S produced by these organisms disrupts intestinal epithelium and mucus barrier → "leaky gut"
- Connects to IBD (Crohn's, UC) and colorectal cancer — both have elevated Desulfovibrio
- **ME/CFS relevance:** ME/CFS microbiome studies show elevated Proteobacteria/Enterobacteriaceae; sulfidogenic bacteria not specifically measured in ME/CFS but the dietary/dysbiosis context is similar

**Quality:** High — peer-reviewed comprehensive review, 2024
**Certainty:** 0.75 (disease associations for IBD strong; ME/CFS speculative)
**Sample size:** Review

## Paper 7: Desulfovibrio H2S Suppresses GLP-1 via Mitochondrial Inhibition

**Citation key:** `Qi2024DesulfovibrioGLP1`
**Authors:** Qi Q, Zhang H, Jin Z et al.
**Journal:** Nature Metabolism, 2024; 6(8):1601–1615
**DOI:** 10.1038/s42255-024-01068-x | **PMID:** 39030389

**Key findings:**
- **Highest-priority paper:** directly demonstrates gut-derived H2S from Desulfovibrio inhibiting mitochondria in intestinal L cells
- Mechanism: Desulfovibrio H2S → inhibits mitochondrial respiration in intestinal L cells → induces unfolded protein response (UPR) → suppresses GLP-1 secretion
- Desulfovibrio enriched in patients with metabolic syndrome (human data)
- Bismuth subsalicylate (H2S chelator) rescues GLP-1 production and ameliorates diet-induced metabolic disorder in male mice
- **Critical bridge paper:** proves gut-to-mitochondria H2S toxicity axis is real and measurable
- GLP-1 suppression link: relevant to post-COVID metabolic dysregulation and satiety disturbances reported in ME/CFS

**Quality:** High — Nature Metabolism, in vivo animal + human correlation
**Certainty:** 0.75 (murine model; human mechanism inferred)
**Sample size:** Murine + human metabolic syndrome cohort (size not specified in abstract)

## Paper 8: H2S Producers → IBS-D Subtype (Human Microbiome)

**Citation key:** `VillanuevaMillan2022IBSsubtypes`
**Authors:** Villanueva-Millan MJ, Leite G, Wang J, Morales W et al.
**Journal:** American Journal of Gastroenterology, 2022; 117(12):2055–2066
**DOI:** 10.14309/ajg.0000000000001997 | **PMID:** 36114762

**Key findings:**
- Human IBS subjects (n = large cohort, Cedars-Sinai): distinct gut microtypes by breath gas profile
- IBS-D (diarrhea): elevated breath H2S + H2; higher relative abundance of Fusobacterium and Desulfovibrio spp.
- IBS-C (constipation): elevated breath methane; higher Methanobrevibacter smithii
- Mechanistic distinction: H2S-producing bacteria → lower microbial diversity, enhanced sulfate reduction pathways
- First human study to systematically link breath gas pattern to specific sulfidogenic taxa + IBS subtype
- Directly supports Trio-Smart breath testing rationale for clinical use

**Quality:** High — peer-reviewed, American Journal of Gastroenterology, systematic
**Certainty:** 0.80
**Sample size:** Large (exact n not extracted; Cedars-Sinai IBS cohort)

## Paper 9: Animal Model — H2S Producers Drive Diarrhea Phenotype

**Citation key:** `VillanuevaMillan2023animalH2S`
**Authors:** Villanueva-Millan MJ, Leite G, Morales W, Sanchez M, Parodi G, Weitsman S, et al.
**Journal:** Digestive Diseases and Sciences, 2024; 69(2):426–436
**DOI:** 10.1007/s10620-023-08197-5 | **PMID:** 38060167

**Key findings:**
- Rat model: gavage with Desulfovibrio piger or Fusobacterium varium → increased stool wet weight (diarrhea phenotype) + increased stool H2S production
- Gavage with Methanobrevibacter smithii → constipation phenotype (decreased stool wet weight)
- Causal evidence: specific bacteria drive specific gas-linked stool phenotypes
- Confirms that H2S-producing organisms are not merely correlated with IBS-D but causally drive motility changes
- Mechanism: H2S affects colonic transit and fluid secretion (mucosal H2S signaling)

**Quality:** High — experimental animal model establishing causality, 2023
**Certainty:** 0.80 (animal; human translation requires confirmation)
**Sample size:** Adult Sprague-Dawley rats; multiple treatment groups

## Paper 10: Low-Sulfur Diet Reshapes Microbiome in UC (Pilot RCT)

**Citation key:** `Ye2025lowSulfurUC`
**Authors:** Ye, Raman, Taylor, Yousuf, Panaccione et al.
**Journal:** International Journal of Molecular Sciences, 2025; 26(10):4596
**DOI:** 10.3390/ijms26104596 | **PMID:** 40429741

**Key findings:**
- 8-week open-label RCT: n=9 reduced-sulfur (RS) diet vs n=13 controls; mild-moderate UC
- RS group reduced sulfur intake from 411 mg/day to 172 mg/day (−58%)
- Microbiome: increased alpha diversity + Shannon index in RS group; decreased pathobiont Eggerthella lenta; increased F. prausnitzii
- Metabolome: increased anti-inflammatory metabolites (indoleacetyl glutamine); reduced LPS-binding protein (−5280 ng/mL)
- Clinical: 43% of RS group showed ≥50% fecal calprotectin reduction vs 25% controls
- **Limitation:** very small n (pilot); UC not ME/CFS; open-label
- **ME/CFS relevance:** demonstrates dietary sulfur restriction is feasible, measurable, and produces measurable microbiome/metabolome shifts; rationale for ME/CFS subgroup with ISO

**Quality:** Medium (pilot RCT, small n, open-label)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Sample size:** n=22 total (9 RS, 13 control)

## Negative / Nuanced Findings

### Paul et al. 2020 — Biphasic and Context-Dependent Effects
**Citation key:** `Paul2020h2sMitoRedox`
**Authors:** Paul BD, Snyder SH, Kashfi K
**Journal:** Redox Biology, 2021 (epub 2020); 38:101772
**DOI:** 10.1016/j.redox.2020.101772 | **PMID:** 33137711

**Key nuanced findings:**
- H2S is **not simply toxic** — at low concentrations it preserves mitochondrial function and is cytoprotective
- Overexpression of H2S-producing enzymes CBS and 3-MST in Down syndrome causes excess H2S, impairs mitochondria — but these are endogenous sources, not gut-derived
- H2S role is context-dependent: hypoxia/acidosis shifts the balance toward toxicity at lower concentrations
- "Complete picture of the impact of H2S on bioenergetics is lacking" — authors caution against oversimplification
- **Critical for integration:** the document must not frame H2S as uniformly toxic; dose, compartment, and metabolic context all matter

**Quality:** High — Redox Biology, peer-reviewed, explicit about uncertainty
**Certainty:** 0.85 as nuanced/negative result
**Sample size:** Review — no cohort

## Synthesis: H2S Pathway Relevant to ME/CFS

**The plausible mechanism chain:**

1. Western/sulfur-rich diet + gut dysbiosis → overgrowth of Desulfovibrio, Bilophila wadsworthia, Fusobacterium
2. Sulfidogenic bacteria produce excess luminal H2S
3. Normally: colonic epithelial cells and liver (portal clearance via SQOR → ETHE1 → sulfite oxidase → sulfate) detoxify H2S efficiently
4. In ME/CFS: reduced cardiac output + orthostatic hypoperfusion → impaired hepatic O2 → reduced portal H2S clearance; CoQ10 deficiency (if present) → impaired SQOR activity
5. H2S spillover into systemic circulation → inhibits Complex IV in peripheral tissues
6. Concentration-dependent: low H2S = signal molecule; gut lumen H2S = 0.1–2 mM (far above Complex IV IC50 of ~1–3 μM) but most cleared before reaching mitochondria
7. Failure of clearance → fatigue amplification, worsened PEM, metabolic dysfunction

**What is NOT established for ME/CFS:**
- No ME/CFS microbiome studies specifically measured sulfidogenic taxa
- No H2S breath testing studies in ME/CFS populations
- No causal evidence linking gut H2S to ME/CFS mitochondrial metrics
- The mechanism is biologically plausible but currently speculative for ME/CFS

**Gap level:** Mechanistically compelling but evidentially thin — suitable for hypothesis/speculation environment, not achievement
