# Literature Summary: Food Insecurity in ME/CFS and Long COVID

**Topic slug:** food-insecurity-disease-burden
**Date:** 2026-06-23

## Anchor Paper

### Dey et al. (2026) — ME/CFS and/or Long Covid Food Insecurity Survey (New Zealand)

- **Source:** Fatigue: Biomedicine, Health & Behavior, 1–15.
- **DOI:** 10.1080/21641846.2026.2688051
- **Funding:** ANZMES (Associated New Zealand Myalgic Encephalomyelitis Society)
- **License:** CC-BY
- **Key findings:** (full text not accessible; metadata only — see DOI)
  - First survey examining food insecurity prevalence among ME/CFS and Long COVID patients in NZ
  - Reports both marginal, moderate, and severe food insecurity rates
  - Links disease severity to food access difficulties
  - Discusses implications of reduced employment capacity on nutrition
  - Highlights compounding effect of disability benefit inadequacy and food costs
- **Quality:** Peer-reviewed journal; survey design; NZ-specific (limited generalizability)
- **Certainty:** 0.65 (n not confirmed from abstract alone; peer-reviewed; single study)

## Direct Food Insecurity Literature

### 1. Lin et al. (2025) — Long COVID and Food Insecurity in US Adults (JAMA Network Open)

- **PMID:** 40924424 | **DOI:** 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.30730
- **Design:** Retrospective cross-sectional survey (NHIS 2022-2023)
- **Sample:** 21,631 adults (1,807 food insecure)
- **Key findings:**
  - Food insecurity associated with current long COVID: aOR 1.73 (95% CI 1.39-2.15)
  - Food insecurity associated with lower recovery odds if prior long COVID: aOR 0.70 (95% CI 0.54-0.92)
  - 15% of food insecure reported current long COVID vs 7% of food secure
  - SNAP participation (p-interaction=0.04) and unemployment (p-interaction=0.04) modified associations
- **Quality:** Large nationally representative sample; validated food insecurity scale; top journal
- **Certainty:** 0.80

### 2. Datta et al. (2025) — Long COVID and Financial Hardship (Health Services Research)

- **PMID:** 39618261 | **DOI:** 10.1111/1475-6773.14413
- **Design:** Cross-sectional (BRFSS 2022)
- **Sample:** 271,076 adults
- **Key findings:**
  - Long COVID associated with 1-11 pp increase in food insecurity across income/education groups
  - Employment loss/reduced hours mediated 6-20% of financial distress
  - Effect present across all socioeconomic strata but stronger in lower-income groups
- **Quality:** Very large sample; nationally representative; peer-reviewed
- **Certainty:** 0.75

### 3. Feldman et al. (2025) — SDoH and Long COVID Risk in RECOVER Cohort (Annals of Internal Medicine)

- **PMID:** 40720834 | **DOI:** 10.7326/ANNALS-24-01971
- **Design:** Prospective cohort (RECOVER)
- **Sample:** Large multi-site NIH RECOVER cohort
- **Key findings:** Social determinants of health (including food access) associated with long COVID risk
- **Quality:** NIH-funded; multi-site; top journal; prospective
- **Certainty:** 0.70

## ME/CFS Socioeconomic Burden Literature

### 4. Bowden et al. (2026) — ME/CFS Labour Market Outcomes in NZ (BMC Public Health)

- **PMID:** 42032509 | **DOI:** 10.1186/s12889-026-27499-7
- **Design:** Nationwide cross-sectional using Integrated Data Infrastructure (IDI), propensity score matching
- **Sample:** 1,902 individuals with ME/CFS on health/disability benefit
- **Key findings:**
  - Only 18.3% employed vs 83.8% general population
  - 64.7% on Supported Living Payment (vs 49% other benefit recipients)
  - Higher ED visits (18.8% vs 12.8%), higher medication use (32.8% vs 14.2% for >10 meds)
  - Lower disability support service use (1.6% vs 7.2%) despite higher need
  - Disproportionately female and European; ethnic inequities (Māori, Pacific, Asian underrepresented)
- **Quality:** Nationwide data; large sample; peer-reviewed; NZ-specific
- **Certainty:** 0.75

### 5. Weigel et al. (2025) — Sustained Illness Burden in Australian ME/CFS (PLOS ONE)

- **PMID:** 41460857 | **DOI:** 10.1371/journal.pone.0338433
- **Design:** Longitudinal (12 months, 3 timepoints)
- **Sample:** 32 pwME/CFS (68.8% female; mean age 44; median illness duration 12.5 years)
- **Key findings:**
  - Median 30 symptoms at each timepoint — no significant change over 12 months
  - Health status, physical health, daily/work activity most impacted
  - QoL significantly reduced vs population norms at all timepoints
  - Majority unable to maintain employment
- **Quality:** Longitudinal design; well-characterized cohort (CCC/ICC criteria); small n
- **Certainty:** 0.55

### 6. Vester et al. (2026) — Burden of Disease in ME/CFS Scoping Review (Applied Health Econ Health Policy)

- **PMID:** 40986167 | **DOI:** 10.1007/s40258-025-01006-2
- **Design:** Systematic scoping review (PRISMA-ScR), 6 databases
- **Sample:** 20 studies included
- **Key findings:**
  - Costs per patient: $2,916–$119,611 USD
  - Indirect costs (lost productivity) dominate
  - DALYs for US: 0.714M (2016) to 5.77M (2022)
  - Employment rates and school attendance severely impacted
- **Quality:** Systematic methodology; broad scope; peer-reviewed
- **Certainty:** 0.70

### 7. Samms & Ponting (2025) — Unequal Access to ME Diagnosis in England (BMC Public Health)

- **PMID:** 40259275 | **DOI:** 10.1186/s12889-025-22603-9
- **Design:** Population database analysis (NHS Hospital Episode Statistics)
- **Sample:** 100,055 diagnosed with ME/CFS (G93.3)
- **Key findings:**
  - White individuals ~5x more likely diagnosed than other ethnicities (stronger bias than other diseases)
  - Higher deprivation associated with lower diagnosis rates
  - Female-to-male ratio 3.88:1 overall; peaks at ~6:1 in 4th-5th decades
  - Prevalence varies 10x across ICBs (0.086%-0.82% for females)
- **Quality:** Population-level data; very large n; well-powered; peer-reviewed
- **Certainty:** 0.80

## Dietary/Nutritional Literature in ME/CFS

### 8. Aguilar-Aguilar et al. (2020) — Food Implications in Central Sensitization Syndromes (J Clin Med)

- **PMID:** 33352747 | **DOI:** 10.3390/jcm9124106
- **Design:** Narrative review
- **Scope:** FM, CFS, MCS
- **Key findings:**
  - Dietary modifications may improve QoL at low cost
  - Micronutrient deficiencies common in CSS
  - Evidence contradictory regarding role of food in CSS
  - Calls for personalized treatment considering nutritional status, food tolerances
- **Quality:** Review article; not systematic; provides conceptual framework
- **Certainty:** 0.45

### 9. Jones & Probst (2017) — Dietary Modification for CFS: Systematic Review (Aust NZ J Public Health)

- **PMID:** 28616881 | **DOI:** 10.1111/1753-6405.12670
- **Design:** Systematic review (PRISMA), 5 databases, 1994-2016
- **Sample:** 22 studies
- **Key findings:**
  - Limited evidence for dietary modifications in CFS
  - Polyphenol intakes (animal studies) and omega-3 showed some positive outcomes
  - D-ribose supplementation showed some human benefit
  - Evidence overall narrow and inconsistent
- **Quality:** Systematic methodology; now dated (pre-2016)
- **Certainty:** 0.50

### 10. Dorczok et al. (2025) — Dietary Supplementation for Fatigue in ME/CFS Systematic Review (Nutrients)

- **PMID:** 39940333 | **DOI:** 10.3390/nu17030475
- **Design:** Systematic review (PRISMA), 1994–2024
- **Sample:** 14 studies (N=809)
- **Key findings:**
  - L-carnitine, guanidinoacetic acid, oxaloacetate, CoQ10+selenium, NADH showed fatigue reductions
  - High risk of bias across studies (missing data, selection bias)
  - 6 studies reported adverse effects (nausea, insomnia)
  - No firm conclusions possible
- **Quality:** Systematic; current; well-conducted but limited by primary study quality
- **Certainty:** 0.55

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **No direct food insecurity prevalence data for ME/CFS** (except NZ anchor paper) — the Long COVID food insecurity studies (Lin, Datta) don't separate ME/CFS from general long COVID.
2. **No studies examine bidirectional relationship** — does food insecurity worsen ME/CFS outcomes? Or does ME/CFS cause food insecurity? Likely both, but unmeasured.
3. **No intervention studies** addressing food access in ME/CFS populations.
4. **Severity-specific data missing** — bedbound/severe patients may face extreme food access barriers.
5. **Nutritional status assessment** in ME/CFS is limited to supplementation trials, not dietary quality or food security.
6. **Geographic concentration** — most evidence is from US, UK, Australia, NZ. No low/middle-income country data.
7. **Ethnic inequities** documented in diagnosis (Samms 2025, Bowden 2026) likely compound food insecurity but unmeasured directly.
8. **Mechanistic hypotheses** absent — no papers propose pathways linking food insecurity to ME/CFS pathophysiology.

## Integration Recommendation: PROCEED

The evidence base is sufficient but uneven. The Long COVID–food insecurity link is well-established (certainty ≥0.70 from multiple large studies). For ME/CFS specifically, the socioeconomic burden literature clearly documents employment loss and poverty risk (Bowden, Vester, Weigel) that would predict food insecurity. The anchor paper directly fills a gap. Total: 11 papers, 1 anchor (certainty 0.65), 3 strong direct (≥0.70), 4 ME/CFS socioeconomic (0.55-0.80), 3 dietary/nutritional (0.45-0.55). Recommend integration as socioeconomic disease burden dimension rather than pathophysiological mechanism.
