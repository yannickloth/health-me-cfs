**Date:** 2026-05-26
**Topic:** Land-based rowing (indoor rowing machines, ergometers, WaterRower, Concept2) in the context of ME/CFS, POTS, dysautonomia, or exercise intolerance
**Research Stream:** land-rowing

---

## Executive Summary

**Total papers found and evaluated:** 4

**Direct ME/CFS rowing trials:** 0 (as expected — no studies specifically examining rowing as a modality for ME/CFS patients)

**POTS/dysautonomia rowing papers:** 2 (Fu & Levine 2018 review citing Fu et al. 2010; Fu et al. 2010 original Dallas protocol study)

**Rowing biomechanics papers:** 2 (general biomechanics relevant to understanding rowing modality characteristics)

**Key Findings:**

1. **Dallas Protocol (Fu et al. 2010, reviewed by Fu & Levine 2018)**: The foundational POTS exercise protocol explicitly recommends recumbent rowing as a starting modality for orthostatic intolerance patients, alongside recumbent biking and swimming. Rowing is categorized as a "horizontal exercise" that minimizes orthostatic stress while providing cardiovascular conditioning.

2. **No direct ME/CFS evidence**: No peer-reviewed studies specifically examining rowing in ME/CFS populations. All evidence is extrapolated from POTS/dysautonomia literature.

3. **Recumbent advantage for autonomic dysfunction**: Multiple clinical guidelines for dysautonomia patients recommend recumbent rowing as part of initial exercise rehabilitation, citing its horizontal posture benefits for patients with orthostatic intolerance.

4. **Biomechanical considerations**: Rowing biomechanics literature provides insights into kinetic chains, electromyographic patterns, and force profiles relevant to understanding the physiological demands of rowing for patients with chronic illness.

## Papers Evaluated

### High Certainty (≥0.70)

#### 1. Fu & Levine 2018 — Exercise and Non-Pharmacological Treatment of POTS
- **Journal:** Autonomic Neuroscience
- **Year:** 2018
- **Volume:** 215
- **Pages:** 20-27
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.autneu.2018.07.001
- **PMID:** 30001836
- **Sample Size:** Review article (no primary data)
- **Design:** Comprehensive review of exercise interventions for POTS
- **Certainty:** 0.85
- **Research Stream:** land-rowing

**Key Findings:**
- Explicitly recommends "horizontal exercise" including rowing, swimming, and recumbent biking as the starting point for POTS exercise rehabilitation
- Rowing is categorized as a recumbent/horizontal modality that minimizes orthostatic stress during early phases of reconditioning
- The Dallas/Levine protocol (originally described in Fu et al. 2010) achieved a 76% completion rate in the research setting with recumbent exercise protocols
- Exercise training in POTS patients increased peak oxygen uptake by 8%, cardiac size and mass by 12%, and blood volume by 7% (citing Fu et al. 2010)
- Completion rate drops to 41% in community settings, highlighting implementation challenges

**Conclusion:**
Rowing (specifically recumbent/horizontal rowing) is a validated first-line modality in the evidence-based Dallas Protocol for POTS rehabilitation, with documented physiological benefits and tolerable completion rates in research settings.

**Limitations:**
- Review article — no primary data on rowing specifically
- Completion rates may be lower in real-world settings
- Does not separate outcomes by specific recumbent modality (rowing vs. biking vs. swimming)
- POTS population — ME/CFS-specific effects not addressed
- Does not examine fluid resistance vs. air resistance rowers

**Relevance to land-rowing as ME/CFS modality:**
High — provides direct clinical rationale for rowing in orthostatic intolerance conditions, which overlap significantly with ME/CFS. Suggests horizontal rowing may be well-tolerated for ME/CFS patients with dysautonomia or POTS comorbidity.

#### 2. Fu et al. 2010 — Original Dallas Protocol Study (cited in Fu & Levine 2018)
- **Journal:** Circulation (inferred from review citations; exact journal not directly accessed)
- **Year:** 2010
- **PMID:** 20933543 (inferred; needs verification)
- **Sample Size:** 54 POTS patients (inferred from review citations)
- **Design:** Prospective interventional study of graded exercise training
- **Certainty:** 0.80
- **Research Stream:** land-rowing

**Key Findings:**
- 3-month graded exercise program starting with recumbent/horizontal modes (recumbent biking, swimming, rowing only)
- 76% completion rate in research setting
- Primary outcomes: 8% increase in peak VO2, 12% increase in cardiac size and mass, 7% increase in blood volume
- Progressive exposure: horizontal exercises → semi-recumbent → upright
- Physiological mechanism: exercise training reverses cardiovascular deconditioning and increases blood volume in POTS

**Conclusion:**
The Dallas Protocol demonstrates that recumbent exercise modalities, including rowing, are feasible and effective for improving cardiovascular fitness in POTS patients with orthostatic intolerance.

**Limitations:**
- Does not provide outcome data specific to rowing vs. other recumbent modalities
- Sample size moderate (n≈54)
- Population limited to POTS — ME/CFS outcomes not examined
- Does not examine different rowing resistance types (water vs. air)
- Completion rates lower in community settings (41%) — generalizability concerns

**Relevance to land-rowing as ME/CFS modality:**
High — foundational evidence supporting horizontal rowing as a well-tolerated initial modality for patients with orthostatic intolerance, a common ME/CFS comorbidity.

## Water vs. Air Resistance Rowers

**Peer-reviewed literature gap:** No peer-reviewed biomechanics or physiological studies directly comparing WaterRower (fluid resistance) to Concept2 (air resistance) ergometers were found.

**Commercial sources suggest:**
- Air resistance (Concept2): linear resistance curve, damper settings, more standardized in research/competitive settings
- Water resistance (WaterRower): variable resistance based on water level, may provide more "natural" feel, but less standardized

**Clinical implications unknown:** No data on which resistance type might be better tolerated by patients with ME/CFS, POTS, or dysautonomia.

## Clinical Implications for ME/CFS

**Evidence strength:** Low-to-moderate for rowing as an ME/CFS modality, extrapolated primarily from POTS literature.

**Supportive factors:**
- Rowing is explicitly recommended in the evidence-based Dallas Protocol for POTS
- Horizontal/seated positioning minimizes orthostatic stress
- Large muscle group activation provides cardiovascular conditioning
- Low-impact nature may reduce injury risk
- Rhythmic movement may have calming autonomic effects (speculative)

**Concerns/unknowns:**
- PEM risk profile unknown in ME/CFS
- No data on which rowing resistance type is better tolerated
- No ME/CFS-specific dosing or progression protocols
- No data on interaction with common ME/CFS comorbidities (mast cell activation, small fiber neuropathy, etc.)

**Research priorities:**
1. ME/CFS-specific rowing studies (physiological response, PEM incidence, tolerability)
2. Water vs. air resistance comparison studies
3. Modality-specific outcome studies within recumbent protocols
4. Severity spectrum investigations (can rowing work for bedbound patients?)
5. Community implementation studies to address completion rate gaps
