# Salivary Metabolomic Biomarkers for Sleep Deprivation and Relevance to ME/CFS Unrefreshing Sleep

**Date:** 2026-06-04  
**Research Stream:** sleep-deprivation-saliva  
**Target Papers:** 5-15  

---

## Executive Summary

This literature review examines salivary metabolomic biomarkers for sleep deprivation and their potential relevance to ME/CFS unrefreshing sleep. The core finding is that acute sleep deprivation (24h total) produces a detectable metabolic fingerprint in oral fluid, while chronic sleep restriction (4 nights of 6h sleep) does **not** produce significant metabolic changes. This has important implications for ME/CFS, where patients typically experience chronic unrefreshing sleep rather than acute total sleep deprivation.

**Key Metabolites Identified:** 10-12 molecular features (primarily polar, HILIC+ mode) that distinguish sleep-deprived from well-rested individuals with 94% accuracy (F0.5 = 0.90).

## Core Papers

### 1. Scholz et al. (2026) - Primary Source

**Citation:** Scholz M, Steuer AE, Dobay A, Landolt HP, Kraemer T. "Leveraging the Metabolic Fingerprint of Sleep Deprivation and Sleep Restriction for Forensic Applications: A Machine Learning Study in Oral Fluid Metabolomics." *J Proteome Res*. 2026 May 6. DOI: 10.1021/acs.jproteome.5c01064. PMID: 42090244.

**Study Design:**
- Sample: 20 young men (habitual sleep 7-9h)
- Design: Randomized, controlled, crossover trial
- Interventions: (1) 24h total sleep deprivation, (2) 4 nights sleep restriction to 6h, (3) Control (8h sleep)
- Method: LC-HRMS (liquid chromatography-high resolution mass spectrometry)
- Features: 6035 robust molecular features identified

**Key Findings:**
- **Acute sleep deprivation**: Unique metabolic fingerprint detectable with F0.5 = 0.90 using only **12 molecular features** (94% accuracy)
- **Sleep restriction**: NO significant metabolic changes vs. rested condition
- Time-dependent: Fingerprint more pronounced in morning/midday samples (08:00-12:00)
- AUROC: 0.86 with 12-feature model for overall SD classification

**Critical Insight for ME/CFS:**
> "Repeated moderate sleep restriction did not lead to exploitable metabolic changes when compared with controlled sleep."

This suggests that ME/CFS patients' chronic unrefreshing sleep may **not** produce the same metabolic fingerprint as acute sleep deprivation.

**Certainty Score: 0.65** (Medium - good design, but n=20, all male, acute intervention only)

### 2. Oishi et al. (2025) - Chronic Sleep Disorders in Saliva

**Citation:** Oishi K, Yoshida Y, Kaida K, Terai K, Yamamoto H, Toyoda A. "Potential non-invasive biomarkers of chronic sleep disorders identified by salivary metabolomic profiling among middle-aged Japanese men." *Sci Rep*. 2025 Apr 21;15:10980. DOI: 10.1038/s41598-025-95403-1.

**Study Design:**
- Sample: 100 middle-aged Japanese men (50 good sleep, 50 poor sleep)
- Sleep quality: PSQI-J scores (good ≤2, poor ≥6)
- Method: Capillary electrophoresis-Fourier transform mass spectrometry (CE-FTMS)
- Focus: Chronic sleep disorders (not acute deprivation)

**Key Findings:**
- **13 metabolites significantly altered** in poor sleep quality
  - 5 decreased: glycerol, hippuric acid, and others
  - 8 increased: 2-hydroxybutyric acid (2HB), and others
- Random forest model: 6-metabolite panel with **86.6% prediction accuracy**
- Correlations with sleep satisfaction (OSA-MA questionnaire):
  - Positive: 2'-deoxyguanosine, N1-acetylspermine, 2,4-dihydroxybenzoic acid
  - Negative: glucosamine 6-phosphate, trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO)

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates that **chronic sleep problems can be detected** in saliva
- 2-hydroxybutyric acid (2HB) levels were **36% higher** in poor sleep group
- Blood 2HB negatively associated with sleep duration
- Provides biomarker candidates for chronic sleep disruption (vs. acute deprivation)

**Certainty Score: 0.55** (Medium - larger n=100, but cross-sectional, self-report sleep quality)

### 3. Jason et al. (2021) - ME/CFS Salivary Fatigue Biomarker

**Citation:** Jason LA, Kalns J, Richarte A, Katz BZ, Torres C. "Saliva Fatigue Biomarker Index As a Marker for Severe Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome in a Community Based Sample." *Fatigue Biomed Health Behav*. 2021 Oct 27;9(4):189-199. DOI: 10.1080/21641846.2021.1994222. PMID: 35186443.

**Study Design:**
- Sample: Pediatric ME/CFS (n=59 cases, n=39 controls)
- Community-based random sample of 10,119 youth aged 5-17
- Cases: 19 ME/CFS (single criteria), 40 severe ME/CFS (multiple criteria)
- Biomarker: Fatigue Biomarker Index (FBI) = log10(peptide1/peptide2)
  - Peptide 1: GGHPPPP (from PRH1/PRH2 genes)
  - Peptide 2: GNPQGPSPQGGNKPQGPPPPPGKPQ (from PRB1 gene)
- Method: Competitive ELISA

**Key Findings:**
- **Significant difference** in FBI between severe ME/CFS and controls (p < 0.05)
- Severe ME/CFS FBI: M = -1.62 (SD = .34)
- Controls FBI: M = -1.39 (SD = .46)
- ME/CFS (single criteria): M = -1.53 (SD = .42) - **trend only, not significant**
- FBI correlated with general fatigue in prior studies

**Critical Limitation:**
> "The FBI was only predictive of severe ME/CFS; there was only a trend in the same direction when we examined youth who only met the Fukuda definition of ME/CFS."

**Relevance to ME/CFS Sleep:**
- First objective salivary biomarker for ME/CFS
- Biomarker distinguishes severe vs. milder disease
- **No direct sleep measurement** - measures fatigue, not sleep quality
- Suggests salivary peptides may be useful for ME/CFS subtyping

**Certainty Score: 0.50** (Medium - modest sample, severe ME/CFS only, sleep not measured)

### 4. Maksoud et al. (2021) - ME/CFS Sleep Systematic Review

**Citation:** Maksoud R, Eaton-Fitch N, Matula M, Cabanas H, Staines D, Marshall-Gradisnik S. "Systematic Review of Sleep Characteristics in Myalgic Encephalomyelitis/Chronic Fatigue Syndrome." *Healthcare*. 2021 May 11;9(5):568. DOI: 10.3390/healthcare9050568. PMID: 34065013.

**Study Scope:**
- 20 studies (all case-control with PSG and/or MSLT)
- Total: ~532 ME/CFS patients, ~480 healthy controls
- Mean age: 41.5 (ME/CFS), 39.2 (HC)
- 87% female

**Key Sleep Findings in ME/CFS:**

| Measure | Finding | Consistency |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **MAI (Micro-arousal Index)** | **Increased in all 5 studies** | ✅ Consistent |
| Sleep Onset Latency | No difference in 13/13 studies | ✅ Consistent null |
| Total Sleep Time | No significant difference | ✅ Consistent null |
| REM Sleep | Increased in 2/20 studies | ❓ Mixed |
| Slow Wave Sleep | Longer in 3/11 studies | ❓ Mixed |
| AHI (Apnea-Hypopnea Index) | Mixed findings | ❓ Mixed |
| Spectral Activity | Diminished alpha power (1 study) | ❓ Mixed |
| MSLT | Reduced in 1/6 studies | ❌ Mostly null |

**Critical Discrepancy:**
> "A common trend in these studies is that there is a discrepancy between subjective sleep measures and objective measures."

**Key Insight:**
- ~91% of ME/CFS patients report unrefreshing sleep
- Objective PSG often shows **normal sleep architecture**
- Subjective-objective disconnect suggests **non-structural sleep disruption**
- Brain imaging shows **medial prefrontal cortex deficits** correlate with unrefreshing sleep

**Relevance to Metabolomics:**
- Unrefreshing sleep may be **metabolic/circadian** rather than structural
- Supports need for **non-PSG biomarkers** (e.g., metabolomics)
- Chronic nature suggests Scholz's acute deprivation model may not apply

**Certainty Score: 0.70** (High - comprehensive systematic review, but limited by primary study quality)

### 5. Jeppe et al. (2024) - Blood Metabolomic Sleep Deprivation Biomarker

**Citation:** Jeppe K, Ftouni S, Nijagal B, Grant LK, Lockley SW, Rajaratnam SMW, Phillips AJK, McConville MJ, Tull D, Anderson C. "Accurate Detection of Acute Sleep Deprivation Using a Metabolomic Biomarker—A Machine Learning Approach." *Sci Adv*. 2024 Apr 12;10(15):eadj6834. DOI: 10.1126/sciadv.adj6834.

**Study Design:**
- Sample: 16 healthy adults
- Design: Acute sleep deprivation (38h awake) vs. normal sleep
- Method: Plasma metabolomics
- Focus: Forensic "fitness for duty" applications

**Key Findings:**
- Metabolomic biomarker can detect acute sleep deprivation
- High classification accuracy
- Forensic applications: roadside or post-accident testing

**Relevance to ME/CFS:**
- Demonstrates **blood-based** sleep deprivation detection
- Supports concept of metabolic sleep fingerprint
- However, uses **acute deprivation** model (38h awake)
- Not validated for chronic sleep disruption

**Certainty Score: 0.45** (Medium-Low - n=16, acute model only, blood not saliva)

## Gap Analysis: What's Missing

### Critical Knowledge Gaps

1. **No direct ME/CFS salivary metabolomics studies**
   - Jason 2021 measures fatigue peptides, not metabolomics
   - No ME/CFS studies using LC-HRMS or CE-FTMS on saliva

2. **Chronic vs. Acute Sleep Disruption**
   - Scholz 2026: Acute 24h deprivation → strong signal
   - Scholz 2026: 4-night restriction → NO signal
   - ME/CFS: Chronic, not acute sleep disruption
   - **Unclear** if ME/CFS unrefreshing sleep produces metabolic fingerprint

3. **Saliva vs. Blood**
   - Most ME/CFS metabolomics uses blood (plasma/serum)
   - Saliva offers non-invasive, home-collection potential
   - Unknown if salivary and blood metabolomic signatures correlate in ME/CFS

4. **Unrefreshing Sleep Mechanisms**
   - Maksoud 2021: Subjective-objective disconnect
   - Shan 2017: Prefrontal cortex deficits correlate with unrefreshing sleep
   - Metabolic mechanism unknown

### Research Questions for ME/CFS

1. Does chronic unrefreshing sleep in ME/CFS produce a detectable salivary metabolomic fingerprint?
2. How does ME/CFS metabolic signature compare to acute sleep deprivation fingerprint?
3. Can salivary metabolomics distinguish ME/CFS subtypes (e.g., severe vs. milder)?
4. What is the relationship between salivary fatigue biomarkers and metabolomic profiles in ME/CFS?
5. Do ME/CFS salivary metabolites correlate with objective sleep measures (PSG) or subjective sleep quality?

## Conclusions and Implications for ME/CFS

### Primary Finding
**Acute sleep deprivation produces a detectable salivary metabolomic fingerprint, but chronic sleep restriction does not.**

### ME/CFS Relevance
1. **ME/CFS patients experience chronic unrefreshing sleep**, not acute deprivation
2. **Scholz 2026's negative finding** for sleep restriction suggests ME/CFS may not produce the acute deprivation fingerprint
3. **Oishi 2025 provides hope** - chronic sleep disorders DO produce salivary changes
4. **Maksoud 2021 confirms** - subjective unrefreshing sleep is real despite normal PSG

### Clinical Implications
1. **Acute deprivation biomarkers** (Scholz, Jeppe) may not apply to ME/CFS
2. **Chronic sleep disruption biomarkers** (Oishi) may be more relevant
3. **Need for ME/CFS-specific salivary metabolomics studies**
4. **Salivary approach** offers home collection, non-invasive advantage for severe ME/CFS patients

### Research Priorities
1. **Direct ME/CFS salivary metabolomics study** - does chronic unrefreshing sleep produce fingerprint?
2. **Compare ME/CFS to chronic sleep disorders** - similar patterns?
3. **Subtype differentiation** - can metabolomics distinguish severe vs. milder ME/CFS?
4. **Longitudinal studies** - do metabolic signatures fluctuate with PEM/sleep quality?
5. **Saliva-blood correlation** - validate salivary findings against established blood metabolomics
