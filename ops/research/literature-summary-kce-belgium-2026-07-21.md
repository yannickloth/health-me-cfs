## Methodology

- **Framework:** NEED (Needs Examination, Evaluation and Dissemination) — KCE-developed methodology for identifying unmet health-related needs
- **Design:** Mixed-methods (quantitative survey + qualitative interviews)
- **Part 1 — Patient needs assessment:**
  - Online survey: n=749 adults with diagnosed ME/CFS living in Belgium
  - 19 semi-structured in-depth qualitative interviews (maximal variation sampling by gender, time since diagnosis, severity)
  - Survey instrument: Adapted generic NEED questionnaire + EQ-5D-5L quality of life measure
  - Recruitment: Patient associations, KCE website, social media (2-month window in 2025)
  - Languages: Dutch (88.7%) and French (11.3%)
  - Ethics: Approved by Hôpital Universitaire de Bruxelles-ULB (P2025/142/CCB B4062025000053)
- **Part 2 — Care organisation analysis:**
  - Systematic review of peer-reviewed and grey literature on ME/CFS service models
  - International comparison: UK, France, Netherlands, Italy (expert interviews + document analysis)
  - Long COVID care pathway analysis for Belgium + comparison countries
- **Introduction chapter:** Not a formal systematic review; pragmatic overview of literature including NICE NG206 (2021), CDC review (2022), and key guidelines/reviews

## Key Findings

### Demographics and Disease Characteristics
- Survey: 89.9% female, mean age 50.6 years (SD 0.03), 88.6% Flanders resident
- ~50% diagnosed >10 years; only 4% diagnosed in past 6 months
- Top comorbidities: fibromyalgia (63.3%), IBS (56.9%), orthostatic hypotension (20.6%), POTS (17.0%), long COVID (16.4%), small fibre neuropathy (12.8%), MCAS (10.5%)
- 73.2% reported ≥1 ME/CFS-related comorbidity

### Quality of Life (EQ-5D-5L)
- Mean utility score dropped from 0.84 (pre-illness) to 0.36 (current) — p<0.001
- This is substantially below Belgian general population mean of 0.79 (Sciensano 2018)
- Comparable to conditions widely recognised as severely disabling
- Greater HRQoL decline associated with: age 30–50, diagnostic delay >2 years, long COVID comorbidity, care access issues, financial difficulties
- French-speaking participants had greater decline (-0.57 vs -0.47 for Dutch-speaking, p=0.005)

### Symptom Burden
- Top physical symptoms: abnormal fatigue (98.7%), PEM (97.7%), sleep disorders (97.2%), difficulty concentrating (96.0%), memory problems (93.7%), generalised pain (93.1%)
- >75% rated these as very/extremely burdensome
- Psychological symptoms: stress (89.9%), fear/anxiety (74.6%); moderate-to-severe burden in 75%
- Mental health impairment secondary to physical and social consequences

### Diagnostic Trajectory
- 50% waited >2 years from first medical consultation to diagnosis
- 39% waited >5 years
- Diagnostic delay associated with significantly worse HRQoL (p=0.010)
- Key barriers: inconsistent diagnostic criteria, lack of professional expertise, misattribution of symptoms to psychological causes

### Treatment Experiences (Patient-Reported)
- Physiotherapy (manual techniques): generally positive experiences
- GET: predominantly perceived as harmful — majority reported symptom exacerbation and substantial treatment burden
- CBT: mixed results; ~50% improvement, ~20% deterioration, ~30% no change
- Psychological counselling: ~50% improvement, ~30% no change
- 26% reported moderate/substantial improvement with GET — subgroup characteristics not characterised
- Pacing: widely used self-management strategy; evidence base limited but patient-preferred
- Pharmacological interventions: no evidence supporting use per NICE; patients report off-label use

### Healthcare Organisation
- Belgium: single NIHDI-funded reference centre (Dutch-speaking Flanders); other centres operate outside convention with out-of-pocket costs
- Only UK has nationally structured ME/CFS referral pathway
- France, Netherlands, Italy: rely on clinician-led expertise centres — geographical inequities
- Netherlands: ZonMw 10-year biomedical research programme (€28.5M), national guideline expected 2026
- UK: 2025 Cross-Sector Delivery Plan (research, education/attitudes, living with ME/CFS)
- Italy: draft legislation under consideration for ME/CFS recognition as disabling disease
- Most countries lack updated national guidelines; where they exist, often >10 years old
- No robust evidence identified supporting any specific predefined service model

### Social and Economic Impact
- Productivity losses: 1,189 individuals in Belgian invalidity database (31 Dec 2024), 82% female, 72% aged 45+
- Mean invalidity duration: 8.6 years — among longest of all conditions studied under NEED
- 75% reported negative impact on personal relationships
- Majority stopped working or reduced hours
- Majority reported financial difficulties

### Recognition and Stigma (Supplement Qualitative Analysis)
- Recognition identified as central unmet need across healthcare, policy, society
- Psychologisation of ME/CFS experienced as inequitable care
- Lack of knowledge/training/expertise among healthcare professionals
- Consequences: diagnostic delays, inappropriate care, social isolation, financial insecurity

## Recommendations / Suggestions

The report explicitly states it **does not constitute a clinical guideline** but offers policy-relevant suggestions:

1. **Strengthen recognition and visibility** of ME/CFS among healthcare professionals and public — reduce stigma
2. **Improve early diagnosis** by enhancing clinical knowledge in training and lifelong learning
3. **Advance scientific understanding** — translational research combining clinicians, researchers, patients; clinical trials; overlap with post-infectious syndromes
4. **Structured care pathways** — reference centres aligned with current evidence, multidisciplinary collaboration, clearly defined primary-secondary care coordination
5. **Workplace adaptations** — flexible arrangements accommodating energy limitations; employer guidance

## Strengths
- First comprehensive needs assessment for ME/CFS in Belgium
- Validated NEED methodology with policymaker and patient validation
- Active patient involvement throughout all research phases (4 patient associations)
- Mixed methods providing complementary quantitative + qualitative data
- International comparison contextualising Belgian service gaps
- High recency (June 2026)

## Limitations
- Self-reported diagnoses (not independently verified)
- Sampling bias: recruited via patient associations; digital exclusion risk
- Linguistic imbalance: 88.7% Dutch vs 11.3% French
- Not available in German (third official language)
- Survey instrument not validated for cognitive function in ME/CFS
- Recall bias for retrospective questions
- Under-representation of severe/very severe patients
- Introduction chapter not a systematic review
- Care organisation literature review limited scope
- No cost-effectiveness analysis of service models
