### Paper 1 — Naess et al. 2012

**Full citation:**
Naess H, Nyland M, Hausken T, Follestad I, Nyland HI.
"Chronic fatigue syndrome after Giardia enteritis: clinical characteristics, disability and long-term sickness absence."
*BMC Gastroenterology* 2012;12:13.
DOI: 10.1186/1471-230X-12-13
PMID: 22316329
PMCID: PMC3292445

**Suggested BibTeX key:** `Naess2012giardia_cfs`

**BibTeX entry:**
```bibtex
@article{Naess2012giardia_cfs,
  author  = {Naess, Halvor and Nyland, Marte and Hausken, Trygve and Follestad, Ingrid and Nyland, Harald I},
  title   = {Chronic fatigue syndrome after {Giardia} enteritis: clinical characteristics, disability and long-term sickness absence},
  journal = {BMC Gastroenterology},
  year    = {2012},
  volume  = {12},
  pages   = {13},
  doi     = {10.1186/1471-230X-12-13},
  pmid    = {22316329},
  url     = {https://doi.org/10.1186/1471-230X-12-13},
  keywords = {Giardia, post-infectious CFS, Bergen outbreak, clinical characterisation, disability, sickness absence}
}
```

**Abstract summary:**
Case series from the Bergen 2004 Giardia waterborne outbreak. Of 1262 patients with
laboratory-confirmed giardiasis, 96 were referred with persisting post-infectious fatigue;
58 (60%) met CDC/Fukuda criteria for CFS. Mean illness duration at referral was 2.7 years.
Outcome at referral: 16% improved, 28% stable, 57% progressively worsening. Mean FSS
score 6.6. SF-36 showed characteristic impairments in physical functioning, vitality, and
social functioning. All patients had long-term sickness absence from work or study.
Overall, at least 5% of laboratory-confirmed giardiasis cases developed full CFS.

**Key findings relevant to ME/CFS:**
- Quantifies minimum incidence of post-infectious CFS after a defined parasite exposure (5% of all lab-confirmed cases)
- Describes clinical phenotype: physical, vitality, and social functioning disproportionately impaired; mental health relatively spared
- Progressive worsening course in majority (57%) — not self-limiting at 2.7 years
- Supports parasitic infection as a sufficient trigger for full-criteria ME/CFS, not merely fatigue
- Complements the longer follow-up studies from the same Bergen cohort

**Certainty level:** Medium-high
- Prospective outbreak cohort with laboratory confirmation of pathogen
- Single outbreak, single city (external validity limited)
- CFS diagnosed clinically at referral, not population-wide screening
- No matched controls (case series design)
- n=58 CFS cases from well-defined exposure

```bibtex
  keywords = {Giardia, post-infectious CFS, Bergen outbreak, 5-year follow-up, differential diagnosis, natural course}

```

**Abstract summary:**
Among 347 patients who met Chalder criteria for chronic fatigue 3 years after the Bergen
Giardia outbreak, 253 were invited and 53 participated in structured clinical evaluation
at 5 years by psychiatry, neurology and infectious disease specialists. CFS (Fukuda) was
diagnosed in 41.5% (22/53); idiopathic chronic fatigue in 13.2% (7/53). Important
differential diagnoses or comorbidities: sleep apnoea/hypopnoea syndrome (n=5), depression
(n=6), anxiety disorders (n=5). Fatigue had resolved in 20.8% (11/53). Self-reported
fatigue scores in the cohort improved significantly from 3 to 5 years (p<0.001).

**Key findings relevant to ME/CFS:**
- 41.5% of persistently fatigued post-Giardia patients met CFS criteria at 5 years — a very high proportion
- Giardia can induce CFS persisting at least 5 years
- Important: sleep apnoea and depression are differential diagnoses that must be excluded, not assumed to be the explanation
- Partial natural recovery observed (20.8% resolved at 5 years); fatigue scores improved from 3 to 5 years
- Provides clinical characterisation data at 5-year mark for the Bergen natural history arc

**Certainty level:** Medium
- Follow-up of subset (53/253 eligible); self-selection bias possible
- Multi-specialist clinical evaluation is a strength
- Fukuda criteria applied systematically
- n=22 CFS cases; small for subgroup analyses

### Paper 3 — Hanevik et al. 2014

**Full citation:**
Hanevik K, Wensaas KA, Rortveit G, Eide GE, Mørch K, Langeland N.
"Irritable bowel syndrome and chronic fatigue 6 years after giardia infection: a controlled prospective cohort study."
*Clinical Infectious Diseases* 2014;59(10):1394–1400.
DOI: 10.1093/cid/ciu629
PMID: 25115874
PMCID: PMC4207419

**Suggested BibTeX key:** `Hanevik2014giardia_6yr`

**BibTeX entry:**
```bibtex
@article{Hanevik2014giardia_6yr,
  author  = {Hanevik, Kurt and Wensaas, Knut-Arne and Rortveit, Guri and Eide, Geir Egil and Mørch, Kristine and Langeland, Nina},
  title   = {Irritable bowel syndrome and chronic fatigue 6 years after giardia infection: a controlled prospective cohort study},
  journal = {Clinical Infectious Diseases},
  year    = {2014},
  volume  = {59},
  number  = {10},
  pages   = {1394--1400},
  doi     = {10.1093/cid/ciu629},
  pmid    = {25115874},
  url     = {https://doi.org/10.1093/cid/ciu629},
  keywords = {Giardia, post-infectious CFS, Bergen outbreak, 6-year follow-up, IBS, controlled cohort}
}
```

**Abstract summary:**
Controlled prospective cohort study. 748 exposed (lab-confirmed Giardia 2004) and 878
matched controls responded to postal questionnaire 6 years post-infection. IBS prevalence
at 6 years: 39.4% in exposed vs. controls (RR 3.4, 95% CI 2.9–3.9). Chronic fatigue
prevalence: 30.8% in exposed vs. controls (RR 2.9, 95% CI 2.3–3.4). From 3 to 6 years:
IBS decreased by 6.7% (RR 0.85), chronic fatigue decreased by 15.3% (RR 0.69). Increasing
age was a risk factor for persisting chronic fatigue. Authors conclude Giardia elicits
very long-term but slowly self-limiting complications.

**Key findings relevant to ME/CFS:**
- First controlled prospective study of this cohort with matched controls — strongest design in the Bergen series
- At 6 years: almost 3x excess risk of chronic fatigue in Giardia-exposed vs. unexposed population
- Slow partial recovery: prevalence declining but still highly elevated at 6 years
- Provides temporal trajectory data: 3-year peak, partial improvement by 6 years
- Confirms that parasitic Giardia infection is a bona fide ME/CFS trigger at population scale
- IBS and chronic fatigue tend to co-occur (consistent with gut-brain and immune mechanisms)

**Certainty level:** High (for this literature)
- Controlled design with matched population controls
- Large sample (n=748 exposed, n=878 controls)
- Laboratory-confirmed Giardia exposure
- Prospective design with repeated measures
- Postal questionnaire (self-report) is a limitation for outcomes


**Abstract summary:**
Longitudinal study in an adult outbreak-associated cohort following acute Cryptosporidium
parvum infection. Self-reported symptoms up to 12 months: weight loss 31%, abdominal pain
38%, diarrhoea 33%, eye pain 9%, joint pain 33%, fatigue 22%, IBS-consistent symptoms 28%.
Two participants received formal IBS diagnoses. First study to describe sequelae of C. parvum
up to 12 months; notes similarity to sequelae reported for C. hominis.

**Key findings relevant to ME/CFS:**
- Fatigue persisting at 22% prevalence 12 months after Cryptosporidium infection — a significant proportion
- Joint pain (33%) and multi-system symptoms suggest systemic post-infectious syndrome, not purely GI
- Extends the post-infectious ME/CFS trigger spectrum to Cryptosporidium (beyond Giardia)
- Limitation: no CFS diagnostic criteria applied; 12 months is short follow-up for ME/CFS assessment
- Provides preliminary evidence; full CFS ascertainment would require longer follow-up

**Certainty level:** Low-medium
- Small outbreak cohort (sample size not specified in abstract — likely small)
- 12-month follow-up insufficient to assess ME/CFS trajectory
- CFS not formally diagnosed (symptom questionnaire only)
- No control group
- Single outbreak; C. parvum subtype; context-specific

```bibtex
  keywords = {ME/CFS, infectious triggers, patient-reported, mononucleosis, trigger spectrum, onset}

```

**Abstract summary:**
Multi-site, multi-country study of 1773 individuals diagnosed with ME, CFS, or ME/CFS who
provided qualitative data on infectious triggers. 60.3% reported an infectious illness
preceding onset. Most frequent: mononucleosis (30% of infections). Over 100 other infectious
illnesses also mentioned. Authors conclude many distinct infectious agents may be associated
with ME/CFS onset.

**Key findings relevant to ME/CFS:**
- Confirms post-infectious onset in ~60% of a large multi-country ME/CFS cohort
- No single pathogen dominates: the trigger is a diverse spectrum, not one agent
- Mononucleosis (EBV) is the single most common but accounts for only 30% — the remaining 70% is distributed across >100 other agents
- Parasitic triggers are implicitly included in "over 100 other infectious illnesses"
- Establishes the empirical basis for the "diverse trigger spectrum" framing in any discussion of post-infectious ME/CFS
- Important limitation: self-report / retrospective perception — recall bias and misattribution are risks
- No parasitic triggers quantified separately; this is a gap in the study

**Certainty level:** Medium
- Large sample (n=1773) across multiple sites and countries — strength
- Entirely self-reported, retrospective (patients recall events potentially years prior) — weakness
- Qualitative coding of open-ended responses — coding reliability not detailed
- No control group; cannot determine causation from correlation
- No conflict of interest declared

## Part 4: Assessment of Blastocystis, Dientamoeba, and Toxoplasma Literature

### Blastocystis hominis and ME/CFS

**Search results:** Only 1 PubMed hit for Blastocystis + CFS (PMID 18937874 — Boorom 2008, IBS focus).
The existing `Blastocystis2013letter` (Dunwell 2013, BJGP) is the primary ME/CFS-specific citation.

**Boorom et al. 2008** (PMID 18937874) — This is a broad review of Blastocystis and IBS, not CFS-specific.
It discusses fatigue as a symptom of chronic Blastocystis infection and PAR-2 mechanism.
Not recommended for addition: it is a low-quality (non-peer-reviewed foundation-authored) review,
and the Dunwell 2013 letter covers the ME/CFS-specific angle.

**Assessment:** The literature on Blastocystis and ME/CFS specifically is extremely sparse.
The existing Dunwell 2013 letter is the only direct ME/CFS citation. No new high-quality paper found.

---

### Dientamoeba fragilis and ME/CFS

**Search results:** Three papers found. None directly study ME/CFS as an outcome.

- **Holtman et al. 2017** (PMID 27784723, *Family Practice*): D. fragilis in children, primary care.
  Found no association between D. fragilis and GI symptoms. Fatigue mentioned peripherally.
  Not relevant to ME/CFS directly.

- **Schwartz & Nelson 2003** (PMID 12865103, *J Emerg Med*): D. fragilis presenting as acute appendicitis.
  Notes fatigue among chronic GI symptoms. Old case report, not ME/CFS relevant.

- **Gefen-Halevi et al. 2022** (PMID 35134178, *J Travel Med*): Returning travellers with persistent
  abdominal symptoms. D. fragilis (18.7%) and Blastocystis (26.6%) common. Main complaint: fatigue
  (79.2%). Covers persistent post-travel syndrome, not ME/CFS specifically.

**Assessment:** No ME/CFS-specific Dientamoeba literature exists beyond the Dunwell 2013 letter.
The evidence base is essentially: clinical reports of fatigue as a symptom, not post-infectious ME/CFS.
No new additions recommended for ME/CFS-specific references.bib.

---

### Toxoplasma gondii and Chronic Fatigue

**Search results:** Multiple hits, but none meet criteria for ME/CFS-specific evidence.

- Most Toxoplasma + fatigue literature concerns psychiatric comorbidity (schizophrenia, depression),
  not ME/CFS as a distinct syndrome.
- **Savitz & Yolken 2023** (PMID 35606640): Antimicrobial treatments in ME/CFS context — mentions
  Toxoplasma in the psychiatric disease context but not ME/CFS specifically.
- The most cited Toxoplasma + CFS paper (Montoya lab) is about serology — not a strong causal link.

**Assessment:** Toxoplasma gondii is not established as an ME/CFS trigger in the peer-reviewed
literature. The Palo Alto Medical Foundation Toxoplasma Serology Laboratory connection (Montoya)
is methodological, not causal. No new Toxoplasma papers recommended for addition.

## Notes for chapter-integrator

These papers are primarily relevant to:
- **ch07 or equivalent post-infectious chapter** — Giardia/parasite as ME/CFS trigger
- **Pathogen spectrum discussion** — Cryptosporidium extending the evidence base beyond Giardia
- **Immunology chapter** — NK cell reduction (Hanevik 2012), sCD40L (Hanevik 2017)
- **Disease course chapter** — Natural history (Morch 2013, Hanevik 2014 show slow partial recovery)
- **Trigger spectrum / etiology chapter** — Jason 2022 (diverse trigger spectrum)

The Bergen Giardia outbreak series (2004 outbreak → papers from 2012–2022) forms the strongest
natural experiment in post-infectious ME/CFS research: a defined exposure event, laboratory
confirmation, matched controls, and repeated follow-up over 10 years.
