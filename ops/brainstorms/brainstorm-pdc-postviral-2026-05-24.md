# pDC Dysfunction Brainstorm: ME/CFS & Post-Viral Conditions
**Date:** 2026-05-24
**Topic:** Plasmacytoid dendritic cell (pDC) dysfunction in ME/CFS/post-viral syndromes
**Parent Topic:** NET/DNase imbalance

---

## Evidence Base Summary

### pDC Biology Essentials
- pDCs are <0.1% of PBMCs — extremely rare cells
- Primary source of type I IFN-α (150× more than myeloid DCs), exclusive source of circulating DNase1L3
- Sense viruses via TLR7→MyD88→IRF7→IFN-α; not productively infected (no ACE2/TMPRSS2)
- Express CD123, CD303, NRP1/CD304; identified as lineage-CD11c-CD123+ by flow
- Diversify post-activation into P1 (IFNα-producing), P2 (dual), P3 (antigen-presenting) populations
- SARS-CoV-2 exploits NRP1/CD304 on pDCs to suppress IFN production (viral immune evasion)

### Core Findings
1. **"pDC desert" established:** 19/23 COVID-19 studies show blood pDC depletion; severity-proportional (Van der Sluis 2022, PMID 35858624)
2. **Blood→tissue migration conserved:** Blood↓/tissue↑ pattern across SLE, RA, psoriasis, melanoma, multiple cancers (Li 2017, PMID 29085364)
3. **Persistent depletion:** Blood pDC deficiency at 7 months post-COVID in both hospitalised and non-hospitalised patients (Pérez-Gómez 2021, PMID 34290398)
4. **Chemokine axes:** CXCR3/CXCL9-11, CXCR4/CXCL12, chemerin/ChemR23, CCR7/CCL19-21 drive migration
5. **Garcia 2024:** pDC depletion + DNase1L3↓ in acute COVID-19 blood (n=145)
6. **Augustin 2026:** pDC expansion in gut tissue of post-COVID syndrome (n=43, contradicts Garcia)
7. **pDC tolerance functions:** IDO, granzyme B, IL-10, Treg induction via ICOSL

### Key Knowledge Gaps
- Zero pDC enumeration or function studies in ME/CFS
- Zero pDC chemokine profiling in ME/CFS
- No pDC-DNase1L3 functional assays in any post-viral condition
- pDC-Microglia crosstalk entirely theoretical (no human data)
- No pDC-directed therapeutics tested in post-viral syndromes
- pDC metabolism completely unstudied in any disease context

## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### H1: pDC Migratory Trap Hypothesis
**Mechanistic rationale:** SARS-CoV-2 viral fragments persist in gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) via reservoir mechanisms; these fragments continuously produce TLR7 agonists creating a chemokine gradient (CXCL9/10/11) that traps migrated pDCs in gut tissue. The trapped pDCs become chronically activated, shifting from IFNα production to tolerance phenotype (P3-like), losing capacity to return to circulation.

**Evidence link:** Blood→tissue migration conserved pattern (Li 2017); pDC expansion in gut tissue post-COVID (Augustin 2026); CXCR3/CXCL9-11 chemokine axis drives migration; viral persistence in GALT documented in Long COVID

**Certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show (a) blood pDC depletion, (b) increased pDC markers in intestinal biopsy samples, (c) elevated CXCL9-11 in stool but not serum, and (d) tissue pDCs will express CD86+MHC-II+ (P3 phenotype) but not IFNα transcripts.

---

### H2: DNase1L3 Imbalance-Driven NETopathy Loop
**Mechanistic rationale:** Blood pDC depletion reduces circulating DNase1L3 below threshold needed to degrade extracellular DNA nets. Unopposed NETs accumulate, forming autoantigen scaffolds that trigger TLR7/9 activation in remaining pDCs. This creates a self-amplifying loop: pDC loss → DNase1L3↓ → NETs↑ → pDC activation → exhaustion → further loss. The loop is sustained by tissue-resident pDCs that produce DNase1L3 locally but cannot access circulating NETs.

**Evidence link:** pDCs exclusive source of circulating DNase1L3; Garcia 2024 shows simultaneous pDC depletion + DNase1L3↓ in acute COVID; NET accumulation in COVID autopsies; DNase1L3 deficiency models develop autoimmunity

**Certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show (a) inverse correlation between circulating pDC count and circulating DNase1L3 activity (r < -0.7), (b) elevated cfDNA/NET markers in plasma (MPO-DNA, citH3), (c) pDC TLR7/9 gene signatures correlated with NET load, and (d) exogenous DNase1L3 supplementation restores pDC function in vitro.

---

### H3: pDC Metabolic Exhaustion Hypothesis
**Mechanistic rationale:** Chronic viral RNA sensing via TLR7 forces pDCs into sustained OXPHOS production to fuel type I IFN synthesis. In ME/CFS, mitochondrial dysfunction (documented in energy metabolism chapter) limits ATP generation, causing pDC metabolic exhaustion. Exhausted pDCs switch to glycolytic "tolerance mode," losing IFNα capacity while maintaining anti-inflammatory IDO/granzyme B secretion. This shift persists even after viral clearance due to epigenetic imprinting.

**Evidence link:** IFNα production energy-intensive; pDCs normally use OXPHOS for IFN synthesis; mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS well-established; immune cell metabolic exhaustion documented in chronic viral infections (HIV, CMV)

**Certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS pDCs will show (a) reduced mitochondrial membrane potential (JC-1 assay), (b) increased glycolytic flux (ECAR), (c) decreased ATP/ADP ratio, (d) blunted IFNα response to TLR7 agonist but intact IDO production, and (e) metabolic reprogramming agents (metformin, rapamycin) restore IFNα capacity ex vivo.

---

### H4: pDC-Gut Microbiome Dysbiosis Feed-Forward Loop
**Mechanistic rationale:** pDC depletion reduces IFNα-mediated regulation of gut epithelial tight junctions and antimicrobial peptide production. This causes dysbiosis with expansion of bacterial species that produce TLR7-stimulating RNA (e.g., certain gut-associated viruses). The dysbiotic microbiome continuously activates migrated pDCs in GALT, trapping them and perpetuating tolerance phenotype. Systemic effects arise from bacterial translocation of RNA/DNA metabolites that activate residual blood pDCs.

**Evidence link:** IFNα regulates gut barrier integrity; pDCs survey gut lumen via dendritic extensions; gut dysbiosis documented in ME/CFS; pDC expansion in gut tissue post-COVID (Augustin 2026); bacterial RNA can activate TLR7

**Certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show (a) correlation between pDC count and gut barrier permeability (serum zonulin, LPS), (b) increased bacterial RNA in mesenteric lymph nodes, (c) pDC chemokine profiles (CXCL9-11) correlate with dysbiosis indices, and (d) fecal microbiota transplantation increases circulating pDC counts within 8 weeks.

---

### H5: pDC-Neuroimmune Crosstalk Dysfunction
**Mechanistic rationale:** pDCs normally traffic to meninges and produce IFNα that regulates microglial priming and neuroinflammation thresholds. In ME/CFS, pDC depletion reduces IFNα in CNS, causing microglial dysregulation and exaggerated neuroinflammatory responses to mild stimuli (e.g., sensory input, stress). Additionally, pDC-derived granzyme B modulates neuronal synaptic plasticity; depletion removes this regulatory input, contributing to cognitive symptoms.

**Evidence link:** pDCs found in meninges and choroid plexus; IFNα crosses BBB and regulates microglia; microglial activation documented in ME/CFS neuroimaging; granzyme B modulates synapse pruning; neuroinflammatory dysregulation correlates with symptom severity

**Certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show (a) reduced IFNα and granzyme B in CSF, (b) microglial activation signatures (TSPO PET) inversely correlated with blood pDC count, (c) pDC infusion in animal models reduces microglial hyperresponsiveness, and (d) CSF pDC trafficking markers correlate with cognitive symptom clusters.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### R1: ME/CFS pDC Enumeration Multi-Tissue Mapping Study
**Mechanistic rationale:** Directly test blood depletion hypothesis in ME/CFS by enumerating pDCs across blood, gut tissue, nasal mucosa, and lymph nodes using standardized flow cytometry panels. Establish whether blood→tissue migration pattern observed in COVID/ME/CFS is conserved in ME/CFS, and identify which tissue compartments harbor migrated pDCs.

**Evidence link:** Blood depletion established in COVID (19/23 studies); tissue expansion documented in post-COVID gut (Augustin 2026); zero pDC enumeration studies in ME/CFS; conserved migration pattern across autoimmune diseases (Li 2017)

**Certainty:** 0.85 (establishing existence)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show reduced circulating pDCs (lineage-CD11c-CD123+CD303+) relative to healthy controls (p < 0.01), with tissue-specific enrichment patterns that correlate with symptom domains (gut pDCs with GI symptoms, nasal pDCs with upper respiratory symptoms).

---

### R2: pDC-DNase1L3 Functional Assay Development
**Mechanistic rationale:** Establish quantitative relationship between pDC count/activation state and DNase1L3 secretion using in vitro pDC stimulation assays. Develop standardized assay to measure DNase1L3 activity per pDC, test whether pDC dysfunction in ME/CFS is quantitative (fewer cells) vs qualitative (impaired secretion per cell), and establish therapeutic thresholds for intervention.

**Evidence link:** pDCs exclusive source of circulating DNase1L3; Garcia 2024 shows simultaneous depletion; no functional assays in any post-viral condition; DNase1L3 deficiency models develop autoimmunity

**Certainty:** 0.70 (establishing methodology)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS pDCs will produce significantly less DNase1L3 per cell upon TLR7 stimulation compared to healthy controls (p < 0.001), and DNase1L3 activity will correlate inversely with circulating NET markers (MPO-DNA, citH3) and autoantibody titers.

---

### R3: pDC Chemokine Profiling in ME/CFS Blood vs Tissue
**Mechanistic rationale:** Comprehensive chemokine profiling (CXCL9, CXCL10, CXCL11, CXCL12, CCL19, CCL21, chemerin) in blood, stool, and tissue biopsies to identify which axes are driving pDC migration in ME/CFS. Compare to post-COVID patients to determine if chemokine signatures are conserved across post-viral syndromes or ME/CFS-specific.

**Evidence link:** Multiple chemokine axes drive pDC migration (CXCR3/CXCL9-11, CXCR4/CXCL12, chemerin/ChemR23, CCR7/CCL19-21); zero chemokine profiling in ME/CFS; established axes in other diseases

**Certainty:** 0.75

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show elevated tissue-specific chemokines (e.g., CXCL9-11 in stool, CCL19-21 in lymph nodes) with corresponding chemokine receptor upregulation (CXCR3, CCR7) on tissue-resident pDCs, and circulating pDCs will show exhausted chemokine receptor expression (downregulation).

---

### R4: pDC Metabolic Profiling Using Single-Cell Seahorse
**Mechanistic rationale:** Directly test metabolic exhaustion hypothesis using single-cell Seahorse analysis of sorted pDCs from ME/CFS patients vs controls. Measure OCR (OXPHOS) and ECAR (glycolysis) at baseline and after TLR7 stimulation to determine if metabolic reprogramming underlies functional exhaustion. Establish whether mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS extends to immune cells.

**Evidence link:** pDC metabolism completely unstudied; mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS well-established; immune cell metabolic exhaustion documented in chronic viral infections; metabolic profiling available via Seahorse technology

**Certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS pDCs will show reduced basal OCR, reduced maximal respiratory capacity, and increased ECAR/OCR ratio compared to controls (p < 0.01), and metabolic dysfunction will correlate with IFNα production capacity and symptom severity scores.

---

### R5: pDC-Treg Axis Functional Studies
**Mechanistic rationale:** pDCs induce Treg differentiation via ICOSL-ICOS interaction and IDO production. In ME/CFS, test whether pDC depletion or functional impairment reduces Treg induction capacity, contributing to immune dysregulation. Establish whether Treg deficits are mediated by pDC dysfunction (pDC-Treg axis) or are independent.

**Evidence link:** pDC tolerance functions include Treg induction via ICOSL; Treg dysfunction documented in ME/CFS; ICOSL-ICOS interaction critical for Treg differentiation; IDO produced by pDCs promotes Treg generation

**Certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show reduced Treg frequencies (CD4+CD25+FoxP3+) that correlate with pDC counts (r > 0.6), and in vitro co-culture assays will demonstrate impaired ME/CFS pDC ability to induce Tregs from naïve CD4+ T cells, which is rescued by ICOSL agonist or IDO supplementation.

---

### R6: [AUTO-ESCALATE] pDC-Autoantibody Interaction Mapping
**Mechanistic rationale:** Screen ME/CFS serum for anti-pDC autoantibodies (anti-CD123, anti-CD303, anti-CD304, anti-NRP1) and test whether these antibodies functionally impair pDC activation, migration, or DNase1L3 secretion. Establish whether autoimmunity against pDCs contributes to depletion (antibody-mediated clearance) or functional impairment (receptor blockade).

**Evidence link:** Autoantibodies documented in ME/CFS; anti-dendritic cell antibodies in SLE; antibody-mediated immune cell depletion in other diseases; no studies on anti-pDC antibodies in ME/CFS

**Certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will have significantly higher titers of anti-pDC autoantibodies compared to controls (p < 0.01), and patient serum will inhibit pDC activation (IFNα production) in vitro, which is reversed by IgG depletion or Fc receptor blockade.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication

### D1: TLR7 Agonist (Resiquimod/GS-9620) Low-Dose Pulses
**Mechanistic rationale:** Chronic TLR7 stimulation in post-viral conditions may exhaust pDCs. Paradoxically, ultra-low-dose intermittent TLR7 agonist exposure may re-sensitize pDCs via epigenetic reprogramming and IFN pathway priming. Used clinically for HBV latency reversal; dosing titrated to avoid cytokine storm. Goal: restore pDC IFNα production capacity without triggering exacerbation.

**Evidence link:** TLR7 agonists used clinically (HBV, cancer); dose-dependent effects; immune re-sensitization documented in exhausted T cells; no pDC-specific trials in post-viral conditions

**Certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a crossover RCT, ME/CFS patients receiving low-dose resiquimod (0.01 mg/kg weekly) will show increased pDC IFNα production ex vivo and reduced fatigue scores compared to placebo (p < 0.05), without significant cytokine elevation or symptom exacerbation.

---

### D2: DNase1L3 Replacement Therapy (Recombinant Enzyme)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Directly address DNase1L3 deficiency by administering recombinant DNase1L3 to degrade circulating NETs and extracellular DNA. Breaks the NET-autoantigen-pDC activation loop by removing the trigger (DNA/RNA nets). Clinical precedent: dornase alfa (DNase1) for cystic fibrosis; DNase1L3 has higher specificity for chromatin.

**Evidence link:** DNase1L3 deficiency in ME/CFS (hypothesized); DNase1L3 degrades NETs effectively; dornase alfa approved for CF; DNase1L3 reduces autoimmunity in mouse models; no human trials for DNase1L3

**Certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a double-blind RCT, ME/CFS patients receiving weekly IV DNase1L3 (dose titrated to achieve 2× baseline activity) will show reduced circulating NET markers (MPO-DNA, citH3), increased DNase1L3 activity, and improved fatigue scores compared to placebo (p < 0.05).

---

### D3: CXCR4 Antagonist (Plerixafor) to Mobilize Trapped pDCs
**Mechanistic rationale:** If pDCs are trapped in tissues via CXCR4/CXCL12 axis (bone marrow/GALT retention), CXCR4 antagonist plerixafor (AMD3100) can mobilize them back into circulation. Used clinically for stem cell mobilization; short-acting, reversible blockade. Goal: restore blood pDC counts without disrupting tissue immune surveillance.

**Evidence link:** CXCR4/CXCL12 retains hematopoietic cells in bone marrow; plerixafor mobilizes stem cells; pDCs express CXCR4; tissue trapping hypothesis plausible; no pDC mobilization studies

**Certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a proof-of-concept study, ME/CFS patients receiving single subcutaneous plerixafor (0.24 mg/kg) will show rapid increase in circulating pDCs within 6 hours (peak 12-18 hours), correlating with transient symptom improvement, without adverse effects.

---

### D4: Metformin for pDC Metabolic Reprogramming
**Mechanistic rationale:** Metformin activates AMPK, which enhances mitochondrial biogenesis and reduces glycolytic dependency. If pDC metabolic exhaustion underlies dysfunction, metformin may restore OXPHOS capacity and IFNα production. Well-tolerated, known safety profile. Dose titrated to achieve metabolic effect without hypoglycemia (non-diabetic population).

**Evidence link:** Metformin improves mitochondrial function; AMPK activation restores immune cell metabolism; metformin modulates dendritic cell function; mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS; no pDC-specific studies

**Certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** In an 8-week RCT, ME/CFS patients receiving metformin (500 mg BID) will show improved pDC mitochondrial membrane potential (JC-1), increased IFNα production ex vivo, and reduced fatigue scores compared to placebo (p < 0.05).

---

### D5: IDO1 Inhibitor (Epacadostat) to Break Tolerance Loop
**Mechanistic rationale:** Chronic pDC activation induces tolerance via IDO1 production, which suppresses T cell function and promotes Treg expansion. If ME/CFS involves pDC-mediated immune tolerance, IDO1 inhibition may restore effector T cell function and reduce immunosuppression. Used clinically in cancer immunotherapy; reversible inhibition.

**Evidence link:** IDO1 produced by activated pDCs; IDO1 mediates tolerance; epacadostat tested in cancer trials; immune tolerance/suppression in ME/CFS; no pDC-specific IDO1 trials

**Certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a 12-week RCT, ME/CFS patients receiving epacadostat (100 mg BID) will show reduced kynurenine/tryptophan ratio, increased effector T cell function, and reduced immunosuppressive Treg frequencies, with corresponding symptom improvement (p < 0.05).

---

### D6: Anti-ChemR23 Antibody to Block Chemerin-Mediated pDC Trafficking
**Mechanistic rationale:** Chemerin/ChemR23 axis drives pDC migration to inflamed tissues. If excessive chemerin signaling traps pDCs in gut tissue (Augustin 2026), blocking ChemR23 may reduce tissue retention and restore circulating pDC counts. Anti-chemokine receptor antibodies used in inflammatory diseases (anti-CCR5, anti-CXCR3).

**Evidence link:** Chemerin/ChemR23 drives pDC migration; pDC expansion in gut tissue post-COVID; anti-chemokine receptor antibodies in clinical use; chemerin elevated in inflammatory conditions

**Certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a phase 1 trial, ME/CFS patients receiving anti-ChemR23 antibody (monthly IV, dose-escalation) will show reduced tissue pDC markers (stool CXCL10, intestinal biopsy pDCs), increased circulating pDCs, and improved GI symptoms (p < 0.05).

## Category 4: Supplements

### S1: Vitamin D3 to Enhance pDC Differentiation
**Mechanistic rationale:** Vitamin D3 promotes dendritic cell differentiation and enhances TLR7-mediated IFNα production in pDCs. Vitamin D deficiency is common in ME/CFS; supplementation may increase pDC numbers and restore function. Dose titrated to achieve serum 25(OH)D 60-80 ng/mL (optimal immune function range).

**Evidence link:** Vitamin D modulates dendritic cell function; enhances TLR7 signaling; deficiency common in ME/CFS; vitamin D supplementation restores immune cell function; no pDC-specific studies

**Certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a 12-week RCT, ME/CFS patients with vitamin D deficiency (25(OH)D < 30 ng/mL) receiving high-dose vitamin D3 (10,000 IU weekly) will show increased pDC counts, enhanced IFNα production ex vivo, and improved fatigue scores compared to standard-dose (2,000 IU weekly) (p < 0.05).

Oxidative stress in ME/CFS may impair pDC function; selenium supplementation may enhance antioxidant capacity and restore IFNα production. Dose titrated to achieve serum selenium 120-150 ng/mL (optimal range).

### S3: Quercetin to Inhibit pDC Exhaustion via Senolytic Effects

**Evidence link:** DNase1L3 degrades NETs; anti-chemokine receptors mobilize cells; combination logical; no pDC-specific studies

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a phase 1 trial, ME/CFS patients receiving both weekly IV DNase1L3 (dose titrated) and monthly anti-ChemR23 antibody will show greater reduction in NET markers, greater pDC count increase, and greater symptom improvement than either alone (p < 0.05).

## Category 7: Mathematical Models

### M1: Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) Model of pDC-Tissue Dynamics
**Mechanistic rationale:** Develop compartmental ODE model with blood pDC pool (B), tissue pDC pool (T), and bone marrow pDC production (M). Include chemokine-driven migration (B↔T), production (M→B), depletion (death/apoptosis), and tissue trapping (reduced return migration). Model can predict time course of pDC depletion and identify critical parameter thresholds (chemokine levels, production rates) for intervention.

**Evidence link:** Blood→tissue migration pattern established; chemokine axes identified; depletion patterns documented; ODE modeling standard in immunology; no pDC-specific models

**Certainty:** 0.70 (model development)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Model calibrated to acute COVID data (Van der Sluis 2022) will accurately predict 7-month depletion trajectory (Pérez-Gómez 2021) and identify tissue chemokine levels as the most sensitive parameter for restoring blood pDC counts (sensitivity analysis > 0.8).

### M2: Partial Differential Equation (PDE) Model of pDC Spatial Distribution
**Mechanistic rationale:** Develop spatiotemporal PDE model incorporating diffusion (pDC movement through tissue), chemotaxis (chemokine gradients), and reaction (activation/death). Model can simulate pDC distribution across blood, lymph nodes, GALT, and meninges, predicting how chemokine gradient alterations affect pDC localization.

**Evidence link:** Chemokine gradients drive pDC migration; spatial distribution documented (gut expansion); PDE modeling of immune cell migration established; no pDC-specific spatial models

**Certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** Model will predict that localized CXCL9-11 elevation in GALT creates a pDC sink with >80% of circulating pDCs trapped in gut tissue within 48 hours, and that CXCR3 blockade reduces tissue trapping by >60% (simulation validated against Augustin 2026 data).

### M3: Agent-Based Model (ABM) of pDC-Viral RNA Interaction
**Mechanistic rationale:** Develop ABM simulating individual pDC agents sensing viral RNA via TLR7, producing IFNα, undergoing activation/exhaustion, and migrating. Model can incorporate stochasticity, individual cell heterogeneity, and feedback loops (IFNα autocrine signaling). Can test hypothesis that chronic low-level RNA sensing drives exhaustion.

**Evidence link:** pDC TLR7 signaling pathway known; IFNα feedback documented; ABM established in immunology; no pDC-specific ABM

**Certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** ABM will show that persistent low-level RNA stimulation (10% of acute dose) drives pDC exhaustion within 4 weeks, with >70% of cells transitioning to tolerance phenotype (P3-like), and that intermittent stimulation (48h on/48h off) preserves IFNα capacity.

### M4: Stochastic Model of pDC-DNase1L3-NET Feedback Loop
**Mechanistic rationale:** Develop stochastic model incorporating pDC depletion, DNase1L3 secretion, NET formation/degradation, and pDC activation by NETs. Model can test stability of the system and identify bifurcation points where the feedback loop becomes self-sustaining. Incorporate noise to explain interpatient variability in ME/CFS onset/severity.

**Evidence link:** pDC-DNase1L3-NET loop plausible; feedback loops in autoimmunity; stochastic modeling of immune networks established; no pDC-specific models

**Certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Model will identify a critical DNase1L3 threshold (~50% of baseline) below which the feedback loop becomes bistable (self-sustaining), and that stochastic variation in initial pDC depletion explains >40% of variance in ME/CFS severity (variance decomposition analysis).

### DX3: Chemokine Signature Panel for pDC Tissue Trapping
**Mechanistic rationale:** Develop multiplex chemokine panel (CXCL9, CXCL10, CXCL11, CXCL12, CCL19, CCL21, chemerin) in blood, stool, and saliva. ### DX4: Ex Vivo pDC IFNα Stimulation Assay
**Mechanistic rationale:** Stimulate isolated PBMCs or sorted pDCs with TLR7 agonist (imidazoquinoline) and measure IFNα production (ELISA, Luminex). Compare to healthy controls. **Evidence link:** TLR7 stimulation standard; IFNα measurement established; functional assays diagnostic in other diseases; no ME/CFS-specific assays

**Certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS pDCs will produce <40% of control IFNα upon TLR7 stimulation (p < 0.001), and IFNα production will correlate with pDC count (r > 0.5) and fatigue severity (r < -0.4), indicating combined quantitative+qualitative dysfunction.

**Certainty:** 0.50

## Ethical and Safety Considerations

### TLR7 Agonists (D1)
- Risk: Cytokine storm, symptom exacerbation
- Mitigation: Ultra-low dose, hospital monitoring, stopping rules
- Contraindication: Active infection, autoimmune flare

### CXCR4 Antagonist (D3, C3)
- Risk: Stem cell mobilization, leukocytosis, splenic enlargement
- Mitigation: Single dose, CBC monitoring, avoiding in hematologic disorders
- Contraindication: Leukemia, myelofibrosis

### DNase1L3 Replacement (D2, C5)
- Risk: Immunogenicity, off-target DNA degradation
- Mitigation: Human recombinant enzyme, anti-drug antibody monitoring
- Unknown: Long-term safety (no human trials yet)

### FMT (NP4)
- Risk: Infection transmission, dysbiosis worsening
- Mitigation: Screened donors, FMT enemas (safer than colonoscopy)
- Contraindication: Immunocompromised, active colitis

## Appendices

### Appendix A: pDC Flow Cytometry Gating Strategy
```
1. FSC/SSC: Single cells, lymphocyte gate
2. Live/dead: Viable cells (negative)
3. Lineage dump: CD3- CD14- CD19- CD20-
4. CD11c-: Exclude myeloid DCs
5. CD123+: pDC marker
6. CD303+ or CD304+: Confirm pDC identity
7. Activation: CD86+, HLA-DR+ (optional)
8. Chemokine receptors: CXCR3+, CXCR4+, CCR7+ (optional)
```

### Appendix B: DNase1L3 Assay Protocol
1. Collect serum (avoid hemolysis)
2. Dilute 1:10 in assay buffer
3. Add chromogenic substrate (fluorescein-labeled DNA)
4. Incubate 37°C, 30 min
5. Measure fluorescence (ex/em 485/535 nm)
6. Calculate activity (U/mL) using standard curve
7. Normalized to total protein (optional)

### Appendix C: TLR7 Stimulation Assay
1. Isolate PBMCs (Ficoll gradient)
2. Sort pDCs (CD123+CD303+) or use whole PBMC
3. Plate 1×10^5 cells/well in 96-well
4. Stimulate with imiquimod (1 μg/mL) or resiquimod (0.1 μg/mL)
5. Incubate 24h at 37°C, 5% CO2
6. Collect supernatant
7. Measure IFNα by ELISA (pg/mL)
8. Calculate stimulation index (stimulated/unstimulated)

### Appendix D: Chemokine Multiplex Panel
- **CXCL9 (MIG):** Gut trapping
- **CXCL10 (IP-10):** Inflammation, trapping
- **CXCL11 (I-TAC):** High-affinity CXCR3 ligand
- **CXCL12 (SDF-1):** Bone marrow/GALT retention
- **CCL19 (MIP-3β):** Lymph node homing
- **CCL21 (6Ckine):** Lymph node homing
- **Chemerin:** ChemR23-mediated migration
- **CX3CL1 (Fractalkine):** Neuroimmune crosstalk (optional)

### Appendix E: pDC Metabolic Assay (Seahorse)
1. Sort pDCs (≥10,000 cells required)
2. Plate in XF96 cell culture microplate (poly-D-lysine coated)
3. Seahorse assay medium: XF RPMI, 10 mM glucose, 2 mM glutamine, 1 mM pyruvate
4. Mito Stress Test:
   - Basal OCR/ECAR
   - Oligomycin (ATP-linked respiration)
   - FCCP (maximal respiration)
   - Rotenone/Antimycin A (non-mitochondrial respiration)
5. Glycolysis Stress Test (optional):
   - Basal ECAR
   - Glucose
   - Oligomycin
   - 2-DG
