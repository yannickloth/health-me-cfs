# Brainstorm: Neuroimmune POTS/ME/CFS/Long COVID
## Date: 2026-06-08 | Source: Blitshteyn, Doherty, Steinman 2026 (ITT)

### 1. Novel Hypotheses

#### H1: GPCR Autoantibody-Mediated Baroreflex Gain Resetting at Brainstem Level via NTS Receptor Internalization
- **Mechanistic rationale:** α1-adrenergic and M2/M4 muscarinic autoantibodies from the periphery may cross the area postrema (CVO with leaky BBB) and bind their cognate receptors on NTS neurons. Chronic low-level agonism triggers GPCR internalization (β-arrestin-mediated), reducing the density of functional adrenergic/muscarinic receptors on NTS second-order baroreflex neurons. This shifts the baroreflex operating point — the brainstem "reads" MAP as lower than actual because fewer receptors transduce the afferent signal. Result: inappropriate compensatory sympathetic activation despite normal MAP, exactly the pattern in hyperadrenergic POTS. This provides a mechanistic integration: peripheral GPCR autoantibodies (Sunami 2024, Gunning 2019) + brainstem neuroinflammation (Blitshteyn 2025, Wagoner 2019). The NTS is adjacent to the area postrema; autoantibodies access via CVO, then internalize NTS GPCRs over weeks-months, producing the chronic baroreflex resetting that distinguishes POTS from acute hypovolemic tachycardia.
- **Evidence link:** @Sunami2024GPCRPOTS, @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS, @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation, @Wagoner2019MedullaInflammation, @Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS+POTS patients, CSF GPCR autoantibody titers (α1, M2, M4) correlate with baroreflex sensitivity deficit (r > 0.4), and the baroreflex operating point (MAP at half-max HR) is shifted rightward compared to healthy controls. GPCR autoantibody-positive patients show reduced NTS fMRI BOLD response to baroreflex activation (Valsalva phase IV vs phase II) compared to titer-matched GPCR-negative patients.
- **Tier:** 2

#### H2: Dorsolateral Medulla Neuroinflammation as Antigen-Driven Niche — B Cell Germinal Center-Like Structures in the Brainstem
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Blitshteyn 2025 proposes dorsolateral inferior medulla as CNS localization for POTS/LC. If this region harbors persistent antigen (viral remnants, neo-epitopes from oxidative damage), meningeal B cell aggregates or perivascular cuffs could form, creating local autoantibody production that amplifies the GPCR autoantibody cascade. The NTS/RVLM/DMV are adjacent to the fourth ventricle choroid plexus — a known immune surveillance site. Chronic neuroinflammation could recruit B cells via CXCL13/CXCR5, establish tertiary lymphoid structures, and drive intrathecal GPCR autoantibody synthesis. This predicts: (a) CSF GPCR autoantibodies differ from serum (intrathecal production), (b) oligoclonal bands specific to GPCR targets, (c) TSPO-PET signal in the dorsolateral medulla correlates with CSF autoantibody titer. The existing paper's brainstem neuroinflammation + autoantibody sections would merge into a unified intrathecal autoimmune hypothesis.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation, @Wagoner2019MedullaInflammation, @Moen2025NeuroimmuneLongCOVID, @VanElzakker2024NeuroinflammationPASC
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** CSF from ME/CFS+POTS patients contains GPCR autoantibodies (α1, β2, M2) with a different titer ratio than paired serum (CSF/serum index > 0.5 for ≥ 1 GPCR), indicating intrathecal synthesis. CSF oligoclonal bands are present in ≥ 20% of these patients. TSPO-PET signal in the dorsolateral medulla correlates with CSF GPCR autoantibody titer (r > 0.5) but not with serum titer. Postmortem medulla of ME/CFS/POTS patients shows CD138+ plasma cells and CD20+ B cells in perivascular cuffs around the NTS.
- **Tier:** 2

#### H3: Molecular Mimicry Between SARS-CoV-2 Spike S1 and β2-Adrenergic Receptor — Shared Linear Epitope Drives Autoantibody Generation
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Blitshteyn 2026 cites molecular mimicry as a shared post-infectious mechanism but does not identify specific epitopes. SARS-CoV-2 spike S1 subunit contains a linear epitope (amino acid sequence alignment) with partial homology to the extracellular N-terminus of the β2-adrenergic receptor. B cells primed against the spike epitope cross-react with β2-AR, generating functional autoantibodies that either agonize or block the receptor. This predicts: (a) β2-AR autoantibody-positive Long COVID patients have serum that cross-reacts with SARS-CoV-2 spike S1 in competition ELISA, (b) the cross-reactive epitope maps to a specific β2-AR peptide (predictable from sequence alignment), (c) immunization of mice with this spike peptide generates β2-AR autoantibodies and autonomic dysfunction. This hypothesis provides a mechanistic link between the infectious trigger (SARS-CoV-2, EBV) and the GPCR autoantibody response, filling a gap in the Blitshteyn 2026 framework where molecular mimicry is invoked but molecularly underspecified. EBV EBNA-1 may share a different epitope with M2 muscarinic receptor, explaining why different triggers produce different autoantibody profiles.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Wallukat2021GPCRLongCOVID, @Moen2025NeuroimmuneLongCOVID, @ElRhermoul2023AutoimmunityLCPOTS
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** β2-AR autoantibody-positive LC sera will inhibit spike S1 binding to β2-AR peptide in competition ELISA (≥ 30% inhibition vs control), whereas β2-AR autoantibody-negative LC sera will not. The shared epitope is a 15-mer peptide with ≥ 60% sequence identity between spike S1 (residues 450-464) and β2-AR N-terminus (residues 30-44). Mice immunized with this peptide develop β2-AR autoantibodies and show elevated resting HR and reduced HRV compared to controls (p < 0.05).
- **Tier:** 1

#### H4: GPCR Autoantibody-Driven Mast Cell Sensitization — Bidirectional Amplification Loop in ME/CFS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The existing paper integrates mast cell activation (Theoharides 2026) and GPCR autoantibodies (Azcue 2026) as separate channels. However, mast cells express β2-adrenergic and M3 muscarinic receptors — these are the same GPCR targets of ME/CFS autoantibodies. β2-AR autoantibodies (agonistic) would chronically activate mast cell β2-AR, paradoxically not suppressing but priming mast cell degranulation (chronic stimulation causes β2-AR desensitization on mast cells, removing the adrenergic brake). M3 autoantibodies directly activate muscarinic receptors on mast cells, triggering histamine and tryptase release. This creates a bidirectional amplification: GPCR autoantibodies → mast cell degranulation → inflammatory mediators (histamine, PGD2, cytokines) → increased BBB permeability → more autoantibody CNS access → worse brainstem neuroinflammation → autonomic dysregulation → further immune dysregulation. The loop explains why MCAS-targeted treatment (cromolyn, ketotifen) and immunomodulation (IVIG, immunoadsorption) both show benefit — they interrupt different points of the same autoantibody-mast cell loop.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Moen2025NeuroimmuneLongCOVID, @Sunami2024GPCRPOTS, Theoharides 2026 (existing paper integration)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients with both elevated GPCR autoantibodies and MCAS diagnosis, serum tryptase and PGD2 correlate with β2-AR autoantibody titer (r > 0.4). Ex vivo skin mast cells from GPCR autoantibody-positive patients show heightened degranulation to substance P (≥ 2× histamine release vs controls) and this hyperreactivity is partially blocked by β2-AR antagonist (propranolol) and M3 antagonist (tiotropium). Immunoadsorption reduces both GPCR autoantibody titers and serum tryptase levels in parallel.
- **Tier:** 2

#### H5: Autonomic Ganglia as Shared Autoimmune Target — Ganglionic AChR Autoantibodies Bridge POTS and ME/CFS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Blitshteyn 2026 cites autonomic ganglia as autoimmune targets (ganglionic AChR antibodies) but the evidence is stronger in POTS than in ME/CFS. Ganglionic AChR antibodies target the nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (α3 subunit) on autonomic ganglia, impairing signal transmission from pre- to post-ganglionic autonomic neurons. If present in ME/CFS, these autoantibodies would explain: (a) the global autonomic failure pattern (not just orthostatic but also GI, thermoregulatory, pupillary, secretomotor) documented in severe ME/CFS, (b) the poor response to peripherally-acting autonomic drugs (midodrine, pyridostigmine) because the block is at the ganglion, not the effector, (c) the overlap with autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy (AAG) — a rare but treatable condition where IVIG and immunoadsorption are standard. The hypothesis: a subset of ME/CFS patients (particularly those with pan-autonomic involvement — all domains of COMPASS-31 abnormal) have ganglionic AChR autoantibodies that directly impair ganglionic transmission. These patients would be AAG look-alikes and would respond to the AAG treatment protocol (IVIG 2 g/kg monthly or immunoadsorption).
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @ElRhermoul2023AutoimmunityLCPOTS, @Ruzieh2017AutoantibodiesOI, Blitshteyn 2026 ganglionic AChR citation
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients with COMPASS-31 > 60 (severe pan-autonomic dysfunction), ≥ 15% are positive for ganglionic AChR (α3) autoantibodies vs ≤ 2% in healthy controls and ≤ 5% in mild ME/CFS (COMPASS-31 < 30). Ganglionic AChR-positive patients show autonomic reflex screen (ARS) abnormalities characteristic of AAG (diffuse postganglionic sudomotor failure, absent sympathetic skin response, cardiovagal impairment out of proportion to orthostatic HR change). In a pilot open-label trial, IVIG (2 g/kg monthly × 3 months) in ganglionic AChR-positive ME/CFS patients improves COMPASS-31 by ≥ 30% vs baseline.
- **Tier:** 2

#### H6: Cholinergic Anti-Inflammatory Pathway Failure as Common Proximal Mechanism — Vagus Nerve GPCR Autoantibody Blockade
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Blitshteyn 2026 identifies the vagus nerve / cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (CAP) as a common mechanism. The CAP operates through: vagal efferents → splenic nerve → norepinephrine release → β2-AR on splenic CD4+ T cells → acetylcholine release → α7-nAChR on macrophages → TNF-α suppression. GPCR autoantibodies (particularly M2/M4 muscarinic and β2-adrenergic) could block this pathway at multiple points: (a) M2 autoantibodies on cardiac vagal terminals impair vagal efferent signaling, reducing the anti-inflammatory signal before it reaches the spleen, (b) β2-AR autoantibodies on splenic T cells block the adrenergic-to-cholinergic conversion step, (c) M4 autoantibodies could interfere with presynaptic muscarinic autoreceptors on vagal terminals, further reducing acetylcholine release. The result: the vagus brake on systemic inflammation is disengaged, producing the low-grade inflammatory state characteristic of ME/CFS, POTS, and LC. This predicts: (a) CAP function (measured by TNF-α suppression during vagus nerve stimulation or slow breathing) is impaired in GPCR autoantibody-positive patients, (b) the degree of impairment correlates with autoantibody titer, (c) immunoadsorption restores CAP function. TaVNS (NP category) would work best in patients with intact post-ganglionic CAP but impaired vagal efferent drive; it would fail in patients with β2-AR autoantibodies blocking the splenic T cell step.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Moen2025NeuroimmuneLongCOVID, @Azcue2026 (existing paper, β2-AAb × HRV r=0.45)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, the magnitude of TNF-α suppression during transcutaneous vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS, 25 Hz, left tragus, 60 min) is inversely correlated with β2-AR autoantibody titer (r < -0.5). Patients with β2-AR autoantibody titers above the median show < 15% TNF-α suppression vs sham, while those below median show ≥ 30% suppression. Immunoadsorption (3 sessions) in β2-AR-positive patients restores taVNS-induced TNF-α suppression to ≥ 25% within 2 weeks of the last session.
- **Tier:** 1

### 2. Research Directions

#### R7: Head-to-Head GPCR Autoantibody Assay Comparison — ELISA vs. Luminex vs. Functional Bioassay in Same ME/CFS Cohort
- **Design:** N=200 ME/CFS (Canadian criteria), 100 healthy controls, 100 disease controls (POTS, LC). Blood drawn at single timepoint, processed 3 ways: (a) CellTrend ELISA (current standard, detects total IgG against β1, β2, α1, α2, M2, M3, M4, AT1, ET-A, ET-B), (b) Luminex bead-based multiplex (same targets, different detection chemistry — the method used in Germain 2025 null result), (c) functional bioassay (CHO cells expressing human β2-AR or M3-mAChR, measuring cAMP or Ca2+ flux — the Fedorowski/Kharraziha method). Primary outcome: concordance rate across methods (Cohen's κ for positive/negative classification per target). Secondary: correlation of titer with functional activity, assay sensitivity/specificity vs clinical diagnosis, and identification of discordant samples (positive by one method but not another) for mechanistic follow-up.
- **Rationale:** The central unresolved question in the GPCR autoantibody field: why does Germain 2025 (n=172, Luminex/REAP) find null results while ELISA studies (Gunning 2019, Fedorowski 2017, Azcue 2026) consistently find positive results? Possible explanations: assay sensitivity, detection chemistry (ELISA uses conformational epitopes better preserved by plate coating than bead coupling), IgG subclass bias (Luminex may miss IgG4), or sample handling differences. This head-to-head comparison in the same cohort would resolve the contradiction and establish a gold standard for clinical testing. The functional bioassay provides the ground truth — if the autoantibodies are pathogenic, the bioassay should detect them regardless of the binding assay method.
- **Evidence:** @Germain2025autoantibody (pre-existing, null), @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS, @Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic, @Kharraziha2020GPCRseverity, @Azcue2026 (existing paper)
- **Certainty:** 0.85 — needed regardless of outcome
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ELISA and functional bioassay show moderate-to-high concordance (Cohen's κ > 0.6) for β2-AR and M3 targets. Luminex shows lower concordance with both (κ < 0.4). Discordant samples (ELISA+/Luminex−) are enriched for IgG4 subclass. The functional bioassay confirms pathogenicity in ≥ 80% of ELISA-positive samples, establishing ELISA as the preferred clinical assay.
- **Tier:** 1

#### R8: Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG) for GPCR Autoantibody-Positive ME/CFS — Stratified RCT
- **Design:** N=60 GPCR autoantibody-positive (β2-AR and/or M3 titers > 2 SD above healthy mean), N=60 GPCR autoantibody-negative ME/CFS. Double-blind, placebo-controlled (IVIG 2 g/kg monthly vs saline × 4 months). Primary endpoint: change in PROMIS Fatigue at 4 months. Secondary: COMPASS-31, SF-36 PF, DSQ-PEM, autoantibody titers. Stratification by disease duration (< 3 years vs ≥ 3 years) and infection trigger (COVID vs other).
- **Rationale:** Blitshteyn 2026 cites IVIG as an immunomodulatory treatment used across all three conditions but notes that RCTs are pending. Several IVIG case series in ME/CFS (Novak 2022, the existing paper's MCAS-POTS IVIG data) show benefit, but no stratified RCT exists. The key design innovation: pre-select for GPCR autoantibody positivity (enrichment strategy). If GPCR autoantibodies are pathogenic (H1-H4), IVIG should work preferentially in this subgroup by providing anti-idiotypic antibodies that neutralize autoantibodies, Fc-mediated immune modulation, and FcRn saturation (accelerating pathogenic IgG clearance). The autoantibody-negative arm is the control showing specificity.
- **Evidence:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Moen2025NeuroimmuneLongCOVID, @Blitshteyn2025Immunotherapies (pre-existing)
- **Certainty:** 0.60
- **Falsifiable prediction:** GPCR autoantibody-positive group shows ≥ 15-point improvement in PROMIS Fatigue (T-score) vs placebo (p < 0.05); GPCR autoantibody-negative group shows < 5-point improvement vs placebo (p = NS). Improvement correlates with reduction in GPCR autoantibody titer (r > 0.4). Disease duration > 3 years is a negative predictor of response (responder rate ≤ 25% vs ≥ 50% in < 3 years).
- **Tier:** 1

#### R9: TSPO-PET in Dorsolateral Medulla — Brainstem Neuroinflammation Stratification in ME/CFS+POTS
- **Design:** N=40 ME/CFS+POTS (20 with prominent autonomic symptoms, 20 without), N=20 POTS without ME/CFS, N=20 healthy controls. All receive TSPO-PET (11C-PBR28 or 18F-GE-180) with region-of-interest analysis focused on dorsolateral medulla (NTS, RVLM, DMV), midbrain (PAG), and hypothalamus. Primary: group differences in TSPO signal (SUVr) in dorsolateral medulla. Secondary: correlation with GPCR autoantibody titer, COMPASS-31, standing HR increment, CBF (transcranial Doppler during HUT).
- **Rationale:** Blitshteyn 2026 cites brainstem neuroinflammation as a CNS localization but the evidence is modest (0.45-0.60). VanElzakker 2024 found TSPO-PET neuroinflammation in Long COVID but the brainstem was not the primary focus. Wagoner 2019 is pediatric and indirect. Direct TSPO-PET in the dorsolateral medulla with a focused hypothesis would: (a) establish whether brainstem neuroinflammation is present in ME/CFS as predicted, (b) test whether it correlates with autonomic symptom severity, (c) determine if GPCR autoantibody-positive patients have higher neuroinflammation (linking the two pillars — autoantibodies and neuroinflammation — in vivo).
- **Evidence:** @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation, @VanElzakker2024NeuroinflammationPASC, @Wagoner2019MedullaInflammation, @Rua2024BrainstemCOVID (pre-existing)
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS+POTS patients show significantly higher TSPO-PET signal in dorsolateral medulla compared to both healthy controls (p < 0.01) and POTS-only patients (p < 0.05). TSPO signal correlates with: (a) GPCR autoantibody titer (r > 0.4), (b) COMPASS-31 orthostatic subscale (r > 0.5), (c) standing HR increment (r > 0.3). GPCR autoantibody-positive patients have ≥ 20% higher medullary TSPO signal than autoantibody-negative patients.
- **Tier:** 1

#### R10: Passive Transfer of ME/CFS IgG into Mice — Assessing GPCR Autoantibody Pathogenicity for Autonomic and Fatigue Phenotypes
- **Design:** Purified IgG from 3 groups (n=10 donors each, each tested in 5 mice): (a) GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (β2-AR and/or M3 elevated), (b) GPCR autoantibody-negative ME/CFS, (c) healthy controls. Mice receive IP IgG (200 mg/kg, days 0, 2, 4). Outcomes: (a) autonomic: HRV (implanted telemetry), (b) fatigue: voluntary wheel running (days 5-12), (c) pain: von Frey and Hargreaves (days 6-8), (d) motor: open field and rotarod (day 7), (e) cognitive: novel object recognition (day 10). Blood-brain barrier permeability (Evans blue) at termination (day 12). Confirmatory: depletion of GPCR autoantibodies from positive IgG (protein A + GPCR peptide affinity column) → effect abolition.
- **Rationale:** Santos Guedes de Sá 2026 (Cell) showed LC IgG transfers fatigue, pain, and balance deficits to mice, and Chen 2026 and Mignolet 2026 showed LC IgG transfers autonomic dysfunction. The missing experiment: does ME/CFS IgG (specifically GPCR autoantibody-positive) produce comparable phenotypes? If yes, GPCR autoantibodies are causal (not epiphenomenal). If only GPCR-positive IgG transfers phenotypes, this establishes the specificity of GPCR autoantibodies as the pathogenic factor. The existing paper's certainty that GPCR autoantibodies are pathogenic (0.50-0.60 in ME/CFS) would increase substantially with direct passive transfer evidence.
- **Evidence:** Santos Guedes de Sá 2026 (Cell, LC IgG passive transfer — fatigue, pain, IENF damage), Chen 2026 (LC IgG — fatigue+autonomic), @Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic (POTS IgG contracts rat arterioles), @Li2019AdrenergicRabbit (POTS IgG → tachycardia in rabbits)
- **Certainty:** 0.70
- **Falsifiable prediction:** GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS IgG reduces voluntary wheel running by ≥ 30% vs control IgG (p < 0.01), reduces HRV (SDNN ↓ ≥ 25%), and produces mechanical allodynia (von Frey threshold ↓ ≥ 30%) in recipient mice. GPCR autoantibody-negative ME/CFS IgG produces none of these effects. Depletion of β2-AR autoantibodies from positive IgG abolishes ≥ 60% of the fatigue and autonomic effects, confirming target specificity.
- **Tier:** 1

#### R11: BC 007 Dose-Finding Trial in GPCR Autoantibody-Positive ME/CFS — Proof-of-Mechanism
- **Design:** Open-label, dose-escalation. N=30 GPCR autoantibody-positive (β2-AR and/or M3 > 2 SD). 3 cohorts of 10: (a) single IV infusion of BC 007 at 50 mg, (b) 150 mg, (c) 300 mg. Primary: functional neutralization of GPCR autoantibodies (change in CHO-cell cAMP/Ca2+ activity of patient serum at 48h post-infusion vs pre-infusion). Secondary: PROMIS Fatigue change at 1 week, 4 weeks; CBF (transcranial Doppler MCA velocity during HUT) at 1 week; microcirculation (retinal OCT-A) at 4 weeks; safety.
- **Rationale:** Hohberger 2021 showed BC 007 (DNA aptamer) neutralizes GPCR autoantibodies in a single LC/CFS patient with sustained 4-week benefit. Dose-ranging data is absent. Blitshteyn 2026 cites BC 007 as an emerging immunomodulatory therapy. The key question: is the neutralization dose-dependent, and what dose achieves ≥ 80% functional neutralization? The trial mechanism is direct: BC 007 binds to GPCR autoantibodies (not the receptor), forming inert complexes that are cleared. The single-infusion design allows the autoantibody time course to serve as its own control (autoantibodies should return to baseline after aptamer clearance, ~2-4 weeks).
- **Evidence:** @Hohberger2021NeutralizationGPCR (n=1), @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, Berlin Cures BC 007 development program
- **Certainty:** 0.45
- **Falsifiable prediction:** BC 007 produces dose-dependent functional neutralization: ≥ 80% reduction in CHO-cell GPCR activity at 300 mg, ≥ 50% at 150 mg, < 30% at 50 mg. Fatigue improvement (PROMIS ≥ 10 points at 1 week) occurs in ≥ 50% of the 300 mg cohort. CBF during HUT improves by ≥ 15% at 1 week post-300 mg infusion. Autoantibody functional activity returns to ≥ 80% of baseline by week 4, with parallel fatigue return.
- **Tier:** 1

#### R12: HLA-Targeted Screening for GPCR Autoantibodies in ME/CFS — Replication and Extension of Shin 2019
- **Design:** N=200 ME/CFS (Canadian criteria), N=100 POTS, N=100 healthy controls. All participants receive: (a) HLA typing (DRB1, DQB1, DPB1 — sequence-based typing), (b) GPCR autoantibody panel (CellTrend ELISA, 10 targets), (c) clinical phenotyping (COMPASS-31, CIS fatigue, DSQ-PEM, autonomic testing). Primary: replication of Shin 2019 DRB1*15:01 and DQB1*06:02 association with POTS. Extension: test these same HLA alleles for association with GPCR autoantibody titers (not just POTS diagnosis) in ME/CFS. Secondary: discover novel HLA associations (genome-wide in these MHC loci) for GPCR autoantibody positivity in ME/CFS.
- **Rationale:** Shin 2019 (n=93 Korean POTS) found DRB1*15:01 and DQB1*06:02 associations — suggesting autoimmune genetic predisposition. This has not been replicated in any population, nor extended to ME/CFS. If the same HLA alleles are associated with GPCR autoantibody positivity (not just POTS diagnosis), this establishes that these alleles mediate the autoimmune response to GPCR targets regardless of clinical phenotype — unifying the three conditions under a shared genetic predisposition. If HLA-DRB1*15:01 is enriched in GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS as well as POTS, the Blitshteyn 2026 thesis that these are neuroimmune disorders with shared genetic architecture is directly supported.
- **Evidence:** @Shin2019HLAPOTS, @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @ElRhermoul2023AutoimmunityLCPOTS
- **Certainty:** 0.55
- **Falsifiable prediction:** DRB1*15:01 allele frequency is: (a) higher in GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS than in autoantibody-negative ME/CFS (OR > 2.5), (b) comparable between GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS and POTS (no significant difference), (c) associated with higher β2-AR autoantibody titers (effect size d > 0.5). The association of DRB1*15:01 with GPCR autoantibody positivity in ME/CFS is independent of POTS comorbidity (logistic regression).
- **Tier:** 1

### 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

#### DR13: Immunoadsorption + Belimumab Combination — Remove Existing + Block New Autoantibodies
- **Mechanism:** Immunoadsorption (IA) removes existing GPCR autoantibodies but the effect is transient — B cell clones survive and repopulate the antibody pool within 3-6 months (Stein 2023, Tolle 2020). Belimumab (anti-BAFF, approved for SLE) blocks BAFF-mediated survival of autoreactive B cells, reducing new autoantibody production. The combination: IA removes existing pathogenic IgG → belimumab prevents the B cell clones from re-expanding → sustained autoantibody suppression. This addresses the fundamental limitation of IA (short-lived benefit) and is directly supported by the Blitshteyn 2026 framework (both IA and BAFF inhibition discussed as immunomodulatory strategies). The existing paper has speculation on this combination (certainty 0.42) but it needs operationalization as a clinical trial. Patient selection: IA-responsive (≥ 30% improvement post-IA, as per Stein 2024 criteria) + GPCR autoantibody-positive.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Stein2023immunoadsorptionInterim (pre-existing), @Tolle2020immunoadsorption (pre-existing), BAFF elevation in ME/CFS literature
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a 2-arm open-label pilot (n=10 IA+belimumab vs n=10 IA alone), the combination group retains ≥ 80% of peak post-IA fatigue improvement at 12 months (PROMIS), while the IA-alone group relapses to ≤ 30% of peak. Combination group shows sustained GPCR autoantibody suppression (titers ≤ 50% of baseline); IA-alone shows rebound to ≥ 80% baseline within 6 months.
- **Tier:** 1

#### DR14: Efgartigimod for FcRn-Mediated IgG Depletion in GPCR Autoantibody-Positive ME/CFS — FcRn Blockade as Autoantibody Clearance
- **Mechanism:** Efgartigimod (human IgG1 Fc fragment) binds FcRn with high affinity, competing out endogenous IgG from the neonatal Fc receptor. This accelerates IgG catabolism (half-life reduced from ~21 to ~5 days), lowering total IgG and pathogenic autoantibodies by 50-70% within 2-4 weeks. Unlike IVIG (which is cost-prohibitive for monthly use at $5-10K/dose and requires 4-6 hour infusions), efgartigimod is a 1-hour IV infusion or SC injection. Unlike immunoadsorption (invasive, requires vascular access), efgartigimod is simple IV administered monthly. The mechanism directly targets the autoantibody pillar of the Blitshteyn 2026 framework. Negative Phase 2 in unselected ME/CFS (ch14 treatment map) may reflect patient selection failure — the trial did not enrich for GPCR autoantibody positivity. A re-do with pre-selection.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, efgartigimod FDA approval in MG (ADAPT trial, Howard 2021, Lancet Neurology), existing paper ch14 treatment map (negative Phase 2 in unselected)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Efgartigimod (10 mg/kg IV weekly × 4, then biweekly × 8) in GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (n=20, open-label): (a) reduces total IgG by ≥ 50% at week 4, (b) reduces GPCR autoantibody functional activity (CHO cell assay) by ≥ 60%, (c) improves PROMIS Fatigue by ≥ 10 points in ≥ 40% of patients by week 8. Response is predicted by baseline autoantibody titer (high titer → greater benefit, r > 0.5).
- **Tier:** 2

#### DR15: Low-Dose Rituximab (200 mg × 2) in GPCR Autoantibody-Positive ME/CFS — B Cell Depletion Without PEM Trigger
- **Mechanism:** RituxME (Fluge 2019) was negative but used 500 mg × 2 (standard lymphoma dose) in unselected patients. GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS may respond to low-dose rituximab (200 mg × 2, two weeks apart) — a regimen that achieves B cell depletion in autoimmune cytopenias with lower cytokine release syndrome (CRS) risk. The standard rituximab dose causes significant CRS (fever, rigors, fatigue — exactly the symptoms that PEM-induction would amplify in ME/CFS). Low-dose rituximab achieves B cell depletion with minimal CRS in ITP, AIHA, and autoimmune neuropathy. If GPCR autoantibodies are the pathogenic target, partial B cell depletion (50-70% B cell reduction) may be sufficient to reduce autoantibody production without the fulminant CRS that may have confounded RituxME. Timing of retreatment: when B cells recover to ≥ 50% of baseline (monitored by flow cytometry), typically 4-6 months. The existing paper discusses rituximab as an immunomodulatory treatment but the negative RituxME result is a barrier; low-dose with autoantibody stratification may overcome it.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, Fluge 2019 (RituxME, negative), low-dose rituximab in ITP (Godeau 2007, Blood — 200 mg equivalent efficacy to 375 mg/m²), existing paper ch14 immunomodulatory treatments
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Low-dose rituximab (200 mg IV, days 0 and 14) in GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (n=15, open-label): (a) achieves B cell depletion (CD19+ < 20 cells/µL) in ≥ 80% of patients, (b) CRS rate (≥ grade 2 infusion reaction) ≤ 10%, (c) ≥ 30% of patients show ≥ 30% PROMIS Fatigue improvement at 3 months, (d) improvement correlates with reduction in GPCR autoantibody functional activity (r > 0.5).
- **Tier:** 2

#### DR16: Naltrexone (Low-Dose) + Verapamil Combination — TLR4 Blockade + Calcium Channel Modulation for GPCR Autoantibody-Driven Microglial Activation
- **Mechanism:** Low-dose naltrexone (LDN, 1.5-4.5 mg) is a Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) antagonist at low concentrations — it blocks microglial TLR4-mediated neuroinflammation. Verapamil (L-type calcium channel blocker, 40-80 mg bid) reduces intracellular Ca2+ flux, which is the downstream signal from GPCR autoantibody-induced receptor activation (both α1-adrenergic and M3 muscarinic signal through Gq → PLC → IP3 → Ca2+). The combination: LDN reduces microglial inflammatory output; verapamil blunts the cellular response to GPCR autoantibody signaling in neurons, cardiomyocytes, and vascular smooth muscle. For the ME/CFS+POTS overlap, verapamil may also reduce standing HR (used off-label for POTS in some clinics) and improve coronary microvascular flow. The rationale builds on the neuroimmune framework: if GPCR autoantibodies drive neuroinflammation via both direct neuronal GPCR activation and microglial TLR4 signaling, a dual blockade approach targets both arms. LDN is already widely used in ME/CFS (anecdotal benefit, no RCT). Verapamil is rarely used but mechanistically rational.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic (GPCR AAb activates cellular signaling through Ca2+), LDN → TLR4 mechanism (Younger 2014, Pain Medicine), verapamil POTS use (limited)
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8-week LDN (4.5 mg qhs) + verapamil ER (120 mg qd) in GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS+POTS (n=20, open-label): (a) ≥ 40% show ≥ 30% improvement in PROMIS Fatigue, (b) standing HR decreases by ≥ 5 bpm (verapamil effect), (c) serum neurofilament light (NfL) decreases by ≥ 15% (reduced neuroaxonal damage), (d) LPS-stimulated PBMC TNF-α production (ex vivo) decreases by ≥ 25% (reduced TLR4 response).
- **Tier:** 3

#### DR17: C1-Esterase Inhibitor (Berinert) for Complement-Mediated Endothelial Damage in GPCR Autoantibody-Positive POTS+ME/CFS
- **Mechanism:** The existing paper's complement-glycocalyx speculation (ch10) proposes that complement activation degrades endothelial glycocalyx, worsening orthostatic intolerance. GPCR autoantibodies (IgG1 subclass) fix complement via C1q binding, activating the classical complement pathway and generating C5a and C3a (anaphylatoxins that degranulate mast cells and activate endothelial cells). C1-esterase inhibitor (C1-INH, the endogenous regulator of the classical and lectin complement pathways) blocks complement activation at the earliest step, before C5a/C3a generation. In hereditary angioedema (C1-INH deficiency), C1-INH replacement prevents bradykinin-mediated vascular leak — a mechanism that parallels the glycocalyx loss and endothelial permeability described in POTS/ME/CFS. For the subset of GPCR autoantibody-positive patients with elevated complement split products (C3a, C5a, sC5b-9), C1-INH could: (a) block complement-mediated endothelial activation, (b) reduce mast cell degranulation triggered by C5a/C3a, (c) protect glycocalyx integrity. The existing paper's HG factor structure (ch14 treatment map) lists "Complement Activation Cascade" but no specific treatment.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, ch10 complement-glycocalyx speculation (certainty 0.35), C1-INH in HAE (well-established), complement activation in autoimmune disease
- **Certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS+POTS with elevated C3a/C5a (> 1.5 × ULN), C1-INH (1000 U IV, then 1000 U weekly × 3, open-label n=10): (a) reduces C3a/C5a to ≤ 1.2 × ULN within 24h, (b) reduces standing HR by ≥ 8 bpm at week 4, (c) plasma glycocalyx markers (syndecan-1) decrease by ≥ 25% from baseline, (d) tryptase levels decrease by ≥ 30% (reduced mast cell degranulation).
- **Tier:** 3

### 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

#### SU18: Cholinergic Precursor Stack (Alpha-GPC + Huperzine A + CDP-Choline) for Vagal Anti-Inflammatory Tone Enhancement
- **Mechanism:** Blitshteyn 2026 identifies vagus nerve / cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (CAP) as a shared mechanism. Enhancing cholinergic tone could augment CAP function, compensating for the vagal dysfunction hypothesized in ME/CFS. Alpha-GPC (L-alpha-glycerylphosphorylcholine) is a central acetylcholine precursor that crosses the BBB and increases neuronal ACh synthesis. Huperzine A is a reversible acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (from Chinese club moss) that increases synaptic ACh half-life. CDP-choline (citicoline) provides both choline and uridine — supporting membrane phosphatidylcholine synthesis and ACh production. The stack: alpha-GPC 600 mg + Huperzine A 50-100 µg + CDP-choline 500 mg, taken before the two main meals (when vagal activity should be highest for digestive function). The rationale: augmenting cholinergic tone at the vagal efferent terminal should: (a) improve gastrointestinal motility (M3 receptor activation in gut), (b) enhance CAP-mediated TNF-α suppression, (c) increase cardiac vagal tone (HRV improvement). Risk: excessive cholinergic stimulation (bradycardia, salivation, lacrimation, diarrhea) — Huperzine A dose must be well below the 200 µg range used in Alzheimer's.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune (CAP dysfunction), alpha-GPC cholinergic augmentation literature, Huperzine A safety at low doses (Alzheimer's trials: 200-400 µg bid), existing paper HRV and vagal tone sections
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8-week cholinergic stack in ME/CFS (n=20, open-label): (a) increases 24h HRV (rMSSD) by ≥ 15% vs baseline, (b) reduces LPS-stimulated PBMC TNF-α production ex vivo by ≥ 20%, (c) improves COMPASS-31 GI subscale by ≥ 25%, (d) reduces PROMIS Fatigue by ≥ 8 points. The effects are larger in patients with low baseline cholinergic tone (HRV rMSSD < 20 ms).
- **Tier:** 3

#### SU19: Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) + Luteolin + Pterostilbene — Glial Cell Modulation Stack for Brainstem Neuroinflammation
- **Mechanism:** Blitshteyn 2025 brainstem neuroinflammation and the experimental medicine framework suggest microglial activation as a key CNS mechanism. PEA is an endocannabinoid-like molecule that activates PPAR-α on microglia, reducing pro-inflammatory cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6) and shifting toward a neuroprotective phenotype. Luteolin is a flavonoid that inhibits mast cell degranulation and microglial activation (TLR4, MyD88 pathways). Pterostilbene (a resveratrol analog with better bioavailability) activates SIRT1 and NRF2, reducing oxidative stress in microglia and astrocytes. The combination: PEA (400 mg bid) + luteolin (100 mg qd, micronized for bioavailability) + pterostilbene (50 mg bid). This is the natural counterpart to immunomodulatory drugs but targeting CNS glial activation rather than systemic autoantibodies. The stack is already used in ME/CFS community and 7.5 mg daily PEA is available in the UK (food supplement). High-dose PEA (1200 mg/day) has been used for 8-12 weeks. Specificity: patients with evidence of neuroinflammation (TSPO-PET positive or elevated CSF NfL) would be the target subgroup. Capsaicin 1% topical (also in DR17) could be a co-intervention to improve blood-brain barrier function via TRPV1-mediated BBB tightening.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation, @VanElzakker2024NeuroinflammationPASC, PEA mechanism (Mattace Raso 2014, Int J Mol Sci), luteolin MCAS use (Theoharides 2020 pre-existing), pterostilbene SIRT1 (McCormack 2011)
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 12-week PEA+luteolin+pterostilbene in ME/CFS (n=20, open-label, enriched for neuroinflammatory profile — CSF NfL > 75th percentile or TSPO-PET+): (a) reduces CSF NfL by ≥ 20% vs baseline, (b) improves PROMIS Fatigue by ≥ 10 points, (c) improves processing speed (Digit Symbol) by ≥ 15%, (d) improves COWA (verbal fluency) by ≥ 15%. The CSF NfL reduction correlates with fatigue improvement (r > 0.4).
- **Tier:** 3

#### SU20: Sphingosine-1-Phosphate (S1P) Precursors — Serine + Palmitate + Vitamin B6 for GPCR Receptor Trafficking and Immune Homeostasis
- **Mechanism:** S1P is a sphingolipid signaling molecule that: (a) regulates lymphocyte egress from lymph nodes (S1P1/5 receptors), (b) modulates GPCR trafficking and signaling at the cell surface (S1P interacts with GPCR signaling platforms in lipid rafts), (c) maintains endothelial barrier integrity. In ME/CFS, the ceramide:S1P rheostat is shifted toward ceramide (existing paper ch17 universal mechanisms). Supplementing S1P precursors (L-serine 500 mg + palmitate from dietary fat + vitamin B6 as cofactor for serine palmitoyltransferase) could partially shift the rheostat toward S1P. The relevance to the Blitshteyn 2026 framework: if GPCR autoantibodies disrupt receptor signaling, sufficient S1P levels are necessary for proper GPCR trafficking to the cell surface (S1P2/3 regulates GPCR internalization). S1P is also the ligand for S1P1 on endothelial cells, which maintains barrier function — relevant to the BBB disruption model. This is mechanistically upstream of most interventions: rather than blocking autoantibodies or their effects, it supports the cellular machinery that maintains proper GPCR expression and function.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, ceramide:S1P rheostat in ME/CFS (paper ch17), S1P-GPCR interaction literature (Pyne 2010, Nat Rev Mol Cell Biol), serine palmitoyltransferase pathway
- **Certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8-week S1P precursor supplementation (L-serine 1000 mg bid + B6 25 mg qd with meals containing fat) in ME/CFS (n=20, open-label): (a) increases plasma S1P by ≥ 30% from baseline, (b) reduces ceramide/S1P ratio by ≥ 25%, (c) improves COMPASS-31 autonomic score by ≥ 15%, (d) improves PROMIS Fatigue by ≥ 8 points. Baseline low S1P (< 300 pmol/mL) predicts greater response.
- **Tier:** 3

#### SU21: NAD+ Precursors (NR or NMN) + Apigenin for CD38 Inhibition — Preserving NAD+ for SIRT1-Mediated Autonomic Function
- **Mechanism:** NAD+ depletion is documented in ME/CFS (paper ch20-23). CD38 is the primary NADase (consuming NAD+ to generate cADPR). GPCR autoantibody-mediated signaling activates CD38 in immune cells and neurons via Ca2+ mobilization (M3 muscarinic/Gq → PLC → IP3 → Ca2+ → CD38). Chronic GPCR autoantibody agonism would thus accelerate NAD+ consumption, contributing to the energy deficiency. Apigenin (a flavonoid, 50 mg) is a CD38 inhibitor that blocks NAD+ consumption, preserving NAD+ for SIRT1 (which regulates mitochondrial biogenesis, PGC-1α, and autonomic function). NR (nicotinamide riboside, 300 mg bid) or NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide, 250 mg qd) replenish NAD+. The stack: NR/NMN + apigenin to both reduce NAD+ consumption and increase supply. SIRT1 deacetylates and activates PGC-1α, which drives mitochondrial biogenesis — a countermeasure to the mitochondrial deficit common across the three conditions.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, CD38-NAD+ depletion axis (Chini 2020, Nat Rev Nephrol), NR in ME/CFS (Castro-Marrero 2021 — small pilot, modest benefit), NR in LC (Altay 2020, Biosci Rep — positive pilot)
- **Certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 12-week NR (300 mg bid) + apigenin (50 mg qd) in GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (n=20, open-label): (a) increases whole blood NAD+ by ≥ 40% vs baseline, (b) improves PROMIS Fatigue by ≥ 10 points, (c) improves resting HRV (rMSSD) by ≥ 15%, (d) improves VO2max ramp CPET by ≥ 5% (submaximal only — graded cycle test to 80% of predicted HRmax, not exhaustive). Benefits are larger in patients with high baseline CD38 activity (PBMC CD38 expression > median).
- **Tier:** 3

### 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

#### NP22: Transcutaneous Auricular Vagus Nerve Stimulation (taVNS) Stratified by GPCR Autoantibody Profile
- **Mechanism:** taVNS (left tragus, 20-25 Hz, 0.5-2 mA, 30-60 min daily) activates the auricular branch of the vagus nerve, increasing vagal efferent outflow to heart, lungs, and GI tract, and activating the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (TNF-α suppression via spleen). Blitshteyn 2026 identifies CAP dysfunction as a common mechanism. However, as proposed in H6, β2-AR autoantibodies may block the splenic T cell step of the CAP, rendering taVNS ineffective in the subset of patients with high β2-AR autoantibody titers. The stratification: (a) low β2-AR AAb → taVNS should improve HRV, reduce TNF-α, and improve fatigue, (b) high β2-AR AAb → taVNS may be ineffective because the anti-inflammatory signal is blocked at the splenic T cell β2-AR. This stratification explains the variable taVNS response in ME/CFS (some benefit, some null). The experiment: 8-week taVNS (30 min daily) with autoantibody stratification as the predictor. If correct, autoantibody-negative patients respond to taVNS; autoantibody-positive patients need immunomodulation first, then taVNS as maintenance.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune (CAP dysfunction), @Azcue2026 (β2-AAb × HRV correlation), taVNS in ME/CFS pilot data, @H6 (CAP-Ab hypothesis)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In an 8-week taVNS trial (N=50 ME/CFS, stratified by β2-AR AAb titer), low-AAb patients show: (a) ≥ 20% HRV improvement (rMSSD), (b) ≥ 20% reduction in serum TNF-α, (c) ≥ 8-point PROMIS Fatigue improvement vs sham. High-AAb patients show < 10% improvement on all outcomes. The β2-AR AAb × treatment interaction is significant (p < 0.05). High-AAb patients who undergo immunoadsorption (3 sessions) before taVNS show restored taVNS response at 4 weeks post-IA.
- **Tier:** 1

#### NP23: Slow Paced Breathing (0.1 Hz) with Heart Rate Variability Biofeedback — Targeting Baroreflex Gain and Vagal Tone
- **Mechanism:** Slow paced breathing at 0.1 Hz (6 breaths/min) entrains the baroreflex, producing respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) and increasing heart rate variability (HRV) via vagal modulation. In ME/CFS+POTS, 0.1 Hz breathing may: (a) improve baroreflex sensitivity (the gain of the pressure-mediating feedback loop), (b) increase vagal efferent tone, counteracting the sympathetic predominance, (c) entrain the autonomic nervous system to a more flexible, less rigid state. The key distinction from existing work (Ryabkova 2024: PCC partially normalizes HRV during SPB but ME/CFS does not): ME/CFS patients may not show acute HRV improvement during SPB because vagal receptors are blocked by M2/M4 autoantibodies (H6) — but chronic SPB training (10 min twice daily × 8 weeks) may restore baroreflex sensitivity through neuroplasticity even in the presence of autoantibodies, as an "exercise for the baroreflex." This is distinct from taVNS (direct electrical vagal stimulation) — SPB is behavioral vagal conditioning. The Blitshteyn 2026 framework predicts that SPB would be more effective in patients with low cholinergic autoantibody titers and less effective in high-titer patients (similar to NP22 stratification).
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, Ryabkova 2024 (SPB + HRV in PCC vs ME/CFS, pre-existing), baroreflex gain training literature (Joseph 2005, Appl Psychophysiol Biofeedback), slow breathing in POTS (some clinical use)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8-week daily HRV-biofeedback + 0.1 Hz SPB (10 min bid) in ME/CFS+POTS (N=30): (a) resting baroreflex sensitivity increases by ≥ 25% vs baseline, (b) standing HR increment decreases by ≥ 5 bpm, (c) COMPASS-31 orthostatic subscale improves by ≥ 20%, (d) PROMIS Fatigue improves by ≥ 8 points. M2/M4 autoantibody titer inversely predicts response (b = -0.3, p < 0.05). Patients with low M2/M4 titers (< median) show ≥ 2× clinical response vs high-titer patients.
- **Tier:** 2

#### NP24: Graded Cold Pressor/Cold Face Immersion Autonomic Conditioning — Baroreflex Resetting for Hyperadrenergic POTS
- **Mechanism:** Cold face immersion (CFI, diving reflex — face immersion in 10°C water for 15-30 sec) triggers the trigeminal-cardiac reflex: trigeminal nerve (V1) → spinal trigeminal nucleus → NTS → vagal activation → bradycardia and peripheral vasoconstriction. This is the most potent physiological vagal stimulus known (diving reflex can produce HR drops to 30-40 bpm). Repeated CFI (graded: start at room temperature, decrease over 4 weeks to 10°C) could: (a) strengthen the trigeminal-vagal reflex arc (synaptic plasticity at NTS level), (b) increase resting vagal tone and baroreflex sensitivity, (c) provide a "vagal reset" that reduces sympathetic predominance. For hyperadrenergic POTS (high baseline sympathetic tone, normal SV, van Campen 2024 subtype), graded CFI provides a non-pharmacological method to recruit the full vagal reserve. The trigeminal-vagal reflex bypasses the autoantibody blockade at the GPCR level (H6) because the vagal efferents are activated directly via the trigeminal brainstem pathway rather than through the CAP's splenic β2-AR step. Blitshteyn 2026's brainstem neuroinflammation framework (dorsolateral medulla, NTS) is directly relevant — CFI engages the same NTS circuitry hypothesized to be dysfunctional.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation, diving reflex physiology (Khurana 1980, J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry — classical), existing paper cold face immersion mechanism (ch10), trigeminal-cardiac reflex (Schaller 2007, J Neurol)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 4-week graded CFI (3×/day, starting 22°C → decreasing 2°C per week to 10°C, 15 sec immersion) in hyperadrenergic POTS+ME/CFS (n=15, open-label): (a) increases resting HRV (rMSSD) by ≥ 25%, (b) reduces standing plasma NE by ≥ 20%, (c) reduces standing HR by ≥ 8 bpm, (d) improves PROMIS Fatigue by ≥ 8 points. Effect persists at 4-week follow-up (learned reflex potentiation).
- **Tier:** 3

#### C25: Tiered Neuroimmune Care Protocol by Autoantibody-Autonomic Profile
- **Framework:** A 4-tier protocol stratifying patients by GPCR autoantibody status + autonomic function testing + disease severity, linking each tier to specific interventions (pharmacological and non-pharmacological):

| Tier | Profile | Interventions |
|------|---------|---------------|
| T1 | GPCR AAb(-), mild-moderate dysautonomia | taVNS (NP22) + 0.1 Hz SPB biofeedback (NP23) + cholinergic stack (SU18) + pacing |
| T2 | GPCR AAb(+), moderate dysautonomia | T1 + immunomodulation (IVIG or IA or BC 007, depending on availability) + LDN+verapamil (DR16) |
| T3 | GPCR AAb(+), severe dysautonomia, MCAS+ | T2 + mast cell stabilization (cromolyn/ketotifen) + graded CFI (NP24) + S1P precursors (SU20) |
| T4 | GPCR AAb(+), pan-autonomic failure, bedbound | T3 + efgartigimod or low-dose rituximab (DR14/15) + C1-INH if complement+ (DR17) + NAD+ stack (SU21) |

- **Rationale:** The current approach to ME/CFS+POTS treatment is highly heterogeneous — patients try interventions in random order, leading to frustration and treatment abandonment (Uppal 2026). Stratifying by GPCR autoantibody status (the core biomarker from the Blitshteyn 2026 framework) provides a rational triage: autoantibody-negative patients can focus on non-pharmacological vagal enhancement and pacing; autoantibody-positive patients need immunomodulation as a prerequisite. The protocol is testable in a stepped-wedge cluster RCT across 3-4 clinics with primary endpoint of COMPASS-31 at 6 months.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS, @Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic, @Uppal2026 (qualitative patient experiences, pre-existing)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Stepped-wedge implementation (4 clinics, 12-month roll-out, N=200): tiered protocol reduces COMPASS-31 at 6 months by ≥ 20% vs usual care (p < 0.01). T2-T4 patients receive immunomodulation ≥ 8 weeks earlier than usual care (earlier recognition of autoantibody-positive profile). Treatment adherence is ≥ 70% across all tiers.
- **Tier:** 1

#### C26: Sequential Immunomodulation + Vagal Stimulation Protocol — IA → Belimumab → taVNS Maintenance
- **Mechanism:** The three pillars of neuroimmune modulation — autoantibody removal, autoantibody production suppression, and vagal anti-inflammatory enhancement — applied sequentially: Phase 1 (weeks 1-3): Immunoadsorption (3 sessions) to rapidly remove existing GPCR autoantibodies. Phase 2 (weeks 4-24): Belimumab monthly to prevent autoantibody rebound by blocking BAFF-dependent B cell survival. Phase 3 (weeks 8-24): taVNS daily to enhance vagal CAP function, now effective because β2-AR autoantibodies have been reduced (H6). The rationale: each step enables the next. IA alone fails because B cells rebound. Belimumab after IA suppresses rebound but takes 4-8 weeks for effect on autoantibody levels (BAFF blockade reduces B cell survival slowly). taVNS fails in high-autoantibody states (NP22) but should work once autoantibodies are reduced. The sequential protocol may achieve sustained remission by covering all three mechanisms.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Tolle2020immunoadsorption (pre-existing), @Stein2023immunoadsorptionInterim (pre-existing), @H6 (CAP-Ab hypothesis), @NP22 (taVNS stratification)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a single-arm pilot (n=10 GPCR AAb+, moderate-severe ME/CFS+POTS), the IA→belimumab→taVNS sequential protocol achieves: (a) ≥ 50% PROMIS Fatigue improvement at 6 months in ≥ 60% of patients, (b) sustained GPCR AAb suppression (≤ 30% of baseline at 6 months), (c) taVNS-induced TNF-α suppression restored from baseline (at week 12, taVNS suppresses TNF-α by ≥ 25% vs sham). IA-alone historical controls (matched) show ≤ 25% sustained improvement at 6 months.
- **Tier:** 2

#### C27: Home-Based GPCR Autoantibody Monitoring + Treatment Adjustment Loop — Self-Titration Protocol
- **Framework:** Dried blood spot (DBS) sampling enables at-home GPCR autoantibody monitoring (DBS is stable at room temperature for 2 weeks, compatible with ELISA). A self-titration protocol: patients collect DBS weekly for 4 weeks to establish baseline variance, then monthly. When GPCR autoantibody titer rises above the individual's 75th percentile (exceedance of personal threshold), the patient activates a pre-defined step-up in their treatment tier (e.g., increase LDN dose from 1.5 to 3.0 mg, or schedule extra taVNS sessions, or request IA). When titer falls below the 25th percentile (trough), the patient may step down (reduce immunomodulation, rely on maintenance taVNS+SPB). This creates a closed-loop treatment system where autoantibody titer (the core biomarker) guides therapy in real time, analogous to glucose monitoring in diabetes. Practicality: DBS kit costs ~$15/sample; CellTrend ELISA for 10 targets costs ~$200/sample. Monthly monitoring would cost ~$215/month — comparable to a tier 3 drug co-pay. For the severe ME/CFS population (bedbound, unable to attend clinic), this is the only feasible monitoring modality.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, DBS autoantibody monitoring feasibility (already used in SLE, HIV, hepatitis), @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS (ELISA-based), existing paper patient monitoring sections
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a 6-month feasibility pilot (n=15 GPCR AAb+, moderate-severe ME/CFS), home DBS sampling with monthly CellTrend analysis: (a) ≥ 80% of scheduled samples are returned (feasibility), (b) ≥ 50% of patients show ≥ 1 actionable titer exceedance (triggering treatment adjustment), (c) the time from exceedance to treatment adjustment is ≤ 7 days (vs 4-6 weeks in usual care), (d) titer exceedance events are associated with PEM flares (OR ≥ 3.0 for PEM in the week following exceedance vs non-exceedance weeks).
- **Tier:** 2

### 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

#### M28: Intrathecal Autoantibody Production ODE — Compartment Model for CNS vs Peripheral GPCR AAb Kinetics
- **Extension:** Add a 2-compartment (plasma + CSF) autoantibody model to the existing GPCR AAb ODE (ch28 speculation). Variables: A_p(t) = plasma autoantibody titer, A_csf(t) = CSF autoantibody titer. Equations: dA_p/dt = k_prod·B(t) - k_clear·A_p - k_brain_in·A_p + k_brain_out·A_csf; dA_csf/dt = k_intrathecal·B_cns(t) + k_brain_in·A_p - k_brain_out·A_csf - k_csf_clear·A_csf. The key addition: k_intrathecal represents B cell/plasma cell activity within the CNS compartment (relevant to H2 — germinal center-like structures in dorsolateral medulla). The BBB permeability term k_brain_in is regulated by inflammation (TNF-α, IL-1β) and mast cell mediators (histamine, tryptase) — coupling to the existing P_BBB model (ch28). The model predicts: (a) early disease: A_csf ∼ k_brain_in·A_p (peripheral source dominates), (b) established disease: k_intrathecal becomes the dominant term as CNS B cell aggregates form, A_csf/A_p ratio increases, (c) immunoadsorption removes A_p rapidly but A_csf declines slowly (k_csf_clear is slow ∼7-14 days half-life), explaining discordant clinical response (slow CNS improvement despite rapid peripheral autoantibody clearance). This directly informs treatment sequencing: intrathecal autoantibody production (if present) requires CNS-penetrant immunomodulation (rituximab CNS penetration is limited; daratumumab does not enter CNS).
- **Existing model context:** ch28 GPCR AAb ODE (certainty 0.55), ch28 B cell ODE (certainty 0.45), ch28 BBB model P_BBB
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The 2-compartment model fitted to paired serum+CSF autoantibody data (n=20 ME/CFS, 3 timepoints over 6 months) shows: (a) A_csf/A_p ratio > 0.05 (CSF concentration relative to plasma) in ≥ 30% of patients, (b) patients with high intrathecal production (k_intrathecal > 0.1/day) have CSF oligoclonal bands (≥ 2 bands not present in serum), (c) k_intrathecal correlates with TSPO-PET signal in dorsolateral medulla (r > 0.5). The model correctly predicts that IA reduces A_p by ≥ 70% but A_csf by ≤ 30% within 2 weeks.
- **Tier:** 2

#### M29: Cholinergic Anti-Inflammatory Pathway (CAP) ODE — Extending the Vagus-Immune Axis
- **Extension:** Add a 4-variable CAP subsystem to the existing integrated model (ch30). Variables: V_eff(t) = vagal efferent activity, A_ch(t) = acetylcholine concentration at splenic T cell synapse, T_act(t) = activated splenic CD4+ T cell fraction, M_TNF(t) = splenic macrophage TNF-α production. Equations: V_eff driven by baroreflex output (existing S/V from ch29) and external input (taVNS current, SPB respiratory modulation). A_ch = f(V_eff, M2/M4 AAb) where M2/M4 AAb block ACh release at vagal terminals via presynaptic autoreceptor desensitization (H6). T_act = g(A_ch, β2-AR AAb) where β2-AR AAb block the conversion from ACh signal to T cell activation (the CAP bottleneck). M_TNF = h(T_act) — TNF-α suppression is the output. The β2-AR AAb blockade parameter α_β2 = 1/(1 + [AAb_β2]/K_d) reduces the gain of the vagal anti-inflammatory signal. Coupling to systemic inflammation: M_TNF feeds into the existing cytokine model (ch30). The model predicts: (a) above a critical β2-AR AAb threshold, the CAP is completely disabled — taVNS and SPB produce no TNF-α suppression, matching NP22/H6, (b) below threshold, CAP gain increases with V_eff — taVNS is effective, (c) immunoadsorption rapidly restores CAP function by reducing [AAb_β2] below threshold.
- **Existing model context:** ch30 integrated 64-variable model, ch29 baroreflex S/V, existing cytokine network
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The CAP ODE fitted to taVNS-TNF-α time course data predicts: (a) the relationship between [AAb_β2] and taVNS-induced TNF-α suppression is sigmoidal (not linear) with a critical threshold at ∼2 × ULN, (b) above threshold, taVNS suppresses TNF-α by < 10%, (c) below threshold, by > 30%. The critical threshold prediction is testable: stratify ME/CFS patients by β2-AR AAb quartile and measure acute taVNS TNF-α suppression (single session). The quartile × suppression interaction should follow the sigmoid, not a linear gradient.
- **Tier:** 2

#### M30: GPCR Antibody-Receptor Binding + Internalization ODE — Predicting Receptor Availability and Signaling Output
- **Extension:** Extend the GPCR AAb ODE (ch28) with explicit receptor dynamics: free receptors R(t), autoantibody-occupied receptors [AAb·R](t), internalized receptors R_int(t). The signaling output S(t) depends on the balance: agonistic autoantibodies activate the receptor (signal like the endogenous ligand), while antagonistic autoantibodies block signaling. Total receptors = R + [AAb·R] + R_int. The binding equilibrium: d[AAb·R]/dt = k_on·AAb·R - k_off·[AAb·R] - k_int·[AAb·R] (internalization of occupied receptors). The internalization rate k_int depends on β-arrestin recruitment, which is AAb-epitope-specific (some autoantibodies favor β-arrestin → strong internalization → desensitization; others favor G protein → sustained signaling). The model captures the cell-based functional data from Fedorowski 2017/Kharraziha 2020: different autoantibody profiles produce different signaling/internalization ratios, explaining variable clinical phenotypes. For example: α1-AR agonistic AAb → strong Gq signaling → vasoconstriction → supporting standing BP (explaining why some POTS patients have normal BP despite tachycardia). M2 AAb with β-arrestin bias → receptor internalization → vagal withdrawal → increased HR (no vasodilation). The model predicts: (a) patients with internalization-biased autoantibodies (rapid k_int) will have worse autonomic function (because fewer functional receptors remain on cell surface), (b) immunoadsorption removes AAb → R recovers on a timescale of days (receptor recycling from intracellular pools), (c) this recovery is faster for receptors with high recycling rates (β2-AR recycles rapidly, M3 recycles slowly).
- **Existing model context:** ch28 GPCR AAb ODE (certainty 0.55), @Fedorowski2017Antiadrenergic (functional cell assay), @Kharraziha2020GPCRseverity
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a cohort of GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (n=20), the model fitted to individual patient data (autoantibody titer + in vitro signaling/internalization ratio for each GPCR + clinical autonomic function) correctly predicts: (a) which GPCR targets produce the strongest symptom associations (those with internalization-biased AAb → stronger phenotype), (b) the timescale of autonomic improvement after immunoadsorption (days for β2-AR-mediated effects, weeks for M3-mediated effects — because M3 recycles slowly). The in vitro signaling/internalization ratio for a patient's IgG (measured in CHO cells) correlates with standing HR increment (r > 0.5) and ΔETCO2 during HUT (r < -0.4).
- **Tier:** 3

#### M31: Multi-Scale Neuroimmune Network DAG — Integrating GPCR Autoantibodies, Brainstem Neuroinflammation, Vagal CAP, and Autonomic Output
- **Extension:** The existing DAG (ch29) includes autoantibodies and neuroinflammation as nodes but does not capture the Blitshteyn 2026 framework's specific connections. A multi-scale DAG with 4 layers: (1) **Genetic/Trigger layer**: HLA-DRB1*15:01, post-infectious trigger (SARS-CoV-2, EBV), molecular mimicry epitope → (2) **Autoimmune layer**: GPCR autoantibody production (β2, M2, M4, α1), intrathecal synthesis (H2), ganglionic AChR antibodies (H5) → (3) **CNS layer**: brainstem neuroinflammation (TSPO-PET+ dorsolateral medulla), baroreflex gain resetting via NTS GPCR internalization (H1), CAP blockade (H6), neurovascular coupling failure → (4) **Clinical output layer**: orthostatic HR increment, CBF, fatigue, PEM, pain, cognitive dysfunction. The canonical paths: Path A (GPCR AAb → baroreflex resetting → orthostatic tachycardia) and Path B (GPCR AAb → CAP blockade → systemic inflammation + vagal withdrawal → fatigue). Path C (intrathecal AAb → brainstem neuroinflammation → central sensitization → PEM amplification). The DAG would identify: (a) the most central nodes (highest betweenness centrality = intervention targets), (b) the minimal set of nodes that need to be measured for patient stratification, (c) the predicted independent effect of each intervention (taVNS influences V_eff → CAP → TNF-α pathway; IA influences AAb → all downstream paths).
- **Existing model context:** ch29 DAG, ch30 integrated 64-variable model, ch32 GSA
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The multi-scale DAG's centrality analysis identifies GPCR autoantibody production node and brainstem neuroinflammation node as the two highest-betweenness nodes — interventions targeting either (IA for AAb, taVNS or anti-inflammatory for brainstem) should produce the largest downstream effects. The DAG predicts that combining both (IA + taVNS sequential, C26) should produce supra-additive effects because they target independent high-centrality nodes. The predicted effect size of IA + taVNS > IA alone + taVNS alone (interaction term) is testable in a 2×2 factorial trial design.
- **Tier:** 1

### 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

#### CD32: Sjögren's Syndrome ⇔ ME/CFS — Shared M3 Muscarinic Autoantibodies and Sicca-Autonomic Phenotype
- **Mechanism:** Sjögren's syndrome (SjS) is characterized by anti-Ro/SSA and anti-La/SSB autoantibodies, but also includes M3 muscarinic receptor autoantibodies in a subset (Waterman 2000, J Autoimmun). M3 autoantibodies in SjS block salivary and lacrimal gland muscarinic signaling, producing sicca (dry eyes, dry mouth). In ME/CFS+POTS, M3 autoantibodies are associated with GI dysmotility (Sunami 2024, Ekman 2025) and autonomic dysfunction. The bridge: Sjögren's M3 autoantibodies → exocrine gland dysfunction → sicca; ME/CFS M3 autoantibodies → GI smooth muscle dysfunction → IBS-like symptoms. In both, the mechanism is impaired M3 signaling (shared antibody target, different end organs). This predicts: (a) ME/CFS patients with high M3 autoantibody titers will have sicca symptoms (dry eyes, dry mouth) and abnormal salivary flow even without SjS autoantibodies (anti-Ro/La negative), (b) these patients may respond to cevimeline (M3 agonist, FDA-approved for SjS sicca), (c) SjS patients with fatigue have a higher prevalence of M3 autoantibodies than SjS without fatigue. Blitshteyn 2015 (POTS chart review) found Sjögren's was among the autoimmune comorbidities, but the mechanism was not specified.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2015AutoimmuneMarkers (SjS comorbidity in POTS), @Sunami2024GPCRPOTS (M3 in POTS GI symptoms), @Ekman2025 (SFN→GI in POTS), Waterman 2000 (M3 AAb in SjS)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients (n=100, all anti-Ro/La negative tested), M3 autoantibody titer correlates with: (a) sicca symptoms (ESSDAI ocular subscale, r > 0.4), (b) Schirmer test (abnormal in ≥ 30% of high-M3 tertile vs ≤ 10% of low tertile), (c) COMPASS-31 GI subscale (r > 0.3), (d) salivary gland ultrasound (parenchymal inhomogeneity, ≥ 20% of high-M3 tertile). Open-label cevimeline (30 mg tid, 8 weeks) in high-M3 ME/CFS (n=10): ≥ 50% show improvement in both sicca and GI symptoms.
- **Tier:** 2

#### CD33: Myasthenia Gravis (MG) ⇔ ME/CFS — Ganglionic AChR Autoantibodies and Fatigable Weakness
- **Mechanism:** MG is caused by autoantibodies against the nicotinic AChR at the neuromuscular junction (muscle AChR), producing fatigable muscle weakness that worsens with activity and improves with rest — a close analog to PEM. The extended parallel: some MG patients have autoantibodies against ganglionic AChR (α3 subunit) instead of muscle AChR, producing autoimmune autonomic ganglionopathy (AAG) with symptoms including orthostatic hypotension, GI dysmotility, and fatigue (Gibbons 2009, Neurology). The MG/AAG → ME/CFS bridge predicts: (a) a subset of ME/CFS patients may have low-titer ganglionic AChR autoantibodies not detected by standard MG assays (which use muscle AChR), (b) these patients would show fatigable weakness on quantitative myasthenia gravis score (QMGS) — not just generalized fatigue — and would respond to pyridostigmine or IVIG (standard MG treatments), (c) the MG treatment algorithm (pyridostigmine first, then steroids, then IVIG/rituximab) could be adapted for AChR-positive ME/CFS. The Blitshteyn 2026 framework supports this: "autonomic ganglia as autoimmune targets" is cited but not connected to the MG clinical phenotype.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune (ganglionic AChR), Gibbons 2009 (AAG, ganglionic AChR antibodies), MG treatment algorithms
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients with clinical features of fatigable weakness (ptosis, diplopia, dysphagia, head drop — documented in ≥ 15% of ME/CFS on careful exam): (a) ≥ 10% are positive for ganglionic AChR (α3) autoantibodies vs ≤ 1% in ME/CFS without these features, (b) muscle AChR antibodies (standard MG panel) are negative, (c) QMGS is abnormal in ≥ 50% of α3-positive patients (≥ 3 points), (d) open-label pyridostigmine (60 mg tid, 4 weeks) improves both QMGS (≥ 2-point reduction) and PROMIS Fatigue (≥ 8 points) in α3-positive patients but not in α3-negative patients matched for severity.
- **Tier:** 2

#### CD34: Post-COVID POTS ⇔ Post-EBV ME/CFS — Shared Molecular Mimicry Epitope Convergence
- **Mechanism:** Both SARS-CoV-2 and EBV are post-infectious triggers for POTS and ME/CFS (Blitshteyn 2026). The concept of molecular mimicry is invoked but not molecularly specified. A bioinformatic approach would search the SARS-CoV-2 proteome and EBV proteome for linear epitopes with homology to GPCR extracellular domains (β2-AR, M2, M4, α1-AR). If a shared epitope exists (a peptide sequence present in both viruses with homology to a GPCR), it would explain how two different viruses trigger the same autoantibody response. This predicts: (a) cross-reactivity: sera from post-COVID POTS patients bind to EBV peptides and vice versa, (b) the cross-reactive epitope maps to the same GPCR region, (c) computational epitope prediction identifies the candidate. This is not mere speculation: the existing literature on Guillain-Barré and Campylobacter jejuni (ganglioside mimicry) provides a validated template for how a specific microbial epitope sequence drives an autoantibody response to a specific self-antigen. Blitshteyn 2026's molecular mimicry concept would become molecularly testable.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Wallukat2021GPCRLongCOVID (LC β2-AR AAb), @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS (POTS GPCR AAb), @ElRhermoul2023AutoimmunityLCPOTS
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Computational alignment (BLASTp) of SARS-CoV-2 and EBV proteomes against human GPCR extracellular domains identifies ≥ 1 candidate 8-15 aa peptide present in both viruses with ≥ 6/8 aa identity to a GPCR (β2-AR or M2). Sera from post-COVID POTS patients (n=20) show binding to the EBV peptide (ELISA, ≥ 2× above control). Sera from post-EBV ME/CFS patients (n=20) show binding to the SARS-CoV-2 peptide. Healthy controls and non-post-infectious patients show no cross-reactive binding. The identified peptide, when administered to mice with adjuvant, generates GPCR autoantibodies and autonomic dysfunction.
- **Tier:** 1

#### CD35: Fibromyalgia ⇔ ME/CFS ⇔ POTS — Central Sensitization + GPCR Autoantibodies + Small Fiber Neuropathy as Shared Triad
- **Mechanism:** Fibromyalgia (FM) is primarily considered a central sensitization syndrome (pain-dominant), ME/CFS is primarily post-exertional malaise (fatigue-dominant), and POTS is primarily orthostatic (autonomic-dominant). However, all three share: (a) small fiber neuropathy (SFN) — documented in FM (Evans 2022, Utku 2024 pre-existing), ME/CFS (Azcue 2023), and POTS (Ekman 2025), (b) GPCR autoantibodies — documented in ME/CFS and POTS as above, and emerging in FM (Goebel 2021 IgG passive transfer → pain), (c) central sensitization — documented in POTS (Mathew 2026, 67%) and FM (diagnostic criterion), assumed in ME/CFS. The triad hypothesis: SFN provides the peripheral trigger (ectopic nociceptive and autonomic afferent firing) → GPCR autoantibodies shift the gain of the neuroimmune interface (H4: mast cell sensitization, H6: CAP blockade) → central sensitization amplifies the CNS interpretation of afferent signals, producing the dominant symptom domain (pain in FM, fatigue in ME/CFS, autonomic in POTS). The clinical phenotype is determined not by the presence/absence of each triad element but by the relative weighting: high SFN + high GPCR AAb + low central sensitization → POTS-dominant; high central sensitization + moderate SFN + moderate GPCR AAb → FM-dominant; high GPCR AAb + moderate central sensitization + high SFN → ME/CFS-dominant. This predicts that the triads overlap and that treatment should target the dominant element: POTS-dominant → α1 support and compression; FM-dominant → central sensitization treatment (duloxetine, pregabalin); ME/CFS-dominant → immunomodulation.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune (SFN overlap), @Mathew2026 (central sensitization in POTS), @Ekman2025 (SFN→GI in POTS), @Azcue2023 (SFN in ME/CFS, pre-existing), Goebel 2021 (FM IgG → pain passive transfer)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a transdiagnostic cohort (n=50 FM, n=50 ME/CFS, n=50 POTS, n=50 FM+ME/CFS+POTS), cluster analysis of (IENFD + GPCR autoantibody titer + CSI score) yields 3 clusters that map to the predicted triad: Cluster A (high AAb, low IENFD, moderate CSI) = POTS-dominant; Cluster B (moderate AAb, moderate IENFD, high CSI) = FM-dominant; Cluster C (high AAb, high IENFD reduction, moderate CSI) = ME/CFS-dominant. Treatment response (to standard-of-care per clinic) is predicted by cluster membership (accuracy ≥ 70%).
- **Tier:** 1

### 9. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

#### D36: Composite Neuroimmune Panel — GPCR Autoantibody Profile + HLA-DR + Autonomic Function + TSPO-PET for ME/CFS Subtype Stratification
- **Description:** A 4-domain composite panel that stratifies ME/CFS into actionable neuroimmune subtypes:
  1. **GPCR autoantibody profile** (CellTrend, 10 targets): positive/negative per target + titer quantile
  2. **HLA-DR/DQ typing**: DRB1*15:01, DQB1*06:02 status
  3. **Autonomic function testing**: HRV (24h rMSSD), standing HR increment, baroreflex sensitivity (sequence method)
  4. **Neuroinflammation imaging**: TSPO-PET (brainstem SUVr) in a subset, or CSF NfL as proxy
  The panel generates a 4-digit code (e.g., AAb+/HLA+/ANS+/PET+) that directly maps to treatment algorithm. Each "+" indicates the domain is abnormal. The more "+" domains, the stronger the neuroimmune etiology and the more aggressive the immunomodulation should be. 4+/4 → autoantibody-driven neuroimmune disorder → IA + taVNS + IVIG. 0/4 → non-immune mechanism → pacing + supplements + pain management. The code would be recorded in all RCTs to enable post-hoc stratification.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune (all 4 domains), @Shin2019HLAPOTS (HLA), @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS (AAb), @VanElzakker2024NeuroinflammationPASC (TSPO-PET), @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation (brainstem)
- **Certainty:** 0.45
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a cohort of 100 ME/CFS patients stratified by the composite panel, the neuroimmune subtype (≥ 3/4 domains positive) vs non-immune subtype (≤ 1/4 positive) differ significantly in: (a) immunomodulatory treatment response (≥ 50% vs ≤ 10%, IA+IVIG open-label), (b) disease trajectory over 12 months (neuroimmune subtype: stable or progressive; non-immune: fluctuating or remitting), (c) post-infectious onset (≥ 80% of neuroimmune vs ≤ 30% of non-immune). The composite panel has test-retest reliability ICC > 0.70 over 4 weeks.
- **Tier:** 1

#### D37: CSF GPCR Autoantibody Panel — Intrathecal Autoantibody Index as Biomarker of CNS Autoimmunity
- **Description:** Paired serum + CSF GPCR autoantibody measurement (CellTrend, 10 targets) with calculation of an "antibody index": AI = (CSF AAb / serum AAb) / (CSF albumin / serum albumin). AI > 1.5 indicates intrathecal autoantibody synthesis (the autoantibody is produced within the CNS compartment, not merely leaked from serum). This is the standard diagnostic method for intrathecal antibody production in neuroinfectious and neuroimmune diseases (CSF oligoclonal bands, antibody index for measles/rubella/zoster in MS). In ME/CFS, AI > 1.5 for any GPCR target would be diagnostic of CNS-restricted autoimmunity and would: (a) identify patients needing CNS-penetrant immunomodulation (rituximab, mycophenolate, daratumumab — despite limited CNS penetration, high-dose regimens achieve therapeutic levels), (b) explain why some patients have severe brain fog despite minimal peripheral autoantibodies (CNS compartment is the relevant space), (c) provide a biomarker for treatment response (AI decreases with successful immunomodulation). The lumbar puncture is invasive but justifiable in moderate-severe patients where the result changes treatment.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation (brainstem), @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, antibody index methodology (Reiber 1998, standard CSF diagnostics), @H2 (CNS B cell aggregates)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients with severe brain fog (cognitive PROMIS T-score ≥ 65) despite normal or borderline serum GPCR autoantibodies, paired CSF/serum analysis shows: (a) AI > 1.5 for ≥ 1 GPCR target in ≥ 25% of patients, (b) CSF oligoclonal bands present in ≥ 30% of AI-positive patients, (c) CSF GPCR autoantibody targets differ from serum targets in ≥ 40% of AI-positive patients (supporting intrathecal B cell clonal expansion to CNS-restricted epitopes). AI-positive patients show TSPO-PET signal in brainstem (r > 0.5 between AI and SUVr).
- **Tier:** 2

#### D38: Blood Neurofilament Light (NfL) as Neuroaxonal Damage Biomarker in Neuroinflammatory ME/CFS Subtype
- **Description:** Plasma NfL is a validated biomarker of neuroaxonal damage in MS, ALS, and other neurodegenerative conditions — released when axons are injured. In the ME/CFS neuroinflammatory subtype (elevated TSPO-PET, CSF autoantibodies), CNS neuroinflammation may produce measurable axonal damage, detectable as elevated plasma NfL. A single blood draw (EDTA plasma, Simoa assay — 10 µL, $50/test, CLIA-certified) would: (a) identify patients with ongoing neuroaxonal damage who need neuroprotective immunomodulation, (b) provide a quantitative biomarker for treatment monitoring (NfL reduction indicates immunomodulation success), (c) predict cognitive decline (baseline NfL predicts future cognitive worsening). The Blitshteyn 2026 framework predicts that brainstem neuroinflammation (Blitshteyn 2025) causes localized axonal damage in the NTS/RVLM region — which may release NfL into the CSF and then plasma. Elevated NfL would be specific to the neuroinflammatory subtype (not all ME/CFS).
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2025Neuroinflammation, @VanElzakker2024NeuroinflammationPASC, NfL in MS (Kuhle 2016, JAMA Neurology — established), NfL in neurodegeneration (bridges existing paper's neuroinflammatory speculation)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, plasma NfL (Simoa) is elevated in the neuroinflammatory-predicted subgroup (TSPO-PET+ and/or CSF AAb AI>1.5) compared to healthy controls (mean > 15 pg/mL vs < 10 pg/mL, p < 0.01). NfL correlates with: (a) TSPO-PET SUVR in dorsolateral medulla (r > 0.4), (b) cognitive processing speed (Digit Symbol, r < -0.4), (c) CSF NfL (r > 0.7, validating plasma as proxy). In an open-label immunomodulation pilot (IA, n=10 NfL-elevated patients), NfL decreases by ≥ 20% at 3 months post-IA in parallel with cognitive improvement (r > 0.5).
- **Tier:** 2

#### D39: Standing ETCO2 + HR + CBF Velocity Triad — Point-of-Care Orthostatic Neuroimmune Index
- **Description:** A 5-minute point-of-care test performed in clinic or at home: the patient stands (from supine, 10 min supine → 5 min stand) while wearing (a) nasal cannula capnometer (ETCO2), (b) finger PPG or chest strap HR monitor, (c) transcranial Doppler (TCD) for MCA velocity (simplified — a single 2-MHz probe fixed at the temporal window). The output is 3 trajectories: ΔHR, ΔETCO2, ΔMCAv. The composite "Orthostatic Neuroimmune Index" (ONI) = ΔHR × (1/ΔMCAv) × (1/ΔETCO2) — a high index means large HR increase, large CBF drop, and large ETCO2 drop (the Miranda-Hurtado 2026 pathway in full effect). The ONI distinguishes subtypes: high ONI = SV-driven hypocapnic vasoconstriction pathway (target CO2-retention strategies); moderate ONI = mixed; low ONI with high ΔHR = hyperadrenergic (target sympathetic blockade). The ONI is dynamic: patients with active GPCR autoantibody-mediated CAP blockade (H6) would have a higher ONI because vagal withdrawal (M2/M4 AAb) amplifies HR increase without improving CBF (the protective effect of vagal tone on CBF autoregulation is lost). Blitshteyn 2026's neuroimmune framework implies that the orthostatic response is not purely hemodynamic — it reflects the state of the autoimmune + autonomic interaction. The ONI captures this interaction non-invasively in 5 minutes.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @MirandaHurtado2026 (SV→ETCO2→CBF), @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS (AAb + autonomic), @Hedge2026 (test-retest unreliability → ONI may be more reliable than ΔHR alone)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The ONI has better 4-week test-retest reliability (ICC > 0.65) than ΔHR alone (ICC < 0.50 per Hedge 2026). ONI correlates with GPCR autoantibody composite titer across β2, M2, M4 (r > 0.4). The ONI distinguishes ME/CFS+POTS from ME/CFS without POTS (AUC > 0.75) and predicts: (a) response to CO2-retention strategies (acetazolamide, capnometry biofeedback) — high ONI patients benefit more, (b) taVNS efficacy — low ONI patients (pure hyperadrenergic, low SV component) respond better to taVNS.
- **Tier:** 1

#### D40: GPCR Autoantibody Subclass-Specific ELISA (IgG1/IgG4 Ratio) as Pathogenicity Biomarker
- **Description:** Replace the standard total IgG GPCR AAb ELISA (CellTrend) with subclass-specific ELISAs for IgG1 and IgG4 against β2-AR, M2, M3, M4, and α1-AR. The IgG1/IgG4 ratio for each target becomes the diagnostic output — not the total titer. Rationale: IgG1 autoantibodies fix complement, bind FcγR on immune cells, and are pro-inflammatory (pathogenic). IgG4 autoantibodies are non-inflammatory, bivalent but monofunctional (Fab-arm exchange), and may even be blocking/protective. In myasthenia gravis, the IgG1/IgG4 ratio of AChR autoantibodies predicts response to complement inhibitors (eculizumab, which blocks C5 — only effective in IgG1-dominant MG). In ME/CFS, the Germain 2025 null result (no GPCR AAb detection) may be because the Luminex platform detects predominantly IgG1/3 (high-affinity binding) while CellTrend ELISA detects total IgG including IgG4. If ME/CFS GPCR autoantibodies are predominantly IgG4 (non-pathogenic), the two assays are measuring different things, explaining the contradiction. The subclass ratio would: (a) resolve the assay controversy, (b) identify truly pathogenic (IgG1-dominant) patients for immunomodulation, (c) guide complement inhibitor use in IgG1-dominant patients (eculizumab trial, testable). The existing paper's IgG4-IgG1 isotype switch speculation (certainty 0.42) would be directly tested.
- **Evidence link:** @Blitshteyn2026Neuroimmune, @Germain2025autoantibody (null, pre-existing), @Gunning2019GPCRPOTS (ELISA positive), existing paper ch28 isotype switch speculation (0.42)
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (n=50, CellTrend-positive), subclass analysis reveals: (a) ≥ 40% are IgG4-dominant (IgG1/IgG4 < 1), (b) ≥ 30% are IgG1-dominant (> 1), (c) IgG1-dominant patients have significantly worse COMPASS-31, fatigue, and PEM severity than IgG4-dominant patients (effect size d > 0.6), (d) IgG1/IgG4 ratio predicts IA response (AUC > 0.75): IgG1-dominant patients respond to IA; IgG4-dominant patients do not.
- **Tier:** 1
