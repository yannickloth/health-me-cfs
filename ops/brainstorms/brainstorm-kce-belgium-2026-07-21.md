# Brainstorm: KCE Belgian ME/CFS Health Technology Assessment Integration

**Date:** 2026-07-21
**Source papers:** Cornelis2026KCE420, Cornelis2026KCE420S
**Source type:** Mixed-methods health systems needs assessment (n=749 survey + 19 qualitative interviews)
**Decision:** PROCEED

**Key evidence anchors:**
- EQ-5D-5L: 0.84→0.36 (p<0.001), Belgian population mean 0.79 — 57% decline
- Comorbidities: FM 63.3%, IBS 56.9%, orthostatic hypotension 20.6%, POTS 17.0%, long COVID 16.4%, SFN 12.8%, MCAS 10.5%; 73.2% ≥1 comorbidity
- Diagnostic delay: 50% >2yr, 39% >5yr; associated with worse HRQoL (p=0.010)
- Treatment responses: GET predominantly harmful (26% improved); CBT 50% improved/20% deteriorated/30% no change; psychological counselling 50% improved/30% no change
- Mean invalidity duration: 8.6yr — among longest of all KCE NEED conditions
- Pacing: widely used, patient-preferred, limited evidence base
- Recognition: central unmet need — psychologisation experienced as inequitable care
- International comparison: UK structured pathway, NL ZonMw €28.5M 10yr programme, IT draft legislation
- Linguistic differential: French-speaking greater HRQoL decline (-0.57 vs -0.47, p=0.005)
- Severe/very severe patients under-represented
- No cost-effectiveness analysis available of any service model

**Scope note:** This is a health systems/policy needs assessment, not a mechanistic study. Brainstorming focuses on healthcare delivery, patient experience, treatment patterns, and health services research implications. Categories 1–12 are preserved but ideas prioritise systems-level insight over molecular mechanism.

## Quality Ranking

Ideas ranked by combined factors: mechanistic plausibility, direct evidence link from KCE data, falsifiability, clinical/policy actionability, and distinctiveness from already-integrated content.

#### R1. GET Responder Subgroup: 26% with Improvement — What Characterises Them?

**Category:** 2 — Research Direction
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The 26% GET improvement rate is the most under-analyzed finding in the KCE report. It is reported as "predominantly harmful" but the 26% signal is not characterised. If GET helps a minority, identifying the responder phenotype would (a) prevent universal GET harm, (b) reveal mechanistic heterogeneity, (c) enable personalised prescribing. Candidate responder characteristics: less-severe baseline, no PEM to light activity, preserved VO₂max on CPET, no hypermobility/EDS, no POTS comorbidity, post-viral rather than gradual onset, earlier disease duration. The null hypothesis — that the 26% is placebo/regression-to-mean — is testable.

**Evidence link:** KCE patient-reported GET experience; NICE NG206 GET against guidelines; PACE trial subgroup analyses (no reliable subgroups identified but flawed trial); 2-day CPET distinguishes PEM-positive from PEM-negative patients; GET responder/non-responder literature sparse. Fibromyalgia GET subtypes partially characterised (pool-based vs land-based, aerobic vs resistance).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65

**Falsifiable prediction:** If GET responders are characterised by preserved anaerobic threshold (AT >40% predicted VO₂max on CPET-1) and absence of PEM to sub-threshold activity, then a prospective trial restricting GET to patients meeting these criteria will show (a) improvement rate >50% (vs 26% in unselected sample), (b) deterioration rate <5% (vs majority in unselected), (c) no CPET-2 deterioration. If improvement rate after selection is still ~26%, the signal is placebo/noise.

**Non-specialist consequence:** We could stop telling all ME/CFS patients to avoid exercise and instead offer a cheap CPET test to find the minority who actually benefit.

#### R2. CBT Deterioration Subgroup: 20% Get Worse — Are They the Same as GET-Harmed?

**Category:** 2 — Research Direction
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** CBT shows 50% improvement, 20% deterioration, 30% no change. The 20% deterioration rate is clinically significant — not a negligible adverse event rate. Two hypotheses: (1) the deteriorating group overlaps with GET-harmed group (shared vulnerability — inability to tolerate behavioural activation, PEM-generating "activity scheduling"); (2) the deteriorating group is distinct — CBT-specific harm from cognitive restructuring that invalidates patient's bodily experience (reinforcing self-blame stigma factor from Terman 2020). Distinguishing these has practical consequences: hypothesis 1 → fix CBT activity component; hypothesis 2 → limit cognitive restructuring, shift to validation-focused approaches.

**Evidence link:** KCE patient-reported CBT outcomes (50/20/30 split). PACE trial subgroup: CBT had similar profile to GET but with lower adverse events. CBT mechanisms in ME/CFS: behavioural activation component (exercise-like) vs cognitive restructuring (validation-undermining). Terman 2020 self-blame factor. Qualitative: patients report CBT makes them feel blamed when told "thoughts cause fatigue."

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) If the deteriorating group overlaps: CBT deteriorators show PEM after behavioural activation components (strongest predictor). (b) If distinct: CBT deteriorators show elevated self-blame stigma score at baseline and report worsened self-blame post-treatment. (c) Dismantling trial: CBT without behavioural activation shows improvement rate maintained at ~50% but deterioration drops from 20% to <5%.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The problem isn't therapy — it's which therapy component. Remove the "push yourself" parts and CBT might be safe.

#### R3. French-Speaking Worse HRQoL: Access Disparity or Pathophysiology Signal?

**Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** French-speaking Belgian patients had greater HRQoL decline (-0.57 vs -0.47 for Dutch-speaking, p=0.005). Four candidate explanations, not mutually exclusive: (1) **Access disparity** — the single NIHDI-funded reference centre is Dutch-speaking, Wallonia has fewer ME/CFS services; French-speakers face higher out-of-pocket costs, longer wait times, more untreated disease. (2) **Diagnostic delay × language** — French-speaking clinicians may have lower ME/CFS awareness → longer diagnostic delay → worse outcomes (KCE already shows diagnostic delay → worse HRQoL). (3) **Stigma severity** — cultural differences in illness perception between Flanders and Wallonia (Wallonia has higher sick-leave rates generally, suggesting different illness-validity cultures). (4) **Socioeconomic confounding** — Wallonia has higher unemployment, lower GDP per capita, and financial difficulties independently worsen HRQoL (KCE finding).

**Evidence link:** KCE linguistic differential (p=0.005). Belgian regional health disparities literature (Wallonia-Flanders health gradient). KCE diagnostic delay → worse HRQoL pathway. Financial difficulties → HRQoL (KCE). No study has examined regional ME/CFS access within Belgium.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55 (access disparity hypothesis); 0.25 (pathophysiology hypothesis)

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) If access-driven: controlling for time-to-diagnosis and distance-to-specialist eliminates the language effect (β_lang → non-significant). (b) If stigma-driven: Terman 2020 stigma scale scores are higher in French-speaking patients after controlling for access. (c) If socioeconomic: controlling for income/poverty eliminates effect. (d) If none eliminate: unmeasured pathophysiological difference (genetic, environmental, infectious trigger type) — unlikely but testable.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If the French-speaking disadvantage is just worse access, opening a reference centre in Wallonia would close the gap within 3 years — a cheap, testable policy reform.

#### R4. Pacing Efficacy: Why Is the Patient-Preferred Strategy Without an Evidence Base?

**Category:** 2 — Research Direction
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** Pacing is the most widely used self-management strategy and patient-preferred, yet the KCE report notes "evidence base limited." This is an evidence-generation failure: pacing is inherently harder to standardise and randomise than GET/CBT, and industry-funded trials favour branded interventions. A well-designed pacing trial is urgently needed, but the design question is non-trivial: pacing requires individualised energy envelope measurement (2-day CPET or wearable-based estimation), active monitoring (HRV, step count), and PEM avoidance as primary endpoint (not fatigue or function, which pacing may not improve — it may maintain function rather than improve it).

**Evidence link:** KCE pacing prevalence. Pacing mechanisms (energy envelope model, ch17 pacing, ch38 energy envelope ODE). 2-day CPET as PEM gold standard. Wearable-based pacing trials emerging (e.g., Visible app, Garmin body battery, Oura ring readiness score). Wormgoor 2023 PEM-focused care reduces dissatisfaction.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60 (need is clear), 0.40 (trial design feasibility — individualisation vs standardisation tension)

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) A pacing RCT using individualised energy envelope (CPET-derived AT as ceiling) and PEM frequency as primary endpoint will show pacing superior to treatment-as-usual on PEM reduction (≥30% fewer crashes/month) but not on SF-36 physical function (pacing preserves, doesn't improve). (b) Failure to show benefit implies either (i) energy envelope estimation is inaccurate, (ii) patients cannot adhere despite knowledge, or (iii) PEM has non-exertional triggers dominating.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Doctors dismiss pacing as "just rest" because there's no trial. A positive trial would give clinicians something they can recommend with confidence — and patients something their doctors will believe.

#### R5. 8.6-Year Invalidity: ME/CFS as the Most-Disabling Condition in the Belgian NEED Framework

**Category:** 7 — Mathematical Model Extension
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE finds ME/CFS has a mean invalidity duration of 8.6 years — among the longest of all conditions studied under the NEED framework. This has not been contextualised: what is the next-longest invalidity duration? Is 8.6yr exceptional or typical for chronic conditions? This invites a formal disease burden modelling extension: probability of return-to-work at time t given diagnosis, modelled as P_return(t) = P_recovery(t) × P_vocational(t), where P_recovery(t) follows the paper's multi-attractor model (ch41) and P_vocational(t) captures structural barriers (stigma, employer accommodation, disability assessment). The KCE data could calibrate this model for the first time.

**Evidence link:** KCE invalidity database (1,189 individuals, 82% female, 72% aged 45+, 8.6yr mean). KCE return-to-work rates not reported (gap). Multi-attractor model (ch41). CastroMarrero2019employment (Spanish employment data). NICE NG206 return-to-work section.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) P_return(t) fits a survival curve with median time-to-return >10yr, (b) structural barriers (γ_vocational) contribute ≥40% of non-return probability independent of disease severity, (c) countries with structured ME/CFS workplace accommodation (UK Access to Work) show significantly steeper return slope than Belgium.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If 8.6 years is the typical disability duration, and workplace accommodation cuts that in half, the financial case for employer intervention is overwhelming — saving Belgian social security tens of millions.

#### R6. Comorbidity Cascade: 73.2% with ≥1 Comorbidity — Is This Diagnostic Splitting or True Multi-System Disease?

**Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE reports 73.2% of patients have ≥1 ME/CFS-related comorbidity (FM 63.3%, IBS 56.9%, POTS 17.0%, MCAS 10.5%). This raises a fundamental nosological question: is ME/CFS a single disease with multi-system manifestations, or are these truly separate comorbid conditions — each diagnosed by different criteria, by different specialists, with different billing codes? Hypothesis: these "comorbidities" share a common upstream driver (e.g., connective tissue pathology → FM + POTS + MCAS + IBS + ME/CFS, via mast cell-matrix interactions; or post-infectious autoimmunity → multiple targets). If true, treating the upstream driver treats all conditions simultaneously. If false, each requires separate management and the co-occurrence is coincidental or referral bias.

**Evidence link:** KCE comorbidity prevalence. Connective tissue hypothesis (ch14d, brainstorm-connective-tissue-2026-05-13). MCAS-ME/CFS overlap (brainstorm-mast-cell-ige-connective-tissue-2026-06-19). FM-ME/CFS shared mechanisms (brainstorm-shared-mechanisms-fibromyalgia-mecfs-longcovid-2026-05-12). EDS/hypermobility in ME/CFS (ch14d). Separate diagnostic criteria for each condition (ACR for FM, Rome IV for IBS, consensus for POTS/MCAS).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) If common upstream driver: treatment of presumed driver (e.g., mast cell stabilisation) improves all comorbid conditions simultaneously in the same patient — improvement is correlated across conditions. (b) If diagnostic splitting: improvement in one condition does not predict improvement in others; separate treatments each help separately. (c) Network analysis of comorbidity patterns should reveal clusters (e.g., FM + IBS + POTS cluster, vs MCAS + SFN cluster), suggesting multiple upstream drivers.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If it's one upstream problem, one drug treats five diagnoses — instead of seeing five specialists for five prescriptions.

#### R7. Under-Representation of Severe/Very-Severe: How Large Is the Invisible Severity Tail?

**Category:** 9 — Diagnostic/Biomarker Idea
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE acknowledges severe/very severe patients are under-represented (recruited via patient associations, online survey — excludes bedbound patients with severe cognitive/sensory intolerance unable to complete online forms). This is a universal ME/CFS research problem. The KCE data provides a natural experiment: the survey severity distribution can be compared to known population severity estimates (e.g., Nacul 2011: ~25% severe/very severe) to estimate the under-count factor. If severe patients represent 25% of the ME/CFS population but only, say, 5% of KCE participants, the invisible tail factor is 5× — meaning all KCE findings (QoL, treatment responses, comorbidity) are biased toward less-severe phenotypes. This is itself a major research finding: the KCE's own data can quantify its own sampling bias.

**Evidence link:** KCE sampling limitation (self-acknowledged). Nacul 2011 severity distribution. Fennell 2021 severe/very severe qualitative data (n=16). Severe-specific care papers (ch14a). ACHTSAM home visit model (Fricke 2026). The KCE supplement (420S) may contain subgroup analyses — check.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) The KCE severity distribution differs from population estimates (e.g., Nacul 2011) by ≥4× for severe/very severe categories. (b) When corrected for under-representation, the true EQ-5D-5L score for the severe subgroup is <0.20 (vs overall 0.36). (c) True invalidity duration for severe patients is >12yr (vs overall 8.6yr). (d) Treatment harm rates (GET deterioration) are underestimated by ≥50% because severe patients — most likely to be harmed — are excluded.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The KCE numbers are optimistic. The real situation is worse — and the sickest patients, who need help most, are the ones being left out of the data.

#### R8. GET Harm Characterisation: What Kind of Harm and How Severe?

**Category:** 8 — Cross-Disease Bridge
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** KCE reports GET as "predominantly perceived as harmful" with "symptom exacerbation and substantial treatment burden." But it does not characterise harm type, severity, or duration. Is GET harm: (a) temporary PEM (resolves in days-weeks), (b) permanent functional decline (energy ratchet step — irreversible), (c) triggering of new comorbid conditions (POTS, MCAS, FM onset after GET), (d) psychological harm (self-blame from "failing" GET — stigma reinforcement)? This matters enormously: reversible harm → GET could be studied safely in screened subgroups (R1 above); irreversible harm → GET should be contraindicated for all. The 2-day CPET literature shows GET-irreversible decline in some patients but not systematically characterised.

**Evidence link:** KCE GET patient-reported experience. 2-day CPET literature (Keller 2014, Snell 2013, Systrom 2017). Energy ratchet model (ch42, subsec-03). GET adverse events literature (PACE reanalysis). NICE NG206 strong recommendation against GET. Physiotherapy (manual) generally positive — contrast with GET suggests exercise type matters.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55 (harm is real), 0.25 (harm type characterisation missing)

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) In patients reporting GET harm with permanent decline, CPET-2 shows VO₂max decline that does not recover within 6 months (vs temporary PEM where CPET normalises within 2 weeks). (b) Ratchet model predicts that ≥10% of GET-harmed patients experience a permanent ratchet step. (c) GET-harm patients have distinct biomarker profile (elevated post-exercise lactate, reduced VO₂ recovery slope, elevated creatine kinase) not present in patients who tolerate manual physiotherapy.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If GET causes permanent damage in even 10% of patients, it's a public health disaster, not a treatment — and that 10% shouldn't have to prove it every time they see a new doctor.

#### R9. Psychiatric Comorbidity or Psychological Consequence? The 75% "Psychological Symptom" Burden

**Category:** 11 — Null Hypothesis Assessment
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** KCE reports 89.9% stress, 74.6% fear/anxiety, 75% moderate-to-severe psychological symptom burden, but explicitly states "mental health impairment secondary to physical and social consequences." This framing is a deliberate break from the psychologisation tradition. However it raises a testable null hypothesis: psychological distress is NOT secondary — it is part of a shared pathophysiology (neuroinflammation driving both fatigue and anxiety/depression). Testing this changes clinical approach: secondary → treat by improving ME/CFS (managing PEM, reducing disability); shared pathophysiology → treat with neuroinflammation-targeting drugs (LDN, COX-2 inhibitors) that might improve both fatigue and mood. The KCE data can't resolve this (cross-sectional) but can frame the hypothesis.

**Evidence link:** KCE psychological symptom burden (75%). KCE secondary attribution framing. Neuroinflammation-depression link (ch07, ch14g, ch39 microglial priming). LDN improves both fatigue and mood in some patients (patient reports + small trials). Sickness behaviour model (Dantzer 2008): IL-1β/TNF-α → fatigue, anhedonia, cognitive impairment — same symptoms. TSPO-PET in ME/CFS (Nakatomi 2014, brainstorm-tspo-pet-imaging-2026-05-07).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP) correlate with both physical fatigue AND psychological distress scores, after controlling for disease severity. (b) Anti-neuroinflammatory treatment (LDN, minocycline) improves both physical and psychological symptoms in parallel. (c) If secondary: improvement in physical function (via pacing, treatment) improves mood; if shared pathophysiology: mood improves independent of physical function improvement (anti-inflammatory effect).

**Non-specialist consequence:** If depression in ME/CFS is brain inflammation, not sadness about being sick, then antidepressants are the wrong drug and anti-inflammatories are the right one.

#### R10. Recognition Deficit: Taxonomising Unmet Recognition Needs by Stakeholder Level

**Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** KCE identifies recognition as "central unmet need" across healthcare, policy, and society. The supplement (420S) qualitative open-question analysis likely provides granular data on who fails to recognise (GP, specialist, family, employer, disability assessor, media) and how (dismissal, psychologisation, disbelief, minimisation, hostility). This can be taxonomised into a Recognition Deficit Matrix: stakeholder × failure mode → consequence pathway. This taxonomy would guide intervention design: GP dismissal requires different intervention (education + diagnostic algorithm) than employer disbelief (workplace accommodation guidelines + legal protection) or family minimisation (family education programs).

**Evidence link:** KCE recognition finding (main report + supplement). Qualitative recognition data (supplement 420S — needs extraction). Stigma literature taxonomy (Terman 2020 four-factor model maps partially). Hussein 2024 healthcare barriers. KCE recommendations 1–5 (all recognition-targeted).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Recognition deficit profiles differ by stakeholder: GP deficit = knowledge ("I don't know what this is"), family deficit = belief ("you don't look sick"), employer deficit = accommodation. (b) Targeted interventions matched to deficit type outperform generic anti-stigma campaigns. (c) Severe patients face a qualitatively different recognition deficit (family dominant, not healthcare dominant) compared to mild/moderate.

**Non-specialist consequence:** "Recognition" as a blanket ask is too vague to fix. We need a map — who doesn't believe you, why, and what would change their mind.

#### R11. International Comparison as Natural Experiment: Does Structured Pathway Reduce Invalidity?

**Category:** 8 — Cross-Disease Bridge
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE international comparison (UK structured pathway, NL research programme, IT draft legislation, FR clinician networks) is a natural quasi-experiment. If nationally structured ME/CFS pathways improve outcomes, countries with pathways should show: lower invalidity duration, higher employment rates, earlier diagnosis, lower patient-reported harm from inappropriate treatments. Conversely, if pathways don't improve outcomes, the structural investment isn't helping and something else (biomedical ignorance, treatment effectiveness ceiling) is rate-limiting. The KCE can't test this directly with Belgian data alone, but it can frame the testable hypotheses.

**Evidence link:** KCE international comparison (UK, NL, FR, IT). UK Cross-Sector Delivery Plan (2025). NL ZonMw €28.5M 10yr programme (national guideline expected 2026). IT draft legislation for ME/CFS recognition. NICE NG206 implementation audit data (UK). Belgian NIHDI single reference centre model.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) UK: diagnosis time reduced after NG206 (2021) vs pre-NG206. (b) Netherlands: after 2026 guideline + 10yr research programme, invalidity duration declines by ≥2yr within 5 years. (c) Italy: after legislation recognising ME/CFS as disabling disease, disability benefit approval rates increase and appeal rates decrease. (d) Null result: no measurable outcome difference between pathway and non-pathway countries → structural investment insufficient; biomedical discovery needed first.

**Non-specialist consequence:** We're spending millions on healthcare pathways without checking if they work. Comparing countries would tell us — and save money if the answer is no.

#### R12. Pacing-as-Standard vs Pacing-as-Alternative: A Missing Trial Design Problem

**Category:** 6 — Combinations + Access
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** Pacing is patient-preferred but evidence-limited (KCE). GET/CBT are better-evidenced but patient-hated (and GET harmful for many). The problem is partly trial design: the existing evidence base compares GET/CBT to treatment-as-usual (which may include unstructured pacing). No trial has tested pacing against GET head-to-head with PEM as primary endpoint. The KCE patient preference data provides equipoise: patients strongly prefer pacing, so randomisation to pacing vs GET is acceptable (unlike trials where patients refuse GET arm). A head-to-head pacing vs GET trial with 2-year follow-up would answer: which patient-preferred strategy actually works?

**Evidence link:** KCE patient preference for pacing. KCE GET harm/favourability data. NICE NG206 no pacing-specific recommendations (evidence gap). 2-day CPET as objective PEM measure. Pacing literature (Goudsmit 2012, but small and old). Energy envelope model (ch38).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Pacing superiority: pacing arm shows ≥30% fewer PEM events than GET arm over 12 months, with equivalent or better functional status at 24 months. (b) GET superiority: GET arm shows better SF-36 physical function at 24 months with PEM events not statistically different. (c) Null: no difference — both inferior to combined pacing + selective exercise (physiotherapy-style, not GET-style, graded to energy envelope not protocol).

**Non-specialist consequence:** Pacing is what patients already do. A trial showing it works better than GET would end the 30-year argument in one paper.

#### R13. EQ-5D-5L Responsiveness: Does the Instrument Capture PEM Fluctuations?

**Category:** 12 — Evidence Quality Concern
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE uses EQ-5D-5L, which measures HRQoL "today" (the day of survey completion). ME/CFS is characterised by fluctuating symptoms (PEM → crashes → partial recovery). The 0.36 utility score is therefore a point estimate on an unknown point of the fluctuation cycle. If patients completed the survey during a crash, 0.36 overestimates disability; if during a better day, it underestimates. The EQ-5D-5L has unknown intra-individual variability in ME/CFS — no test-retest reliability study factoring PEM state exists. This is important because cost-effectiveness models (QALY calculations) based on point-estimate utility scores systematically mis-specify the value of interventions in fluctuating conditions.

**Evidence link:** KCE EQ-5D-5L methodology. PEM fluctuation (hallmark symptom, ch02). EQ-5D literature in fluctuating conditions (multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis — intra-individual variability documented). No ME/CFS-specific EQ-5D-5L reliability study.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) EQ-5D-5L scores in ME/CFS show intra-individual variability ≥0.15 (vs MCID ~0.08) across measurement points separated by ≤2 weeks, driven primarily by PEM state. (b) Utility scores measured during PEM crash are ≥0.10 lower than during non-crash periods. (c) QALY gains from interventions mismeasured by ≥20% if point-estimate utility is used without PEM-state stratification.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Cost-effectiveness models that decide what treatments get funded may be wrong by 20% because they measured QoL on the wrong day.

#### R14. Long COVID Comorbidity as Natural Experiment: Does Post-COVID ME/CFS Differ from Non-COVID ME/CFS?

**Category:** 2 — Research Direction
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** KCE reports 16.4% long COVID comorbidity and long COVID associated with worse HRQoL. This provides a within-cohort comparison: post-COVID ME/CFS patients (n ~123) vs non-COVID ME/CFS patients. If post-COVID ME/CFS is the same disease (same symptom profile, same treatment response, same comorbidities), then long COVID IS post-infectious ME/CFS — supporting the post-infectious model. If different, they may be distinct conditions. The KCE data can answer this without new data collection: compare demographics, symptom profile, comorbidity patterns, diagnostic delay (likely shorter for post-COVID), treatment response between the two subgroups. The supplement (420S) may already contain these subgroup analyses.

**Evidence link:** KCE long COVID prevalence (16.4%) + HRQoL association. Long COVID literature (Davis 2021, 2023; Altman 2023). Long COVID → ME/CFS overlap literature (Wong 2024, Komaroff 2023). ch14d cross-disease bridges.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) If same condition: post-COVID subgroup has identical symptom profile distribution to non-COVID (same % with PEM, cognitive impairment, orthostatic intolerance, sleep disorder), shorter diagnostic delay (post-COVID pathway), higher clinician acceptance. (b) If different: post-COVID subgroup has distinct symptom clusters (more respiratory, less pain) and different comorbidity profile (less FM, more POTS). (c) KCE supplement subgroup analyses (420S) should report this — if not, it's a missed opportunity.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If long COVID and ME/CFS are the same thing triggered by different viruses, all the long COVID research money applies to ME/CFS — and vice versa.

#### R15. Physiotherapy vs GET: The Intervention Boundary That Patients Already Know

**Category:** 5 — Non-Pharmacological Intervention
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** KCE reports physiotherapy (manual techniques) as "generally positive experiences" while GET (graded exercise) is "predominantly harmful." This is a critical distinction buried in the report: patients can distinguish between passive/manual physiotherapy (massage, manual therapy, joint mobilisation — no exertion requirement) and active/graded exercise (effort, protocol, ignoring symptoms). The paper's exercise physiology models (ch38) would predict this: manual therapy doesn't exceed anaerobic threshold; graded exercise does. But the distinction needs formalising: what defines the boundary between "safe" and "harmful" physical intervention in ME/CFS? Hypothesis: safe physiotherapy stays below the individual anaerobic threshold (AT), doesn't require effort, doesn't provoke PEM; harmful physiotherapy crosses AT or provokes PEM.

**Evidence link:** KCE physiotherapy vs GET distinction. Anaerobic threshold in ME/CFS (2-day CPET literature, Systrom 2017). Energy envelope model (ch38). PEM mechanisms (ch36). Qualitative: patients report that massage/manual therapy is restorative while exercise is damaging. Puzzle: why? Muscle metabolism? Autonomic? Immune? Microvascular?

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Manual physiotherapy does not elevate blood lactate or heart rate above AT in ME/CFS patients. (b) CPET before and after a course of manual physiotherapy shows no VO₂max decline (vs GET which does). (c) Patients who tolerate manual physiotherapy but deteriorate with GET have preserved AT but impaired VO₂ recovery kinetics (the problem is recovery, not capacity). (d) Randomised comparison: 12 weeks of sub-AT manual physiotherapy vs standard GET — manual arm shows improvement without deterioration events; GET arm shows mixed results.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Not all physical therapy is the same. Massage helps, exercise hurts — and we should study why instead of lumping them together.

#### R16. Psychologisation as Iatrogenic Harm: Measuring the Harm of "It's in Your Head"

**Category:** 11 — Null Hypothesis Assessment
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE identifies psychologisation as "inequitable care" and recognition as the central unmet need. This invites an interventionist null hypothesis: psychologisation (dismissal, psychiatric referral, "CFS" diagnosis, GET/CBT prescription) causes measurable harm above and beyond the disease itself. Specifically: patients who experience psychologisation before correct diagnosis have (a) longer total diagnostic delay, (b) worse HRQoL at diagnosis controlling for duration, (c) more PEM events during the undiagnosed period (from inappropriate exercise advice), (d) higher self-blame stigma, (e) healthcare avoidance behaviour. If none of these are true, psychologisation is unpleasant but not medically harmful — and resources should go to biomedical research, not anti-stigma campaigns.

**Evidence link:** KCE psychologisation qualitative data + recognition finding. Terman 2020 self-blame/perceived discrimination factors. Stigma → health outcomes pathway (Froehlich 2022). KCE: diagnostic delay → worse HRQoL (p=0.010) — partially attributable to psychologisation causing delay.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Number of "dismissive encounters" correlates positively with diagnostic delay after controlling for patient factors (assertiveness, health literacy, severity). (b) Dismissive encounter count predicts HRQoL at diagnosis after controlling for delay duration (delay mediates part, but dismissal adds independent harm). (c) If null: psychologisation is distressing but does not independently worsen objective outcomes — mere co-occurrence with long disease duration (confounding). Test via path analysis: dismissal → delay → HRQoL (indirect) vs dismissal → HRQoL (direct, controlling for delay).

**Non-specialist consequence:** If being told "it's in your head" actually makes you sicker, doctors need to be trained — or sued. If it's just upsetting but not harmful, the priority shifts to research.

#### R19. KCE Survey Instrument: Validated for ME/CFS Cognitive Function?

**Category:** 12 — Evidence Quality Concern
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE explicitly notes the survey instrument was "not validated for cognitive function in ME/CFS." This is a both a limitation AND a finding: survey completion is cognitive exertion. ME/CFS patients with cognitive impairment (96.0% concentration difficulty, 93.7% memory problems) may give unreliable answers due to cognitive fatigue during completion, self-select out (severe patients who can't complete 30+ minute survey), or provide systematically different responses depending on when in the PEM cycle they respond. This biases findings: worse cognitive function → higher dropout → sample skewed toward cognitively preserved patients → underestimated true cognitive symptom burden. Tool: the online survey + EQ-5D-5L + adapted NEED questionnaire should be validated against in-person cognitive testing (e.g., MoCA, digit span, TMT-A/B) in a subset to quantify response reliability.

**Evidence link:** KCE methodology limitation (self-reported). ME/CFS cognitive impairment prevalence in KCE (96.0%, 93.7%). Survey fatigue in ME/CFS (qualitative — Fennell 2021). Cognitive testing literature in ME/CFS (ch03, sec-07). Online survey methodology in chronic illness literature.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) ME/CFS patients with MoCA <26 (objective cognitive impairment) have lower survey completion rates and higher item non-response rates than MoCA >26 patients. (b) Survey responses differ systematically by time-of-day (morning responders less impaired) and PEM proximity. (c) Cognitive function test (digit span embedded in survey) correlates with survey data quality metrics (completion time, item variance, straight-lining).

**Non-specialist consequence:** If brain fog makes the questionnaire unreliable, the 0.36 QoL score and all other survey data has a built-in error — and we don't know which direction.

#### R20. GET / CBT Improvement vs Deterioration: A Latent Class Mixture Model

**Category:** 7 — Mathematical Model Extension
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** GET shows 26% improvement/74% harm-or-no-change. CBT shows 50/20/30 split. These are not continuous distributions — they suggest latent classes (responders, deteriorators, non-changers). A Gaussian mixture model or latent class analysis of treatment response could identify the number of latent subpopulations, their characteristics, and the probability of class membership given baseline variables (severity, comorbidity, PEM pattern, duration, onset type). The KCE data (n=749) is large enough for exploratory latent class analysis. This is methodologically more appropriate than linear regression (which assumes continuous treatment effect) and can identify bimodal response patterns (some benefit, some harm) that average to "null" in standard analyses.

**Evidence link:** KCE GET/CBT response distributions. Latent class analysis methodology (applied to treatment response in psychiatry — depression treatment responders/non-responders). PACE trial reanalysis (Wilshire 2018: no latent class analysed). Daly-Lynn 2022 (GET/CBT latent classes?). No ME/CFS treatment latent class analysis published.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) LCA identifies 3 classes for GET: responders (~25%), non-changers (~50%), deteriorators (~25%). (b) Baseline PEM severity discriminates deteriorators from the other two classes (high baseline PEM → deteriorate). (c) Baseline AT on CPET discriminates responders from non-changers (preserved AT → respond). (d) CBT LCA identifies 3 classes: responders (behavioural activation benefits, ~50%), non-changers (neither benefit nor harm, ~30%), deteriorators (cognitive restructuring harms, ~20%). (e) If LCA finds only one class for either treatment, the bimodal appearance is measurement noise and the "26% improve" is just the upper tail of a normal distribution.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The same treatment helps some and hurts others. A statistical method that separates these groups out would tell you which one you are before you try it.

#### R22. Validation Script RCT: Does 30 Seconds of Clinician Validation Change Outcomes?

**Category:** 5 — Non-Pharmacological Intervention
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE finds recognition is the central unmet need. The simplest intervention: a 30-second validation script delivered at the first medical encounter ("ME/CFS is a real biomedical illness. What you're experiencing is real, and I believe you. Let's work together on managing it."). This is testable: GP practices randomised to script training vs standard introduction. Outcome: patient satisfaction, perceived stigma (Terman 2020 scale adapted), treatment adherence, 12-month HRQoL. This is so low-cost and low-risk that even a null result is useful (tells us recognition alone is insufficient — need biomedical intervention). A positive result would be globally scalable.

**Evidence link:** KCE recognition finding. Wormgoor 2023 PEM-focused care reduces dissatisfaction. Qualitative: "being believed" as turning point in patient narratives (Melby 2024, Asbring 2002). Clinician communication literature (therapeutic alliance predicts outcomes in chronic illness). Terman 2020 validated stigma scale.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Patients receiving validation script report 30% lower perceived discrimination scores at 3-month follow-up vs control. (b) Treatment adherence (pacing recommendations, specialist referrals) increases in validation-script arm. (c) 12-month HRQoL improvement in validation-script arm vs control (effect size d <0.3 — small but real). (d) Effect decays by 24 months if not reinforced → need for repeated validation. (e) If null: validation alone doesn't change objective outcomes — stigma is epiphenomenal, not causal.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If 30 seconds of believing your patient measurably improves their health, every medical school in the world should teach it — and we need to test it first.

#### R23. EQ-5D-5L vs SF-36 vs PROMIS: What Should ME/CFS Trials Measure?

**Category:** 12 — Evidence Quality Concern
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE uses EQ-5D-5L for its brevity and comparability (Belgian population norms, Sciensano 2018). But EQ-5D-5L has only 5 domains (mobility, self-care, usual activities, pain/discomfort, anxiety/depression) — it does NOT measure fatigue, PEM, cognitive function, sleep, or orthostatic intolerance directly. These are the core ME/CFS symptom domains. SF-36 measures more domains (but long, cognitively demanding). PROMIS provides computer-adaptive testing (shorter, more precise) and has ME/CFS-specific item banks. Hypothesis: EQ-5D-5L systematically underestimates ME/CFS HRQoL decrement by 10–20% compared to multi-domain instruments because it measures disability (self-care inability) rather than symptoms (PEM, cognitive fog) — and in ME/CFS, symptom burden drives QoL more than functional limitation.

**Evidence link:** KCE EQ-5D-5L use. EQ-5D vs SF-36 comparison in ME/CFS (no direct comparison published). PROMIS in ME/CFS (Jason 2021, Williams 2022). ME/CFS core symptom domains (IOM 2015: PEM, fatigue, sleep, cognitive, orthostatic). EQ-5D-5L does not capture PEM at all — the hallmark symptom.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) EQ-5D-5L utility scores correlate poorly (r <0.5) with PEM frequency/severity and cognitive impairment severity. (b) Adding 2 ME/CFS-specific items (PEM impact, cognitive function) to EQ-5D-5L increases sensitivity to change by ≥30%. (c) EQ-5D-5L is less responsive to ME/CFS treatment effects than SF-36 or PROMIS (smaller standardised response mean in trials).

**Non-specialist consequence:** The standard QoL questionnaire used to decide what treatments the government pays for doesn't even ask about the main symptom of the disease. That's a measurement error with billion-euro consequences.

#### R24. Diagnostic Delay as Treatment Window: The Pre-Diagnosis Period as Modifiable Risk Factor

**Category:** 2 — Research Direction
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** KCE finds diagnostic delay >2yr in 50%, >5yr in 39%, and delay → worse HRQoL (p=0.010). The pre-diagnosis period is the window when disease-modifying intervention could have the greatest impact (before ratchet steps, before epigenetic consolidation past irreversibility thresholds, before treatment nihilism). Yet during this window, patients typically receive: no diagnosis, no disease-appropriate care, potentially harmful advice (exercise, "it's in your head"). A "pre-diagnosis ME/CFS alert" tool in primary care — triggered by: post-infectious fatigue >6 months + PEM + unrefreshing sleep + cognitive impairment (DePaul or DSQ-PEM screening) — could flag patients before specialist diagnosis, enabling pacing education and harmful treatment avoidance while waiting. This is a screening-not-diagnosis approach.

**Evidence link:** KCE diagnostic delay data + HRQoL association. Post-infectious fatigue literature (ch05-trigger-mechanisms). DePaul Symptom Questionnaire (DSQ-PEM, validated PEM screening tool). Pacing education benefit (early pacing → better prognosis, qualitative). Ratchet model (ch42): early pacing → fewer ratchet events. Wormgoor 2023: PEM-focused care reduces dissatisfaction.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Primary care practices using PEM screening + basic pacing education during diagnostic waiting period show lower PEM frequency at diagnosis compared to standard waitlist practices. (b) Time from first PEM-screening-positive visit to specialist diagnosis does not differ (screening doesn't speed diagnosis, just provides interim management). (c) 5-year functional outcomes better in screened+educated group vs unscreened group, controlling for baseline severity and time to diagnosis.

**Non-specialist consequence:** You don't need a diagnosis to stop harming patients. A 5-minute questionnaire in the GP's office could flag ME/CFS and tell the patient to pace — while they wait 3 years for a specialist who will tell them the same thing.

#### R25. Recognition as Axiological Harm: Beyond Epistemic Injustice

**Category:** 10 — Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE frames recognition as an unmet need. But consider the counter: perhaps the entire "recognition" framing is a political/social demand, not a medical one. Patients with cancer, MS, and ALS also want recognition — but it doesn't appear as the #1 finding in their needs assessments because biomedical understanding already provides it. The ME/CFS recognition deficit exists because we don't understand the disease biology — not because of a separate "recognition problem" that can be solved by education alone. If this is correct, anti-stigma campaigns and clinician education are palliative (reduce suffering from stigma) but not curative (don't change disease trajectory). Resources should go entirely to biomedical research, not recognition interventions. This is the most uncomfortable hypothesis — it implies that the patients' central identified need (recognition) is a consequence, not a cause, and cannot be addressed directly.

**Evidence link:** KCE recognition as central unmet need. Patient qualitative data (420S). HIV stigma reduction literature: stigma declined AFTER biomedical advances (ART, transmission science), not before. Peptic ulcer history: stigma eliminated after H. pylori discovery, despite decades of antacid marketing. MS history: legitimacy came after MRI biomarker, not after patient advocacy alone (though advocacy accelerated research funding).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) If recognition interventions alone work (R22 validation script, R5.1 clinician education), measurable patient outcomes (HRQoL, PEM frequency, invalidity duration) improve without any change in biomedical understanding. (b) If recognition is consequence-not-cause: massive investment in anti-stigma campaigns produces improvement in stigma scores but no change in HRQoL, PEM, or invalidity duration. (c) Longitudinal: stigma scores drop in the decade after a definitive ME/CFS biomarker discovery, without any anti-stigma intervention — just from biomedical legitimacy.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Patients say the biggest problem is not being believed. If they're right, we need education campaigns. If they're wrong — and only a cure will make people believe them — then all the awareness posters are a waste of money that should have gone to lab research.

#### R27. Pacing Dose-Response: Is There a Minimum Effective "Rest Budget"?

**Category:** 5 — Non-Pharmacological Intervention
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** Pacing is widely used but there is no "dose" specified. Hypothesis: there exists a minimum rest fraction below which PEM frequency increases nonlinearly. The energy envelope model (ch38) implies: P(PEM crash) is a function of (exertion / envelope_ratio). If envelope_ratio exceeds some critical threshold θ_crit, crash probability rises sharply. This θ_crit can be estimated from patient diary data (step count, HRV, self-reported exertion + PEM log). If θ_crit varies by severity (more severe → lower θ_crit), this parameter could personalise pacing recommendations: not "rest when tired" but "never exceed 70% of your estimated envelope" for moderate patients, or "never exceed 40%" for severe. The KCE pacing prevalence + wearable data (if collected in supplement) could estimate θ_crit.

**Evidence link:** KCE pacing prevalence. Energy envelope model (ch38, subsec-03). PEM probability function (ch42, ratchet model). Wearable-based pacing (Visible, Garmin, Oura). Heart rate pacing literature (sub-AT pacing, Davenport 2020?). Borg RPE in ME/CFS.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) PEM probability as function of exertion/envelope ratio follows a sigmoid curve with sharp transition at θ_crit ≈ 0.7–0.8 for moderate patients. (b) θ_crit decreases monotonically with severity (mild θ_crit ≈ 0.85; moderate ≈ 0.75; severe ≈ 0.60; very severe ≈ 0.40). (c) Patients pacing below their severity-specific θ_crit have ≥50% fewer PEM events than patients pacing above. (d) If θ_crit does not exist (linear relationship), PEM probability is proportional to exertion with no safe threshold — pacing is harm reduction, not prevention.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Pacing currently means "rest when you're tired." If there's a number — "never go above 70% of your daily limit" — it becomes a treatment with instructions, not a lifestyle suggestion.

#### R28. Belgian Regional ME/CFS Prevalence Estimation: Using KCE Data + Invalidation Database

**Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The Belgian invalidity database records 1,189 individuals with ME/CFS (Dec 2024). This is almost certainly a massive undercount: ME/CFS prevalence typically estimated at 0.2–0.4% (Nacul 2011, Jason 2015). Belgium population 11.7M → expected ME/CFS cases 23,400–46,800. The invalidity database captures only those formally recognised — a ratio of ~40:1 (diagnosed:invalidity-recognised) or ~20:1. This ratio itself is a measure of access failure. Reverse-estimate: if 1,189 represents the most severe and permanently disabled fraction (severity levels 4–5), and severe patients are ~25% of cases, then expected total = 4,756 (still 4–10× below prevalence estimates). This implies either (a) ME/CFS prevalence in Belgium is lower than international estimates, (b) invalidity database dramatically under-ascertains (most likely), or (c) most patients don't qualify for invalidity.

**Evidence link:** KCE invalidity database (1,189, Dec 2024). ME/CFS prevalence literature (Nacul 2011, Jason 2015). NICE NG206 prevalence estimates. Belgian population 11.7M. KCE under-representation of severe patients (sampling bias in opposite direction — severe patients under-counted, making the discrepancy more extreme).

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Using capture-recapture methodology (survey sample + invalidity database), estimated total Belgian ME/CFS population is 25,000–50,000 (consistent with international prevalence). (b) Invalidation database capture rate is 2–5% of cases — indicating severe under-recognition in Belgian social security. (c) Regional variation: Flanders invalidity capture rate > Wallonia (access disparity mirroring KCE linguistic finding).

**Non-specialist consequence:** Belgium thinks it has ~1,200 ME/CFS patients. It probably has 30,000. That's 28,800 people the healthcare system doesn't even know about.

#### R29. Caregiver Burden Assessment: A Missing Variable in the KCE Analysis

**Category:** 2 — Research Direction
**Origin:** brainstorm

**Mechanistic rationale:** The KCE measures patient HRQoL, diagnostic delay, comorbidity, treatment experience, and invalidity. It does NOT measure caregiver burden — despite 75% of patients reporting negative impact on personal relationships and the severe patients being dependent on family care. Caregiver burden (Zarit Burden Interview, Caregiver Strain Index) is predictive of: patient institutionalisation, caregiver burnout/depression, family dissolution, and even patient mortality (elder care literature). In ME/CFS, caregiver burden is particularly salient for severe/very severe patients (bedbound, tube-fed, fully dependent). Measuring it would quantify the societal cost of ME/CFS beyond the patient.

**Evidence link:** KCE personal relationship impact (75%). Caregiver burden literature (Zarit, Schulz 1999). Severe ME/CFS care dependency (Fennell 2021, ch05-severe-reality). KCE supplement (420S) — possibly contains caregiver data from qualitative interviews.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** (a) Caregiver burden (Zarit) is significantly higher for severe/very severe ME/CFS caregivers than for caregivers of other chronic conditions matched for functional dependency (because PEM requires constant energy monitoring, not just physical care). (b) Caregiver burden correlates with patient HRQoL after controlling for severity. (c) Caregivers of unrecognised/dismissed patients (stigma overflow to family) have higher burden than caregivers of recognised patients. (d) If null: caregiver burden is proportional to functional dependency level regardless of diagnosis — ME/CFS is not uniquely burdensome on caregivers.

**Non-specialist consequence:** We measure how sick the patient is but not how exhausted the partner who looks after them is. If the partner burns out, the patient ends up in a care home — at 10× the cost to the state.
