## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses — Mechanistic Connections Not Yet in Literature

### 1.1 The Latency-Lag Hypothesis: Herpesvirus lytic reactivation drives a "two-hit" trial failure
**Mechanistic rationale:** HHV-6 and EBV establish lifelong latency with intermittent lytic reactivation. A 6-month antiviral trial that catches a period of low reactivation frequency may show no benefit even if the drug is mechanistically active. The treatment-duration problem is compounded by the *timing mismatch* between drug administration and reactivation events.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013 (6mo valganciclovir: primary negative, secondary positive); Watt 2012 (longer → better response, p=0.0002). Herpesvirus reactivation is stochastic — mean reactivation interval in healthy adults is ~30–60 days (HHV-6), longer in EBV.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** In HHV-6/EBV seropositive ME/CFS patients on continuous antiviral therapy, clinical response will correlate with the number of reactivation events captured during the treatment period (measured by serial PCR or anti-IgM trajectories), not simply with cumulative drug exposure.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this is true, then two patients receiving the same drug for the same duration may have very different outcomes simply because of luck in when their viruses reactivated — making "negative" trials even harder to interpret without viral monitoring.

### 1.2 The Cumulative-Damage Threshold: Negative trials may reflect irreversible pathology beyond a critical illness duration
**Mechanistic rationale:** The duration critique assumes that extending treatment captures more benefit. But if there is a point where chronic infection/neuroinflammation/mitochondrial dysfunction causes *structural* damage (synaptic loss, autonomic denervation, cardiomyocyte atrophy), then extending treatment past that point would still produce a negative trial. The "window" observed by Strayer 2020 (2–8yr) may reflect this threshold.
**Evidence link:** Strayer 2020 (only 2–8yr window responds); Klempner 2001 (patients avg 4.7yr, already failed 3+ prior courses — may have had irreversible autonomic damage).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients with >8yr illness duration, biomarkers of irreversible pathology (e.g., reduced heart rate variability, reduced CSF DCX+ neuronal integrity, cardiac MRI fibrosis) will correlate with non-response to all treatment modalities, not just duration-limited ones.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would mean there is a biological deadline for treatment effectiveness — delaying intervention by years may permanently close a therapeutic window, shifting the goal from recovery to preventing further decline.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 1.3 The Multi-Target Necessity: Monotherapy trials are structurally negative in multi-mechanism disease
**Mechanistic rationale:** If ME/CFS requires simultaneous interventions against HHV-6, EBV, enterovirus, and possibly intracellular bacteria (like Lyme's Babesia/Bartonella pattern), then ANY monotherapy trial is testing an inadequate treatment spectrum — even if the drug is mechanistically correct. This is not a "negative trial" but a category error in trial design.
**Evidence link:** Popov 2026 (standard Lyme monotherapy misses co-infections); Feng 2019 (Borrelia requires combination therapy); Montoya 2013 (valganciclovir covers HHV-6/EBV but not enterovirus). ME/CFS virome studies show high rates of HHV-6 + EBV + enterovirus co-occurrence.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable prediction:** An ME/CFS antiviral trial testing triple combination (anti-HHV-6 + anti-EBV + anti-enterovirus) for ≥12 months in patients with multi-virus serology will show significantly higher response rates than the sum of historical monotherapy trial responses.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this is right, then every single-drug trial in ME/CFS history that tested a single antiviral against a single virus may have produced a false negative — not because antivirals don't work, but because one drug against one virus cannot fix a multi-virus problem.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 1.4 The Immune Reconstitution Inflammatory Syndrome (IRIS) Analogy: Worsening before improvement is misinterpreted as harm
**Mechanistic rationale:** In HIV, starting antiretrovirals in advanced disease often causes paradoxical worsening (IRIS) as the recovering immune system recognizes previously tolerated pathogens. A similar phenomenon in ME/CFS — where treatment unmasks inflammatory responses to latent pathogens — could produce early worsening that causes trial dropout or negative interim analyses. This would make short trials appear harmful even when longer treatment would be beneficial.
**Evidence link:** Fluge 2015 (23wk mean response lag for rituximab — some patients worsened before improving). Some valganciclovir patients report initial symptom flare.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients starting broad-spectrum antiviral therapy, early worsening (first 4–8 weeks) of inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, neopterin) will predict subsequent clinical improvement at 12+ months, while patients without early inflammatory changes will have lower response rates.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this mechanism exists, a trial that stops at 3 months because patients felt worse would mistakenly conclude the treatment is dangerous when it was actually starting to work — exactly opposite of the correct interpretation.

## Category 2: Research Directions — Experiments That Resolve Key Uncertainties

### 2.1 Head-to-Head Duration Trial: 6-month vs 12-month valganciclovir in HHV-6/EBV seropositive ME/CFS
**Mechanistic rationale:** Directly test the duration hypothesis. The strongest evidence would come from randomizing duration rather than comparing to placebo.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (p=0.0002 for duration-response); Montoya 2013 (6mo: mixed primary/secondary split).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12-month valganciclovir will show significantly greater improvement in fatigue severity and cognitive function than 6-month valganciclovir at 12-month follow-up, in patients with elevated HHV-6/EBV IgG.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This single trial would settle whether longer antiviral treatment produces more benefit — it is the most direct test of the entire duration critique.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 2.2 DeLong-Style Biostatistical Reanalysis of All ME/CFS Antiviral/Immunomodulatory Trials
**Mechanistic rationale:** Apply DeLong 2012's methodology (power analysis, effect size extraction, sample size calculation for equivalent conclusions) to the full ME/CFS negative-trials corpus.
**Evidence link:** DeLong 2012 (found all 4 Lyme retreatment RCTs underpowered); Smith 2015 ("most trials limited by size AND duration").
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.70
**Falsifiable prediction:** A DeLong-style reanalysis of ≥10 ME/CFS negative treatment trials will find that ≥60% lacked adequate power to detect clinically meaningful effect sizes (≥0.3–0.5 SMD), given their actual observed variability.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would show clearly and quantitatively whether ME/CFS negative trials are genuinely negative or just too small to detect real benefits — providing an evidence-based answer rather than speculation.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 2.3 Prospective Stratified Trial by Disease Duration: Early (<3yr), Middle (3–8yr), Late (>8yr)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Strayer 2020's post-hoc finding of a 2–8yr window requires prospective confirmation. If confirmed, it transforms trial design.
**Evidence link:** Strayer 2020 (2–8yr: 2x improvement; outside: no benefit); Fluge 2019 (negative overall but no duration stratification).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a prospective RCT of any immunomodulatory treatment (e.g., rintatolimod, IVIG, valganciclovir) stratified by illness duration at enrollment, patients in the 3–8yr stratum will show significantly greater treatment benefit than the <3yr and >8yr strata, after controlling for baseline severity.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If confirmed, every future ME/CFS trial would need to enroll patients within a specific disease-duration window — and past negative trials that did not do this would be reinterpreted as flawed rather than definitive.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 2.4 Adaptive Platform Trial Testing Sequential Duration Escalation
**Mechanistic rationale:** Instead of fixed-duration placebo-controlled trials, use an adaptive design where patients who do not respond at 6 months are re-randomized to continue or switch. This directly maps the dose-duration-response relationship.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (duration-response); Fluge 2015 (wide response lag range 8–66wk). Adaptive designs are recommended by the ME/CFS research community but never implemented.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** An adaptive platform trial with 3-month interim analyses and duration escalation (3mo → 6mo → 12mo → 18mo) for non-responders will identify a responder subset that requires ≥12 months of treatment — a subset undetectable in fixed-duration trials.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This design would find the right treatment duration for each patient, rather than imposing an arbitrary trial length that may be too short for everyone.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas — Repurposed Drugs, New Targets, Dose/Timing

### 3.1 Extended-Course (≥12 month) Maribavir for HHV-6 in ME/CFS
**Mechanistic rationale:** Maribavir inhibits UL97 kinase (CMV/HHV-6) with better tolerability than valganciclovir. No trial in ME/CFS. The duration critique predicts previous valganciclovir trials were too short; maribavir's better safety profile enables longer treatment.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013 (6mo valganciclovir: positive signal); Watt 2012 (duration-response). Maribavir approved for CMV in transplant recipients.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12-month maribavir in HHV-6 seropositive ME/CFS will show ≥40% response rate at 15 months (Hamilton depression? no — Chalder Fatigue Scale or SF-36 PF), exceeding historical valganciclovir 6-month response rates.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would provide a better-tolerated way to test whether truly prolonged antiviral therapy works — if maribavir succeeds where valganciclovir showed mixed results, the duration critique is confirmed.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 3.2 Letermovir for HHV-6: Novel Mechanism, Better Safety
**Mechanistic rationale:** Letermovir targets the HHV-6 terminase complex (unique mechanism) with minimal myelotoxicity. It could be combined with valganciclovir for dual-mechanism HHV-6 coverage without additive bone marrow suppression.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013; Popov 2026 (co-infection coverage principle). Letermovir is approved for CMV prophylaxis.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
**Falsifiable prediction:** Letermovir + valganciclovir combination in HHV-6/EBV co-positive ME/CFS patients will show higher EBV viral load reduction than valganciclovir alone, and the combination will be tolerable for ≥12 months.
**Non-specialist consequence:** A drug duo that attacks HHV-6 from two angles simultaneously could overcome the spectrum problem that has plagued single-drug antiviral trials in ME/CFS.

### 3.3 Pulse-Dosing Valacyclovir/Valganciclovir to Match Reactivation Cycles
**Mechanistic rationale:** Instead of continuous daily dosing, pulse dosing (e.g., 7 days on, 7 days off) may better match herpesvirus reactivation cycles while reducing cumulative drug exposure and toxicity. This could enable longer total treatment duration.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (duration-response). Herpesvirus reactivation is cyclical not constant.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
**Falsifiable prediction:** Pulsed valacyclovir (7/7) over 12 months in HHV-6 seropositive ME/CFS will show similar efficacy to daily dosing with fewer adverse events, enabling longer treatment.
**Non-specialist consequence:** Alternating weeks on and off drug could be easier to tolerate for a year or more — making extended treatment practical where continuous daily dosing caused side effects.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 3.4 Tenofovir Disoproxil for Endogenous Retrovirus (HERV) Reactivation
**Mechanistic rationale:** HERVs (particularly HERV-W/MSRV) are reactivated in ME/CFS and neuroinflammatory conditions. Tenofovir inhibits HERV reverse transcriptase. No trial in ME/CFS. Standard ME/CFS antivirals (valganciclovir, acyclovir) have no HERV activity — a spectrum gap.
**Evidence link:** Popov 2026 (spectrum principle). HERV-W envelope protein (syncytin-1) is found in MS lesions; tenofovir reduces HERV expression in HIV patients. No ME/CFS-specific HERV studies found in Phase 1.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
**Falsifiable prediction:** Twelve months of tenofovir in HERV-W seropositive ME/CFS patients will reduce HERV-W envelope protein levels and correlate with clinical improvement, while valganciclovir (no anti-HERV activity) will not.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would identify an entirely new viral target in ME/CFS that current antiviral trials have completely ignored — revealing a spectrum gap even wider than previously thought.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 3.5 Combination Antiviral Cocktail: Valganciclovir + Famciclovir + Tenofovir
**Mechanistic rationale:** A three-drug regimen covering HHV-6 (valganciclovir), EBV/VZV (famciclovir), and HERVs (tenofovir). This directly tests the multi-target hypothesis.
**Evidence link:** Popov 2026; Feng 2019; Watt 2012; Montoya 2013. Horowitz 2020 (dapsone combination in Lyme: 98% improved).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12-month triple antiviral in virome-positive ME/CFS (≥2 of HHV-6/EBV/HERV/enterovirus seropositive) will produce ≥50% response rate at 15 months.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would be the most aggressive test of the "spectrum" critique — if even triple antiviral coverage fails, then the infection hypothesis itself would need serious revision.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### 4.1 Lysine + Propolis + Zinc as Adjunctive "Long-Duration" Maintenance for EBV
**Mechanistic rationale:** Lysine antagonizes arginine for viral replication; propolis (caffeic acid phenethyl ester) inhibits EBV lytic cycle; zinc inhibits herpesvirus replication. These are safe enough for indefinite use and could supplement a finite antiviral course to extend the effective treatment duration *after* drug cessation.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013 (positive signal at 6mo). Lysine ≥3g/day reduces EBV recurrence; zinc lozenges reduce cold duration. No ME/CFS-specific studies.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
**Falsifiable prediction:** Twelve months of lysine + propolis + zinc after a 6-month valganciclovir course will reduce EBV reactivation rate and maintain clinical gains better than valganciclovir alone followed by no maintenance.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this works, patients could use inexpensive, low-side-effect supplements to maintain antiviral benefits long after stopping prescription antivirals — making the "duration" problem solvable without indefinite drug exposure.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 4.2 Lactoferrin as Broad-Spectrum Antiviral: Test of the Spectrum Hypothesis
**Mechanistic rationale:** Lactoferrin inhibits HHV-6, EBV, enterovirus, and HSV entry into host cells. It is a cheap, safe food supplement that could serve as a "broad spectrum" control arm. If lactoferrin alone produces any signal, it would support the multi-pathogen hypothesis.
**Evidence link:** Popov 2026 (spectrum principle). In vitro: lactoferrin inhibits HHV-6 and enterovirus. One small open-label ME/CFS study showed improvement.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
**Falsifiable prediction:** Six months of high-dose lactoferrin (1000–1500 mg/day) in unselected ME/CFS will produce a ≥15% improvement in Chalder Fatigue Scale vs placebo, but with high variance reflecting heterogeneous viral triggers.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If a food supplement that broadly blocks viral entry shows any effect, it would suggest that multiple viruses contribute to ME/CFS — validating the spectrum critique at minimal cost and risk.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### 5.1 Duration-Extended Pacing with Viral Load Monitoring: "Treatment Until Clearance"
**Mechanistic rationale:** The exposure-symptom correlation in ME/CFS may partially reflect viral reactivation triggered by physical/cognitive stress. Pacing that reduces reactivation-triggering stress, combined with serial viral load monitoring, could test whether symptom improvement lags behind viral suppression by weeks to months.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (duration-response); Fluge 2015 (23wk response lag).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients with elevated HHV-6 PCR, a 12-month pacing intervention with serial viral monitoring will show that symptom improvement occurs 8–16 weeks *after* viral load suppression, consistent with a slow recovery of immune-mechanism equilibrium.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would mean that even when viral activity is suppressed, patients may need to wait months for symptoms to improve — explaining why short clinical trials produce negative results.

## Category 6: Combinations + Access

### 6.1 Tiered Treatment Access: Duration Escalation Based on Early Response
**Mechanistic rationale:** Current clinical practice often stops treatment after 3–6 months if no benefit. The duration critique predicts that responders may need much longer. A tiered protocol: 6mo → 12mo → 18mo → 24mo with objective response criteria at each step.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (p=0.0002 for duration-response); Strayer 2020 (window).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a tiered-treatment cohort, ≥30% of patients who do NOT respond at 6 months WILL respond by 12 months, and ≥15% of 12-month non-responders will respond by 18 months.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this is true, current practice of stopping treatment at 3–6 months could be denying a substantial fraction of patients the extended treatment they need — and a simple protocol change could identify them.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 6.2 Diagnostic Trial of Extended Treatment: "Treatment as Diagnosis"
**Mechanistic rationale:** When standard diagnostics cannot distinguish between "treatment failed" and "duration too short," a therapeutic trial of extended treatment becomes a diagnostic tool. This is clinically accessible without requiring new trials.
**Evidence link:** DeLong 2012 (re-analysis shows benefit); Watt 2012 (duration-response); Klempner 2001 (patients averaged 4.7yr illness pre-treatment).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable prediction:** A 12-month open-label valganciclovir extension (current: 6-month RCT) will identify a new responder subset — patients who worsened or were unchanged at 6 months but improve by 12 months — estimated at 15–30% of the initial cohort.
**Non-specialist consequence:** For patients who fail a standard course, trying a longer course before concluding "this drug doesn't work for me" could reveal hidden responders — but requires acceptance that extended drug exposure carries its own risks.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 Reaction-Diffusion Model of Viral Latency Reactivation + Drug Pharmacokinetics
**Mechanistic rationale:** Model viral reactivation as a stochastic Poisson process with variable inter-event intervals, drug concentration as a decaying function, and clinical improvement as an integral of "reactivation events captured" over time. This would predict that short trials fail because the cumulative probability of capturing a reactivation event is too low.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013 (6mo trial — may have missed reactivation events); Watt 2012 (longer duration → more cumulative reactivation capture).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** The model will predict that the probability of detecting a treatment effect in a 6-month trial is ≤0.5 when the mean inter-reactivation interval exceeds 60 days, rising to ≥0.8 at 12 months.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This model would give trial designers a quantitative tool: "How many months of treatment does your trial need to have an 80% chance of detecting a real effect?" — replacing guesswork with a calculation.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 7.2 Power-Analysis Meta-Model for Retrospective Trial Evaluation
**Mechanistic rationale:** Extend DeLong 2012's approach: build a meta-model that, given initial study parameters (n, effect size, dropout, SD), computes the minimum treatment duration needed for 80% power. Apply to all 35 ME/CFS trials in Smith 2015.
**Evidence link:** DeLong 2012; Smith 2015 (systematic review: most trials limited by size AND duration).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** ≥70% of the 35 ME/CFS treatment trials in Smith 2015 will have a required treatment duration for 80% power that exceeds the actual trial duration — i.e., they were structurally incapable of detecting a real effect within their timeframe.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would produce the first quantitative summary of just how under-duration most ME/CFS trials have been — a number that advocates and researchers can use to argue for longer trials in the next generation of studies.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 7.3 Dynamical Systems Model of Treatment Response Lag
**Mechanistic rationale:** Model the ME/CFS system as a multi-stable attractor (sick state ↔ recovery state) with treatment slowly shifting the landscape. Response lag arises from the time needed to escape a deep attractor basin — which scales with illness duration (longer in attractor → deeper basin). This predicts duration-dependent lag: longer-ill patients need longer treatment.
**Evidence link:** Fluge 2015 (23wk mean lag, range 8–66wk); Strayer 2020 (window effect).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
**Falsifiable prediction:** The model will predict response lag = k × ln(illness duration), where k ≈ 8 weeks per e-fold of illness duration. In practice: a patient ill 2yr has ~8wk lag; 5yr → 20wk lag; 10yr → 32wk lag.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this model holds, it gives clinicians a rough formula: "treat for at least half as long as the patient has been ill before expecting improvement" — radically changing trial design assumptions.

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### 8.1 Post-Stroke Treatment Duration: A Successful Paradigm for Chronic Intervention
**Mechanistic rationale:** Stroke rehabilitation has shown that treatment *duration* is a primary determinant of recovery, with trials of <6 months showing modest benefit while 12-month+ interventions produce substantial gains. The same biological principle — neural plasticity and recovery require extended intervention — may apply to ME/CFS-associated autonomic and cognitive dysfunction.
**Evidence link:** Stroke recovery literature (e.g., AVERT, FLAME trials). DeLong 2012 (Lyme under-duration).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** A meta-analysis of ME/CFS treatment trials stratified by duration will show a positive correlation between trial duration and effect size (r ≥ 0.4), mirroring the stroke rehabilitation literature, after controlling for intervention type.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If recovery from nervous system injury consistently requires >6 months of treatment across diseases, then expecting ME/CFS to respond in 3-month trials would be biologically naive.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 8.2 Long COVID Treatment Trials: A Controlled Test of the Duration Critique
**Mechanistic rationale:** Long COVID trials use many of the same interventions tested in ME/CFS (antivirals, immunomodulators) but are newer and may replicate the same duration errors. Observing whether Long COVID trials suffer from the same duration confounds would provide an independent test of the critique.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013; Watt 2012.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** A systematic review of COVID antiviral treatment trials at 6 months follow-up will show that trials of ≤3 months duration systematically underestimate treatment effects compared to trials of >6 months, replicating the ME/CFS pattern.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would show that the duration problem is not specific to ME/CFS but is a general flaw in chronic-disease treatment research — and that Long COVID research is at risk of making the same mistakes ME/CFS research already made.

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### 9.1 The "Pause After Treatment" Biomarker: Viral Load at +3 Months Off-Drug
**Mechanistic rationale:** If treatment suppresses but does not eradicate a pathogen, viral load will rebound after drug cessation. Measuring viral load 3 months *after* completing a course could distinguish "cured" from "suppressed." A post-treatment viral rebound would indicate that the drug was working but the duration was too short to achieve clearance.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (longer → better response); Fallon 2008 (cognitive improvement lost at 24wk off-drug).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients treated with valganciclovir for 6 months, post-treatment HHV-6/EBV PCR at +3 months will be a stronger predictor of clinical relapse at +6 months than any on-treatment measure.
**Non-specialist consequence:** A simple blood test 3 months after stopping treatment could tell patients and doctors whether they benefited from drug but needed to stay on it longer — directly informing the clinical decision to resume therapy.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 9.2 Treatment-Predictive Biomarker: Early NK Cell Function Recovery at 3 Months
**Mechanistic rationale:** NK cell dysfunction is well-documented in ME/CFS. If treatment is working, NK function should improve before clinical symptoms. An NK function assay at 3 months could predict eventual 12-month response, allowing early identification of patients who need extended treatment.
**Evidence link:** Fluge 2015 (23wk lag) — if NK function improves at 12 weeks but symptoms improve at 24 weeks, the NK assay provides an early surrogate.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients starting immunomodulatory therapy, NK cytotoxicity at month 3 predicts clinical response at month 12 with AUC ≥0.75, independent of treatment assignment.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If available, this blood test at 3 months could tell a patient "your immune system is responding — you need to stay on this drug for 9 more months to feel the benefit" vs "no response, stop now," preventing premature discontinuation.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 9.3 In-Treatment Viral Load Trajectory as Trial Stratification Variable
**Mechanistic rationale:** Not all patients have active viral replication at trial entry. Serial on-treatment viral load monitoring could identify the subset with active viral replication who are the only ones likely to benefit. Negative trial results stratified by viral load trajectory would reveal hidden responder subsets.
**Evidence link:** Montoya 2013 (virus-selected population showed stronger signal).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable prediction:** Re-analysis of ME/CFS antiviral trial data stratifying by on-treatment viral load change (suppressed vs stable vs rebound) will reveal a significant treatment effect in the "suppressed" subgroup that is obscured in the pooled analysis, and this effect will increase with treatment duration.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would show that past trials may have been negative because they included patients whose viruses were not active during the trial period — like testing antibiotics on patients without a bacterial infection and concluding antibiotics don't work.

## Category 10: Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS (CRITICAL — MANDATORY)

### 10.1 The Duration Critique May Explain Only Lyme, Not ME/CFS
**Mechanistic rationale:** Lyme disease has a known causative pathogen (B. burgdorferi) with established biofilm/persister biology. ME/CFS has no established single pathogen. The duration critique may apply specifically to infectious diseases and not to conditions where the primary driver is autoimmune, metabolic, or neurological. The parallel may be analogical rather than homologous.
**Evidence link:** Fluge 2019 (rituximab Phase III: 12 months, adequate duration, genuinely negative).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65
**Falsifiable prediction:** Prospective reanalysis of ME/CFS treatment trials using DeLong's power-analysis method will find that trial duration explains ≤10% of variance in trial outcomes, with sample size and patient heterogeneity explaining the remainder.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this is correct, pushing for longer ME/CFS trials would waste resources that should go toward better patient stratification and larger sample sizes.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 10.2 The Placebo Response Is a More Likely Confound Than Duration
**Mechanistic rationale:** In blinded ME/CFS trials, placebo response rates are frequently >30%. The positive signal in "too short" trials may be placebo amplified by the Hawthorne effect of frequent monitoring. Duration extension could increase placebo exposure, not treatment exposure.
**Evidence link:** Fluge 2019 (35.1% placebo response). Krupp 2003 (RR 3.5 for fatigue improvement — but small n=55).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a trial with extended treatment duration, early improvement (weeks 1–12) will predict later improvement equally in drug and placebo groups, suggesting the duration effect is placebo maintenance rather than drug accumulation.
**Non-specialist consequence:** Longer trials could simply give placebo responders more time to feel better — and then we would mistake extended placebo effect for drug efficacy.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 10.3 Natural Recovery Confounds Duration Interpretation
**Mechanistic rationale:** ME/CFS has a significant spontaneous recovery rate (~5–10% per year in some cohorts). Extended-duration trials that show benefit at 12+ months may inadvertently capture natural recovery that would have happened anyway. Without a concurrent placebo control, duration is inseparable from natural history.
**Evidence link:** Smith 2015 (systematic review notes natural recovery confound). Fluge 2019 (negative at 12mo with adequate placebo control).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS natural-history cohorts, patients with illness duration 2–8 years (the "window") have a higher rate of spontaneous improvement than those <2 or >8 years, independent of treatment — making Strayer 2020's window finding potentially an artifact of natural history.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this is true, some patients who appear to benefit from long treatments may simply have recovered on their own — and the longer the trial, the more natural recovery masquerades as treatment effect.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 10.4 The Wrong-Population Critique Is Stronger Than the Duration Critique
**Mechanistic rationale:** The Lyme trials failed not primarily because they were too short, but because they enrolled patients with advanced, multi-treatment-failure disease (average 4.7yr, 3+ prior courses). In ME/CFS, the most important confound may be illness duration at enrollment (Strayer's window), not trial duration per se.
**Evidence link:** Klempner 2001; Cameron 2006 (generalizability critique); Strayer 2020.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** A meta-regression of ME/CFS treatment trials will show that mean illness duration at enrollment predicts trial outcome more strongly than trial treatment duration — i.e., enrolling patients with early/mid-stage disease matters more than how long you treat them.
**Non-specialist consequence:** This would shift the focus from "how long to treat" to "whom to treat" — suggesting that enrolling patients with <8 years illness is the single most important design change, more important than trial duration.

*origin: brainstorm*

## Category 11: Null Hypothesis Assessment — What If Duration Critique Has No Role? (CRITICAL — MANDATORY)

### 11.1 All Negative ME/CFS Trials Are Genuinely Negative
**Mechanistic rationale:** The simplest explanation: treatments that have failed in adequately powered RCTs (rituximab, galantamine, fluoxetine, hydrocortisone) failed because the underlying mechanism is wrong, not because duration was too short. The duration critique risks becoming a post-hoc rationalization for failed hypotheses.
**Evidence link:** Fluge 2019 (12mo rituximab, n=151, genuinely negative). Blacker 2004 (galantamine, n=434, negative). Vercoulen 1996 (fluoxetine, n=96, negative).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable prediction:** An adequately powered (n≥200 per arm) 18-month trial of valganciclovir in HHV-6/EBV positive ME/CFS will show no significant improvement over placebo, refuting the duration critique for antiviral therapy.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If this is true, the ME/CFS field would be better served by abandoning the infection hypothesis and focusing resources on the autoimmunity, metabolic, or neurological mechanisms that have stronger evidence.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 11.2 The Duration Critique Causes Therapeutic Misestimation
**Mechanistic rationale:** Over-emphasizing duration may lead to systematically overestimated treatment effects in the literature. If every negative trial is re-interpreted as "too short," no treatment is ever ruled out, and the evidence base becomes unfalsifiable — you can always explain away a negative result by appealing to duration.
**Evidence link:** DeLong 2012 (re-analysis shows effect). But this creates a perverse incentive: every failed trial can be dismissed as "under-duration" without evidence.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** A systematic review using the duration critique framework will find that trials classified as "inadequate duration" have a publication-bias-adjusted effect size indistinguishable from zero, suggesting the duration critique protects null findings from falsification.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If unchecked, the duration critique becomes a shield that prevents ME/CFS research from ever conclusively ruling out ineffective treatments — keeping patients on expensive, risky therapies without evidence they work.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 11.3 The True Confound Is Attrition Bias, Not Duration
**Mechanistic rationale:** Longer trials have higher dropout rates. Completers in long trials are selectively healthier/more motivated than dropouts, creating a survivor bias that inflates apparent treatment effects. The "duration effect" in Watt 2012 (p=0.0002) may reflect selective retention of responders.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (retrospective, not RCT — confounding by indication; patients who stayed on drug longer were self-selected). Fluge 2019 (12mo with 12% dropout — still negative).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** In extended-duration treatment trials for ME/CFS, an intention-to-treat analysis will show lower effect sizes than per-protocol analysis, and the difference will increase linearly with trial duration, indicating that survivor bias drives the apparent duration benefit.
**Non-specialist consequence:** Longer trials that show better outcomes may simply have lost the patients who were not responding — it is selection of who stays, not treatment duration, that creates the appearance of benefit.

## Category 12: Evidence Quality Concerns (CRITICAL — MANDATORY)

### 12.1 The Key Evidence for Duration Is Retrospective and Uncontrolled
**Mechanistic rationale:** The strongest evidence for the duration critique in ME/CFS (Watt 2012) is a retrospective chart review with no randomization, no placebo control, and confounding by indication (patients who received longer treatment may have been systematically different). This is hypothesis-generating evidence, not hypothesis-testing.
**Evidence link:** Watt 2012 (retrospective n=61, p=0.0002 for duration-response). No prospective duration-RCT exists.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.70 (concern about a specific paper) versus 0.40 (general pattern concern)
**Falsifiable prediction:** A prospective RCT randomizing ME/CFS patients to 6 vs 12 months of valganciclovir will show no significant difference in outcomes, demonstrating that Watt 2012's duration finding was confounded by indication.
**Non-specialist consequence:** The central evidence for the duration critique in ME/CFS may be an artifact of how patients were selected for long treatment, not a genuine drug-duration relationship.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 12.2 DeLong 2012: Over-Reliance on a Single Reanalysis
**Mechanistic rationale:** The entire Lyme-to-ME/CFS bridge rests heavily on DeLong 2012's biostatistical reanalysis. This is one paper, not a replicated finding. The reanalysis used retrospective power calculations, which are methodologically controversial (post-hoc power is not a valid inference tool).
**Evidence link:** DeLong 2012. Controversy in the statistical literature: post-hoc power calculations are considered by many statisticians to be uninformative (Hoenig & Heisey 2001).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** A pre-registered reanalysis of the Lyme retreatment trials using Bayesian methods instead of post-hoc power will show that the data are consistent with both efficacy and null, with Bayes factors <3 — i.e., the data are insufficient to support either conclusion, not "trials were underpowered."
**Non-specialist consequence:** The DeLong reanalysis that drives the duration critique may itself be methodologically questionable — the field may be building an argument on contested statistics.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 12.3 Selection Bias in the Lyme Trials Is More Important Than Duration
**Mechanistic rationale:** Cameron 2006's critique is about *who* was enrolled, not *how long* they were treated. The Klempner trial enrolled patients who had already failed 3+ courses — a population that may have irreversible pathology. Duration is a secondary concern if the wrong population was tested.
**Evidence link:** Cameron 2006; Klempner 2001 (mean 4.7yr illness). The Klempner trial was designed to test retreatment, not first-line treatment. Applying its results to treatment-naive patients is the error.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65
**Falsifiable prediction:** A reanalysis of the Klempner data stratified by number of prior treatment courses will show that treatment-native or 1-prior-course patients actually benefited (≥30% improvement in fatigue), while the negative result was driven by the multi-treatment-failure subgroup.
**Non-specialist consequence:** The duration critique may overcomplicate matters — the simple explanation is that Klempner tested treatment on the wrong patients, and 90 days might be perfectly adequate for treatment-naive patients.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 12.4 Horowitz 2020 — Very Low Evidence Quality Driving a High-Impact Claim
**Mechanistic rationale:** The dapsone combination 98% response claim (Horowitz 2020) is a case series (n=40, no control group, no blinding, subjective outcomes). Yet it is cited as evidence that combination treatment works. This represents the worst of low-evidence driving high-certainty claims.
**Evidence link:** Horowitz 2020 (certainty: 0.30 in Phase 1 assessment — lowest of all included papers).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.80 (concern about this specific paper)
**Falsifiable prediction:** A randomized controlled trial of dapsone combination therapy for chronic Lyme will show a response rate <50%, substantially lower than the 98% reported in the uncontrolled case series.
**Non-specialist consequence:** If the most dramatic evidence for the combination approach comes from a study design that notoriously overestimates benefits, patients may be pursuing expensive and risky combination therapies based on inflated expectations.

*origin: brainstorm*

### 12.5 The Lyme Triad (Duration + Spectrum + Window) Is Not Independently Testable in ME/CFS
**Mechanistic rationale:** The three factors of the Lyme framework are confounded — duration can't be tested independently of spectrum and window in any practical trial design (if you extend duration, you also shift the window; if you broaden spectrum, you change the drug). The framework provides useful post-hoc explanation but may be unfalsifiable as a unified claim.
**Evidence link:** Strayer 2020 (window); Popov 2026 (spectrum); Watt 2012 (duration). No factorial trial exists.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable prediction:** A 2×2 factorial trial in HHV-6/EBV ME/CFS (6mo vs 12mo × valganciclovir vs valganciclovir+enterovirus coverage) will fail to show that either factor independently predicts outcome, but their interaction term will be significant — confirming the confound.
**Non-specialist consequence:** Even with the best trial design, it may be impossible to disentangle whether duration, spectrum, or treatment window caused a positive result — meaning the framework may always remain a useful concept but never a testable hypothesis.
