## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 — S100A8/A9 (Calprotectin) as the Shared HS–ME/CFS DAMP That Activates NLRP3 (Cert 0.30) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Mechanistic rationale.** HS is characterized by massive neutrophil infiltration of dermal tunnels and abscesses, with NETosis releasing S100A8/A9 (calprotectin) — a potent DAMP that activates TLR4 and RAGE, which in turn prime and activate NLRP3. Serum S100A8/A9 is elevated in HS and correlates with disease severity. In ME/CFS, exercise releases DAMPs (HMGB1, ccf-mtDNA, S100A8/A9) that are candidate NLRP3 activators during PEM. If S100A8/A9 is the specific molecular bridge — the DAMP that converges from two different trigger sites (skin follicles in HS, muscle/mitochondria in ME/CFS) onto the same NLRP3→IL-1β→fatigue effector pathway — this would explain how two mechanistically distinct initiating events produce phenotypically similar fatigue via a common downstream mediator. S100A8/A9 has the advantage of being serum-measurable (ELISA, widely available), making the hypothesis directly testable in both conditions without invasive tissue sampling.

**Evidence link.** HS neutrophil/NETosis literature (established but not in the 15-paper set); S100A8/A9 in HS serum (multiple studies); S100A8/A9→TLR4/RAGE→NLRP3 priming (well-established pathway); existing paper inflammation registry has DAMP sources listed (HMGB1, ccf-mtDNA) but no S100A8/A9 → NLRP3 node. Zero studies have measured S100A8/A9 in ME/CFS.

**Falsifiable prediction.** Serum S100A8/A9 will be elevated vs healthy controls in both active HS (d≥1.0) and ME/CFS (d≥0.5), with HS levels correlating with Hurley stage and ME/CFS levels correlating with PEM severity. In ME/CFS, post-CPET S100A8/A9 will rise at 2–4h, preceding the IL-1β peak by 2–6h (cross-correlation lag, r>0.4). HS patients achieving HiSCR ≥50 on biologics will show ≥30% reduction in S100A8/A9. Falsified if S100A8/A9 is normal in ME/CFS or does not rise post-exercise.

**Gap.** Zero S100A8/A9 data in ME/CFS. The DAMP hierarchy (which of HMGB1, S100A8/A9, ccf-mtDNA, ATP is dominant in ME/CFS PEM) is completely unstudied. S100A8/A9 is also elevated in obesity, metabolic syndrome, and aging — all confounds in the HS population.

### 1.2 — IL-17 as the Cytokine That Most Efficiently Translates Peripheral NLRP3 Activation into CNS Fatigue (Cert 0.35) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Mechanistic rationale.** HS is an IL-17-driven disease (Th17 expansion, secukinumab and bimekizumab approved for HS). IL-17 crosses the blood-brain barrier more efficiently than TNF-α or IL-1β via a saturable transport system, and directly activates IL-17 receptors on astrocytes and microglia, inducing neuroinflammation, glutamate excitotoxicity, and sickness behavior. In ME/CFS, Th17 skewing is documented but inconsistently. If IL-17 is the specific cytokine that links peripheral NLRP3 activation to CNS fatigue, then: (a) anti-IL-17 therapy (secukinumab, bimekizumab) should reduce fatigue more than anti-TNF therapy (adalimumab) in HS, because IL-17 has superior CNS access; (b) HS patients on anti-IL-17 should show greater fatigue improvement than those on anti-TNF, after controlling for equivalent skin response (HiSCR); (c) ME/CFS patients with the highest fatigue should have the highest serum IL-17 — a prediction testable with existing ME/CFS cytokine datasets. IL-17 thus serves as a "candidate specificity filter" — if it doesn't outperform other cytokines as a fatigue predictor, the shared HS-ME/CFS pathway is likely mediated by a different (or redundant) cytokine.

**Evidence link.** IL-17 BBB transport (Huppert 2010, Kebir 2007); IL-17 in HS (established, trial-proven for secukinumab/bimekizumab); ME/CFS Th17 literature (mixed, e.g., Hornig 2015 found IL-17 elevation, others did not); existing paper ch14d HS section mentions IL-17 as part of the HS inflammatory architecture but does not single it out as the fatigue-specific bridge. Zero post-hoc HS trial fatigue analyses comparing anti-IL-17 vs anti-TNF exist.

**Falsifiable prediction.** In a pooled post-hoc analysis of HS phase 3 trials (adalimumab, secukinumab, bimekizumab), anti-IL-17-treated patients will show ≥0.3 SD greater reduction in any available fatigue-related patient-reported outcome (DLQI emotional domain, pain VAS, or EQ-5D usual-activities dimension used as fatigue proxies) vs anti-TNF-treated patients after controlling for HiSCR achievement. The effect will be largest at Week 16 (before maximal skin improvement) and will narrow by Week 52 (as skin improvement equalizes). In ME/CFS, serum IL-17 will correlate with fatigue severity (FSS, r≥0.4) more strongly than IL-1β, TNF-α, or IL-6 in a multivariable model. Falsified if IL-17 does not outperform TNF-α or IL-1β as a fatigue predictor in either condition.

**Gap.** No validated fatigue endpoint in any HS trial. IL-17 measurement is more technically demanding than IL-6 (shorter half-life, requires careful sample handling). Th17 cells are a minor population — serum IL-17 may not reflect tissue-level production. Redundant cytokine pathways (IL-17A, IL-17F, IL-17A/F heterodimer, IL-23 upstream) mean blocking IL-17A alone may incompletely suppress the pathway.

### 1.3 — Keratinocyte-Intrinsic NLRP3 Priming as a "Two-Hit" Model for ME/CFS Inflammasome Susceptibility (Cert 0.25) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Mechanistic rationale.** HS keratinocytes are intrinsically abnormal — follicular occlusion, Notch signaling defects, and altered differentiation — even before visible inflammation develops. This creates a "primed" state (Signal 1 of NLRP3 activation: NF-κB-driven pro-IL-1β and NLRP3 protein upregulation) in which minor triggers (commensal bacteria, friction, sweat) produce disproportionate inflammasome activation (Signal 2). In ME/CFS, the analogous "primed" state could be epigenetically maintained NLRP3 upregulation in monocytes and microglia — the same molecular logic (constitutively primed inflammasome → exaggerated response to subthreshold triggers) but in a different cell type and body compartment. HS thus provides proof-of-concept that chronic NLRP3 priming can exist as a stable cellular state in a human disease, maintained by epigenetic mechanisms (histone H3K4me3 at the NLRP3 promoter, reduced DNA methylation at the IL1B locus) rather than by ongoing stimulation. If ME/CFS monocytes share this epigenetic priming signature, the inflammasome "hypersensitivity" that characterizes PEM has an identifiable molecular substrate — and HS, where tissue is accessible, becomes the discovery platform for the epigenetic marks that maintain it.

**Evidence link.** HS keratinocyte intrinsic defects (Notch/γ-secretase literature, though not in the 15-paper set); NLRP3 epigenetic priming literature (Terc 2023, NLRP3 promoter histone acetylation in trained immunity); existing paper epigenetic section (ch28) discusses trained immunity but not NLRP3 priming specifically. Zero studies have compared HS keratinocyte and ME/CFS monocyte epigenomes.

**Falsifiable prediction.** ME/CFS CD14+ monocytes will show (a) increased H3K4me3 at the NLRP3 promoter (ChIP-qPCR, ≥1.5× vs healthy controls); (b) increased NLRP3 mRNA at baseline (RT-qPCR, ≥2×); (c) decreased DNA methylation at CpG islands in the IL1B and NLRP3 loci (bisulfite sequencing, ≥10% difference in mean methylation). These epigenetic marks will correlate with the magnitude of IL-1β release after ex vivo ATP stimulation (a functional readout of inflammasome "gain"). HS lesional keratinocytes (accessible via punch biopsy) will show the same epigenetic marks, and the degree of overlap (shared differentially methylated regions) will identify the conserved inflammasome-priming signature. Falsified if ME/CFS monocyte NLRP3 chromatin is indistinguishable from healthy controls.

**Gap.** No ME/CFS monocyte epigenome focused on inflammasome genes exists. HS keratinocyte–ME/CFS monocyte cross-tissue comparison is logistically challenging (requires coordinated dermatology + ME/CFS specialist recruitment). Epigenetic priming could be cell-type-specific — shared marks across keratinocytes and monocytes are not guaranteed.

## 2. Research Directions

### 2.1 — Serial Cytokine + Fatigue Sampling Through an HS Flare Cycle (Cert 0.40) [proposal] [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** The cleanest human model for studying the temporal relationship between systemic cytokines and fatigue: HS patients experience spontaneous, predictable inflammatory flares of known tissue origin (pilosebaceous unit) with prodromal fatigue (Ring 2017). No study has ever measured the cytokine→fatigue temporal relationship in any human inflammatory disease with this level of temporal resolution and naturalistic triggering. Design: n=20 HS patients with ≥1 predictable flare/month, daily electronic symptom diary (FSS, VAS-fatigue, VAS-pain, VAS-sleep) + serial serum (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, IL-17A, CRP, S100A8/A9) collected Q12h during flares and weekly during inter-flare periods, for 8 weeks. Primary analysis: cross-correlation function (CCF) and Granger causality testing the hypothesis that cytokine peaks precede fatigue peaks by 4–12h. Secondary: compare cytokine profile during HS flares vs post-exercise in ME/CFS (existing datasets or concurrent arm). This would establish — for the first time in any human disease — the causal direction and latency of the cytokine→fatigue relationship without experimental cytokine administration.

**Evidence link.** Ring 2017 (prodromal fatigue in 32% of HS flares, 12–24+h latency); HS flare clinical observability; existing ME/CFS post-exercise cytokine datasets. No serial Q12h cytokine sampling through any spontaneous inflammatory flare has been published in any disease.

**Falsifiable prediction.** Serum IL-6 and TNF-α will peak 4–8h before the peak of patient-reported fatigue (CCF r>0.5 at lag 1–2 timepoints). IL-1β may peak earlier (2–4h) and with a smaller amplitude (shorter half-life, harder to capture). IL-17A may peak later (12–24h, consistent with Th17 transcriptional kinetics). Pain and fatigue peaks will be temporally dissociable (pain 2–4h before fatigue). Falsified if cytokine and fatigue peaks are temporally coincident (within the Q12h sampling resolution), or if cytokines do not rise during flares.

**Gap.** Q12h serum sampling is burdensome (fingerstick DBS could reduce burden). HS flares are unpredictable in timing — 8 weeks may capture too few flares. Requires HS patients with frequent, predictable flares — a selected subgroup. Cytokine half-lives (minutes for TNF-α, hours for IL-6/CRP) mean Q12h sampling may miss the true peak. Cost: ~$50,000 for 20 patients × 8 weeks at Q12h sampling during flares.

### 2.2 — Two-Day CPET in HS Patients: Does Any HS Subgroup Show Objective PEM? (Cert 0.45) [proposal] [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** The definitive experiment to determine whether HS fatigue shares PEM pathophysiology with ME/CFS. PEM has never been objectively measured in HS. If HS patients show 2-day CPET deterioration (reduced peak VO₂ on Day 2, increased VE/VCO₂, higher RPE at matched workloads), this would be the first demonstration that PEM exists outside ME/CFS — a fundamental challenge to the assumption that PEM is ME/CFS-specific. If they show NO deterioration (as Long COVID patients with self-reported PEM did), then HS fatigue is mechanistically distinct (Type 2, inflammatory-cytokine-driven) from ME/CFS fatigue (Type 1, post-exertional-metabolic), and the epidemiological OR 1.72 reflects shared confounds or a distinct fatigue mechanism. Design: n=30 HS with high fatigue (FSS≥4), n=30 HS without significant fatigue (FSS<3), n=30 ME/CFS (Canadian criteria, positive control), n=30 healthy controls, all undergoing standardized 2-day CPET. Primary outcome: delta peak VO₂ (Day 2 − Day 1). Secondary: PEM symptom exacerbation (DSQ-PEM, daily for 7 days post-CPET).

**Evidence link.** 2-day CPET paradigm validated in ME/CFS (van Campen 2020, Lim 2020 meta-analysis); Long COVID CPET-2 null result (referenced in ch14d); 0 HS CPET studies exist. Arcani 2023 (Type 1 vs Type 2 fatigue dissociation in SLE — the same dissociation may apply to HS).

**Falsifiable prediction.** Under the "HS fatigue = Type 2 inflammatory fatigue" model: HS patients (both high- and low-fatigue) will show normal CPET-2 performance (delta peak VO₂ <3%), indistinguishable from healthy controls, while ME/CFS patients show significant deterioration (delta peak VO₂ >8%). Under the "HS fatigue = Type 1 PEM in a subset" model: a subset of HS patients (20–30%, those with the highest fatigue and prodromal symptoms) will show CPET-2 deterioration comparable to mild ME/CFS. Falsified between-group differentiation: if even high-fatigue HS patients show no CPET-2 impairment, the shared PEM mechanism hypothesis is rejected.

**Gap.** Expensive (~$120,000). HS patients may not tolerate CPET (skin pain from friction/sweating during exercise). HS patients who can complete CPET may represent a milder subset — selection bias toward the null. Requires dermatology + exercise physiology collaboration.

### 2.3 — Metformin as an NLRP3 Activity Probe in ME/CFS: Proof-of-Concept Crossover Trial (Cert 0.35) [proposal] [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** Petrasca 2023 demonstrated metformin (AMPK activator → mTOR inhibition → NLRP3 suppression) reduces inflammatory markers in HS PBMCs. Metformin is generic, cheap, oral, well-tolerated, and has established safety in non-diabetic populations (PCOS, prediabetes). A pilot randomized crossover trial directly tests whether NLRP3 pharmacologic suppression reduces fatigue in ME/CFS — a causal test of the NLRP3→fatigue hypothesis that requires no new drug development. Design: n=30 ME/CFS, double-blind crossover: metformin 1500mg/day × 8 weeks vs placebo, 4-week washout. Primary endpoint: FACIT-F. Mechanistic endpoints: monocyte NLRP3 and IL1B mRNA (RT-qPCR), serum IL-1β and IL-18 (ELISA), and monocyte pSTAT1 (phospho-flow, linking to the shared monocyte pathway). If metformin reduces fatigue AND NLRP3/IL-1β, this is the first human evidence for a pharmacologically accessible NLRP3→fatigue pathway in ME/CFS. If metformin does NOT reduce fatigue despite reducing NLRP3/IL-1β, then NLRP3 is not a causal fatigue mediator in ME/CFS. If metformin does not reduce NLRP3/IL-1β (AMPK→mTOR→NLRP3 suppression may be tissue-dependent and ineffective in circulating monocytes), then a different NLRP3 inhibitor (MCC950 or oral NLRP3 inhibitors in development) must be tested.

**Evidence link.** Petrasca 2023 (metformin→AMPK→NLRP3 in HS); Kilgour 2026 (P2X7 blockade insufficient in HS, but P2X7 is upstream of NLRP3 and may have different pharmacology); existing paper has metformin mentioned in passing only. No metformin trial in ME/CFS exists.

**Falsifiable prediction.** Metformin will reduce monocyte NLRP3 mRNA (≥1.5-fold) and serum IL-1β (≥20%) vs placebo. If these biomarker changes occur without FACIT-F improvement (≥4-point delta), NLRP3 suppression is insufficient for fatigue reduction — constraining the NLRP3→fatigue pathway to a necessary-but-not-sufficient role or a non-causal association. If metformin reduces FACIT-F by ≥4 points in ≥30% of patients, the effect size supports a phase 2b trial. Falsified if metformin does not reduce NLRP3 biomarkers in ME/CFS monocytes.

**Gap.** GI side effects (nausea, diarrhea) are common with metformin initiation and may confound fatigue measurement in the first 2 weeks. Metformin reduces vitamin B12 — relevant to ME/CFS where B12 status is sometimes borderline. 8 weeks may be insufficient for maximal NLRP3 suppression (SLE/HS metformin benefits accrue over 3–6 months). Crossover design requires adequate washout — metformin's half-life is short but the downstream effects on NLRP3 expression may persist.

## 10. Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS

### 10.1 — HS Fatigue Is Likely Type 2 Inflammatory Fatigue, Not Type 1 PEM (Cert 0.55) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** The most parsimonious and evidence-consistent model: HS fatigue is IL-1β/IL-6/TNF-α-driven sickness behavior — identical in mechanism to the fatigue of active rheumatoid arthritis, SLE flares, and systemic infections. This is Type 2 fatigue (Mertz 2020 classification): constant, related to systemic inflammatory burden, responsive to anti-cytokine therapy, and lacking post-exertional exacerbation. By contrast, ME/CFS fatigue/PEM occurs in the absence of elevated systemic inflammatory markers (CRP/ESR typically normal) and after exertion — a Type 1 pattern. Two conditions can produce the same subjective fatigue report (FSS=5 in HS equals FSS=5 in ME/CFS) via different biological mechanisms. The epidemiological OR 1.72 simply reflects that a subset of HS patients with persistent systemic inflammation develop a chronic sickness behavior syndrome that is phenotypically similar to ME/CFS fatigue — no shared pathophysiology required.

**Evidence supporting this model over the shared-mechanism model:**
- HS fatigue prevalence (30–61%) is comparable to that in active RA and SLE — diseases where fatigue is acknowledged to be cytokine-driven Type 2
- HS has objective systemic inflammatory markers (elevated CRP, ESR, SAA) — ME/CFS typically does not
- HS fatigue correlates with pain, depression, and disease severity (Meral Ketenci 2026) — exactly the pattern seen in other inflammatory diseases, not the ME/CFS pattern
- The Long COVID 2-day CPET null result (no CPET-2 deterioration despite self-reported PEM) establishes a precedent: self-reported post-exertional worsening ≠ objective PEM, and HS patients have never been assessed for objective PEM
- Anemia of chronic disease (Deckers 2016) independently causes fatigue in HS via reduced oxygen-carrying capacity — a mechanism absent in ME/CFS

**Falsifiable prediction (actually testing this model).** HS patients with FSS≥4 will show: (a) no CPET-2 deterioration (delta VO₂<3%), distinguishing them from ME/CFS; (b) correlation between fatigue and CRP (r≥0.4), absent in ME/CFS; (c) fatigue that improves in parallel with CRP reduction on anti-cytokine therapy. This would confirm Type 2 fatigue. Falsified if HS patients show PEM on objective testing OR if HS fatigue does not correlate with systemic inflammatory markers.

### 10.2 — NLRP3 Is Too Ubiquitous to Be a Specific Bridge Between These Two Diseases (Cert 0.60) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** The NLRP3 → IL-1β → fatigue pathway is involved in virtually every chronic inflammatory disease:
- Crystal arthropathies (gout, pseudogout): monosodium urate/CPPD → NLRP3
- Atherosclerosis: cholesterol crystals → NLRP3
- Alzheimer's disease: amyloid-β → NLRP3
- Type 2 diabetes: hyperglycemia/islet amyloid → NLRP3
- Inflammatory bowel disease: commensal bacteria → NLRP3
- SLE: immune complexes → NLRP3
- RA: unknown ligand → NLRP3

If NLRP3 activation were sufficient for ME/CFS, every patient with any of these diseases would have ME/CFS. But they don't: the prevalence of ME/CFS in the general population is 0.2–0.4%. Even in diseases with the highest NLRP3 activation (gout, CAPS), ME/CFS is not a recognized comorbidity. The OR 1.72 in HS means 93% of HS patients do NOT report ME/CFS. NLRP3 activation is therefore necessary but nowhere near sufficient. The missing variable — a second hit, a genetic susceptibility, a prior viral infection, a particular microbiome composition — is the actual determinant of who transitions from NLRP3 activation to ME/CFS. HS cannot illuminate that variable because HS patients haven't been stratified by it. Focusing on NLRP3 as "the bridge" distracts from identifying what converts NLRP3 activation into ME/CFS pathophysiology.

**Evidence.** NLRP3 literature across all major inflammatory diseases; Prens 2022 (93% of HS patients without ME/CFS); CAPS (continuous NLRP3 activation, no recognized ME/CFS comorbidity).

**Falsifiable prediction.** If NLRP3 activation is the rate-limiting step, then disease severity in HS (Hurley III > II > I) should predict ME/CFS prevalence in a dose-response relationship — the more NLRP3 activation, the more ME/CFS. Under the "NLRP3 is not the rate-limiting step" model, ME/CFS prevalence in HS will be flat across Hurley stages (driven by the second hit, not by NLRP3 activation intensity). Falsified if ME/CFS prevalence increases monotonically with Hurley stage.

### 10.3 — Kilgour 2026 Already Partially Falsified the Single-Cytokine NLRP3→Fatigue Model (Cert 0.45) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** The AZD9056 Phase 2 trial (Kilgour 2026) demonstrated that direct NLRP3 pathway inhibition (oral P2X7 receptor antagonist → blocks ATP-mediated NLRP3 activation) fails to produce clinical HS improvement despite restoring PBMC cytokine production ex vivo. If selective NLRP3 pathway inhibition is insufficient even for the most proximal outcome — HS skin lesions — it is extremely unlikely to improve the more distal outcome of fatigue. Two mechanistic interpretations:

1. **Redundancy:** HS inflammation is driven by multiple inflammasomes (NLRP1, AIM2, NLRC4) and TLR pathways (TLR2→NF-κB) in parallel, so blocking NLRP3 alone leaves the inflammatory network largely intact. By extension, even if NLRP3 contributes to fatigue in ME/CFS, a single-cytokine or single-inflammasome approach is unlikely to be therapeutic.
2. **Wrong node:** The P2X7 receptor may be the wrong target even within the NLRP3 pathway — NLRP3 can be activated via other signals (K+ efflux via other channels, lysosomal rupture, mitochondrial ROS) that bypass P2X7.

The fatigue data from this trial has not been published. If it is published and shows no fatigue improvement (the most likely outcome given the null HiSCR result), this is a direct falsification of the P2X7→NLRP3→fatigue hypothesis in a human autoinflammatory disease. Even if fatigue data is never published, the null result on the primary endpoint constrains expectation: the probability that NLRP3 inhibition improves HS fatigue is near zero when it doesn't improve HS skin.

**Evidence.** Kilgour 2026 (JAAD, Phase 2, randomized, negative on HiSCR); existing paper ch14d mentions Kilgour 2026 in the limitation section of spec:hs-nlrp3-mechanistic-bridge but only as a caveat — this point argues it may be more than a caveat: it may be a partial falsification.

**Falsifiable prediction.** If the AZD9056 trial fatigue data is ever published, the active-treatment arm will show no significant fatigue improvement vs placebo. Falsified if AZD9056 improved fatigue despite not improving HiSCR — which would rescue the NLRP3→fatigue hypothesis by dissociating skin and fatigue outcomes.

## 11. Null Hypothesis Assessment

### 11.1 — HS Has No Role in ME/CFS Beyond Shared Confounds (BMI, Smoking, Socioeconomic Status) (Cert 0.45) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** HS and ME/CFS share risk factors: higher BMI (obesity is the strongest HS risk factor), smoking (strongly associated with HS), and lower socioeconomic status (associated with both). Prens 2022 adjusted for age, sex, BMI, smoking, and socioeconomic status — but residual confounding from measurement error (self-reported BMI and smoking) could inflate the adjusted OR. Additionally, unmeasured confounds were not adjusted for:
- **Healthcare access:** HS patients see dermatologists more frequently → higher chance of receiving any diagnosis, including ME/CFS (detection bias)
- **Depression:** strongly associated with both HS (30–36%, Almahdi 2026) and ME/CFS; not adjusted for in Prens 2022
- **Sleep disturbance:** HS patients have impaired sleep from pain/pruritus/drainage (McGrath 2026); sleep disturbance independently causes fatigue and is not adjusted for
- **Physical inactivity:** kinesiophobia and pain reduce physical activity in HS (Meral Ketenci 2026); deconditioning independently causes fatigue

Under the null, the true adjusted OR is ~1.0 after accounting for all shared confounds. The Prens 2022 adjusted OR of 1.72 (95% CI 1.06–2.78) is compatible with a true OR as low as 1.06 — barely above the null, even without accounting for unmeasured confounds. A single study with a lower CI grazing 1.0 does not establish comorbidity.

**Implications if the null is correct:**
- The NLRP3 bridge is irrelevant — there is no true HS-ME/CFS convergence to explain
- The autoinflammatory disease × ME/CFS research gap (lim:autoinflammatory-category-absent) is a genuine null: autoinflammatory diseases do NOT predispose to ME/CFS, and studying them will not illuminate ME/CFS pathophysiology
- ME/CFS screening in HS cohorts (oq:hs-undiagnosed-mecfs-screening) would yield near-zero cases, confirming the null
- HS biologics' effect on fatigue has no relevance to ME/CFS
- HS prodrome is not a model for PEM — the 12–24h prodromal fatigue is non-specific sickness behavior

**Evidence needed to reject the null.** An independent cohort study (UK Biobank, All of Us, Danish National Patient Registry) using clinical HS diagnosis codes + validated ME/CFS diagnosis (not self-report) must replicate OR>1.5 after comprehensive covariate adjustment including depression, sleep quality, physical activity, and healthcare utilization.

### 11.2 — Conflating HS Fatigue with ME/CFS Harms Both Patient Populations (Cert 0.55) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Rationale.** Under the null hypothesis that HS fatigue and ME/CFS are distinct clinical entities with overlapping symptoms but different pathophysiology and treatment requirements, conflating them produces three types of harm:

1. **Harm to HS patients.** Attributing HS fatigue to "undiagnosed ME/CFS" medicalizes a symptom that may be fully explained by modifiable factors — chronic pain, sleep deprivation, anemia of chronic disease, and the psychological burden of a disfiguring skin condition (all documented in the HS literature). Treating HS fatigue as ME/CFS would:
   - Divert attention from treating the drivers (pain, sleep, anemia) that are within dermatology's scope
   - Subject HS patients to pacing recommendations when their fatigue may improve with appropriately graded physical activity (as inflammation is controlled)
   - Stigmatize HS fatigue as "medically unexplained" when it is well-explained by known HS pathophysiology

2. **Harm to ME/CFS patients.** Diluting ME/CFS diagnostic criteria by pathologizing fatigue that (a) is proportional to objective inflammatory burden, (b) correlates with CRP, and (c) resolves with anti-cytokine therapy as "ME/CFS" weakens the specificity of ME/CFS as a diagnosis. ME/CFS entered the literature in part to distinguish post-exertional, non-inflammatory fatigue from the fatigue of active inflammatory disease. Collapsing this distinction reverses decades of nosological progress.

3. **Harm to research.** Pursuing the HS-ME/CFS connection consumes resources (DSQ-PEM screening, CPET, cytokine studies in HS) that could be spent on higher-yield ME/CFS research directions — including the SLE-ME/CFS connection (where anti-NR2 antibodies and shared monocyte pathways are already partially validated) and the post-infectious disease spectrum (where Long COVID has provided decisive counterfactual evidence).

**This is not an argument against studying HS fatigue** — HS fatigue is a major, underrecognized symptom deserving of its own research program. It is an argument against using ME/CFS as the interpretive framework for HS fatigue when the simpler explanation (Type 2 inflammatory fatigue) fits all available evidence without requiring new mechanisms.

**Falsifiable prediction (which would reject this null hypothesis).** Simultaneously demonstrating that: (a) HS patients meeting ME/CFS criteria show objective PEM on 2-day CPET (not just self-reported post-exertional worsening); (b) HS fatigue does not resolve with anti-cytokine therapy despite achieving HiSCR ≥50; (c) HS patients meeting ME/CFS criteria have normal CRP/ESR (i.e., their fatigue is dissociated from their inflammatory burden). Any one of these findings would force reconsideration of the null. All three occurring together would be strong evidence for shared pathophysiology.

## 12. Evidence Quality Concerns

### 12.1 — Prens 2022: Single Study, Self-Reported, Cross-Sectional, Lower CI Near 1.0 (Cert 0.70) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Concern.** The entire epidemiological case rests on one study — Prens 2022 (Lifelines Cohort, n=56,084). Critical limitations:

- **Self-reported HS:** HS diagnosis was based on patient questionnaire, not clinical dermatologist examination. The survey asked about "recurrent boils in the groin/axillae" — which overlaps with folliculitis, acne conglobata, and simple furunculosis. Positive predictive value of self-reported HS is ~70–80% in validation studies, meaning 20–30% of "HS cases" in Prens 2022 may not have HS. Differential misclassification (HS patients who also report fatigue may be more likely to endorse the "boils" question) could inflate the OR.

- **Self-reported ME/CFS:** ME/CFS status was based on a single self-report item, not a validated diagnostic instrument (DSQ-PEM, Fukuda, CCC). Self-reported "chronic fatigue syndrome" has poor concordance with research-criteria-confirmed ME/CFS — many people who report having CFS do not meet criteria, and many who meet criteria have never been diagnosed. The 1.72 OR could reflect either true ME/CFS or a general tendency to report multiple chronic conditions.

- **Cross-sectional:** Cannot establish that HS precedes ME/CFS or vice versa. If ME/CFS → reduced physical activity → weight gain → increased intertriginous friction → unmasking of subclinical HS, the causal direction is reversed and HS is a consequence, not a cause. Longitudinal data would be needed to establish temporality.

- **Lower confidence interval 1.06:** The 95% CI (1.06–2.78) is compatible with a true OR barely above 1.0. With unmeasured confounds (depression, sleep, activity, healthcare utilization), the true adjusted OR could be ≤1.0.

- **No replication:** 4 years since publication, no independent cohort has replicated the HS-ME/CFS association.

**Rating.** The Prens 2022 finding is "suggestive" not "established." Until an independent cohort with clinical HS diagnosis codes and validated ME/CFS diagnostic instruments replicates OR > 1.5, the epidemiological association should be treated as a provisional signal, not a confirmed comorbidity.

### 12.2 — Selection Bias: All HS Fatigue Data Are From Dermatology Clinic Populations (Cert 0.60) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Concern.** McGrath 2026, Almahdi 2026 (systematic review pooling clinic-based studies), Romanelli 2025, Meral Ketenci 2026 — all HS fatigue prevalence data come from dermatology clinic populations. These patients:
- Have HS severe enough to seek specialist care (Hurley II–III disproportionately represented)
- Are receiving or have failed treatment (treatment-refractory fatigue may be overrepresented)
- Have higher pain, higher depression, and worse QoL than community HS patients
- Are predominantly from academic medical centers in Europe/North America (tertiary referral bias)

The 30–61% fatigue prevalence range is almost certainly an overestimate of fatigue in the community HS population. Per Prens 2022, the majority of HS patients in the community are undiagnosed — they have milder disease (Hurley I) and may have lower fatigue rates. If community HS fatigue prevalence were, say, 15–20% rather than 30–61%, the case for a shared HS-ME/CFS mechanism weakens substantially — the overlap is driven by severe disease (where Type 2 inflammatory fatigue is expected) rather than by a disease-intrinsic property of HS.

The direction of bias is not certain — community HS patients may have untreated inflammation and worse fatigue — but the absence of community-based HS fatigue data means all prevalence estimates are of unknown generalizability.

**Mitigation.** A community-based HS cohort (using the Lifelines or UK Biobank HS-identified subset) with validated fatigue measurement (FSS, PROMIS-Fatigue) + ME/CFS screening (DSQ-PEM) would provide unbiased prevalence estimates. Until then, cite the clinic-based 30–61% range with a caveat about selection bias.

### 12.3 — No Validated Fatigue Instrument Has Been Used as an Endpoint in Any HS Biologic Trial (Cert 0.75) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Concern.** The Phase 3 open-question (oq:hs-biologics-fatigue — "does anti-cytokine therapy reduce HS fatigue?") cannot be answered with published data. The reason is not that the answer is negative — it's that the data was never collected:

- **Adalimumab (PIONEER I/II):** HS Clinical Response (HiSCR), pain VAS, DLQI — no FSS, PROMIS-Fatigue, or FACIT-F
- **Secukinumab (SUNSHINE/SUNRISE):** HiSCR, pain VAS, DLQI — no validated fatigue instrument
- **Bimekizumab (BE HEARD I/II):** HiSCR, pain VAS, DLQI — no validated fatigue instrument
- **Anakinra (off-label, small series):** no systematic fatigue measurement
- **AZD9056 (Kilgour 2026):** HiSCR primary — fatigue data may have been collected but is unpublished

The DLQI includes one item ("Over the last week, how much has your skin problem affected your social or leisure activities?") that is sometimes used as a fatigue proxy, but this is inadequate — it conflates fatigue with social avoidance, skin-specific embarrassment, and functional limitation from pain. Any claim that "biologics improve (or do not improve) HS fatigue" is speculation without adequate measurement.

**This is not a negative finding — it is a missing data problem.** The single most actionable recommendation from both the epidemiological association and the NLRP3 bridge is: **include a validated fatigue instrument (FSS or PROMIS-Fatigue Short Form) as a secondary endpoint in the next HS phase 3 trial.** This costs ~$5–10 per patient (5 minutes of questionnaire administration at 4–6 timepoints across a 52-week trial) and would answer oq:hs-biologics-fatigue within the existing trial infrastructure.

**Until such data exist**, the natural-experiment logic (HS biologics test the cytokine→fatigue hypothesis) is logically correct but empirically empty — an untestable framework, not a tested one.

### 12.4 — The NLRP3→Fatigue Mouse Evidence Does Not Model Chronic PEM (Cert 0.55) [Origin: brainstorm]

**Concern.** The preclinical evidence for NLRP3→fatigue comes from acute challenge models:
- Zhang 2016: LPS (acute, single injection) + forced swim → NLRP3 KO mice show less acute sickness behavior
- Zhang 2017: repeated forced swim (acute exercise stress, not chronic) → NLRP3 KO mice show less fatigue-like behavior
- Wang 2025: TCM Jingfang + forced swim

None of these models recapitulate the defining feature of ME/CFS: **chronic, relapsing-remitting post-exertional malaise persisting for months to years after a single (or repeated) trigger.** Acute LPS-induced sickness behavior (anorexia, reduced locomotion, social withdrawal, fever) is an evolutionarily conserved, self-limiting response to infection that resolves within 24–72 hours. It is mediated by IL-1β acting on the hypothalamus and brainstem, and NLRP3 KO abolishes it — as expected.

But PEM in ME/CFS is different:
- It occurs without fever or acute-phase response
- It has a characteristic delayed onset (12–48h post-exertion) and prolonged duration (days to weeks)
- It worsens over time (not a stable deficit) — a "sensitization" or "kindling" phenomenon
- It is associated with metabolic (not just behavioral) deficits: reduced peak VO₂, impaired oxygen extraction, elevated lactate

The 72-hour LPS/swim model captures the acute IL-1β→sickness behavior axis, not the chronic sensitized post-exertional axis. Extrapolating from an acute model to a chronic disease without a chronic NLRP3 activation model that includes PEM features is a category error. A chronic NLRP3 model — e.g., NLRP3 gain-of-function mice followed for months with serial exercise challenges and metabolic phenotyping — does not exist and would be needed to determine whether sustained NLRP3 activation can produce a PEM-like state.

**Mitigation.** The mouse data is supporting, not primary, evidence for the NLRP3 bridge. The bridge should be presented as inferential with the acute→chronic extrapolation explicitly flagged as an untested assumption. The Jin 2026 review provides the theoretical framework for chronic NLRP3 involvement in PEM but is itself a synthesis of acute models and human biomarker data — not a direct demonstration.
