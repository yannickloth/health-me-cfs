# Creative Brainstorm: Severe ME/CFS Care Guide Extensions

**Origin:** Hermisson et al. (2026) transdisciplinary nursing care guide for severe/very-severe ME/CFS
**Date:** 2026-06-06
**Status:** Phase 3 output

---

## Category 1: Novel Care Protocol Hypotheses

### Idea 1.1: Basal Energy Expenditure Protection Protocol (BEPP)

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Rolling PEM implies that even resting metabolic demand may exceed cellular ATP production capacity in severe patients. A protocol that first identifies the patient's minimum sustainable energy state (via symptom tracking during graded rest reduction/expansion) and then prescribes a fixed daily energy budget below that threshold could prevent the ratcheting-down effect by ensuring net positive energy balance each day.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson rolling PEM concept shows overlapping crashes without stabilization. Indirect support from extreme energy conservation protocols in anorexia nervosa refeeding (where metabolic demand must be carefully managed).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients on BEPP (fixed energy budget at 80% of estimated basal tolerance for 14 days) will show measurable improvement in baseline symptoms compared to usual care, as measured by daily symptom scores and reduced crash frequency.

### Idea 1.2: Sensory Fasting Protocol

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Sensory input (light, sound, touch, smell) is a direct metabolic cost mediated by thalamic gating and cortical processing. In severe ME/CFS, the sensory threshold is pathologically low — inputs that are neutral for healthy people trigger nociceptive and metabolic responses. A scheduled "sensory fast" (complete sensory deprivation in darkness, silence, no touch, minimal olfactory stimulation for 2-4 hours daily) could reduce cumulative sensory load below crash threshold.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson care guide documents sensory intolerance as core feature. Supported by MCAS literature linking sensory stimuli to mast cell degranulation (Theoharides 2015) and by fMRI studies showing altered thalamic sensory gating in ME/CFS (Barnden 2019).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Adding a daily 2-hour sensory fast (dark room, noise-cancelling ear protection, no tactile interaction) to standard care reduces PEM episode frequency by ≥30% over 4 weeks compared to standard care alone in very-severe ME/CFS.

### Idea 1.3: Reverse Titration Protocol for Care Interventions

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Standard care protocols start with standard interventions and titrate down if side effects occur. For severe ME/CFS, the correct starting point is zero intervention; individual care elements should be added one at a time with a mandatory observation period (48-72h PEM window) between additions. This identifies which specific care activities trigger PEM versus which are tolerated, allowing a personalized "positive care formulary" and "negative care list."
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson's "all care is a cost" principle. Analogous to drug desensitization protocols and to the graded reintroduction used in refeeding syndrome prevention.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Patients managed with reverse titration (starting from absolute minimum care, adding elements with 72h washout) will have fewer PEM episodes per month than patients receiving standard bundled care, despite receiving fewer total care interventions.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### Idea 2.1: Caregiver Biofeedback PEM Prediction Trial

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Caregivers already monitor proxy signals (facial tension, breathing changes). Wearable biosensors (heart rate variability, electrodermal activity, accelerometry) could provide quantitative correlates of pre-PEM state. A study comparing caregiver judgment alone vs. caregiver + biofeedback for detecting pre-PEM states could establish whether objective physiological monitoring adds predictive value beyond experienced caregiver observation.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson stop signal protocol and dual pacing responsibility both rely on caregiver detection of pre-PEM signs. HRV changes precede PEM in ME/CFS (Nelson 2022, Escorihuela 2020). EDA correlates with sympathetic activation in bedbound patients during minimal exertion.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a crossover trial, caregiver + biofeedback (HRV + EDA) will detect ≥30% more pre-PEM events than caregiver observation alone, with a lead time of ≥5 minutes before crash onset, in severe/very-severe ME/CFS.

### Idea 2.2: Minimal Care Dose-Response Study

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The relationship between total daily "care dose" (sum of all interventions weighted by intensity and duration) and PEM probability is unknown but clinically critical. A dose-response study that systematically varies care intensity (total minutes of care per day, number of care contacts, duration of each contact) while holding content constant could establish the care-PEM dose-response curve.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson principle that all care is an energy cost. No empirical data exists on the dose-response relationship between care intensity and PEM probability.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A U-shaped relationship will be observed: too little care (→ medical complications, dehydration, pressure injuries) and too much care both increase PEM probability, with an optimal care dose of approximately 60-90 minutes total per day for very-severe patients.

### Idea 2.3: Stabilization Debt Tracking Study

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Rolling PEM suggests patients accumulate a "stabilization debt" — the duration of continuous crash-free time required to start recovering baseline function. A longitudinal study tracking daily PEM status, recovery time between crashes, and functional baseline could quantify the relationship between crash-free intervals and recovery trajectory (e.g., is there a minimum continuous crash-free window — 48h? 72h? — required for any functional recovery to begin?).
- **Evidence link:** Directly extends Hermisson rolling PEM concept. Supported by pacing literature showing that crash-free intervals are the only periods during which recovery occurs (Van Ness 2003).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Using daily PEM diaries, a minimum crash-free interval of ≥72 consecutive hours will be required for any measurable improvement in baseline function (measured by FUNCAP-27 severe domain). Patients who never achieve 72h continuous crash-free periods will show progressive functional decline regardless of other interventions.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### Idea 3.1: Pre-Care Procedural Analgesia + Sedation Protocol

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Care procedures (bathing, repositioning, feeding) trigger PEM not only through metabolic cost but also through sensory-pain-mast cell amplification loops. Pre-procedural administration of a low-dose sedative (sublingual lorazepam 0.25-0.5mg) combined with a mast cell stabilizer (ketotifen 1mg) 30 minutes before care could dampen sensory amplification and reduce the PEM-triggering potential of care itself.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson notes that passive care activities trigger PEM. Anxiety-sensitization loop is documented in severe ME/CFS. Ketotifen reduces MCAS-mediated sensory amplification (Theoharides 2015). Lorazepam reduces thalamic sensory gating dysfunction at low doses.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a randomized crossover trial, pre-care ketotifen + low-dose lorazepam reduces the proportion of care sessions that trigger post-care PEM by ≥40% compared to placebo pre-medication, in very-severe ME/CFS patients.

### Idea 3.2: Scheduled Sublingual Ondansetron for Nausea-Associated PEM Trigger

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Nausea and GI distress are common in severe ME/CFS and are themselves energy-costly (vomiting, retching, or even persistent nausea activates the autonomic nervous system, increases metabolic rate, and may trigger PEM via vagal afferent signaling). Prophylactic ondansetron (sublingual, 4-8mg) before care activities that commonly trigger nausea (feeding, oral care, positional changes) could prevent nausea-induced PEM.
- **Evidence link:** GI symptoms are nearly universal in ME/CFS (Maes 2009). Hermisson does not specifically address nausea. Ondansetron is a 5-HT3 antagonist used in chemotherapy-induced nausea; sublingual formulation is feasible for patients who cannot swallow. Vagus nerve activation links nausea to systemic metabolic stress responses.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Prophylactic ondansetron before care procedures reduces both nausea scores and post-procedural PEM occurrence by ≥50% compared to as-needed ondansetron in severe ME/CFS with baseline GI symptoms.

### Idea 3.3: Low-Dose Mirtazapine for Bedbound Patients

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Mirtazapine at low doses (7.5-15mg) has sedative, appetite-stimulating, and antiemetic properties through H1 and 5-HT2A antagonism. For bedbound patients who cannot sleep, have lost appetite, and experience nausea, this single agent could address multiple care barriers simultaneously. Improved sleep and nutrition could increase energy envelope without requiring increased care effort.
- **Evidence link:** Mirtazapine is standard in palliative care for the triad of insomnia, anorexia, and nausea. Sleep disruption is well-documented in severe ME/CFS (Jackson 2016). Hermisson care guide does not specifically address pharmacologic sleep support for bedbound patients.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Bedbound ME/CFS patients receiving low-dose mirtazapine (7.5mg qHS) will show improved sleep efficiency (≥15% by actigraphy), increased oral intake (≥20% by food diary), and reduced nausea scores (≥30% by visual analog scale) over 4 weeks compared to placebo, with no increase in PEM frequency.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Idea 4.1: Elemental Diet Protocol for Minimal-Care Feeding

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Severe ME/CFS patients with eating intolerance face high energy cost from the act of eating (chewing, swallowing, digestion, and the sensory experience of food taste/smell). An elemental diet — pre-digested liquid formula requiring minimal digestive effort — could provide complete nutrition with ≤10 minutes of caregiver feeding time per serving, compared to 30-60 minutes for solid food. The reduced feeding time directly reduces care-related energy expenditure.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson care guide notes that feeding is a high-cost care intervention. Elemental diets are used in Crohn's disease to reduce digestive metabolic load. No data exists in ME/CFS. Mechanistic rationale: reduced splanchnic blood flow competition with skeletal muscle (need more ATP for digestion = less for the rest of the body).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients switched from solid food to an elemental diet (isocaloric, isonitrogenous) for 14 days will show reduced PEM episodes and improved baseline symptom scores due to reduced digestive energy expenditure compared to solid food controls, with equal caloric intake.

### Idea 4.2: Bedbound-Compatible Sodium Bicarbonate for Lactate Buffer

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Severe ME/CFS patients show impaired lactate clearance and metabolic acidosis during minimal exertion (even conversation, eating). Oral sodium bicarbonate (500mg-1g in water, sipped slowly) could buffer the metabolic acidosis of daily activity, reducing the metabolic stress signal that triggers PEM. This is distinct from standard electrolyte management for orthostatic intolerance.
- **Evidence link:** Lactate elevation in ME/CFS even at low exercise intensities (Nijs 2020). Sodium bicarbonate is used in sports medicine for lactate buffering. Hermisson addresses electrolyte management but not metabolic buffering per se.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Oral sodium bicarbonate (1g BID) taken 30 minutes before planned activity will reduce perceived exertion during that activity (Borg 0-10) by ≥1.5 points and reduce post-activity PEM likelihood by ≥25% compared to placebo in severe ME/CFS, in a randomized crossover trial.

### Idea 4.3: Passive Transdermal Micronutrient Delivery

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bedbound patients frequently cannot swallow pills, absorb nutrients enterally (due to GI dysmotility, MCAS, or reduced splanchnic perfusion), or tolerate the metabolic load of oral supplementation. Transdermal delivery of key micronutrients (magnesium as oil, vitamin D in absorption base, B-complex via liposomal cream) bypasses enteral absorption barriers and eliminates the swallowing/sensory burden of oral supplements.
- **Evidence link:** ME/CFS populations show high rates of magnesium (Cox 1991), B12 (Regland 1997), and vitamin D (Ashrafi 2020) deficiencies. Hermisson does not address supplementation routes. Transdermal magnesium is established for muscle cramps in palliative care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Transdermal magnesium oil (200mg elemental Mg/day, applied to forearms) in bedbound ME/CFS patients will raise serum Mg levels comparably to oral magnesium glycinate (200mg/day) over 4 weeks, with higher adherence and fewer GI side effects.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### Idea 5.1: Micro-Pacing with Sub-Activity Threshold Titration

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Current pacing advice stops activities when PEM threshold is reached. Micro-pacing identifies the sub-threshold activity duration for each care task (e.g., 4 minutes of assisted feeding, 3 minutes of bed bath) and builds in mandatory rest before reaching that limit. The key innovation: activities are terminated by protocol, not by symptom. The caregiver counts duration, not the patient's symptoms (which are delayed).
- **Evidence link:** Extends Hermisson's activity spoiler protocol and prophylactic rest. Analogous to the "automatic stop" approach in graded exercise therapy reversal (where fixed duration replaced symptom-titrated exercise).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients receiving micro-paced care (activities terminated after fixed sub-threshold durations determined by 3-day baseline tolerance testing) will have ≥50% fewer PEM episodes per week than patients receiving usual care (activity terminated by patient-reported symptoms), with equivalent total care delivery.

### Idea 5.2: Audio-Controlled Dark Room Protocol

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bedbound patients often cannot be in the dark AND monitor their own needs at the same time. An audio-controlled system (voice commands, simple binary signals like a bell or single-word requests) allows the patient to request care without visual or tactile input. The caregiver responds to audio cues only — no entering the room, no turning on lights, no visual inspection. This reduces the sensory stimulation that comes with each care contact.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson stop signal protocol is inherently audio/visual. Sensory intolerance is documented as a core severe ME/CFS feature. Similar protocols exist in migraine management (dark room, no sensory input).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Patients using an audio-controlled dark room protocol (caregiver responds to voice commands only, no visual entry to room) will show lower daily symptom severity scores (≥20% reduction on sensory intolerance domain) and longer crash-free intervals compared to standard visual care contact schedule.

### Idea 5.3: Thermal Gradient Care Pacing

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Temperature changes (being uncovered for a bath, cold washcloth, warm water) impose a thermoregulatory energy cost that compounds the metabolic cost of care. A protocol that pre-warms the environment and all care materials (water, towels, clothing) to the patient's thermoneutral zone (typically 30-32°C for bedbound patients with low metabolic rates) and minimizes the rate of temperature change during care reduces the homeostatic disruption energy cost.
- **Evidence link:** Thermoregulatory dysfunction is documented in ME/CFS (see thermoregulation topic). Hermisson does not specifically address thermal load during care. Thermal stress is an established energy cost in critical care medicine.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Providing all care at thermoneutral temperature (30°C ambient, 37°C water, pre-warmed materials) reduces post-care PEM incidence by ≥40% compared to standard-temperature care (22°C room, 30°C water, room-temperature materials) in very-severe ME/CFS.

### Idea 5.4: The Five-Minute Rule for Bedbound Care Response

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** For bedbound patients, any unscheduled interruption (caregiver entering, asking a question, adjusting bedding) triggers an orienting response that increases heart rate, cortisol, and metabolic rate for 5-15 minutes even if the interruption itself is minimal. A protocol that batches ALL care interactions into scheduled blocks (no unscheduled entering, no call-outs from outside the room, all non-urgent questions deferred to scheduled care times) could eliminate the "micro-crash" accumulation from startle-orienting responses.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson notes that all care is a cost. The orienting response is well-characterized (Pavlovian; catecholamine-mediated). ME/CFS patients show exaggerated startle responses and delayed recovery of autonomic parameters after unexpected stimuli (Van Oosterwijck 2017).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Eliminating unscheduled room entries (all care batched into ≤4 scheduled blocks per day, no caregiver call-outs or door opening between blocks) reduces daily symptom fluctuation range (peak-to-trough on symptom VAS) by ≥30% compared to unscheduled care patterns in very-severe ME/CFS.

## Category 6: Combinations + Access

### Idea 6.1: The Severe ME/CFS Care Minimum Viable Protocol (MVP)

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Current care recommendations are complex (multiple medications, pacing rules, nutritional strategies, environmental modifications). For severely resource-limited settings (single caregiver, rural area, no specialist access), a simplified MVP with the highest-benefit, lowest-cost, lowest-burden interventions would enable basic stabilization. Candidate MVP: (1) salt + compression for OI, (2) daily sensory fast, (3) prophylactic rest before all care, (4) elemental diet as needed, (5) H1/H2 antihistamines, (6) stop signal protocol.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson protocol assumes multidisciplinary team availability. ME/CFS care access is highly inequitable (Pendergrast 2021). Palliative care simplified protocols (e.g., WHO essential medicines list for pain) demonstrate that minimal-but-structured protocols outperform no protocol.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients receiving the 6-element MVP (implemented by a single caregiver after 1-hour training) will show ≥40% reduction in PEM frequency and ≥25% improvement in quality of life over 8 weeks, comparable to outcomes in specialist-centre protocols with multidisciplinary teams.

### Idea 6.2: Caregiver Dyad Model + Shared Care Respite

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Single-caregiver systems fail when the caregiver becomes ill or exhausted — leaving the patient without care and at high risk for deterioration. A formalized caregiver dyad (two trained caregivers sharing duties, each having scheduled off-duty periods) with a minimum respite ratio (1:1 caregivers:patient hours per week) could prevent the single-point-of-failure problem while reducing burnout-driven care breakdown.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson identifies caregiver burnout as a direct threat to patient stability. Caregiver dyad models are standard in palliative home care where patients have complex needs (e.g., pediatric home ventilation, ALS care). Caregiver respite reduces hospitalization rates in dementia care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients cared for under a formal caregiver dyad model (≥2 trained caregivers, each with ≥8h continuous off-duty per day) will show fewer emergency hospitalizations (≥50% reduction) and more stable symptom trajectories over 6 months compared to patients with single caregivers.

### Idea 6.3: Tele-Caregiver Support + Structured Escalation

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** In-home caregivers of severe ME/CFS patients lack real-time decision support. A tele-health system where caregivers can video-call a ME/CFS-trained nurse for 5-minute check-ins before/after high-risk care procedures (bathing, medication changes, transfers) could reduce care errors and caregiver anxiety without requiring in-person specialist visits that exhaust the patient.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson recommends professional nursing backup but provides no implementation pathway. Telehealth support reduces hospitalization in heart failure home care (Pekmezaris 2018). Caregiver decision-support algorithms exist in dementia care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A structured tele-support system (daily 5-minute nurse check-in + PRN call-back within 15 minutes) reduces caregiver-reported adverse events (falls, medication errors, missed PEM signs) by ≥40% compared to unsupported home care.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### Idea 7.1: Rolling PEM as a Coupled Oscillator Model

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The rolling PEM phenomenon can be formalized as a system of coupled oscillators where each PEM episode resets the recovery clock before it completes. In ODE terms: PEM state variable P(t) has a recovery time constant τ that resets to zero whenever a new trigger (exertion exceeding patient-specific threshold) occurs. If inter-trigger interval < τ, the patient enters a bounded state of incomplete recovery where baseline function ratchets downward.
- **Evidence link:** Directly formalizes the Hermisson rolling PEM concept. Similar coupled oscillator models exist for migraine cycling (Burstein 2015) and bipolar disorder mood episodes (Bonsall 2012).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The coupled-oscillator model will predict that for a given patient, there exists a critical inter-trigger interval τ_crit below which rolling PEM inevitably develops. Empirically, τ_crit will be patient-specific but fall in the range 48-120 hours for most severe patients, consistent with the clinical observation that some patients cannot tolerate daily care.

### Idea 7.2: Caregiver Energy Budget as Constrained Optimization

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The caregiver's allocation of care time across competing needs (hydration, feeding, hygiene, medication, repositioning, emotional support) is a constrained optimization problem: maximize total utility subject to the patient's daily energy envelope (total allowable exertion before PEM threshold). This can be modeled as a knapsack-type problem where each care element has a weight (care metabolic cost) and a value (prevention of medical complications). The output is an optimal daily care schedule.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson's "energy budget manager" concept. Analogous to nursing workload optimization models in ICU settings. The constraint is the patient's energy envelope, the variables are care elements with measurable metabolic costs (via indirect calorimetry during care).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** An optimized care schedule (generated by the constrained optimization model) will produce equivalent or better medical outcomes with 20-30% less total care time compared to intuitive caregiver scheduling, when tested in a simulation-based care planning study.

### Idea 7.3: DAG Addition — Care-Initiated PEM Cascade Node

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The care-PEM pathway is currently absent from ME/CFS causal DAGs. A new node "care activity" with directed edges to "sensory load," "metabolic cost," "orthostatic stress," and "emotional arousal" — all converging on "cellular energy deficit" → "PEM trigger" — would formalize the care-as-cost principle. A moderator variable "caregiver skill" would modulate the care→cost edge weight (trained caregivers cause lower load per care unit).
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson care guide provides the structure. Existing ME/CFS DAGs (e.g., the paper's own model) lack care-related variables.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In the augmented DAG, the indirect path (care activity → sensory load → cellular energy deficit → PEM) will have a path coefficient of ≥0.30 (moderate to strong), meaning care sensory load is a non-trivial PEM trigger independent of the direct metabolic cost of care.

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### Idea 8.1: ALS-Matched Telecare Model for Severe ME/CFS

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** ALS multidisciplinary care — where patients are too weak to travel, require complex home equipment, and need coordinated care across disciplines — is the closest existing model for severe ME/CFS care delivery. ALS care has been successfully delivered via telemedicine with structured equipment loans (ventilator → ME/CFS: oxygen concentrator, hospital bed, hoist) and remote specialist reviews. Adapting ALS care protocols for the different pathophysiology of ME/CFS (reducing rather than increasing energy support) could provide an implementation-ready framework.
- **Evidence link:** ALS telecare reduces hospitalizations and improves quality of life (van den Berg 2015). Hermisson care guide calls for multidisciplinary care but provides no implementation model.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients receiving care through a modified ALS multidisciplinary telecare model (neurologist + physiotherapist + occupational therapist + social worker, monthly video reviews) will show fewer emergency room visits and greater care stability over 12 months than matched historical controls receiving standard fragmented care.

### Idea 8.2: ICU-Acquired Weakness Prevention Overlap

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bedbound severe ME/CFS patients share key features with ICU patients: immobility, muscle wasting, contracture risk, pressure injury risk, and deconditioning. ICU prevention protocols (turning schedules, passive range of motion, pressure offloading, nutritional timing) are evidence-based — but in ME/CFS, these same interventions are PEM triggers that worsen the patient's condition. The ICU-to-ME/CFS adaptation is: perform ALL ICU-standard prevention but at 10-20% frequency, with mandatory pre-intervention rest, and with explicit permission to skip interventions on high-symptom days.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson's "goal is stabilization, not activation" directly contradicts ICU early mobilization culture. No study has tested adapted ICU prevention protocols in ME/CFS.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** An adapted ICU prevention protocol (turning Q4h instead of Q2h, passive ROM once daily instead of BID, nutrition via lowest-effort route) will show lower pressure injury incidence than no protocol but higher rates than full ICU protocol — yet will not increase PEM frequency, whereas the full ICU protocol would double PEM rates.

### Idea 8.3: Palliative Care Continuous Deep Sedation Analogy

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** In palliative care, continuous deep sedation (CDS) is used for refractory suffering when all other interventions fail. For the most severe ME/CFS patients — those with constant rolling PEM, unable to tolerate any sensory input, in unrelenting physiological crisis — CDS-like protocols (scheduled sedation window of 6-12 hours daily, not continuous) could break the rolling PEM cycle by enforcing a prolonged recovery period beyond what physiological sleep provides. This is distinct from conventional sedation and ethically distinct from terminal sedation.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson rolling PEM concept suggests some patients never achieve the sustained rest needed for recovery. Palliative CDS is established for refractory suffering (Cherny 2009). ME/CFS has no such protocol. Ethical framework exists for intermittent deep sedation in non-terminal conditions (e.g., cluster headache, refractory status epilepticus).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A scheduled sedation window (6h/day, using sublingual lorazepam + diphenhydramine, under caregiver supervision) for 7 consecutive days in very-severe ME/CFS patients with confirmed rolling PEM will show ≥50% reduction in average daily symptom severity compared to baseline, without adverse events exceeding those of symptom-driven sedation.

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### Idea 9.1: Care-Triggered PEM Latency Index

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The time delay between a specific care intervention and the onset of recognisable PEM symptoms varies between patients and between care types. A "PEM latency index" (median delay from care trigger to symptom onset, measured by caregiver symptom diary) could stratify patients: short latency (<4h) → need longer prophylactic rest and smaller care micro-batches; long latency (>24h) → risk of unrecognized care→PEM linkage and missed prevention opportunities.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson notes delayed PEM onset makes self-monitoring unreliable. PEM latency is documented (average 24h but range 2-72h) (Van Oosterwijck 2010). No clinical tool quantifies care-triggered latency specifically.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The PEM latency index is a stable intra-patient trait (test-retest reliability ICC ≥0.70 over 4 weeks) that correlates with disease severity (shorter latency = more severe) and predicts response to micro-pacing protocols (short-latency patients benefit more).

### Idea 9.2: Care-Load/Baseline Ratio as Severity Metric

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Current severity scales (Bell, FUNCAP, ICC criteria) are static — they measure what patients CAN do, not what care they NEED. A dynamic severity metric based on the ratio of required care load to baseline tolerance (total minutes of care required per day / minutes of tolerated activity before PEM onset) could capture severity as a moving target and predict imminent rolling PEM when the ratio approaches or exceeds 1.0.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson rolling PEM represents a state where care needs exceed tolerance. Palliative care uses care-need-to-performance ratios (e.g., Barthel index change over time). No such ratio exists for ME/CFS.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A care-load/tolerance ratio ≥0.85 predicts progression to rolling PEM within 2 weeks (sensitivity ≥80%, specificity ≥70%) in a prospective cohort of severe ME/CFS patients.

### Idea 9.3: Daily PEM Diary as Research-Grade Outcome

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Hermisson-based care interventions lack validated outcome measures. A daily PEM diary completed by the caregiver (not the patient, reducing patient burden) that records number, duration, and severity of crashes; inter-crash interval; and most recent care trigger could serve as the primary outcome measure for severe ME/CFS care trials. Key proposed metric: "crash-free interval" (hours between PEM endpoint and next PEM onset) as a continuous outcome more sensitive than crash count.
- **Evidence link:** No validated PEM diary exists for caregiver administration. PEM diaries in research use patient self-report (which is unreliable in severe patients due to cognitive impairment). Activity pacing trials use crash frequency as outcome but with variable definitions.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The caregiver-reported daily PEM diary will show high inter-rater reliability (κ ≥0.80 between two independent caregivers rating the same day) and will correlate with clinician global impression of change (r ≥0.60), supporting its use as a validated outcome measure in severe ME/CFS trials.

### Idea 9.4: Rest-Quality Index for Bedbound Patients

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** "Rest" for bedbound patients is not automatically restorative — it depends on sensory environment, position, temperature, hunger, pain, anxiety, and sleep efficiency. A Rest-Quality Index (RQI) combining heart rate variability (autonomic recovery), actigraphy (movement frequency during rest periods), and patient/caregiver-rated restfulness (0-10) could quantify whether scheduled rest periods are actually achieving recovery. Patients with low RQI despite adequate rest time may need environmental or pharmacologic optimization.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson's prophylactic and post-care mandatory rest assume rest is restorative. Polysomnography in ME/CFS shows non-restorative sleep (Jackson 2016). No index evaluates rest quality separately from sleep quality.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Patients in the lowest tertile of RQI (non-restorative rest despite adequate rest time) will have 2× higher PEM frequency than patients in the highest tertile, independent of total rest time, and will respond preferentially to rest optimization interventions (sensory reduction, pharmacological sleep support, position adjustment).
