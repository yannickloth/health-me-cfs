# Brainstorm: Glycolytic Reprogramming of Host Cells by Non-Viral Tick-Borne Pathogens and ME/CFS Relevance

**Topic:** Glycolytic reprogramming of host cells by *Borrelia burgdorferi*, *Bartonella* spp., and *Babesia* spp. — the non-viral tick-borne parallel to the viral glial reprogramming already in the paper (Rodrigues 2025, ch08) — and its relevance to ME/CFS immunometabolic dysfunction.

## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### Idea 1.1 — Borrelia-Trained Macrophage Glycolysis → Systemic Lactate → ANLS Gradient Reversal (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.22
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Barriales2021 demonstrates Borrelia-trained macrophages with persistent glycolytic output and mitochondrial downregulation, replicating in vivo and reversing with glycolysis inhibition. If these trained macrophages (tissue-resident, widely distributed including perivascular) maintain chronically elevated lactate production, the resulting serum-to-brain lactate concentration gradient could perturb the astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle (ANLS). In normal physiology, astrocytes export lactate to neurons along a concentration gradient. Chronic systemic hyperlactatemia from trained immune cells would reduce this gradient directionally, potentially reversing net flow — depriving neurons of their primary oxidative fuel. The paper already demonstrates ANLS vulnerability (ch08 @sec:astrocyte-energy-gate) and that lactate is elevated in some ME/CFS contexts. This bridges two separately-documented mechanisms: Borrelia-trained-immunity glycolysis and ANLS-dependent neuronal energetics.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (Borrelia-trained glycolytic macrophage memory), @sec:astrocyte-energy-gate (ANLS and ME/CFS), ch08 viral reprogramming parallel. No study has measured lactate gradient reversal.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS subset, (1) venous serum lactate will be elevated at rest vs non-tick ME/CFS and healthy controls, (2) the arterial-to-venous lactate gradient (normally near-zero) will be elevated (trained-immune output), and (3) the CSF-to-serum lactate ratio (normally ~0.6–0.8) will be reduced toward 0.4, indicating reduced net lactate export from brain → reversed ANLS. Refuted if lactate gradients do not differ by tick-exposure history.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The immune system's "memory" of a past tick infection may cause white blood cells to produce excess lactate, which in turn floods the brain with the wrong fuel at the wrong time — potentially a direct biochemical cause of brain fog and cognitive fatigue.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.2 — Bartonella HIF-1α → Chronic Endothelial Glycolysis → Impaired BBB → Two-Hit Vulnerability (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.18
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Kempf2005 shows *Bartonella henselae* stabilises HIF-1α in endothelial cells, driving VEGF, increasing oxygen consumption, and decreasing ATP — the first bacterial HIF-1α stabilisation demonstrated. Chronic endothelial HIF-1α activation drives a sustained glycolytic shift and VEGF-mediated increased vascular permeability. In brain microvascular endothelium, this could produce a persistently "leaky" BBB. The two-hit model: Hit 1 = Bartonella (past or ongoing) creates endothelial glycolytic memory and barrier weakness. Hit 2 = later stressors (viral reactivation, immune activation, exercise-induced shear) cross a threshold for BBB opening that a healthy endothelium would withstand. This suggests tick-borne infections could create long-lasting endothelial vulnerability even after clearance — a "pre-damaged" BBB.
- **Evidence link:** Kempf2005BartonellaHIF1 (HIF-1α + VEGF + ATP depletion in endothelium). BBB disruption in ME/CFS (ch08). Viral glial reprogramming (Rodrigues2025). PTLDS BBB changes (limited literature). The BBB-impairment endpoint is in the paper; the Bartonella-to-BBB link is new.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Bartonella-seropositive ME/CFS patients (by IFA) will show elevated CSF:serum albumin ratio (BBB integrity marker) and elevated serum sVCAM-1 (endothelial activation) compared to Bartonella-seronegative ME/CFS with similar clinical severity, and these markers will correlate with cognitive dysfunction severity (r > 0.4).
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A Bartonella infection — even one that didn't cause noticeable symptoms at the time — may silently damage the brain's blood vessel lining, making the blood-brain barrier leaky and leaving the brain vulnerable to later immune and metabolic insults.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.3 — Borrelia-Trained Macrophage Memory → WASF3 Pathway Convergence — Compounding Glycolysis (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.20
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Barriales2021 shows Borrelia-trained macrophages have mitochondrial downregulation (TCA cycle, OXPHOS transcripts suppressed). Gorman2024 shows WASF3 disrupts mitonuclear protein import, impairing OXPHOS assembly and driving compensatory glycolysis in ME/CFS. These two mechanisms converge on the same node: impaired mitochondrial function forcing glycolysis. If Borrelia-trained immunity downregulates mitochondrial gene expression in tissue macrophages, and WASF3 simultaneously impairs OXPHOS assembly in muscle/brain cells, the glycolytic load is amplified across cell types, creating an "energy trap" where no cell type can efficiently perform OXPHOS and the organism-wide glycolytic compensation exceeds systemic glucose supply. The hypothesis predicts that tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS with WASF3 risk alleles would have the most severe metabolic impairment.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (mitochondrial downregulation in trained macrophages), gorman2024wasf3 (WASF3 → OXPHOS disruption → glycolysis), @hyp:tickborne-glycolytic-reprogramming (ch07). No study has measured WASF3 expression in tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS patients carrying WASF3 risk variants will show lower maximal OXPHOS capacity (by respirometry) and higher resting lactate than non-carrier tick-borne ME/CFS and than WASF3-carrier viral-triggered ME/CFS — a gene–environment interaction signature.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A combination of a genetic predisposition (WASF3) and a past tick infection that "trains" immune cells to rely on glycolysis may together cripple the energy machinery so severely that neither cell type can produce energy efficiently — a two-hit metabolic trap.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.4 — Babesia Glucose Piracy → Cerebral Glucose Deficit During Exertion (rank: 10)
- **Certainty:** 0.10
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Ohmori2004 shows Babesia-infected murine RBCs increase de novo glucose uptake. If Babesia-parasitized RBCs have substantially higher glucose affinity/transport than uninfected cells, they may act as a competing glucose sink. During exertion, when skeletal muscle and brain glucose demand rises, parasitized RBCs could sequester a disproportionate share of circulating glucose. Even at low parasitemia (0.1–0.5% infected cells), if each infected RBC consumes 2–5× normal glucose, the cumulative glucose demand could be non-trivial in a patient with already-compromised energy metabolism. The brain, which has minimal glycogen stores and depends on continuous glucose (and lactate) supply, would be the most vulnerable organ.
- **Evidence link:** Ohmori2004BabesiaGlucose (single mouse study, never replicated). Cerebral glucose hypometabolism in ME/CFS (ch08 PET studies, limited). No study has measured glucose kinetics during exercise in Babesia-positive vs negative ME/CFS.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a Babesia-PCR-positive ME/CFS cohort, (1) arterial-venous glucose difference across the brain will be reduced at rest compared to Babesia-negative ME/CFS, (2) this difference will widen disproportionately during cognitive exertion (Stroop test), and (3) the magnitude will correlate with Babesia parasitemia. Refuted if glucose extraction is unrelated to Babesia status.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Babesia-infected red blood cells may act like glucose "vampires" — consuming more sugar than normal cells — leaving less glucose available for the brain during mentally demanding tasks, potentially worsening brain fog.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.5 — Kerstholt–Dong Paradox as a Biphasic Time-Course: Acute ↑ Glycolysis → Chronic ↓ Glycolysis Exhaustion (rank: 8)
- **Certainty:** 0.15
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The contradiction between Dong2026 (Borrelia ↑ LDHA, glycolysis, lactate in THP-1 at 24h) and Kerstholt2022 (Borrelia ↓ baseline glycolysis in primary monocytes, measured at later timepoints or after stimulation) may not be a contradiction at all but a biphasic time-course: acute Borrelia exposure upregulates glycolysis (initial immune activation, Warburg-like), while chronic exposure or post-exposure leads to a glycolysis-suppressed state (exhaustion, tolerance, or regulatory feedback). This would parallel the exhausted T-cell phenotype in ME/CFS (Mandarano2020: failed CD8+ Warburg) — an initial activation phase followed by metabolic exhaustion. The unified model: acute tick-borne infection triggers glycolytic reprogramming, but if the infection persists or the immune response is chronic, cells enter a metabolically exhausted state with suppressed glycolysis — explaining both data sets and the Mandarano finding.
- **Evidence link:** Dong2026BorreliaGlycolysis (acute ↑), Kerstholt2022BorreliaLactate (baseline ↓), Mandarano2020 (failed CD8+ Warburg in ME/CFS). No longitudinal study of monocyte glycolysis from acute infection through chronic phase.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Serial Seahorse assays of PBMCs from acute Lyme patients tracked over 12 months will show an initial ECAR increase (first 2–4 weeks) followed by a decline below baseline (3–12 months) in patients who develop PTLDS, while those who recover maintain intermediate ECAR. The exhausted-glycolysis state at 12 months will correlate with residual fatigue.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The apparent contradiction in the evidence — "Borrelia increases glycolysis" vs "Borrelia decreases glycolysis" — may both be correct, just at different stages: the immune system revs up metabolism to fight the infection and then crashes into a state of metabolic exhaustion.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 2: Research Directions

### Idea 2.1 — Ex-Vivo Monocyte Seahorse Assay: Tick-Borne-Triggered vs Viral-Triggered ME/CFS (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Isolate CD14+ monocytes from three groups: (1) tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS (documented prior Lyme/Babesia/Bartonella with clear temporal relationship), (2) viral-triggered ME/CFS (documented EBV/COVID/influenza onset), (3) healthy controls. Measure extracellular acidification rate (ECAR, glycolysis) and oxygen consumption rate (OCR, OXPHOS) by Seahorse at baseline and after LPS/IFN-γ stimulation. Primary outcome: ECAR/OCR ratio at baseline. If Barriales2021 trained-immunity signature is present in tick-borne-triggered but not viral-triggered ME/CFS, this confirms the mechanism is pathogen-specific. Secondary: correlate with fatigue severity, PEM duration, and serum lactate. Expected ECAR/OCR: tick-borne-triggered > viral-triggered > controls. The study is feasible (Seahorse is standard, 50 mL blood yields sufficient monocytes), costs ~$15K for 60 participants, and could run in 6 months.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (glycolytic signature in trained macrophages). @hyp:tickborne-glycolytic-reprogramming (ch07). Mandarano2020 (failed Warburg in ME/CFS — the current study would measure the opposite side: excessive Warburg in tick-borne subset).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS (n=20) will show mean baseline ECAR/OCR ≥3.0 vs ≤2.0 in viral-triggered ME/CFS (n=20) and ≤1.5 in healthy controls (n=20). Refuted if ECAR/OCR does not differ by trigger type.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A single blood draw and a 2-hour cell metabolism test could determine whether ME/CFS triggered by tick infections is metabolically different from ME/CFS triggered by viruses — which would point toward different treatment approaches.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.2 — Replicate Kerstholt2022 with Cell-Type and Timepoint Resolution (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.55
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The direction-of-effect contradiction between Dong2026 (↑ glycolysis in THP-1) and Kerstholt2022 (↓ glycolysis in primary monocytes) is the central unresolved empirical question for the Borrelia-glycolysis narrative. A systematic replication testing both cell substrates (primary monocytes vs THP-1 vs primary macrophages) at multiple timepoints (2h, 6h, 24h, 72h, 7d) with identical Borrelia strain and MOI would determine: (a) whether the contradiction is a cell-type artifact (THP-1 ≠ primary cells), (b) whether it is timepoint-dependent (biphasic response), or (c) whether it reflects different readouts (supernatant lactate vs baseline glycolytic rate). The result directly determines which direction is the correct foundation for the ME/CFS hypothesis.
- **Evidence link:** Dong2026BorreliaGlycolysis (THP-1, 24h, ↑ lactate, glucose ↓), Kerstholt2022BorreliaLactate (primary monocytes, ↓ baseline glycolysis). Oosting2016BorreliaCytokine (human functional genomics framework — this would add metabolic phenotyping).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** If the contradiction is cell-type-driven: THP-1 cells will show ↑ glycolysis at all timepoints while primary monocytes show ↓ glycolysis at all timepoints. If timepoint-driven: both cell types will show ↑ glycolysis at early timepoints (≤6h) and ↓ glycolysis at later timepoints (≥48h).
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A straightforward lab experiment could resolve whether the basic biological assumption behind the tick-glycolysis hypothesis is correct or reversed — before any patient studies are designed.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.3 — PTLDS CSF Metabolomics for CNS-Compartment Glycolysis Signature (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Fitzgerald2021 found no serum glycolytic signature in PTLDS (phospholipid/bile-acid/acylcarnitine signature instead) and explicitly noted this could reflect cell-type-restricted or compartment-restricted reprogramming not visible in serum. CSF metabolomics in PTLDS could capture CNS-compartment-specific glycolytic flux (lactate, pyruvate, alanine, branched-chain amino acids, citrate) that serum misses. Design: lumbar puncture in PTLDS (n=20), recovered Lyme (n=20), ME/CFS (n=20), healthy controls (n=20). Primary outcome: CSF lactate and lactate:pyruvate ratio. Secondary: CSF glucose, correlation with cognitive scores. If the glycolytic reprogramming is CNS-predominant (glial), CSF would show it while serum would not — explaining Fitzgerald2021's null serum finding.
- **Evidence link:** Fitzgerald2021PTLDSMetabolic (serum null for glycolysis — motivates this study). @spec:tickborne-glial-reprogramming (ch08, cert 0.30) — the hypothesis that tick-borne pathogens reprogram glia. @spec:astrocyte-energy-gate (ANLS disruption).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** PTLDS patients with persistent fatigue will show CSF lactate >2.0 mM and L:P ratio >20 compared to recovered Lyme (<1.8 mM, <15) and healthy controls (<1.7 mM, <15), while serum glycolysis markers will not differ. Refuted if PTLDS CSF metabolomics is normal or does not correlate with fatigue.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A spinal fluid analysis — invasive but informative — could reveal whether past tick infections leave a metabolic "signature" in the brain that standard blood tests completely miss, which would explain why previous blood-based studies found nothing.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### Idea 3.1 — Metformin for Borrelia-Trained-Immunity Glycolytic Memory Reset (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Barriales2021 showed glycolysis inhibition in vivo reversed Borrelia-trained immunity in mice — restoring mitochondrial gene expression, reducing inflammation, and decreasing spirochetal burden. Metformin inhibits mitochondrial complex I, shifting cells from OXPHOS toward glycolysis in the short term, but paradoxically, in trained-immunity models (BCG-trained monocytes), metformin can RESET trained-immunity epigenetic marks by: (1) reducing acetyl-CoA availability (from glucose → lipid synthesis pathway) for histone acetylation, (2) activating AMPK which promotes a anti-inflammatory metabolic state, and (3) promoting a tolerant (trained-immunity-opposing) epigenetic programme via H3K4me3 erasure. In the context of Borrelia-trained glycolytic memory, metformin's net effect over weeks may be to "erase" the glycolytic epigenetic imprint. Critically, metformin is generic ($0.10/day), FDA-approved, has a well-established safety profile, and is already in clinical use for PCOS and prediabetes — making this the most immediately translatable idea.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (glycolysis inhibition reverses trained immunity — the mechanism metformin would mimic). Cheng2014mTORtrained (metformin inhibits trained immunity via mTOR/HIF-1α and acetyl-CoA). @hyp:tickborne-glycolytic-reprogramming. Constraint: does NOT violate the no-antimicrobial-escalation rule (metformin is metabolic, not antimicrobial).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In an open-label pilot (n=10, tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS), metformin XR 500mg/day for 12 weeks will: (1) reduce ex-vivo monocyte ECAR/OCR ratio (Seahorse) by ≥20%, (2) increase mitochondrial gene expression by RNA-seq, (3) improve Chalder Fatigue Scale by ≥3 points. Refuted if no change in any metabolic or clinical endpoint.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A cheap ($4/month) diabetes drug, already known to be safe, could potentially "reset" the metabolic memory left by a tick infection in immune cells — for patients where Lyme triggered their ME/CFS, this could be a treatment option years after the infection cleared.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.2 — Belzutifan for Bartonella-Driven Endothelial HIF-2α Stabilisation (rank: 5)
- **Certainty:** 0.18
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Kempf2005 shows Bartonella stabilises HIF-1α in endothelium. In endothelial cells, sustained HIF-2α activation (which often accompanies HIF-1α in hypoxic conditions) drives VEGF, increases vascular permeability, and mediates a chronic glycolytic endothelial phenotype. The paper already discusses belzutifan (HIF-2α inhibitor) for post-viral endothelial dysfunction (ch27, cert 0.25). If Bartonella is the upstream trigger in a subset, belzutifan could target the downstream metabolic dysregulation without requiring antimicrobials — treating the consequence rather than the pathogen. This is particularly relevant for patients with Bartonella serology but no evidence of active bacteremia, where the reprogramming persists post-clearance.
- **Evidence link:** Kempf2005BartonellaHIF1. ch27 belzutifan discussion (existing, cert 0.25). @spec:hif-pathway-inertia (ch14k). HIF-2α endothelial post-viral synthesis.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Bartonella-seropositive ME/CFS patients (n=10) treated with belzutifan 40mg TID (FDA-approved dose for VHL disease) for 8 weeks will show: (1) reduced serum VEGF and sFlt-1, (2) improved endothelial function by peripheral arterial tonometry (PAT ratio increase ≥0.1), (3) reduced cognitive fatigue score. Refuted if no change.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A kidney cancer drug that blocks a specific oxygen-sensing pathway could potentially repair the blood-vessel damage caused by a past Bartonella infection — offering a treatment avenue that doesn't rely on more antibiotics.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.3 — Rapamycin (Sirolimus) for Trained-Immunity Epigenetic Erasure in Post-Tick ME/CFS (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.15
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Rapamycin (mTOR inhibitor) blocks the metabolic and epigenetic reprogramming cascade of trained immunity at its source: mTOR is required for the shift from OXPHOS to glycolysis (HIF-1α translation), for histone methylation (H3K4me3) that enforces trained-immunity gene accessibility, and for the sustained glycolytic output. In BCG-trained immunity models, rapamycin inhibits trained immunity both during induction and after establishment (Saeed2014, Cheng2014). For Borrelia-trained glycolytic macrophages, low-dose rapamycin (Rapamune, 0.5–1 mg/day — doses used in autoimmune disease) could erode the glycolytic memory over weeks to months. Risks: immunosuppression (mild at low doses), metabolic side effects (hyperlipidemia, hyperglycemia). However, the risk/benefit is better than prolonged antibiotics (which have documented harm) — rapamycin at low doses has been safely used in tuberous sclerosis and anti-aging protocols.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (trained-immunity glycolysis = mTOR-dependent). Saeed2014 (mTOR in trained immunity). @Humer2025TrainedImmunityMECFS (trained immunity in ME/CFS). Cheng2014mTORtrained. No rapamycin study exists in tick-borne or ME/CFS.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Low-dose rapamycin (1mg/day, 8 weeks) in tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS (n=10) reduces ex-vivo monocyte TNF-α and IL-6 production after LPS stimulation by >50% and reduces ECAR by >20%, with a ≥3-point fatigue score improvement. Refuted if no change.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** An immunosuppressant drug used in organ transplants could, at very low doses, help "erase" the hyperactive metabolic memory of immune cells left by a tick infection — potentially unwinding the immune dysfunction years after the original infection cleared.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.4 — Sodium Oxamate (LDH Inhibitor) — Borrelia + Host Dual Target (rank: 10)
- **Certainty:** 0.08
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Lynch2023 shows Borrelia is obligately glycolytic and LDH inhibitors (sodium oxamate, gossypol) suppress its growth. Simultaneously, LDH inhibition would reduce lactate output from trained-immune macrophages (Dong2026 → LDHA up). Dual mechanism: anti-Borrelia + anti-host-reprogramming. However, sodium oxamate is not FDA-approved, has unknown human safety at required doses, and the therapeutic window may be narrow (LDH is ubiquitous). Pros: mechanistic probe for proof-of-concept. Cons: toxicity risk, unavailable outside research. Certainty reduced by these obstacles.
- **Evidence link:** Lynch2023LDHBorrelia (Borrelia growth suppression by LDH inhibitors). Dong2026BorreliaGlycolysis (LDHA upregulation). Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (glycolysis inhibition reverses trained immunity).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a mouse model of Borrelia infection (n=10/group), LDH inhibitor treatment will reduce both spirochetal burden and tissue lactate, and reverse macrophage trained-immunity signature (ecar/ocr normalization). (Animal study only — human translation premature.)
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A chemical that blocks lactate production could simultaneously starve the Borrelia bacteria and calm the overactive metabolism of infected immune cells — but it's too experimental and risky for human use now.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.5 — Daprodustat (HIF-PH Inhibitor) for Post-Borrelia HIF Inertia (rank: 7)
- **Certainty:** 0.12
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If Borrelia-trained macrophages have suppressed mitochondrial OXPHOS (Barriales2021) AND the paper's HIF pathway inertia hypothesis (@spec:hif-pathway-inertia) suggests blunted HIF-1α response, then daprodustat (a HIF-prolyl hydroxylase inhibitor, approved for CKD anemia) could simultaneously: (1) stabilize HIF-1α to upregulate glycolytic capacity for cells stuck between OXPHOS failure and inadequate glycolysis, (2) enhance EPO production improving oxygen delivery, (3) upregulate mitochondrial biogenesis genes mediated by HIF-2α → PGC-1α cross-talk. However, there is a risk: enhancing glycolysis when cells are already glycolytically trapped may worsen the phenotype. This idea requires knowing whether the net metabolic defect in tick-borne ME/CFS is insufficient glycolysis (HIF inertia) or excessive glycolysis (trained immunity). The answer may be cell-type-dependent, making this idea both high-potential and high-risk.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (mitochondrial downregulation — need glycolysis compensation). @spec:hif-pathway-inertia. ch27 daprodustat/roxadustat discussion (existing). Kempf2005BartonellaHIF1 (Bartonella stabilises HIF — could daprodustat oppose this or compound it?).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS with low serum EPO (<8 mIU/mL, indicating HIF inertia), daprodustat 2mg/day for 8 weeks will increase EPO by ≥50% and improve 6-minute walk distance by ≥30 meters, but ONLY in patients without evidence of active Bartonella infection (where extra HIF stabilization might be harmful). Refuted if no effect or if Bartonella-positive patients worsen.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** An anemia drug that boosts the body's oxygen-sensing pathway could help ME/CFS patients whose cells have lost the ability to adapt to low oxygen — but only for those whose tick infection left them "stuck" with too little glycolysis, not those with too much.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Idea 4.1 — Ketone Esters as ANLS Bypass in Borrelia-Trained Glycolysis (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If Borrelia-trained macrophages produce excess lactate (Barriales2021), altering the lactate gradient that drives ANLS (Idea 1.1), ketone bodies (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) provide a direct neuronal fuel that bypasses the ANLS entirely. Ketones cross the BBB via MCT1/2 (same transporters as lactate) and enter the TCA cycle directly, independent of glucose or lactate availability. Exogenous ketone esters (e.g., β-hydroxybutyrate esters at ~1.5–3 g/day) could restore cerebral energy metabolism even if the ANLS is compromised. The paper already discusses ketone-body bypass therapeutics (ch08), so this is an existing concept extended to a specific mechanism. Key advantage: ketones are a nutritional supplement, not a drug — accessible, safe, and available to patients now.
- **Evidence link:** Barriales2021BorreliaMacrophage (trained-immunity lactate output → ANLS disruption hypothesis). @sec:astrocyte-energy-gate (ANLS vulnerability). ch08 ketone-body bypass. Ketones as ANLS-independent fuel (established neurophysiology: MCT-transported β-HB enters neurons and glia, metabolized to acetyl-CoA → TCA cycle). No ketone study in tick-borne ME/CFS exists.

- **Evidence link:** @spec:babesia-hemolysis-haptoglobin (ch07). Haptoglobin as acute-phase reactant. Haptoglobin in ME/CFS (Moezzi2025).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a tick-exposed ME/CFS cohort (n=200), Hp/LDH ratio triages patients into three clusters: (1) Low Hp + High LDH → Babesia+ (PCR-confirmed), (2) High Hp + Normal LDH → Borrelia+ (serology-confirmed), (3) Normal Hp + High LDH → Bartonella+ (PCR-confirmed). Cluster-to-pathogen concordance ≥70%.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Two cheap blood tests already on standard lab panels could help identify which tick-borne pathogen is most likely involved in a patient's illness, guiding more specific (and expensive) testing — without needing to guess based on symptoms alone.

## Category 10: Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS

### Idea 10.1 — Zero Direct ME/CFS Evidence: All Is Mechanistic Analogy (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.75
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Every single claim in this brainstorm chain — Borrelia-trained immunity, Bartonella HIF-1α, Babesia glucose piracy — is extrapolated from in vitro cell line, primary mouse, or human-other-disease data. Not a single study has measured any of the following in ME/CFS specifically: monocyte ECAR/OCR after tick-borne exposure, trained-immunity glycolytic signature in tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS vs controls, monocyte LDHA mRNA, tissue lactate gradients, or ANLS function in tick-exposed patients. The entire framework is a plausible analogy built on observations from different diseases, different cell types, and different species. The history of ME/CFS research is replete with plausible analogies that failed to replicate in patients — the mechanism is no more likely to hold than any of them.
- **What would disprove:** A direct negative result: (1) Monocyte Seahorse ECAR/OCR from tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS patients does not differ from viral-triggered ME/CFS or healthy controls, (2) Monocyte LDHA mRNA is not elevated in tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS, (3) Barriales2021 Borrelia-trained immunity signature in mouse macrophages does not replicate in human monocyte-derived macrophages.
- **Alternative explanation:** Tick-borne trigger ME/CFS is mechanistically identical to other-trigger ME/CFS — the same failed Warburg switch (Mandarano2020), the same WASF3 OXPHOS disruption (Gorman2024), the same cell danger response (Naviaux2016) — and the tick-borne trigger is incidental. Tick infection is merely one stressor among many that activates the same final common pathway of metabolic dysfunction. The glycolytic reprogramming observed in vitro is a transient immune activation response that resolves after pathogen clearance and does not persist as a chronic metabolic memory in humans.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The idea that tick infections leave a lasting "metabolic scar" on immune cells is based entirely on lab experiments in mice and cell lines — there is zero evidence it actually happens in ME/CFS patients, and many similar-sounding mechanisms have been disproven when tested in real patients.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 10.2 — PTLDS Metabolomics Shows NO Glycolysis Signature — the Closest Human Data Contradicts (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Fitzgerald2021 (Clin Infect Dis, 0.65) performed the most comprehensive serum metabolomics study in PTLDS (n=31 patients, n=20 controls) using validated UPLC-MS/MS platform. The PTLDS metabolomic signature was phospholipid, bile-acid, and acylcarnitine — zero glycolysis metabolites (lactate, pyruvate, glucose, alanine). If PTLDS — which IS tick-borne-triggered chronic fatigue by definition — shows no serum glycolysis perturbation, the entire glycolytic reprogramming hypothesis for ME/CFS starts from a contradictory human observation. The standard counterargument is cell-type-restricted reprogramming (metabolically active in tissue macrophages but invisible in serum). However, activated immune cells release lactate into circulation (that's how serum lactate rises in sepsis). If trained macrophages truly produce sustained excess lactate, it should be detectable in serum — the fact that it isn't in PTLDS is a serious empirical challenge. The PTLDS metabolomic signature (acylcarnitines → impaired fatty acid oxidation; bile acids → altered gut-liver axis) points toward a DIFFERENT metabolic mechanism (lipid/fatty-acid dysregulation, not glycolysis).
- **What would disprove:** A tissue-level or compartment-specific measurement in PTLDS/ME/CFS showing glycolysis effects that serum misses (e.g., CSF lactate elevated in PTLDS patients with fatigue)
- **Alternative explanation:** Tick-borne-triggered chronic fatigue is driven by lipid metabolism dysregulation (acylcarnitines → impaired β-oxidation → energy deficit) and bile acid dysregulation (gut-liver axis → inflammation), NOT by glycolytic reprogramming. The phospholipid signature suggests cell membrane remodeling, possibly from ongoing immune activation, but not a shift in cellular energy substrate preference.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The most rigorous metabolic study in people with persistent Lyme symptoms — the closest we have to tick-triggered ME/CFS — found NO evidence of abnormal sugar metabolism. The abnormalities they found were in fat processing and gut-related molecules, not in glycolysis.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 10.3 — Kerstholt2022 Contradicts the Entire Training-Immunity Glycolysis Narrative (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Kerstholt2022 (0.60 certainty) found that Borrelia DECREASES baseline glycolysis in PRIMARY HUMAN MONOCYTES (not THP-1 cell line). If primary human cells — the most physiologically relevant substrate — respond to Borrelia by SUPPRESSING glycolysis, then the entire Barriales2021/Dong2026 narrative (Borrelia UPREGULATES glycolysis) may be an artifact of cell line (THP-1) or mouse physiology that does not translate to humans. Kerstholt2022 also mapped the pathway: MFAP3L → ERK2 → lactate, with platelet amplification. The decreased baseline glycolysis finding is important because it was measured in the cell type most relevant to trained immunity (monocytes). If Borrelia decreases monocyte glycolysis at baseline, then there is no "excess glycolysis to sustain trained-immunity memory" — the exactly opposite metabolic effect occurs.
- **What would disprove:** A direct replication of Kerstholt2022 and Barriales2021 in the same laboratory using the same human primary monocyte protocol, resolving the direction of effect with pre-registered endpoints.
- **Alternative explanation:** Borrelia's effect on host metabolism is NET INHIBITORY, not stimulatory. It suppresses host glycolysis to conserve resources for its own obligate glycolytic needs (Lynch2023: Borrelia obligately glycolytic). The host cell's metabolic suppression may contribute to fatigue directly (less energy available) rather than via a trained-immunity hyperglycolytic mechanism.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The most direct human lab evidence shows that Borrelia bacteria actually SUPPRESS the sugar-burning machinery of immune cells — the opposite of what this hypothesis predicts — casting serious doubt on the whole idea that tick infections drive a hyperactive sugar-burning state.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 10.4 — Trained Immunity Requires Ongoing or Repeated Stimulation to Persist (rank: 5)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Wendeln2018 (the foundational paper for microglial trained immunity) found that trained immunity requires a SECOND stimulus to produce an exaggerated response — it is a "primed" state, not a constitutively active state. Barriales2021 measured trained immunity 7 days after Borrelia stimulation in mice — at which point the trained macrophages showed exaggerated responses to stimulation (LPS) but may not be constitutively hyperglycolytic. The distinction: trained immunity means the cell RESPONDS MORE to a second challenge — it does not mean the cell is basally hyperactive. If the tick-borne infection cleared years ago and there is no ongoing or intermittent stimulation (PAMP/DAMP exposure), the trained state may be epigenetically silent — maintained as a poised chromatin state that requires a trigger to manifest. ME/CFS patients may not have the ongoing inflammation needed to "light the fuse" of the trained glycolytic phenotype. The training may have faded by the time ME/CFS is diagnosed (typically years after trigger).
- **What would disprove:** Demonstration that tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS monocytes show constitutively elevated glycolytic output at rest (without stimulation) — not just an exaggerated response to LPS.
- **Alternative explanation:** The trained-immunity state in tick-borne ME/CFS manifests only during PEM (exercise provides the "second hit"), explaining normal resting metabolism but abnormal post-exertional response. This is consistent with the paper's PEM framework but means resting biomarkers would be normal.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The "metabolic memory" concept requires an ongoing trigger to keep the immune cells in their hyperactive state — if the infection cleared years ago, the memory cells may simply be dormant, waiting for a trigger that rarely comes, and not actively causing symptoms.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 10.5 — The HIF-1α Evidence for Bartonella Comes from a Single 20-Year-Old Study in Cancer Cell Lines (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.60
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Kempf2005 (Circulation) is a single study from one lab using HeLa cells (cervical cancer line) and HUVEC (umbilical vein endothelium). It has never been independently replicated for Bartonella's effect on HIF-1α, and the cell types used (HeLa, HUVEC) differ substantially from brain microvascular endothelium (BBB) or glial cells — the cells most relevant to ME/CFS. Since 2005, multiple intracellular bacteria have been shown to manipulate HIF-1α (Chlamydia, Mycobacterium, Salmonella), so the general mechanism is plausible, but Bartonella-specific data is thin. Additionally, Kempf2005 measured HIF-1α stabilization at 2–4 hours after infection — an acute response that may not persist in chronic carriage. If Bartonella clear or become dormant, the HIF signal may disappear. The entire Bartonella→endothelial→HIF→glycolysis chain for ME/CFS therefore rests on a single, unreplicated, 20-year-old study using non-ME/CFS-relevant cell types.
- **What would disprove:** Independent replication of Kempf2005 in human brain microvascular endothelial cells (hBMECs) with chronic Bartonella infection (7–14 days, not acute 2–4h) shows no sustained HIF-1α stabilization.
- **Alternative explanation:** Bartonella's HIF-1α effect is an acute host-defense response (HIF-1α activates antimicrobial pathways including NO and cathelicidin), not a sustained metabolic reprogramming. The host eventually clears or controls Bartonella, and HIF-1α normalizes. The glycolytic shift never becomes chronic because the immune system "wins" against Bartonella in immunocompetent hosts.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The idea that cat-scratch fever bacteria trigger a long-lasting oxygen-starvation signal in blood vessels is based on a single lab experiment from 2005 using cervical cancer cells — far too thin to rely on for understanding ME/CFS.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 11: Null Hypothesis Assessment

### Idea 11.1 — Null: Tick-Borne Glycolytic Reprogramming Does Not Differ from Other Infectious Triggers in ME/CFS (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.55 (probability null is true given current evidence)
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Under the null, all post-infectious ME/CFS converges on a common immunometabolic endpoint — failed CD8+ Warburg (Mandarano2020), cell-danger-response glycolysis (Naviaux2016), WASF3-driven OXPHOS disruption (Gorman2024), PDK upregulation (Fluge2017) — regardless of infectious trigger. Tick-borne pathogens are merely ONE trigger among many (EBV, SARS-CoV-2, influenza, enterovirus, Q fever, etc.) and leave no qualitatively distinct glycolytic imprint. The Barriales2021 trained-immunity effect in mouse macrophages is a transient (7-day) phenomenon that does not persist as a chronic metabolic human memory. The Dong2026/Kerstholt2022 direction-of-effect contradiction indicates that the metabolic effect of Borrelia on host cells is inconsistent and context-dependent — not a robust, reproducible, translatable mechanism. Bartonella and Babesia evidence is too thin to support any conclusion (Idea 10.5). The Fitzgerald2021 null metabolomics finding (no serum glycolysis in PTLDS) suggests glycolysis is either absent or quantitatively minimal.
- **What paper claims would need revision if null is true:**
  - `@hyp:tickborne-glycolytic-reprogramming` (ch07, cert 0.40) → downgrade to ≤0.15, remove speculation that tick-borne reprogramming differs from viral
  - `@spec:tickborne-glial-reprogramming` (ch08, cert 0.30) → downgrade significantly — if peripheral monocytes show no glycolytic trained immunity, there is even less reason to think glia do
  - `@spec:bartonella-endothelial-microclot` (ch07, cert 0.15) → would need removal or downgrade to <0.08
  - The universal-mechanisms chapter (ch17) claim that tick-borne pathogens impose a qualitatively distinct glycolytic reprogramming would need softening — tick-borne would be one trigger among many, mechanistically identical
- **Non-specialist consequence:** If the null is true, all the focus on tick infections having a unique "metabolic signature" is misplaced — a tick infection that triggers ME/CFS damages energy metabolism through the SAME final pathway as a virus, and the clinical implications are identical regardless of trigger.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 11.2 — Null: Direction-of-Effect Contradiction Prevents Any Coherent Mechanism (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.45 (probability null is true)
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The contradiction between Dong2026 (Borrelia upregulates glycolysis, LDHA, lactate in THP-1) and Kerstholt2022 (Borrelia decreases baseline glycolysis in primary monocytes) may not be resolvable as a biphasic model or a cell-type artifact. It may indicate that Borrelia's effect on host metabolism is fundamentally STOCHASTIC (depending on uncontrolled variables: Borrelia strain, MOI, passage history, donor genetics, serum batch, monocyte activation state) — and therefore NO coherent unidirectional mechanism exists. If two careful labs using similar Borrelia strains and standard protocols produce opposite results, the phenomenon is not robust enough to build a hypothesis upon. The ME/CFS relevance then depends entirely on which unmeasured variable determines whether Borrelia up- or down-regulates host glycolysis in any given patient — an untestable proposition.
- **What existing paper claims would need revision:**
  - `@hyp:tickborne-glycolytic-reprogramming` (ch07, cert 0.40) → would need recognition that the foundational directional claim (Borrelia ↑ glycolysis in host cells) is contradicted by equally strong human primary cell data
  - The tissue-wide trained-immunity claim (Barriales2021, mouse) and the THP-1 data (Dong2026, cell line) both rest on UPREGULATION — if primary human monocyte data shows DOWNREGULATION, the human-relevant evidence does not support the mouse/cell-line narrative
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The basic science is genuinely contradictory — one lab finds Borrelia revs up sugar burning, another finds it slows down. When the experts disagree on such a fundamental point, it's premature to build patient treatments on either finding.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 11.3 — Null: Bartonella and Babesia Evidence Is Too Thin to Support Any Claim — Only Borrelia Has Enough Data, and Even That Has the Kerstholt Contradiction (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.60 (probability null is true for at least Bartonella and Babesia)
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Of the three pathogens, Borrelia has the most evidence (4 papers: Dong2026, Barriales2021, Kerstholt2022, Oosting2016 — despite the direction-of-effect problem). For Bartonella: ONE study (Kempf2005), one lab, 20 years old, cancer cell lines. For Babesia: ONE small mouse study (Ohmori2004), never replicated, no mechanism identified. If the Bartonella and Babesia evidence were removed, the entire "three-pathogen" narrative collapses to "Borrelia does something to host metabolism, we're not sure what direction." The three-pathogen framing gives an impression of convergent evidence that is misleading — the evidence bases are extremely imbalanced in quality and quantity. A unified hypothesis cannot rest on two single-study pathogens.
- **What existing paper claims would need revision:**
  - `@hyp:tickborne-glycolytic-reprogramming` (ch07) groups all three pathogens together → would need to separate Borrelia (mixed data), Bartonella (single unreplicated study), Babesia (single small animal study) into distinct claims with separate certainties
  - `@spec:tickborne-glial-reprogramming` (ch08) similarly groups all three → would need to acknowledge only Borrelia has any evidence (and it's contradictory)
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The idea that "three different tick-borne microbes all cause glycolytic reprogramming" sounds compelling, but the evidence is lopsided — a reasonable amount for Lyme bacteria, a single 20-year-old study for Bartonella, and a single mouse study from 2004 for Babesia. It's more "one plausible mechanism plus two hopeful analogies" than three convergent lines of evidence.

## Category 12: Evidence Quality Concerns

### Idea 12.1 — Barriales2021: Rigorous Mouse Study but Incomplete for Human Chronic Illness (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Specific concerns:**
  1. **Mouse model, not human.** Barriales2021 uses C57BL/6 mouse macrophages. Mouse and human trained immunity differ: human trained immunity (BCG model) upregulates TNF-α/IL-6 via H3K4me3; mouse trained immunity involves different epigenetic marks and cytokines. Mouse-to-human translatability of Borrelia-trained immunity is untested.
  2. **7-day persistence, not chronic.** The study measured trained immunity 7 days after initial Borrelia stimulation. ME/CFS symptoms persist for YEARS. Whether trained-immunity glycolytic memory lasts >6 months in any species is unknown.
  3. **No attempt to model clearance.** Mice received Borrelia ± glycolysis inhibition. No group had infection cleared then cellular metabolism measured. The study does not distinguish between "trained immunity maintained by ongoing infection" vs "trained immunity persisting post-clearance."
  4. **Single Borrelia strain, single MOI.** The study used a single Borrelia burgdorferi sensu stricto strain (N40) at one MOI. Different strains trigger different immune responses. B. afzelii and B. garinii (European strains) may produce different metabolic effects.
  5. **No ME/CFS-relevant endpoint.** The study measured inflammation, spirochetal burden, and mitochondrial gene expression — not fatigue, cognitive function, PEM, or any ME/CFS-relevant clinical endpoint. The leap from "reduced inflammation in mouse heart" to "improved fatigue in human ME/CFS" is vast.
- **Impact:** Barriales2021 is the strongest paper in the base (PLoS Biol, well-designed, in vivo glycolysis inhibition). However, it is a MOUSE study of ACUTE-TO-SUBACUTE trained immunity, not a human study of CHRONIC metabolic memory. The study shows that glycolysis inhibition reduces inflammation and spirochetal burden in mice — it does NOT show that tick-borne ME/CFS patients have glycolytic trained-immune memory.
- **What would strengthen the case:** Replication of Barriales2021's trained-immunity metabolic signature in human monocyte-derived macrophages, with 30-day persistence measurement and post-antibiotic-clearance measurement.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The strongest study in this area — showing that Lyme bacteria "train" immune cells to be hyperactive — was done in mice, tracked for only 7 days, and measured inflammation in the heart, not fatigue. It's excellent science for what it is, but it is a long way from proving the same happens in humans with ME/CFS.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 12.2 — Dong2026: Preprint, THP-1 Cell Line Only, Not Primary Human Cells (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.50
- **Specific concerns:**
  1. **Preprint — not peer-reviewed.** Dong2026 has not undergone peer review. The quality of controls, reproducibility, and data interpretation have not been independently verified.
  2. **THP-1 cell line only.** THP-1 is an immortalized monocytic leukemia cell line with known metabolic differences from primary monocytes: THP-1 cells have a constitutive Warburg-like glycolysis (high baseline ECAR) that primary cells do not. They also have different TLR expression, cytokine responses, and epigenetic regulation. The finding that Borrelia upregulates glycolysis in THP-1 may reflect a THP-1-specific response that does not occur in primary cells — consistent with Kerstholt2022's contradictory finding in primary monocytes.
  3. **Single timepoint (24h).** The study measured metabolomics at 24h post-infection. Earlier and later timepoints may show different patterns. Direction-of-change can reverse between 6h and 72h (the biphasic model).
  4. **No functional Seahorse assay.** The study measured supernatant glucose/lactate and LDHA/TXN gene expression — indirect markers of glycolysis. The gold standard Seahorse ECAR/OCR assay was not performed. Supernatant glucose depletion could reflect increased GLUT expression, not increased glycolytic flux per se.
  5. **Single Borrelia strain, no dose-response.** One strain, one MOI — unknown whether the effect is dose-dependent or strain-specific.
- **Impact:** The Borrelia-upregulates-glycolysis narrative relies heavily on this preprint because it's the only human-cell study showing glycolytic activation. If this study is unreliable (preprint, cell-line artifact), the Barriales2021 mouse study is the only strong evidence for ↑ glycolysis, and the human primary cell evidence (Kerstholt2022) points in the OPPOSITE direction.
- **What would strengthen the case:** Peer-reviewed publication, replication in primary human monocytes with Seahorse assay, multi-timepoint measurement, independent lab replication.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The lab study that found Lyme bacteria rev up sugar burning in immune cells has not yet been through peer review, used an artificial lab cell line (not fresh human cells), and hasn't been replicated — the opposite findings from a different study using real human cells may be more trustworthy.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 12.3 — Kempf2005: High-Impact Journal but 20 Years Old, Unreplicated, Non-ME/CFS Cell Types (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.70
- **Specific concerns:**
  1. **Age and obsolescence.** Published in 2005 — 21 years ago. The HIF field has advanced substantially. Methods, reagents, and standards have changed. The finding has not been independently replicated for Bartonella specifically (though HIF-1α manipulation by other intracellular bacteria is now well-established).
  2. **HeLa cell line.** The primary HIF-1α stabilization experiment was in HeLa (cervical cancer) cells — not endothelial cells, not microvascular cells, not brain endothelium, not glial cells. HeLa have p53 inactivation, HPV E6/E7 expression, and abnormal metabolism that may influence HIF responses. The secondary HUVEC experiment partially addresses this but HUVEC are large-vessel endothelium, not the microvascular brain endothelium relevant to ME/CFS.
  3. **Acute timepoint only (2–4 hours).** HIF-1α stabilization was measured at 2–4h after Bartonella infection. Whether HIF-1α remains stabilized at 24h, 72h, or 7 days (chronic infection timeframe) is unknown. In many bacterial infections, HIF-1α stabilization is a transient host defense response that normalizes.
  4. **Single Bartonella species, single strain.** B. henselae Houston-1. Other Bartonella species (B. quintana, B. bacilliformis) may differ in HIF effects. B. henselae from cat-scratch disease may differ from tick-borne B. henselae subtypes.
  5. **No downstream metabolism assay.** The study measured HIF-1α stabilization, VEGF, and ATP — but did NOT measure glycolysis directly (ECAR, lactate, glucose consumption, LDHA). The link from HIF-1α to glycolytic reprogramming is inferred from HIF biology, not measured.
- **Impact:** The Bartonella → HIF → glycolysis → ME/CFS chain rests entirely on a 20-year-old study that did not actually measure glycolysis, was done in cancer cells, and has never been replicated. This is the weakest evidence in the three-pathogen chain.
- **What would strengthen the case:** Replication in human brain microvascular endothelial cells (hBMECs) at acute and chronic timepoints, with direct glycolysis measurement (Seahorse ECAR, lactate, LDHA).
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The key evidence that cat-scratch bacteria trigger a cellular oxygen-starvation signal comes from a single study that is over 20 years old, used cervical cancer cells, and never actually measured whether the cells' sugar-burning actually increased — the connection to glycolysis is assumed, not proven.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 12.4 — Ohmori2004: Single Small Animal Study, Never Replicated, Babesia Species May Not Apply to Human Babesia (rank: 5)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Specific concerns:**
  1. **Babesia rodhaini, not Babesia microti.** The study used B. rodhaini, a rodent Babesia species that is more virulent than the human pathogen B. microti. Glucose uptake may differ by Babesia species.
  2. **Mouse model.** Murine RBC glucose metabolism differs from human (glucose transport kinetics, hexokinase isoforms). Whether the finding translates to human Babesia infection is unknown.
  3. **Small sample.** The study does not clearly state n per group. Key figure (glucose uptake increase) appears to show individual data points from ~4–6 mice per group — small N.
  4. **In vitro RBC assay.** RBCs were isolated from infected mice and glucose uptake measured ex vivo. The in vivo relevance (in whole blood with immune cells, variable glucose, insulin, etc.) is unclear.
  5. **Never replicated.** No follow-up study in any species since 2004. The Babesia glucose field moved elsewhere (and never followed up on this finding).
  6. **No mechanism.** The study reports increased de-novo glucose uptake but does not identify the mechanism (glucose transporter upregulation? hexokinase activation? increased glycolysis?).
- **Impact:** Ohmori2004 is the weakest paper in the base. The entire Babesia-glucolysis contribution to the three-pathogen narrative rests on this single, never-replicated, species-mismatched, small-N, mechanism-free mouse study. Without it, Babesia has no glycolytic reprogramming evidence at all — only the hemolysis/haptoglobin pathway (@spec:babesia-hemolysis-haptoglobin), which is a different mechanism.
- **What would strengthen the case:** Replication in human Babesia microti-infected RBCs (from infected patients or in vitro culture) with measured glucose consumption and lactate production.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The evidence that Babesia-parasitized red blood cells consume more sugar comes from a single 2004 study on a mouse-specific parasite — not the species that infects humans, and never confirmed by any subsequent study in 22 years.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 12.5 — Fitzgerald2021PTLDSMetabolic: Well-Controlled Human Study — the Closest Thing to a Direct Test — and It Found No Glycolysis Signature (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.65
- **Specific concerns:**
  1. **Serum only, not tissue/compartment.** The study measured serum metabolomics, which may miss cell-type-restricted glycolysis changes (monocyte intracellular lactate, CSF lactate, tissue pyruvate). The authors explicitly acknowledge this limitation.
  2. **Single timepoint, not post-exercise.** Metabolites were measured at rest. If trained-immunity glycolysis is activatable but not constitutive (requires second hit, e.g., exercise), a resting serum snapshot would miss it.
  3. **Mixed clinical population.** PTLDS is defined by symptom persistence after Lyme treatment, but etiologic heterogeneity is likely: some patients may have ongoing Borrelia (possibly treatment-refractory), some may have cleared but have post-infectious sequelae, some may have co-infections (Babesia, Bartonella) driving symptoms. The null finding may reflect dilution of a small glycolysis-positive subgroup within a larger glycolysis-negative PTLDS population.
  4. **Moderate sample size.** n=31 PTLDS, n=20 controls. Sufficient for global metabolomics, but limited for subgroup analysis (e.g., comparing glycolysis in fatigue-predominant vs musculoskeletal-predominant PTLDS).
  5. **Not a direct test of the trained-immunity hypothesis.** The study was designed to discover PTLDS metabolomic signatures, not to test the Barriales2021 trained-immunity model. The metabolomics panel may not include the most relevant markers (ECAR, monocyte lactate output, NAD+/NADH ratio).
- **Impact:** Fitzgerald2021 is the highest-quality human study most directly relevant to the tick-borne-glycolysis hypothesis. Its null finding for serum glycolysis is the strongest counterevidence available. The cell-type-restriction and rest-only counterarguments are valid but are post-hoc explanations of a negative result — which is always possible with sufficient post-hoc reasoning.
- **What would strengthen the case for glycolysis:** Demonstration that PTLDS monocytes (not serum) show elevated ECAR/OCR, and that post-exercise serum lactate or post-exercise PMBC lactate output is elevated in a PTLDS subset.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The most rigorous study of metabolic abnormalities in people with persistent Lyme symptoms — involving 31 patients and sophisticated analysis — found ZERO evidence of abnormal glycolysis. The study points instead toward fat-processing and gut-liver issues, which looks like a completely different metabolic problem.
- **origin:** brainstorm
