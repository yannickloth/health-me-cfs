# Brainstorm: VNS Fibre Selectivity and ME/CFS
**Date:** 2026-04-10  
**Source paper:** Bu et al. 2026, "A Review of Vagus Nerve Stimulation for Disease," *Comprehensive Physiology* 16(2):e70109.  
**Core insight:** The vagus is a multi-fascicle mixed nerve. Fibre recruitment follows size-dependent thresholds: A-fibres first, then B-fibres (preganglionic autonomic), then C-fibres (unmyelinated visceral/inflammatory). C-fibres carry the inflammatory signals most relevant to ME/CFS but require the highest stimulus intensity. Most clinical VNS devices operate in the A/B recruitment range.

---

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1. The "Wrong Fibres" Hypothesis: VNS Non-Response in ME/CFS Is a Fibre-Selectivity Artifact (Tier 1 — integrate soon)
**Mechanistic rationale:** ME/CFS VNS literature shows highly variable outcomes. The fibre-selectivity framework offers a parsimonious explanation: responders may be those whose primary pathology is B-fibre-mediated (autonomic dysregulation), while non-responders may be those whose pathology is primarily C-fibre-mediated (inflammatory signaling). At standard clinical parameters, devices engage A and B fibres effectively but C-fibres poorly. What appears as "VNS non-response" may actually be "wrong fibre class engagement."  
**Existing ME/CFS links:** ch18 already notes that VNS anti-inflammatory effects are "likely B-fibre-mediated cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway, though fibre classes engaged at studied doses are not characterised." The cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (alpha-7 nAChR on macrophages) is classically attributed to vagal efferent B-fibres. C-fibre afferents carry the sensory arm of inflammatory reflexes — without adequate C-fibre engagement, the brain may never receive the "inflammation detected" signal that triggers the efferent anti-inflammatory reflex arc.  
**Certainty: 0.50.** Biophysics well-established; ME/CFS mapping is a mechanistically grounded extrapolation.  
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a VNS trial with concurrent CNAP monitoring, C-fibre-confirmed engagement will predict anti-inflammatory response (IL-6, TNF-alpha reduction) while A/B-only engagement will predict autonomic but not inflammatory improvement.

---

### H2. C-Fibre Afferent Deafferentation: Chronic Vagal C-Fibre Underactivation Maintains the Neuroinflammatory Set-Point (Tier 1)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Vagal C-fibre afferents communicate peripheral inflammatory status to the brainstem (NTS). In ME/CFS, small fibre neuropathy (documented in 40–60%) may specifically impair C-fibre afferent signaling, creating two consequences: (1) the efferent anti-inflammatory reflex is inadequately triggered because the brainstem doesn't "know" the periphery is inflamed; (2) sickness behavior persists without resolution because the sensory arm (C-fibre afferents) cannot relay the "all clear." The vicious cycle: peripheral inflammation damages C-fibres → C-fibre damage reduces afferent signaling → reduced afferent signaling impairs the anti-inflammatory reflex → impaired reflex perpetuates inflammation.  
**Existing ME/CFS links:** SFN is documented. The gut-vagal pathway (butyrate → enterochromaffin serotonin → vagal afferents in ch07/ch11) specifically engages C-fibres — butyrate deficiency therefore reduces C-fibre input specifically.  
**Certainty: 0.40.**  
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with SFN (reduced IENFD) will show lower vagal C-fibre conduction, higher peripheral inflammatory markers, and poorer response to standard-parameter VNS vs. ME/CFS patients without SFN.

---

### H3. Fibre-Selective VNS Response as ME/CFS Subtype Discriminator (Tier 2)
**Certainty: 0.30.** A graded-intensity VNS protocol measuring physiological endpoints at each fibre-class threshold could generate a "fibre-response profile" for each patient, discriminating autonomic-predominant from inflammatory-predominant subtypes.  
**Falsifiable prediction:** A stepped-intensity VNS protocol will produce at least two statistically distinguishable response clusters in an ME/CFS cohort (n ≥ 40), correlating with independent clinical phenotyping.

---

### H4. Vagal C-Fibre Afferents as the Missing Link in Post-Exertional Malaise Signaling (Tier 1)
**Mechanistic rationale:** During and after exertion, inflammatory metabolites are produced. The vagus carries C-fibre visceral afferents from gut, liver, and spleen. After exertion in ME/CFS, the inflammatory burst may be detected by vagal C-fibre afferents from the gut (exercise redistributes blood flow, causing mucosal ischemia and local inflammation). If this C-fibre afferent volley to the NTS is disproportionately large (primed periphery) or miscalibrated, the result could be an exaggerated sickness behavior response — i.e., PEM. Partially denervated C-fibres (SFN) may exhibit compensatory hypersensitivity, amplifying remaining signals.  
**Certainty: 0.35.**  
**Falsifiable prediction:** Blocking vagal afferent C-fibre signaling (peripheral capsaicin to auricular branch) before submaximal exercise challenge will reduce PEM severity over 72h post-challenge, while not reducing muscle soreness (somatic afferent mediated).

---

### H5. Mast Cell Degranulation as a C-Fibre Activation Trigger: The VNS-MCAS Paradox (Tier 2)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Vagal C-fibres and mast cells communicate bidirectionally (substance P/CGRP from C-fibres → mast cell degranulation; histamine/tryptase → C-fibre activation). VNS reaching C-fibre thresholds could paradoxically trigger mast cell degranulation via antidromic C-fibre activation. This may explain VNS-induced crashes in ME/CFS patients with MCAS features.  
**Certainty: 0.35.**  
**Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-treatment with a mast cell stabilizer before VNS will reduce crash incidence in MCAS-positive ME/CFS patients but not MCAS-negative patients. Serum tryptase will rise post-VNS in MCAS patients (without stabilizer) but not with pre-treatment.

## 2. Research Directions

### R1. Fibre-Class Resolved VNS Parameter Optimization for ME/CFS (Tier 1)
A systematic dose-finding study using CNAP monitoring or evoked vagal brainstem responses to establish parameter sets (current, pulse width, frequency, duty cycle) that engage each fibre class. Test against ME/CFS-relevant endpoints: A-dominant → laryngeal EMG; B-dominant → HRV; C-dominant → inflammatory cytokines. Would allow retroactive reinterpretation of all prior VNS trials.  
**Certainty that informative: 0.80.**

### R2. SFN-Stratified VNS Trial (Tier 1)
ME/CFS patients with/without SFN (skin biopsy IENFD) receive standardized VNS. Primary outcome: inflammatory marker response. Hypothesis: SFN-positive patients show blunted inflammatory response but preserved autonomic response (B-fibres are myelinated and spared in SFN).

### R3. Auricular Branch Fibre Composition Mapping (Tier 2)
The auricular branch of the vagus (target of taVNS) may have low C-fibre density — if so, taVNS is inherently poor at C-fibre engagement regardless of parameters. High-resolution EM fibre counts of ABVN in cadaveric specimens needed.

### R4. Exercise Challenge + VNS Timing Study (Tier 2)
Test whether VNS in the 0–4 hour post-exercise window modifies PEM severity vs. sham. The direction of effect (therapeutic vs. harmful) is itself informative about C-fibre involvement in PEM.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### D1. Alpha-7 nAChR Agonists as "Pharmacological VNS" Bypassing Fibre Selectivity (Tier 1)
Candidates: **GTS-21 (DMXB-A):** selective alpha-7 partial agonist, reduced TNF-alpha/IL-6 in human endotoxemia, Phase II in schizophrenia, never tested in ME/CFS. **Galantamine:** AChE inhibitor + alpha-7 positive allosteric modulator; low side-effect profile; Alzheimer's use. Both would activate the downstream effector (macrophage alpha-7 nAChR) directly, bypassing the entire fibre-selectivity problem.  
**Certainty for rationale: 0.45.** Falsifiable prediction: alpha-7 agonist will reduce TNF-alpha/IL-6 in ME/CFS patients with elevated baseline inflammation, with effect size ≥ standard-parameter VNS, without hoarseness or bradycardia.

### D2. Capsaicin-Based Vagal C-Fibre Reset (Tier 2)
Initial C-fibre activation then desensitization via TRPV1 internalization. Very low-dose capsaicin might restore C-fibre sensitivity in SFN-mediated loss. A diagnostic capsaicin challenge (tragus application, monitoring HRV and cytokines) could distinguish hypersensitive vs. hyposensitive C-fibre states.  
**Certainty: 0.20.**

### D3. Pyridostigmine as VNS Potentiator (Tier 1)
Pyridostigmine amplifies the ACh signal at neuroeffector junctions downstream of whatever fibre class reaches the effector. Reframed: VNS (B-fibres at tolerable parameters) + pyridostigmine (amplifies downstream ACh) could achieve functional C-fibre-equivalent anti-inflammatory effects without C-fibre recruitment. Reframes pyridostigmine not as independent therapy but as VNS potentiator.  
**Certainty: 0.40.** Falsifiable prediction: VNS + pyridostigmine will produce greater IL-6/TNF-alpha reduction than either alone.

### D4. Selective 5-HT3 Agonists for Vagal Afferent C-Fibre Engagement (Tier 3)
Delayed-release ileal formulation to activate gut vagal C-fibre afferents without proximal nausea. Pharmacologically creative but multiple barriers.  
**Certainty: 0.15.**

## 4. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### N1. Gut Microbiome Restoration as Indirect Vagal C-Fibre Therapy (Tier 1)
The butyrate → enterochromaffin serotonin → vagal C-fibre afferent pathway already described in ch07/ch11/ch18 is now reframed as indirect C-fibre therapy. Butyrate-producing bacteria restoration (FMT, targeted probiotics, dietary fibre) activates vagal C-fibres at their natural chemosensory terminals — bypassing the fibre-selectivity problem of electrical VNS devices.  
**Certainty: 0.45.** Falsifiable prediction: successful microbiome restoration (increased fecal butyrate) will increase vagal afferent activity (HRV HF power or vagal evoked potentials), in addition to inflammatory and GI improvements.

### N2. Cold Water Face Immersion Reframed: B-Fibre-Dominant Stimulation (Tier 2)
The dive reflex is mediated by trigeminal afferents triggering vagal B-fibre efferent response (bradycardia, peripheral vasoconstriction). Cold face immersion is therefore B-fibre-dominant: good for autonomic metrics but may not engage C-fibre inflammatory pathways.  
**Certainty: 0.55.** Falsifiable prediction: cold face immersion will improve HRV in POTS-feature patients but NOT reduce IL-6/CRP in patients with elevated baseline inflammation.

### N3. Slow Diaphragmatic Breathing as C-Fibre-Inclusive Natural VNS (Tier 1)
Pulmonary C-fibres (J-receptors) are vagal afferents responsive to deep lung expansion. Slow, deep diaphragmatic breathing with extended exhalation engages A + B + possibly C-fibres, unlike rapid shallow breathing (A-dominant). The specific breathing pattern may therefore determine which vagal fibre classes are engaged.  
**Certainty: 0.35.** Falsifiable prediction: slow diaphragmatic breathing (6 breaths/min, 4:8 inhale:exhale) will produce HRV improvement AND inflammatory marker reduction in a single session; rapid breathing at the same minute ventilation will produce HRV changes only.

### N4. Targeted Probiotic for Vagal C-Fibre Activation (Tier 2)
A probiotic formulation combining high-butyrate producers (F. prausnitzii, R. intestinalis) optimized for ileal delivery (highest vagal innervation density) would be a rationally designed vagal C-fibre therapy. No such formulation exists.  
**Certainty: 0.30.**

## 5. Mathematical Model Extensions

### M1. Bayesian Fibre-Class Mixture Model for Reinterpreting VNS Trial Data (Tier 1)
Y = theta_A * beta_A + theta_B * beta_B + theta_C * beta_C + epsilon. theta values estimated from biophysical fibre recruitment models + Bu et al. anatomical data. beta values estimated across multiple VNS studies with different parameters. Prediction: beta_C >> beta_A, beta_B for inflammatory endpoints; beta_B >> for autonomic endpoints.  
**Certainty that model is identifiable: 0.55.**

### M2. Decompose Vagal Input in Integrated Systems Model (Tier 2)
V_efferent → V_A (somatic), V_B (autonomic), V_C (inflammatory). Anti-inflammatory equation: I_vagal = f(alpha * V_B + beta * V_C). Makes the model honest about what VNS actually does vs. what we assume.

### M3. Sensitivity Analysis: C-Fibre Engagement and Predicted VNS Efficacy (Tier 1)
Vary C-fibre engagement probability 0→1 in the existing model. If predicted efficacy drops dramatically when C-fibre engagement is set to zero, fibre selectivity is a critical uncertainty for the entire VNS treatment strategy.  
**Certainty: 0.65.**

## 6. Cross-Disease Bridges

### X1. POTS: Fibre-Selective VNS Explains Responder/Non-Responder Split (Tier 1)
Stavrakis data shows responders had low baseline vagal tone (HF < 200 ms²). Fibre-selectivity explanation: low-tone POTS patients have deficient B-fibre efferent output; taVNS at standard parameters engages B-fibres and supplements this deficit. Normal-tone POTS patients may have autoantibody-mediated pathology requiring C-fibre engagement instead.  
**Certainty: 0.45.**

### X2. MCAS: Vagal C-Fibre / Mast Cell Axis as Shared Mechanism (Tier 1)
Bidirectional substance P/histamine loop between C-fibres and mast cells as shared mechanism across ME/CFS, POTS, EDS, Long COVID with MCAS features. Net VNS effect (beneficial vs. harmful) depends on fibre-class selectivity of protocol.

### X3. Long COVID: SARS-CoV-2 Selective Vagal C-Fibre Damage (Tier 2)
SARS-CoV-2 neurotropism (nodose ganglion ACE2 neurons). Selective C-fibre vulnerability (no myelin protection). Explains why Long COVID patients can have normal basic autonomic tests (A/B intact) while having profoundly impaired inflammatory regulation (C-fibre damaged).  
**Certainty: 0.30.**

### X4. Fibromyalgia vs. ME/CFS: C-Fibre Sensitization vs. Deafferentation (Tier 3)
FM: C-fibre hyperexcitability. ME/CFS: C-fibre hypoexcitability. VNS at C-fibre thresholds should produce opposite effects. Could serve as diagnostic discriminator.  
**Certainty: 0.25.**

## 7. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### B1. Vagal Evoked Potential Fibre-Class Decomposition as ME/CFS Biomarker (Tier 1)
Different VEP latencies correspond to different fibre classes (A: 5–15 ms; B: 15–50 ms; C: >50 ms). If ME/CFS involves selective C-fibre dysfunction, VEP should show preserved A/B components with absent/attenuated late C components. Measurable with standard EEG + auricular stimulator.  
**Certainty for feasibility: 0.40.** Falsifiable prediction: ME/CFS patients will show reduced amplitude/increased latency of late VEP components vs. controls, preserved early components; correlation with SFN severity, inflammatory markers, PEM severity.

### B2. Bradycardia Threshold as Bedside B-Fibre Function Test (Tier 2)
Incrementally increasing auricular stimulation intensity with heart rate monitoring. "Bradycardia threshold" = intensity at which HR drops >5 bpm. Abnormally high = B-fibre dysfunction or autoantibody blockade. 15-minute bedside test.

### B3. Pupillary Light Reflex as CN X Parasympathetic Proxy (Tier 3)
Indirect proxy; correlation between CN III and CN X parasympathetic function in ME/CFS not established.  
**Certainty: 0.20.**

### B4. VNS Challenge Test as Functional Anti-Inflammatory Biomarker (Tier 1)
Standardized VNS stimulus → measure cytokine response 2–4h later. Analogous to glucose tolerance test: measures dynamic capacity of vagal anti-inflammatory regulation. Hypothesis: cytokine suppression ratio will discriminate ME/CFS from controls (AUC > 0.70) and SFN-positive from SFN-negative ME/CFS.  
**Certainty for concept: 0.45.**
