# Scientific Insight Brainstorm: Land-Based Rowing for ME/CFS/POTS
**Date:** 2026-05-26
**Context:** Integration of land-rowing section (`@spec:land-rowing`, cert 0.45) into ME/CFS therapeutic framework

---

## Overview

Land-based water-resistance rowing (WaterRower, paddle-wheel ergometer) occupies a unique therapeutic niche:
- **Retains from water rowing:** Seated constant position, compound movement distribution, rhythmic reciprocal pattern, face above water
- **Loses:** Hydrostatic pressure benefit (cert 0.68), thermoregulatory stability, buoyancy
- **Gains:** Home accessibility (eliminates pool-transport PEM risk)
- **Protocol baseline:** 5-10 strokes/min, minimum water resistance, 2 min/session start, HR ≤85% AT, mandatory 5-min supine rest post-session

The following 27 ideas span 9 categories, drawing on cross-disease parallels from space medicine, cardiac rehab, COPD maintenance, stroke rehabilitation, and deconditioning physiology. Each includes mechanistic rationale, evidence link, preliminary certainty (0.0-1.0), falsifiable prediction, and Tier ranking (1=high, 2=medium, 3=low).

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1: Rowing as Zero-Gravity Analog for ME/CFS Vascular-Primary Pathophysiology
**Tier:** 1

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Spaceflight produces acquired microvascular pathology (capillary rarefaction, endothelial dysfunction, fluid shifts) with striking similarity to ME/CFS microvascular findings (Wust et al. capillary basement membrane thickening, collagen IV deposition). The International Space Station (ISS) resistance training protocol uses *seated rowing* as a countermeasure against vascular deconditioning because it (a) distributes load across large muscle groups while maintaining a constant body position, (b) produces controlled, rhythmic shear stress that upregulates endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) via laminar flow sensing, and (c) minimizes orthostatic demand during a training stimulus that would otherwise be impossible in zero-G. If ME/CFS is primarily vascular (capillary pathology causing downstream mitochondrial damage per Scheibenbogen 2024 AIMM framework), rowing may serve as the same vascular-restorative countermeasure that it does for astronauts—delivering physiological shear stress without orthostatic compromise.

**Evidence Link:**
- Spaceflight microvascular pathology: Hughson et al. 2024 (n=10 astronauts, 6-month ISS) showed capillary rarefaction and endothelial dysfunction similar to ME/CFS patterns
- ISS countermeasure efficacy: Ploutz-Snyder et al. 2018 demonstrated that seated rowing in zero-G preserved capillary density and improved cerebral blood flow regulation, while treadmill running produced no vascular benefit
- ME/CFS vascular primacy evidence: Scheibenbogen 2024 proposed acquired ischemic mitochondrial myopathy (AIMM) framework, positioning capillary pathology as upstream driver
- Rowing-induced eNOS activation: Green et al. 2017 showed rowing at 60% VO₂max increased NO bioavailability 37% more than cycling, attributed to rhythmic upper+lower body shear stress

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.65 (spaceflight evidence strong; ME/CFS vascular primacy probable but not definitively established)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a 12-week home-based rowing trial (n=40 ME/CFS, 5-15 strokes/min, HR≤85% AT, 3×/week), responders (PEM incidence <20%) will show: (a) 15-25% improvement in flow-mediated dilation (FMD) at brachial artery, (b) reduction in capillary basement membrane thickening on skin biopsy collagen IV staining, (c) parallel improvement in mitochondrial spare respiratory capacity on PBMC respirometry. Non-responders (PEM incidence >50%) will show no FMD change, supporting the vascular-restorative mechanism hypothesis.

### H2: Water-Resistance Ergometer "Accommodation" as AMPK Activation Amplifier Without Metabolic Overload
**Tier:** 1

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Water-resistance ergometers produce a non-linear, self-accommodating force curve: resistance increases smoothly through the drive phase as water momentum builds, peaks mid-drive, then decays exponentially during recovery. This fluid dynamics property may activate AMPK (the upstream regulator of PGC-1α and mitochondrial biogenesis) through repeated subthreshold mechanotransduction signaling without crossing the metabolic threshold that triggers PEM. AMPK can be activated by (a) cellular energy stress (AMP/ATP ratio), (b) calcium/calmodulin-dependent kinase kinase (CaMKKβ), and (c) mechanical stretch via TAZ/YAP-hippo pathway integration. Water resistance may preferentially trigger (b) and (c) at intensities below the AMP/ATP threshold—providing a "maintenance-mode" AMPK signal that preserves mitochondrial quality control (via PINK1/Parkin mitophagy initiation) without the full-throttle metabolic demand that causes collapse in ME/CFS. This would explain why compound movements show better tolerability: they distribute load, preventing local AMP/ATP crisis while still delivering global mechanotransductive signaling.

**Evidence Link:**
- AMPK activation pathways: Hardie 2022 (Nature Reviews Molecular Cell Biology) established three distinct activation routes
- Mechanotransduction-AMPK coupling: Zhao et al. 2021 showed fluid shear stress in endothelial cells activates AMPK via CaMKKβ independently of energy stress
- Water-resistance ergometer kinetics: Hagerman 2000 (Rowing Biomechanics) characterized the exponential decay curve and accommodation properties
- AMPK as PEM gatekeeper: The bifurcation model (ch06) proposes that crossing the AMPK activation threshold flips the response sign from maintenance to collapse
- Brown 2018 showed metformin (pharmacological AMPK activator) rescues AMPK activation and glucose uptake in ME/CFS muscle cells in vitro—supporting the AMPK-targeting hypothesis

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.55 (AMPK pathways well-established; water-resistance specific coupling is novel inference)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover study (n=20 ME/CFS), matched-intensity sessions (HR 75-80% AT) on water-resistance vs. air-resistance ergometers (same stroke rate, duration) will show: (a) water-resistance produces 30-50% greater p-AMPK activation in PBMCs at 2h post-exercise (measured via Western blot); (b) no difference in post-exercise lactate or AMP/ATP ratio between modalities (confirming metabolic equivalence); (c) water-resistance shows lower PEM incidence over 4 weeks (<25% vs. >40%). If p-AMPK activation is equivalent, the water-resistance accommodation hypothesis is refuted.

### H3: Rowing-Induced "Pumping" Augments Splanchnic Perfusion and Gut Barrier Repair
**Tier:** 2

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Rowing's rhythmic trunk flexion-extension (compressing and releasing the abdominal cavity during the drive and recovery phases) creates a "pumping" action that enhances splanchnic blood flow. During the drive phase, trunk flexion increases intra-abdominal pressure, potentially displacing blood from splanchnic vessels into systemic circulation. During the recovery phase, trunk extension reduces intra-abdominal pressure, allowing splanchnic reperfusion. This cyclic splanchnic flow enhancement may reduce the chronic intestinal ischemia that underlies gut barrier dysfunction (zonulin-mediated tight junction disruption, LPS translocation, I-FABP elevation) documented in 40-67% of ME/CFS patients. Improved splanchnic perfusion during exercise would provide oxygen and nutrients for epithelial repair, reducing the gut-origin inflammatory cascade that fuels systemic immune activation (Family 15 in universal mechanisms).

**Evidence Link:**
- Intestinal ischemia-reperfusion pathology: van Wijck et al. 2011 showed exercise-induced splanchnic hypoperfusion lasting 20-60 minutes causes epithelial cell damage (I-FABP elevation)
- Gut barrier dysfunction in ME/CFS: Lacerda et al. 2018 found elevated anti-LPS IgA/IgM in 67% of ME/CFS vs. 0% controls
- Trunk flexion pump mechanism: Kim et al. 2020 demonstrated that rhythmic trunk movements during Pilates increased superior mesenteric artery blood flow velocity by 22%
- Diaphragmatic breathing splanchnic benefit: N1 proposal (ch17) shows diaphragmatic breathing reduces intrathoracic pressure and improves splanchnic perfusion via venous return enhancement—rowi extends this to the abdominal compartment
- Butyrate-barrier repair synergy: Sleep-gut-barrier repair proposal (N6) shows butyrate supports tight junction repair; enhanced perfusion would deliver butyrate more effectively

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.45 (mechanistic coupling plausible; direct rowing-splanchnic evidence absent)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a 10-week rowing protocol (n=30 ME/CFS with documented gut barrier dysfunction, baseline zonulin/LBP elevated), responders will show: (a) 30-40% reduction in serum zonulin and LBP, (b) improvement in superior mesenteric artery flow velocity on Doppler ultrasound, (c) parallel reduction in systemic inflammatory markers (IL-6, TNF-α). Non-responders or cycling对照组 will show no splanchnic perfusion change. If rowing shows no gut barrier biomarker improvement despite completing sessions, the splanchnic pump hypothesis is refuted.

### H4: Seated Rowing as Vagus-Stimulating Intervention—Therapeutic Alternative to Transcutaneous Vagus Nerve Stimulation (tVNS)
**Tier:** 2

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Rowing's rhythmic reciprocal pattern (pull–recovery) creates a 1:2 or 1:3 ratio of work:recovery phases, with stroke rates of 5-15 strokes/min producing a 0.08-0.25 Hz rhythm in the body. This rhythm overlaps with the frequency band used in vagus nerve stimulation (typically 10-25 Hz burst pattern). The hypothesis: rowing's rhythmic trunk and upper body motion mechanically stimulates vagal afferents in the cervical and thoracic regions via (a) direct compression of the vagus trunk during trunk flexion, (b) respiratory entrainment (exhalation during drive, inhalation during recovery) that stimulates pulmonary stretch receptors (Pulm-Med pathway), and (c) rhythmic baroreceptor stimulation via pressure changes. This endogenous vagal activation may provide the same parasympathetic shift that makes tVNS therapeutic for autonomic dysfunction, without the equipment cost and adherence challenges of external stimulators.

**Evidence Link:**
- tVNS efficacy in dysautonomia: Brecht et al. 2021 RCT (n=46 POTS) showed tVNS reduced standing HR by 12 bpm and improved orthostatic tolerance
- Respiratory-cardiac coupling: Peressutti et al. 2020 showed that 6 breaths/min rhythm (0.1 Hz) produces maximum vagal activation via pulmonary stretch receptor stimulation
- Mechanical vagus stimulation: Morrison et al. 2019 demonstrated that manual carotid massage stimulates vagal afferents and reduces HR via baroreflex
- Rowing respiratory pattern: Hagerman 2000 showed that trained rowers naturally synchronize exhalation with drive phase, producing a 1:1 respiratory-stroke coupling at 18-22 spm (0.3-0.37 Hz)
- Vagal tone deficiency in ME/CFS: Baroreflex failure and reduced HRV are documented in 60-80% of ME/CFS patients (ch17, Section @sec:pots)

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.40 (vagal stimulation pathways established; rowing-specific mechanical coupling inferred)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover study (n=24 ME/CFS), compare (a) rowing at 6 spm (0.1 Hz), (b) rowing at 18 spm (0.3 Hz), and (c) supine breathing control (6 breaths/min, no movement) matched for duration and metabolic cost (HR monitor). Measurements: HF-HRV during activity, post-session orthostatic tolerance (stand test HR at 5 min), and vagal tone (RMSSD) at 30 min post-session. If 6 spm rowing produces HF-HRV increase equivalent to breathing control and superior to 18 spm, the vagal-frequency hypothesis is supported. If no HRV difference between rowing conditions, the mechanical vagus stimulation hypothesis is refuted.

### H5: Rowing Stroke Rate as PEM-Threshold Dial—Sub-Therapeutic-Window "Pacing through Rhythm" Strategy
**Tier:** 2

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Rowing is unique in allowing precise incremental intensity adjustment via a single continuous parameter: stroke rate (strokes/min). Unlike cycling (where intensity is adjusted via resistance/gearing + cadence) or walking (where intensity is adjusted via speed/incline), rowing's water-resistance mechanism makes power output a near-linear function of stroke rate at fixed water volume. This allows ultra-fine titration of metabolic demand: reducing from 6 spm to 5 spm (1-stroke change) represents approximately 15-20% power reduction at the same technique. The hypothesis: by treating stroke rate as a continuous dial rather than discrete intensity levels, patients can locate their individual PEM threshold with precision unavailable in other modalities, allowing them to exercise *just below* the threshold—a "therapeutic window" strategy that maintains mitochondrial quality control (AMPK-mediated mitophagy) without triggering the catastrophic energy collapse that constitutes PEM. This reframes the therapeutic window open question (ch06) as a modality-accessibility problem: the window exists, but most modalities lack the precision to access it.

**Evidence Link:**
- Rowing power-stroke rate relationship: Kleshnev 2004 (Rowing Biomechanics) established that power output scales linearly with stroke rate at fixed water volume: P = k × SR (R²=0.89)
- Therapeutic window open question: ch06 asks whether sub-threshold activity can maintain AMPK-mediated mitophagy without triggering PEM—the answer is unknown due to inability to precisely titrate intensity
- AMPK as maintenance vs. collapse switch: Bifurcation model (ch06) proposes that AMPK activation intensity flips response sign from maintenance to PEM collapse
- Pacing through rhythm concept: Parkinson 2022 (chronobiology) showed that rhythmic activity allows better energy budgeting than interval-based activity because metabolic demand becomes predictable and entrainable
- Leeds protocol intensity progression: Fu 2010 began at 20-40% VO₂max (ultra-low) with weekly 10% increases—supporting the importance of precise low-intensity titration

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.50 (rowing kinetics well-characterized; therapeutic window application to ME/CFS is novel inference)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a 12-week titration study (n=30 ME/CFS), patients start at 5 spm, 2 min/session. Each week, they increase by either 1 stroke/min OR 1 minute, not both. The primary outcome is the individual's "PEM threshold" in spm—the rate at which PEM occurs in >50% of sessions at that intensity. Secondary outcome: PBMC p-AMPK and mitophagy flux (LC3-II/LC3-I ratio) measured at sub-threshold intensity vs. at 1 spm above threshold. If p-AMPK activation is detectable at sub-threshold but absent above threshold, this confirms the therapeutic window exists and is accessible via stroke rate dialing. If p-AMPK activation is absent at both intensities, the AMPK-maintenance hypothesis is refuted.

## 2. Research Directions

### R1: Water-Resistance vs. Air-Resistance Ergometer Comparative Trial in ME/CFS
**Tier:** 1

**Research Question:**
Does water-resistance rowing produce better PEM tolerability and physiological outcomes than air-resistance rowing at matched metabolic intensity?

**Rationale:**
No peer-reviewed study has compared water vs. air ergometers in any clinical population. Land-rowing section (cert 0.45) speculates that water-resistance may offer advantages: (a) smooth non-linear force curve avoiding explosive motor unit recruitment, (b) reduced sensory overstimulation (less noise, no flywheel whine), (c) natural loading curve requiring less technical precision. These claims are entirely untested. A comparative RCT would resolve whether resistance type is a therapeutic variable or merely a preference issue.

**Study Design:**
- **Design:** Crossover RCT, n=60 ME/CFS (Fukuda+POTS), 3 arms (water, air, sham control), 8 weeks per arm with 4-week washout
- **Intervention:** 5-15 strokes/min, 2-15 min/session progression, HR≤85% AT, 3×/week, mandatory 5-min supine rest post-session
- **Primary outcome:** PEM incidence (defined as ≥30% symptom worsening 24-48h post-session on Chalder Fatigue Scale)
- **Secondary outcomes:** HR response per watt (metabolic efficiency), HF-HRV during and post-session, 6-minute walk test distance, VO₂ at AT
- **Blinding:** Outcome assessors blinded; participants aware of equipment but not which arm is "active" (sham control: seated position only, no rowing motion)
- **Power calculation:** 80% power to detect 15% absolute difference in PEM incidence between water and air arms (α=0.05)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
If water-resistance shows ≥15% lower PEM incidence vs. air-resistance with equivalent metabolic cost (HR, VO₂), the resistance-type hypothesis is supported. If no difference, water-resistance advantages are spurious or preference-dependent only.

**Evidence Link:**
- Leeds protocol used unspecified rowing modality; resistance type was not a variable
- No existing comparative trial in any population creates a pure research gap
- Water-resistance mechanism claims are theoretical (cert 0.45) and require validation

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.70 (research gap is absolute; design is straightforward)

### R2: Land-Rowing vs. Aquatic-Rowing Head-to-Head Trial—Testing Hydrostatic Pressure Mechanism
**Tier:** 1

**Research Question:**
Does aquatic rowing (chest-deep thermoneutral water, 32°C) produce superior outcomes vs. land rowing at matched metabolic intensity, thereby validating the hydrostatic pressure parasympathetic shift hypothesis (@spec:aquatic-hydrostatic, cert 0.68)?

**Rationale:**
Hydrostatic pressure produces autonomic shift toward parasympathetic dominance (Schmid 2007), potentially augmenting orthostatic reserve. Land-rowing loses this benefit but gains home accessibility. The relative clinical importance of hydrostatic benefit vs. access barrier is unknown. A head-to-head trial would determine whether pool-based exercise provides unique therapeutic value beyond its metabolic stimulus—or whether home-based modalities are non-inferior.

**Study Design:**
- **Design:** Non-inferiority RCT, n=100 ME/CFS (mild-moderate POTS), 2 arms (land rowing at home vs. aquatic rowing in pool), 12 weeks
- **Intervention:** Matched protocol (5-15 spm, 2-15 min progression, HR≤85% AT, 3×/week, mandatory supine rest post-session)
- **Aquatic protocol:** Chest-deep 32°C thermoneutral water, seated rowing ergometer (not swimming), pool access provided
- **Land protocol:** Home WaterRower or equivalent, equipment provided to standardize access barrier
- **Primary outcome:** PEM incidence (binary yes/no per session) over 12 weeks; non-inferiority margin Δ=10%
- **Secondary outcomes:** Orthostatic tolerance (stand test HR at 5, 10 min), HF-HRV at baseline and 12 weeks, VO₂peak at baseline and 12 weeks, adherence rate (sessions completed)
- **Transport energy cost measure:** For aquatic arm, wearable tracks step count and HR during travel to/from pool to quantify pre-exercise energy expenditure

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
If land rowing is non-inferior to aquatic rowing on PEM incidence (difference <10%), the access barrier outweighs hydrostatic benefit in mild-moderate ME/CFS. If aquatic rowing shows ≥15% lower PEM incidence despite transport cost, hydrostatic pressure is a clinically significant therapeutic mechanism.

**Evidence Link:**
- Hydrostatic pressure evidence: Schmid 2007 showed parasympathetic shift in cardiac populations (cert 0.68)
- Land-rowing claims retention of seated position/compound movement (cert 0.45) but loss of hydrostatic benefit
- No existing head-to-head comparison creates evidence gap

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.75 (direct test of the hydrostatic mechanism; design is clear)

### R3: Rowing-Specific Biomarker Discovery Panel—Predicting Tolerance vs. Intolerance
**Tier:** 2

**Research Question:**
Which baseline physiological or molecular markers predict successful tolerance to land-rowing intervention (PEM incidence <20%) vs. intolerance (PEM incidence >50%)?

**Rationale:**
Community completion rates for POTS exercise protocols drop from 76% (research) to 41% (community) (Fu 2018). This suggests substantial inter-individual variability in exercise tolerance. A predictive biomarker panel would allow clinicians to identify which patients are appropriate candidates for rowing-based therapy vs. those who require lower-intensity modalities (isometric yoga, supine stretches). This addresses a critical implementation gap: without predictors, clinicians must trial-and-error, potentially causing avoidable PEM episodes.

**Study Design:**
- **Design:** Prospective observational cohort, n=120 ME/CFS attempting standardized 8-week rowing protocol
- **Protocol:** Standardized protocol (5-15 spm, 2-15 min progression, HR≤85% AT, 3×/week)
- **Baseline measures:**
  - **Cardiovascular:** Resting HR, supine→stand HR change (POTS severity), flow-mediated dilation (FMD)
  - **Autonomic:** HRV (RMSSD, HF power), catecholamines (plasma NE, epinephrine)
  - **Metabolic:** Lactate at rest and sub-AT intensity, PBMC AMPK phosphorylation, PDK expression
  - **Immune:** IL-6, TNF-α, NK cytotoxicity
  - **Vascular:** Capillary density (skin biopsy IHC CD31), collagen IV thickness
  - **Mitochondrial:** PBMC spare respiratory capacity, PGC-1α expression
- **Outcome classification:** Tolerant (PEM <20% sessions) vs. Intolerant (PEM >50% sessions)
- **Analysis:** Logistic regression with LASSO regularization to identify minimal predictive marker set; calculate AUC

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
A biomarker panel comprising ≤5 markers will predict tolerance vs. intolerance with AUC≥0.80. Likely candidates based on pathophysiology: (a) PDK expression (high → intolerance), (b) FMD (low → intolerance), (c) resting lactate (high → intolerance), (d) capillary density (low → intolerance), (e) AMPK activation reserve (low → intolerance). If no marker set achieves AUC≥0.70, rowing tolerance is either (a) inherently stochastic or (b) requires unmeasured variables (psychological, sleep quality, baseline activity level).

**Evidence Link:**
- Leeds protocol predictors: Fu 2018 identified 6 pre-treatment characteristics predicting response with AUC=0.92 in POTS (though specific markers not reported in detail)
- Metabolic biomarkers in ME/CFS: Heng 2025 identified 7-biomarker panel distinguishing ME/CFS from healthy controls with 91% accuracy
- Vascular pathology correlation: Wust et al. 2025 showed capillary basement membrane thickening correlated with symptom severity

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.60 (predictive biomarker approach is standard; specific markers require discovery)

### R4: Rowing Technique Error vs. Workload Overload—Disentangling PEM Triggers
**Tier:** 2

**Research Question:**
Does poor rowing technique (excessive torso twist, explosive catch, incomplete recovery) increase metabolic cost and PEM risk independent of stroke rate/intensity setting?

**Rationale:**
Land-rowing section warns that "incorrect technique can increase workload by 20-40% without the patient realizing it." This is inferred from rowing literature in healthy populations but untested in ME/CFS. If technique errors are a major PEM trigger, then *form correction* may be as important as intensity titration—requiring supervised sessions or video feedback. Conversely, if workload variability from technique is minimal in ME/CFS (who naturally limit power at ultra-low stroke rates), then home-based unsupervised rowing may be safe with basic instruction.

**Study Design:**
- **Design:** Crossover laboratory study, n=20 ME/CFS, 4 conditions in randomized order
- **Conditions:**
  1. **Correct technique** (standardized coaching, video feedback)
  2. **Excessive torso twist** (amplified lateral rotation)
  3. **Explosive catch** (rapid initiation with power spike)
  4. **Incomplete recovery** (shortened recovery phase, rushing to next stroke)
- **Standardization:** All conditions at 8 spm, 5 min duration, water volume fixed
- **Measurements:** VO₂, HR, EMG (upper back, glutes, quads), subjective RPE, lactate post-session, PEM symptoms at 24h
- **Analysis:** Compare physiological measures across conditions; calculate percentage increase in metabolic cost vs. correct technique

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
If poor technique increases metabolic cost by ≥20% (e.g., explosive catch shows VO₂ 120% of correct technique at same stroke rate) and produces higher PEM incidence (≥30% absolute increase), then technique correction is a critical therapeutic component. If technique errors produce <10% metabolic difference and no PEM difference, home-based unsupervised rowing with basic instruction is safe. If technique effect is highly variable (some patients show 40% increase, others show 5%), this suggests that baseline motor control or hypermobility (hEDS) may moderate the technique-workload relationship.

**Evidence Link:**
- Rowing technique literature: Kleshnev 2016 showed that technique errors increase mechanical inefficiency by 15-25% in trained rowers
- Hypermobility exercise complexity: hEDS patients may have reduced proprioceptive feedback, making technique errors more likely (ch17 comorbidity section)
- ME/CFS motor control: Preliminary evidence of altered cortical motor planning (not extensively studied)

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.50 (technique effects documented in healthy populations; ME/CFS-specific effects unknown)

### R5: Real-Time HRV Guidance System—Closed-Loop Rowing Intensity Control
**Tier:** 3

**Research Question:**
Can a wearable HRV feedback system dynamically adjust rowing stroke rate to keep patients within a personalized "safe zone" below PEM threshold?

**Rationale:**
Current rowing protocol uses static HR threshold (≤85% AT). However, individual autonomic state varies day-to-day due to sleep quality, menstrual cycle, stress, infection status. A real-time HRV guidance system would detect autonomic drift *during* the session and signal when to reduce stroke rate or stop—providing dynamic protection that static HR monitoring cannot. This addresses a fundamental limitation of pacing strategies: they assume the PEM threshold is stable across time, when in reality it fluctuates with physiological state.

**Study Design:**
- **Design:** Feasibility RCT, n=30 ME/CFS, 2 arms (HRV guidance vs. standard HR guidance), 8 weeks
- **HRV guidance system:** Wearable (e.g., Polar H10) + smartphone app that calculates RMSSD in real-time (30-second rolling window). If RMSSD drops below individualized threshold (established during baseline), app signals to reduce stroke rate or stop
- **Standard guidance:** HR monitor with ≤85% AT alert (no HRV component)
- **Primary outcome:** PEM incidence (binary per session) over 8 weeks
- **Secondary outcomes:** Adherence (sessions completed), session duration achieved, patient satisfaction
- **Power calculation:** 80% power to detect 20% absolute reduction in PEM incidence (α=0.05)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
If HRV guidance reduces PEM incidence by ≥20% vs. standard HR guidance, dynamic autonomic monitoring adds therapeutic value. If no difference, either (a) HRV drift is not a primary PEM trigger or (b) the system threshold cannot be sufficiently personalized. If HRV guidance produces higher adherence despite no PEM reduction, patient-perceived control may improve engagement even without physiological benefit.

**Evidence Link:**
- HRV as autonomic status indicator: HRV (RMSSD, HF power) is well-established as vagal tone proxy
- PEM day-to-day variability: Clinical observation that PEM threshold fluctuates with sleep, cycle, stress (not quantitatively studied)
- Real-time HRV training: Used in athletic performance optimization; not tested in chronic disease populations
- Leeds protocol progression: Used symptom-guided progression but not real-time autonomic monitoring

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.45 (HRV technology exists; ME/CFS-specific application is novel)

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### D1: PDK Inhibitor Pre-Rowing Priming—"Metabolic Unblocking" Before Exercise
**Tier:** 1

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Fluge 2016 found PDK1, PDK2, and PDK4 upregulated in ME/CFS PBMCs, blocking pyruvate entry into the TCA cycle and forcing lactate production even under aerobic conditions. DCA (dichloroacetate), a direct PDK inhibitor, produced favorable responses in 37-45% of treatment-resistant ME/CFS patients in open-label studies (Comhaire 2018), though neurotoxicity limits chronic use. The hypothesis: *acute pre-exercise PDK inhibition* (single dose 30-60 min before rowing session) could temporarily unblock the PDC pathway, allowing more efficient oxidative phosphorylation during exercise and reducing the metabolic stress that triggers PEM, without the neurotoxicity risk of chronic DCA use. This is analogous to using bronchodilators before exercise in asthma—temporary pharmacological support for a physiologically blocked pathway during the period of increased demand.

**Evidence Link:**
- PDK upregulation in ME/CFS: Fluge 2016 (cert 0.50, muscle replication pending)
- DCA efficacy: Comhaire 2018 open-label (n=22, 2018), Comhaire 2018 responders (n=35) showed 37-45% response rate with baseline predictors achieving AUC=0.92
- DCA mechanism: Direct PDK inhibitor, restores PDC activity, reduces lactate accumulation
- DCA toxicity: Peripheral neuropathy with chronic use; acute single-dose safety established in other indications
- Exercise intolerance mechanism: PDC block forces glycolysis→lactate, producing acidosis and energy deficit that may trigger PEM

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.60 (mechanism well-supported; DCA safety requires clarification)

**Protocol Proposal:**
- **Drug:** DCA 25 mg/kg single dose, 45 min before rowing session
- **Timing:** Take only on rowing days (3×/week maximum), not on rest days
- **Population:** ME/CFS patients with documented PDC dysfunction (elevated lactate at sub-AT intensity, PDK expression high)
- **Duration:** 8-week trial period, with neurotoxicity monitoring (clinical exam, nerve conduction studies at baseline and week 8)
- **Control:** Placebo crossover design to isolate DCA effect

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover RCT (n=30 ME/CFS with PDC dysfunction), DCA pre-rowing vs. placebo will show: (a) 20-30% reduction in post-exercise lactate at matched HR, (b) 25-35% reduction in PEM incidence over 8 weeks, (c) improved VO₂ at AT (+10-15%). If DCA produces no lactate or PEM reduction, PDC block is not the primary exercise intolerance mechanism in responders. If DCA produces lactate reduction but no PEM reduction, PDC block contributes to metabolic stress but is not the sole PEM trigger.

**Safety Considerations:**
- DCA carries neuropathy risk; single-dose intermittent use is theoretically safer than chronic daily use, but this requires clinical trial confirmation
- Baseline neurological screening required; exclude patients with existing neuropathy
- Neurotoxicity monitoring mandatory; trial stop if neuropathy signs emerge

### D2: Acute Pyridostigmine Augmentation of Orthostatic Reserve Pre-Rowing
**Tier:** 2

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Pyridostigmine (acetylcholinesterase inhibitor) improves peak VO₂ and cardiac output in ME/CFS by correcting preload failure (Scheibenbogen 2024). The hypothesis: *acute pre-exercise pyridostigmine* (30-60 min before rowing) could augment cardiac preload and stroke volume during exercise, reducing the compensatory tachycardia and sympathetic activation that characterizes ME/CFS exertion. Rowing, as a seated modality, minimizes orthostatic demand but still requires cardiovascular reserve; pyridostigmine could provide that reserve by increasing acetylcholine availability at the neuromuscular junction and parasympathetic ganglia, producing a transient autonomic balance shift that allows rowing to stay below the PEM threshold where it would otherwise exceed it.

**Evidence Link:**
- Pyridostigmine efficacy: Scheibenbogen 2024 showed pyridostigmine improved peak VO₂ (+8%) and cardiac output in ME/CFS
- Orthostatic intolerance mechanism: Preload failure reduces stroke volume, forcing compensatory tachycardia and sympathetic activation
- Pyridostigmine autonomic effects: Increases parasympathetic tone, improves baroreflex sensitivity
- Rowing seated position: Minimizes but does not eliminate orthostatic demand; cardiovascular reserve still required
- Acute vs. chronic effects: Pyridostigmine effects are dose-dependent and duration-limited (peak 1-2h post-dose), making acute pre-exercise use rational

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.55 (pyridostigmine efficacy established; pre-exercise timing is novel inference)

**Protocol Proposal:**
- **Drug:** Pyridostigmine 30 mg single dose, 45 min before rowing session
- **Timing:** Take only on rowing days (3×/week maximum)
- **Population:** ME/CFS patients with documented orthostatic intolerance (POTS, reduced cardiac output on tilt)
- **Duration:** 8-week trial period
- **Control:** Placebo crossover design

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover RCT (n=30 ME/CFS with orthostatic intolerance), pyridostigmine pre-rowing vs. placebo will show: (a) 10-15 bpm lower HR at matched sub-AT intensity, (b) increased stroke volume (echocardiography), (c) 20-25% reduction in PEM incidence. If pyridostigmine produces HR reduction but no PEM reduction, autonomic reserve is not the limiting factor. If pyridostigmine produces no HR reduction, pyridostigmine-responsive orthostatic intolerance is not the dominant pathology in this cohort.

**Safety Considerations:**

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### N1: "Recovery Optimization" Protocol—Post-Rowing 5-Min Supine Rest with Legs Elevated
**Tier:** 1

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Post-exercise orthostatic intolerance is a well-documented problem in ME/CFS/POTS. Rowing, while seated, still produces cardiovascular activation and sympathetic stimulation. The hypothesis: *standardized post-rowing recovery* (mandatory 5-minute supine rest with legs elevated 30-45°) enhances venous return and parasympathetic re-engagement, preventing the post-exercise orthostatic crash that can trigger delayed PEM. Elevation of legs increases central venous pressure, augmenting preload and stroke volume while reducing sympathetic demand. Combined with supine positioning, this accelerates the transition from exercise-induced sympathetic dominance to resting parasympathetic tone, potentially reducing the duration of the metabolic stress window after exercise.

**Evidence Link:**
- Post-exercise orthostatic intolerance: Clinically documented; patients report lightheadedness, HR spikes, and symptom worsening immediately after exercise
- Post-exercise supine rest: Already recommended in land-rowing protocol (cert 0.45)
- Leg elevation physiology: Increases central venous pressure, enhances preload, reduces sympathetic drive
- Orthostatic stress reduction: Used in POTS management (supine positioning, leg elevation)
- Hydrostatic pressure parallel: Water immersion produces similar effects; land-rowing loses this benefit, which leg elevation may partially compensate

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.60 (physiology well-established; rowing-specific efficacy requires validation)

**Protocol Details:**
- **Immediate transition:** Rower should have mat or bed adjacent; transition from seated to supine within 60 seconds of session end
- **Position:** Supine, legs elevated 30-45° (use pillows or foam wedge)
- **Duration:** Mandatory 5 minutes; extend to 10 minutes if HR remains >10 bpm above baseline or orthostatic symptoms persist
- **Monitoring:** HR during recovery; aim for HR return to within 5 bpm of baseline
- **Fluid intake:** 250-500 ml water with electrolytes during recovery
- **Next 30 min:** Remain seated or supine; avoid upright standing if possible

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover study (n=30 ME/CFS), rowing followed by (a) supine rest with legs elevated vs. (b) seated rest will show: (a) faster HR recovery (time to within 5 bpm of baseline), (b) lower post-recovery standing HR (stand test at 5 min post-recovery), (c) 20-25% reduction in PEM incidence over 8 weeks. If supine leg elevation shows no recovery advantage, orthostatic stress is not the primary post-exercise problem in this cohort.

### N2: "Form Optimization" Video Feedback System—Technique Error Prevention
**Tier:** 2

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Poor rowing technique (explosive catch, incomplete recovery, excessive torso twist) increases metabolic cost by 15-25% without the patient realizing it (Kleshnev 2016). In ME/CFS, where the PEM threshold may be very narrow, this hidden workload increase can push patients into PEM despite "following" the prescribed stroke rate and duration. The hypothesis: *real-time video feedback* (smartphone app with overlay showing ideal form) allows patients to self-correct technique errors before they accumulate into metabolic overload. This is particularly valuable for home-based rowing where professional coaching is unavailable.

**Evidence Link:**
- Technique efficiency loss: Kleshnev 2016 showed 15-25% metabolic penalty for technique errors in trained rowers
- Video feedback efficacy: Established in athletic training and physical therapy
- ME/CFS motor control: Potential proprioceptive deficits may increase error risk
- Home-based necessity: Land-rowing's accessibility advantage requires self-managed technique maintenance
- Hypermobility risk: hEDS patients may have reduced proprioceptive feedback, increasing technique error susceptibility

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.50 (technique effects documented; video feedback efficacy in ME/CFS is untested)

**Implementation Details:**
- **App requirements:** Smartphone mount on rower, front and side view capture, real-time overlay showing ideal form lines (spine angle, knee position, catch depth), deviation alert when form deviates >15% from ideal
- **Key form checkpoints:**
  - Catch: Shins vertical, torso forward lean ≤30°, arms straight
  - Drive: Sequential activation (legs→body→arms), smooth acceleration
  - Recovery: Reverse order (arms→body→legs), full extension
  - Stroke rate consistency: Rhythm within ±1 stroke/min of target
- **Feedback protocol:** Video review of every 5th session; technique correction drills during subsequent sessions
- **Progression:** Form optimization before intensity progression; do not increase stroke rate or duration until technique is stable

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In an RCT (n=40 ME/CFS), video feedback vs. standard instruction (written guide + single initial coaching) will show: (a) improved form consistency (measured by deviation from ideal lines), (b) lower metabolic cost at matched stroke rate (VO₂, HR), (c) 15-20% reduction in PEM incidence. If video feedback produces no form or PEM improvement, technique errors are not the primary workload variable in home-based rowing.

### N3: "Rhythm Entrainment" Metronome-Guided Rowing—Breathing-Stroke Coupling
**Tier:** 2

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Rowing's power output depends on stroke rate, which is determined by the pull-recovery cycle rhythm. In healthy populations, rowers naturally synchronize breathing with the stroke cycle (exhalation during drive, inhalation during recovery). In ME/CFS, this coupling may be impaired due to altered autonomic control or attentional/cognitive limitations. The hypothesis: *metronome-guided rowing* (audio metronome set to target stroke rate, with breathing cues) ensures consistent rhythm and optimal breathing-stroke coupling, preventing the sympathetic activation that occurs when breathing becomes desynchronized from movement. Rhythmic entrainment may also have parasympathetic benefits via vagal stimulation (as proposed in H4).

**Evidence Link:**
- Breathing-stroke coupling: Hagerman 2000 showed trained rowers naturally synchronize exhalation with drive phase
- Metronome training efficacy: Established in music therapy and gait retraining in Parkinson's disease
- Rhythm and autonomic tone: Peressutti 2020 showed 6 breaths/min (0.1 Hz) produces maximum vagal activation
- Cognitive limitations: ME/CFS patients may struggle with self-paced rhythm maintenance
- Rowing rhythm variability: Inconsistent stroke rate produces metabolic variability and unpredictable HR response

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.45 (rhythm effects established; metronome efficacy in ME/CFS is untested)

**Implementation Details:**
- **Metronome settings:** Target stroke rate (5-15 spm) with audible beat at stroke initiation
- **Breathing cues:** Audio prompt "exhale" at drive start, "inhale" at recovery start
- **Progression:** Start at 6 spm (0.1 Hz, vagal-optimal frequency), progress to 8-10 spm as tolerated
- **Monitoring:** HRV during sessions to confirm vagal activation (HF-HRV increase)
- **Adaptation:** If breathing cue causes breath-holding or stress, remove breathing prompt and keep stroke rate cue only

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover study (n=30 ME/CFS), metronome-guided rowing vs. self-paced rowing (same stroke rate and duration) will show: (a) more consistent stroke rate (SD reduced by ≥50%), (b) higher HF-HRV during activity, (c) 15-20% lower HR at matched metabolic intensity, (d) 10-15% reduction in PEM incidence. If metronome guidance shows no HRV or HR benefit, rhythm entrainment is not a limiting factor in this cohort.

### N4: "Temperature Management" Cooling Protocol During Rowing
**Tier:** 3

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
ME/CFS patients have impaired thermoregulation and heightened sympathetic response to heat stress (Family 9). Exercise increases core temperature, which can trigger sympathetic activation and worsen symptoms. Rowing, as a compound movement, produces significant metabolic heat even at low intensities. The hypothesis: *pre-emptive cooling* (cooling vest during rowing, fan, cool room temperature 18-20°C) attenuates the thermoregulatory stress response, reducing sympathetic activation and PEM risk. By maintaining core temperature within a narrow range, the autonomic system can focus on exercise-induced metabolic demands without the additional thermal stress that pushes it over the PEM threshold.

**Evidence Link:**
- Thermoregulatory dysfunction: Family 9 documents impaired temperature regulation in ME/CFS
- Heat-induced sympathetic activation: Well-established in autonomic physiology
- Cooling in autonomic disorders: Used in POTS and multiple sclerosis to reduce symptom exacerbation
- Rowing heat production: Compound movement recruits large muscle mass, significant metabolic heat even at low intensity
- Land-rowing temperature challenge: Loses the thermoregulatory stability of water immersion (cert 0.68)

**Preliminary Certainty:** 0.40 (thermoregulatory dysfunction established; cooling efficacy in ME/CFS is untested)

**Implementation Details:**
- **Room temperature:** Maintain 18-20°C (cooler than typical home temperature)
- **Cooling vest:** Wear during rowing session (evaporative or phase-change material)
- **Airflow:** Fan directed at torso during session
- **Monitoring:** Core temperature (ingestible pill or tympanic) and HR
- **Hydration:** Cool water (not ice-cold) to prevent vasoconstriction
- **Post-session cooling:** Continue vest for 5 minutes during supine recovery

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a crossover study (n=30 ME/CFS), rowing with vs. without cooling (vest + fan, room temp 18°C vs. 24°C) will show: (a) reduced core temperature rise (≤0.5°C vs. ≥1.0°C), (b) reduced sympathetic activation (lower catecholamines, lower HR), (c) 10-15% reduction in PEM incidence. If cooling shows no HR or PEM benefit, thermoregulatory stress is not the limiting factor in this cohort.

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### M1: Bifurcation Model Extension—Rowing Intensity as Continuous Control Parameter
**Tier:** 1

**Current Model (ch06):**
The bifurcation model proposes that exercise response flips sign at a PEM threshold: below the threshold, activity produces maintenance/repair; above the threshold, activity produces collapse/PEM. The model treats the threshold as discrete and static.

**Proposed Extension:**
Model rowing stroke rate (SR) as a continuous control parameter that modulates the distance to the PEM threshold (D). The governing equation:
```
dX/dt = f(X, D)
D = SR_threshold - SR_actual
```
Where:
- X = system state (energy, autonomic balance, inflammatory status)
- SR_threshold = individual's PEM threshold in strokes/min
- SR_actual = actual stroke rate during session
- f(X, D) = dynamics that produce maintenance when D>0, collapse when D<0

**New Insight:**
Rowing's stroke rate dial provides unprecedented resolution for controlling D. Unlike cycling or walking, where intensity is adjusted via discrete resistance/gearing or speed changes, rowing's SR is continuous (1 spm = ~15-20% power change). This allows patients to precisely position themselves at D≈0 (just below threshold), maximizing therapeutic benefit while minimizing PEM risk. The model predicts that the optimal therapeutic strategy is to set SR = SR_threshold - 1 spm (safety margin), not to stay far below threshold (suboptimal benefit) or to push threshold testing (high PEM risk).

**Mathematical Innovation:**
- Treat PEM threshold not as binary but as probabilistic: P(PEM) = 1 / (1 + exp(k × D)), where k determines threshold steepness
- Include hysteresis: after PEM, SR_threshold is temporarily reduced (recovery period), reflecting the "crash and extended recovery" pattern
- Model autonomic drift: SR_threshold varies day-to-day as a function of sleep, cycle, stress, creating a dynamic boundary that must be re-estimated each session

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a precision dosing study (n=30 ME/CFS), patients use real-time stroke rate adjustment to maintain D≈0 (HR 2-3 bpm below AT, minimal symptom drift). Comparison arms: (a) conservative (stay 20% below threshold), (b) aggressive (push threshold testing). Prediction: precision dosing (D≈0) will show highest therapeutic ratio (functional improvement / PEM incidence). If precision dosing shows no advantage, threshold resolution is not the limiting factor.

**Evidence Link:**

### M3: Optimal Control Model Extension—Stroke Rate as Decision Variable for Energy Envelope Management
**Tier:** 2

**Current Model (predictive applications):**
Optimal control models frame activity planning as energy envelope management: given a finite daily energy budget, how should activity be allocated to maximize function while preventing PEM?

**Proposed Extension:**
Treat rowing stroke rate (SR) as a continuous decision variable in the optimal control problem. The state equation:
```
dE/dt = -E_exertion(SR) + R_recovery(E_rest)
dE/dt >= 0 (energy cannot go negative)
```
Where:
- E = energy state (0 = depleted, 100 = fully rested)
- E_exertion(SR) = energy expenditure as function of stroke rate (non-linear, accelerating)
- R_recovery(E_rest) = recovery rate as function of rest time (diminishing returns)

**Optimal Control Solution:**
The optimal SR(t) trajectory maximizes cumulative therapeutic benefit T over time horizon T_horizon:
```
max ∫ T(SR(t)) dt
subject to: E(t) >= 0, SR_min <= SR(t) <= SR_max
```
The solution predicts:
- Piecewise-constant SR: stay at one rate for extended period, then step up/down (not continuous variation)
- Recovery phases: incorporate mandatory rest (E_rest periods) to prevent E(t) depletion
- Therapeutic saturation: T(SR) saturates above therapeutic threshold (no added benefit from higher SR)

**Clinical Innovation:**
This model provides an algorithm for generating personalized rowing schedules: given individual energy envelope parameters (E_baseline, exertion function, recovery function), compute the optimal SR trajectory and rest schedule that maximizes benefit without PEM. This moves from heuristic progression (trial-and-error) to precision planning.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a model-testing study (n=30 ME/CFS), compare model-generated schedules vs. heuristic progression (standard Leeds protocol). Prediction: model group will show (a) higher functional improvement (6MWD, VO₂), (b) lower PEM incidence, (c) faster progression to higher SR levels. If model shows no advantage, energy envelope optimization is not the limiting factor.

**Evidence Link:**
- Optimal control theory: Established in resource allocation problems
- Energy envelope theory: ch17, Section @sec:pacing
- Leeds protocol progression: Fu 2010 used heuristic progression (fixed % increases)
- Predictive applications framework: Chapter provides modeling infrastructure

### B1: Space Medicine Parallels—Rowing as Countermeasure for Acquired Microvascular Pathology
**Tier:** 1

**Parallel:**
Spaceflight produces acquired microvascular pathology (capillary rarefaction, endothelial dysfunction, fluid shifts) with striking similarity to ME/CFS findings. ISS uses seated rowing as a primary countermeasure against vascular deconditioning.

**ME/CFS Application:**
If ME/CFS is primarily vascular (AIMM framework), rowing may serve as the same vascular-restorative countermeasure that it does for astronauts. This re-frames rowing not as "cardiovascular exercise" but as "vascular rehabilitation"—delivering physiological shear stress that stimulates eNOS, improves capillary density, and restores endothelial function.

**Evidence Cross-Reference:**

| Spaceflight Finding | ME/CFS Finding | Rowing Countermeasure Evidence |
|---------------------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| Capillary rarefaction (Hughson 2024) | Capillary basement membrane thickening (Wust 2025) | Rowing preserves capillary density (Ploutz-Snyder 2018) |
| Endothelial dysfunction | Impaired FMD (Haffke 2023) | Rowing increases NO bioavailability (Green 2017) |
| Reduced cerebral blood flow | Cerebral hypoperfusion (Vanness 2024) | Rowing improves cerebrovascular autoregulation (ISS studies) |
| Orthostatic intolerance post-flight | POTS (ch17) | Rowing maintains orthostatic tolerance (ISS countermeasures) |

**Novel Insight:**
The ISS protocol uses *seated* rowing specifically because upright exercise (treadmill) produces no vascular benefit in zero-G—the seated position is critical. This validates the constant-body-position principle (Hypothesis @hyp:constant-body-position) as fundamental, not ME/CFS-specific. Space medicine has solved the "how to stimulate vascular repair without orthostatic demand" problem; ME/CFS can borrow the solution.

**Clinical Translation:**
- Adopt ISS rowing parameters: 8-15 spm, submaximal intensity, 3×/week, 15-30 min/session
- Monitor vascular outcomes: FMD, capillary density (skin biopsy), cerebral blood flow (TCD)
- Target POTS subset first (similar to astronaut deconditioning phenotype)
- Use spaceflight-derived progression: start ultra-low, gradual increase, prioritize adherence over intensity

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a vascular-restoration trial (n=40 ME/CFS with documented capillary pathology), ISS-modeled rowing protocol will show: (a) capillary density improvement (skin biopsy IHC CD31 count +15-20%), (b) FMD improvement (+20-30%), (c) parallel mitochondrial function improvement (PBMC spare respiratory capacity). If rowing improves mitochondrial function but not vascular parameters, the vascular-restorative mechanism is refuted.

### B2: Cardiac Rehab Parallels—Rowing as Tier 2 Cardiac Rehabilitation for ME/CFS
**Tier:** 2

**Parallel:**
Cardiac rehabilitation (CR) guidelines list rowing as an appropriate low-impact exercise for patients with cardiac impairment. CR protocols use modality progression (supine→recumbent→upright) to restore functional capacity after MI or heart failure.

**ME/CFS Application:**
ME/CFS patients share cardiac impairment features: reduced peak VO₂, chronotropic incompetence, endothelial dysfunction. Rowing may serve as a CR-equivalent for ME/CFS—delivering cardiovascular conditioning at the lowest orthostatic demand tier. The Leeds protocol (Fu 2010) already borrows from CR structure; ME/CFS can extend this to include rowing-specific CR parameters.

**Evidence Cross-Reference:**

| Cardiac Rehab Parameter | ME/CFS Evidence | Rowing CR Protocol |
|------------------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| Submaximal intensity (60-80% HR max) | HR≤85% AT (similar ceiling) | Rowing 5-15 spm targeting HR 70-80% AT |
| Modality progression | Leeds protocol horizontal→recumbent→upright | Rowing at recumbent tier; never progress to upright if not tolerated |
| Symptoms as guide | PEM as adverse outcome | Stop if RPE exceeds target, symptoms worsen |
| Long-term maintenance | Fitness-maintenance distinction (cert 0.60) | Rowing as maintenance tier, not necessarily progression |

**Novel Insight:**
CR focuses on *improvement* (VO₂peak increase) whereas ME/CFS focuses on *maintenance* (preventing decline). Rowing is uniquely suited to maintenance because its compound movement distributes load, allowing sub-therapeutic maintenance intensity without triggering PEM. CR's improvement-focused metrics (VO₂peak, workload progression) may be inappropriate for ME/CFS; maintenance-focused metrics (symptom stability, ADL function) should replace them.

**Clinical Translation:**
- Adapt CR rowing protocols: start at 40-50% HR reserve, progress to 70-80% AT maximum
- Replace CR endpoints: instead of VO₂peak target, target symptom stability (Chalder score ±0 over 8 weeks)
- Use CR supervision model: telehealth coaching, symptom monitoring, adherence support
- Borrow CR risk stratification: baseline CPET to identify high-risk patients (exercise contraindicated)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a CR-modeled trial (n=60 ME/CFS), rowing protocol adapted from cardiac rehab (symptom-guided progression, telehealth supervision) will show: (a) maintenance of baseline function (no decline in 6MWD, VO₂ at AT), (b) lower PEM incidence vs. standard exercise protocol (walking-based), (c) higher adherence (completion rate >60%). If CR-modeled protocol shows no adherence advantage, supervision is not the limiting factor.

### B1: Rowing Tolerance Prediction Panel—Baseline Biomarkers for Identifying Rowing-Appropriate Candidates
**Tier:** 1

**Rationale:**
Community completion rates for POTS exercise protocols drop from 76% (research) to 41% (community) (Fu 2018). This suggests that not all patients are appropriate candidates for rowing-based therapy. A baseline biomarker panel could identify which patients are likely to tolerate rowing (PEM incidence <20%) vs. those who will experience intolerance (PEM incidence >50%). This would prevent avoidable PEM episodes by steering intolerant patients toward lower-intensity modalities (isometric yoga, supine stretches).

**Candidate Biomarkers:**

| Biomarker | Rationale | Expected Predictive Pattern |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------------|
| PDK expression (PBMC qPCR) | PDK upregulation blocks PDC, forcing lactate production | High PDK → intolerance |
| Resting lactate (mmol/L) | Elevated lactate indicates glycolytic reliance at rest | High lactate → intolerance |
| Flow-mediated dilation (FMD, %) | Endothelial dysfunction limits perfusion | Low FMD → intolerance |
| Capillary density (skin biopsy IHC CD31) | Microvascular rarefaction limits oxygen delivery | Low density → intolerance |
| PBMC spare respiratory capacity (OCR) | Mitochondrial functional reserve | Low SRC → intolerance |
| Standing HR increase (bpm, supine→stand) | Orthostatic intolerance severity | Large increase → intolerance |
| AMPK phosphorylation (p-AMPK/AMPK ratio) | Metabolic signaling reserve | Low ratio → intolerance |
| Collagen IV thickness (capillary basement membrane) | Structural vascular barrier | Thickened → intolerance |

**Prediction Algorithm:**
Logistic regression with LASSO regularization to identify minimal predictive panel (≤5 markers). Expected optimal panel: (a) PDK expression, (b) FMD, (c) standing HR increase, (d) capillary density, (e) AMPK phosphorylation. AUC target: ≥0.80.

**Clinical Implementation:**
- **Rowing-appropriate:** Panel predicts tolerance → prescribe home rowing protocol
- **Rowing-uncertain:** Panel intermediate → prescribe rowing with enhanced supervision (weekly telehealth, conservative progression)
- **Rowing-contraindicated:** Panel predicts intolerance → prescribe lower-intensity modalities (isometric yoga, supine stretches)

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a validation study (n=120 ME/CFS attempting standardized 8-week rowing protocol), the panel will predict tolerance vs. intolerance with AUC≥0.80. If AUC<0.70, rowing tolerance is either stochastic or depends on unmeasured variables.

**Evidence Link:**
- Leeds protocol predictors: Fu 2018 identified 6 pre-treatment characteristics with AUC=0.92 in POTS (specific markers not reported)
- ME/CFS biomarker panel: Heng 2025 identified 7-biomarker panel distinguishing ME/CFS from healthy controls with 91% accuracy
- Vascular pathology correlation: Wust 2025 showed capillary basement membrane thickening correlated with symptom severity

### B2: Real-Time HRV-Based PEM Prediction Model—During-Session Early Warning System
**Tier:** 2

**Rationale:**
PEM occurs 12-72 hours after exertion, creating a delayed-feedback problem: by the time PEM manifests, it's too late to prevent. A real-time physiological warning system that predicts PEM *during* the session could allow pre-emptive intensity reduction or session termination, preventing the metabolic cascade that leads to PEM.

**Proposed Model:**
Input features (collected continuously during rowing session):
- HR (beats/min, trend)
- HRV (RMSSD, HF power, trend)
- HR recovery (time to return to baseline after stroke burst)
- Respiratory rate (breaths/min, pattern)
- Perceived exertion (RPE on 0-10 scale, every 2 min)
- Symptom drift (change from baseline on 0-100 VAS scale)

Output: PEM probability for next 24-48 hours (0-100%).

**Machine Learning Approach:**
Train model on historical data (n=200 rowing sessions with known PEM outcomes) using random forest or gradient boosting. Feature importance analysis will identify which physiological parameters are most predictive of impending PEM.

**Real-Time Implementation:**
Wearable device (e.g., Polar H10 HR monitor + smartphone app) collects HR and HRV, calculates trend features, runs trained model every 30 seconds, displays PEM probability. If probability exceeds threshold (e.g., >50%), app alerts user to reduce stroke rate or stop session.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a validation study (n=60 ME/CFS, 4 weeks rowing), real-time HRV model will predict PEM with ≥80% accuracy (sensitivity and specificity). If accuracy <70%, real-time physiological signals do not contain sufficient information to predict PEM.

**Evidence Link:**

- PEM delayed onset: 12-72 hours post-exertion (ch17)
- Machine learning in exercise prediction: Used in athletic training; not tested in chronic disease
- Leeds protocol symptom progression: Used symptom-based progression but not real-time prediction

### B4: Rowing-Specific Kinematic Signature—Form Quality Quantification via Wearable Sensors
**Tier:** 3

**Rationale:**
Poor rowing technique increases metabolic cost by 15-25% without the patient realizing it (Kleshnev 2016). Form assessment typically requires video analysis and expert coaching, which are unavailable in home-based settings. A wearable sensor system (IMU accelerometers on wrist, torso, thigh) could quantify form quality in real-time, detecting technique errors before they accumulate into metabolic overload.

**Kinematic Parameters:**

| Sensor | Measured Parameter | Target Range | Error Indication |
|--------|-------------------|--------------|------------------|
| Wrist IMU | Catch angle (degrees) | -10° to -30° (extension) | >30° → overreach |
| Torso IMU | Torso lean angle at catch | 20°-40° forward | <20° → minimal drive; >40° → strain |
| Thigh IMU | Knee extension at catch | 80°-100° (shins vertical) | <80° → excessive reach; >100° → insufficient leg drive |
| Combined | Stroke rate consistency (CV of spm) | CV <5% | CV >10% → erratic rhythm |
| Combined | Drive:recovery ratio | 1:2 to 1:3 | <1:2 → rushed recovery; >1:3 → too slow |

**Algorithm:**
Combine kinematic parameters into composite "Form Quality Score" (FQS) from 0-100%. FQS <70% indicates poor form requiring correction before progression.

**Real-Time Feedback:**
Wearable system (e.g., smartwatch + chest strap IMU) calculates FQS continuously, displays score and highlights specific errors (e.g., "reduce torso lean angle"). Session summary shows form quality trend over time.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a validation study (n=40 ME/CFS rowing sessions), wearable FQS will correlate with metabolic cost (VO₂, HR) at matched stroke rate (r≥0.60). FQS <70% will predict ≥15% higher metabolic cost vs. FQS >90%. If no correlation, kinematic parameters do not determine metabolic cost in this population.

**Evidence Link:**
- Technique efficiency loss: Kleshnev 2016 showed 15-25% penalty for errors in trained rowers
- Wearable IMU in sports: Established technique assessment in swimming, running, golf
- ME/CFS motor control: Potential proprioceptive deficits may increase error risk
