## Category 1: Novel Care Protocol Hypotheses

### Idea 1.1: GPCR Autoantibody-Titrated Care Scheduling

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** GPCR autoantibodies ($beta_2$-adrenergic, M3/M4 muscarinic) fluctuate in concentration and activity across inflammatory cycles. In severe patients, care activities provoke an acute sympathetic response that may transiently increase autoantibody-mediated receptor blockade — a form of care-induced receptor desensitisation. A protocol that times care to the *trough* of the patient's autoantibody activity cycle (identified via serial dried blood spot testing over 72h or inferred from HRV circadian pattern, since $beta_2$-AAb titres correlate with sympathovagal imbalance at r=0.45) could reduce the effective metabolic cost of care by performing it when receptor blockade is lowest.
- **Evidence link:** Azcue et al. (2026) documented $beta_2$-adrenergic AAb correlation with sympathovagal imbalance (r=0.45, p=0.001). Hermisson "all care is a cost" principle implies that receptor availability at time of care modulates that cost. Circadian autoantibody fluctuation is documented in autoimmune disease (SLE, RA) but unmeasured in ME/CFS GPCR AAbs specifically.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Serial GPCR AAb measurement every 4h for 72h will reveal ≥2-fold trough-to-peak variation; patients whose care is clustered in the lowest-tertile AAb window will show ≥30% fewer post-care PEM episodes than those receiving identical care at random times.

### Idea 1.2: Glymphatic-Conscious Care Window Protocol

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Glymphatic clearance is driven by infraslow NE oscillations during slow-wave sleep, which is severely impaired in ME/CFS. The Hermisson care guide imposes scheduled care blocks throughout the day — but some of these blocks may overlap with the brain's partial glymphatic recovery windows (e.g., during scheduled post-care mandatory rest in a dark, quiet room). A protocol that intentionally aligns care-block timing with glymphatic-entraining conditions — dark, silent, supine, with pre-care vagal stimulation (slow breathing 6 breaths/min × 5 min) — could convert mandatory post-care rest into an active glymphatic recovery intervention rather than passive metabolic downtime. The innovation: care rest is not just "no activity" — it is the only available glymphatic recovery window in severe patients whose nocturnal clearance is failed.
- **Evidence link:** Hauglund et al. (2025) demonstrated NE oscillations are necessary and sufficient for glymphatic flow. Hermisson mandates post-care rest but doesn't specify glymphatic-enhancing environmental conditions. Zhu et al. (2025) showed NE oscillation amplitude is suppressed by Z-drugs by ~50%.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Post-care rest conducted in darkness + silence + supine + pre-rest vagal stimulation (6 breaths/min × 5 min) will show improved DTI-ALPS glymphatic index at 4 weeks compared to post-care rest without environmental optimisation, matched for total rest duration, in severe ME/CFS patients.

### Idea 1.3: TRPM3 Pre-Care Tolerance Probe

- **Category:** 1
- **Mechanistic rationale:** TRPM3 ion channel dysfunction is one of the most replicated findings in ME/CFS NK cells. If TRPM3 channelopathy extends to sensory neurons and vascular smooth muscle — consistent with the broader TRP channelopathy model — then TRPM3 functional status on any given day may predict care tolerance. A rapid pre-care probe (e.g., asking the patient to rate discomfort from a standardised 30-second cool compress at 20°C applied to the forearm, which activates TRPM3 in sensory afferents) could serve as a practical bedside surrogate for channel function. Low TRPM3 responsiveness (more discomfort = channel dysfunction = impaired sensory gating) would indicate a "low-tolerance day" where care should be minimised.
- **Evidence link:** TRPM3 channelopathy is documented in ME/CFS NK cells (Cabanas 2018, Eaton-Fitch 2020, replicated in large 2025 cohort). TRPM3 is also expressed in sensory DRG neurons. Hermisson stop-signal protocol relies on caregiver proxy detection — this probe adds a quantitative, non-verbal, pre-care tolerance signal.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.12
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Thermal discomfort rating (0-10) from standardised 20°C, 30-second forearm compress will correlate with daily PEM episode count (r ≥0.40 within-subject) and will correctly classify "tolerate care today" vs "minimise care today" with sensitivity ≥70% in severe ME/CFS.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### Idea 2.1: Standardised Care-Challenge Test for PEM Subtyping

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The Hermisson care guide provides a structured list of care activities with known intensity gradations. This is an untapped research tool: a standardised care-challenge protocol (e.g., 5-minute assisted passive sitting transfer followed by 30-minute supine rest) could serve as a controlled, reproducible, clinically relevant PEM trigger — far more relevant than CPET for severe/bedbound patients who cannot exercise. By measuring the latency, magnitude, and recovery trajectory after identical care challenges repeated weekly, researchers could establish intra-patient PEM reproducibility, subtype patients by PEM response profile (fast trigger/long recovery vs slow trigger/short recovery vs bimodal), and test interventions with a clinically meaningful endpoint.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson protocols are structured and reproducible. PEM latency varies 2-72h (Van Oosterwijck 2010). CPET is inaccessible for bedbound patients. The care-challenge test uses an ethically unavoidable event (care) as a standardised research trigger.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Within-patient PEM severity (VAS) following identical weekly care challenges will show ICC ≥0.60 over 4 weeks, establishing test-retest reliability; patients will cluster into ≥2 distinct PEM response profiles (latent class analysis, ≥2 classes) that predict differential response to pacing interventions.

### Idea 2.2: Bidirectional Caregiver-Patient HSAT2 EV Assay

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If HSAT2 pericentromeric repeat RNA propagates via exosomal transmission — driving MDSC expansion and immune exhaustion — then prolonged close physical contact between caregiver and patient (repositioning, bathing, feeding) creates a transmission-risk scenario. Caregivers handle patient bodily fluids, bedding, and personal items. A study measuring plasma exosomal HSAT2 in caregiver-patient dyads after 6 months of daily care could assess whether HSAT2-containing EVs transfer from patient to caregiver. If caregivers show elevated HSAT2 above population baseline, this identifies a novel occupational biohazard of severe ME/CFS care — and mandates barrier precautions (gloves, masks during high-contact care) that Hermisson doesn't address.
- **Evidence link:** HSAT2 EV propagation demonstrated in cell culture (Evdokimova 2019). ME/CFS patients hypothesised to have elevated plasma HSAT2 EVs. Caregivers have prolonged daily contact with patient skin, saliva, and bedding. No study has measured HSAT2 in caregivers of any disease.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.08
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Plasma exosomal HSAT2 RNA levels will be significantly higher in caregivers of severe ME/CFS patients (n≥20 dyads, 6+ months daily care) compared to age/sex-matched non-caregiver controls; within-dyad HSAT2 sequence variants (by RNA-seq) will be more similar than between-dyad comparisons, suggesting patient→caregiver transmission.

### Idea 2.3: Care-Dose vs Epigenetic Recovery Window Study

- **Category:** 2
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Sharma et al. (2025) demonstrated that PEM involves ongoing epigenetic remodelling at 48h post-CPET, with 98% of differentially methylated fragments being ME/CFS-specific. If a single exertion requires 48h+ for epigenetic recovery, then a care protocol that imposes daily care activities may prevent epigenetic normalisation entirely. A study measuring DNA methylation changes at 0h, 24h, 48h, and 72h after a standardised care intervention (e.g., bed bath + repositioning, ~15 min care time) in severe patients would establish the minimum care-free interval required for epigenetic recovery. This would directly inform the optimal spacing of care blocks — the clinical answer to "how many days between baths to prevent cumulative epigenetic dysregulation?"
- **Evidence link:** Sharma 2025: 98% ME/CFS-specific DMFs at 0-48h post-exertion. Hermisson doesn't specify optimal inter-care interval. Epigenetic recovery time is unknown for sub-maximal exertion (care activities vs CPET).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** DNA methylation at 48h post-care challenge will show ≥50% of the differentially methylated fragments still unresolved (not returned to baseline) in ≥50% of severe patients, indicating that 48h care-free intervals are insufficient for epigenetic recovery after moderate-intensity care.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### Idea 3.1: Pre-Care Sublingual Dexmedetomidine Micro-Dose for Sensory Gating

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Dexmedetomidine is an $alpha_2$-adrenergic agonist used in ICU for conscious sedation that preserves respiratory function while reducing sympathetic tone, anxiety, and sensory processing. At micro-doses (sublingual 0.2-0.5 $mu$g/kg), it could function as a "sensory gate" before high-intensity care procedures — reducing the thalamic amplification of sensory input that triggers PEM in severe patients, without producing sedation. Unlike lorazepam (GABA-ergic, impairs glymphatic clearance), dexmedetomidine enhances glymphatic flow by promoting the NE oscillatory state (via $alpha_2$-mediated LC autoinhibition that permits infraslow oscillation rather than suppressing it). This is mechanistically more targeted than benzodiazepines for pre-care anxiolysis/sensory damping.
- **Evidence link:** $alpha_2$ agonists promote LC-NE infraslow oscillations (Hauglund 2025 mechanism). Dexmedetomidine enhances glymphatic flow in rodent models (Benveniste 2017). Hermisson care guide doesn't address pre-care pharmacological sensory gating. TRP channelopathy in ME/CFS involves impaired sensory gating at the thalamic level.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-care sublingual dexmedetomidine 0.3 $mu$g/kg (single dose, 30 min before care) will reduce post-care PEM incidence by ≥40% compared to placebo pre-medication in very-severe ME/CFS, without producing sedation (Ramsay Sedation Scale ≤2) or respiratory depression.

### Idea 3.2: Low-Dose Fingolimod for Care-Induced Neuroinflammatory PEM Amplification

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Care activities trigger sensory-pain-mast cell-microglia amplification loops that worsen neuroinflammation and produce the "brain fog crash" hours after care. Fingolimod (FTY720), an S1P receptor modulator, prevents lymphocyte egress from lymph nodes and also has direct CNS effects: it reduces microglial activation, promotes oligodendrocyte survival, and enhances blood-brain barrier integrity via S1P1 receptor agonism on endothelial cells. At ultra-low doses (0.25 mg every other day, vs standard 0.5 mg daily for MS), it could dampen the neuroinflammatory amplification of PEM without producing significant immunosuppression. The S1P component of the ceramide:S1P rheostat is deficient in ME/CFS — fingolimod is an S1P analogue that could partially correct this axis.
- **Evidence link:** Ceramide:S1P rheostat shift documented in ME/CFS (Chapter 17 universal mechanisms). S1P deficiency impairs lymphocyte egress and receptor signalling including TRPM3 and GPCRs. Fingolimod reduces microglial activation in MS models. Hermisson does not address neuroinflammatory amplification of care-triggered PEM.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.08
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Ultra-low-dose fingolimod (0.25 mg q48h × 12 weeks) in severe ME/CFS with documented neuroinflammation (by central sensitisation on QST) will reduce post-care cognitive PEM severity by ≥30% vs placebo, with no increase in infection rate (non-inferiority test), and will partially normalise the ceramide:S1P ratio in plasma.

### Idea 3.3: Orexin-A Intranasal Micro-Dose for Care Tolerance Window

- **Category:** 3
- **Mechanistic rationale:** CSF orexin-A is chronically low in ME/CFS, producing daytime somnolence and impaired LC-NE oscillation quality that degrades glymphatic clearance. However, orexin also drives arousal and cognitive engagement — and in very-severe patients, even passive care requires a minimum arousal level for the patient to cooperate (swallowing, turning, communicating stop signals). Intranasal orexin-A (10-20 $mu$g, single dose, 10 min before care) could provide a brief, time-limited arousal window specifically for care cooperation — without the sustained sympathetic activation of systemic stimulants. This is the pharmacological equivalent of "borrowing arousal for care and paying it back during post-care rest." The intranasal route delivers directly to CSF via the olfactory-Trigeminal pathway, bypassing the BBB.
- **Evidence link:** Orexin-A is deficient in ME/CFS CSF (Section ch15-orexin). Intranasal orexin-A improves cognitive performance in sleep-deprived humans and non-human primates (Deadwyler 2007). Hermisson notes that even passive care requires patient cooperation but doesn't address the arousal threshold problem.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.07
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Intranasal orexin-A 15 $mu$g 10 min before care will improve patient cooperation scores (caregiver-rated, 0-10 scale of ease-of-care-delivery) by ≥2 points and reduce care session duration by ≥20% without increasing post-care PEM severity, compared to saline placebo, in a crossover trial in very-severe ME/CFS.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Idea 4.1: HSAT2-Re-Silencing Nutrient Cocktail

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If HSAT2 pericentromeric repeat transcription is perpetuated by loss of CpG methylation at the HSAT2 locus (a consequence of initial viral activation per Fourel's model), then providing the biochemical substrates for DNA remethylation could theoretically restore HSAT2 silencing. A cocktail targeting the one-carbon cycle: L-methylfolate (5-MTHF, 400-800 $mu$g — the active form, avoiding MTHFR polymorphism issues), methylcobalamin (B12, 1000 $mu$g), betaine (trimethylglycine, 1000 mg as a methyl donor), and pyridoxal-5-phosphate (B6, 25 mg as cofactor for serine hydroxymethyltransferase) — all administered in forms that bypass common polymorphisms — could provide the biochemical resources for DNMT-mediated remethylation of the HSAT2 locus. This is mechanistically distinct from generic mitochondrial/energy supplementation — it targets the epigenetic maintenance of the disease state.
- **Evidence link:** HSAT2 is silenced by DNA methylation (Shadle 2019). ME/CFS methylation is dysregulated bidirectionally (Chapter 12). MTHFR polymorphisms are common and impair folate→5-MTHF conversion. Betaine is an alternative methyl donor for homocysteine remethylation (BHMT pathway, liver/kidney), bypassing MTHFR. No study has tested methylation support specifically for HSAT2 re-silencing.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 12 weeks of the methylation cocktail in post-viral ME/CFS patients (≤3 years onset) will reduce plasma exosomal HSAT2 RNA by ≥40% (qPCR) and increase HSAT2 locus methylation (bisulfite sequencing) by ≥15% compared to placebo, with correlation between HSAT2 reduction and NK cell cytotoxicity improvement (r ≥0.50).

### Idea 4.2: Sphingosine-1-Phosphate Precursor Supplementation for Care-Dyad Stability

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The ceramide:S1P rheostat is shifted toward ceramide dominance in ME/CFS — ceramide inhibits mitochondrial complex III, promotes apoptosis, and reorganises lipid rafts affecting TRPM3 and GPCR signalling. S1P promotes mitochondrial biogenesis, immune regulation, and endothelial barrier integrity. Dietary sphingolipid precursors (sphingomyelin from egg yolk, 500 mg/day; or serine + palmitate as the de novo synthesis substrates) could partially shift the rheostat toward S1P, improving cellular resilience to care-induced metabolic stress. This is mechanistically relevant because care procedures produce transient oxidative stress that activates sphingomyelinase, further increasing ceramide at the expense of S1P. Pre-loading the S1P synthesis pathway could buffer against care-induced ceramide spikes.
- **Evidence link:** Ceramide:S1P rheostat documented in Chapter 17 universal mechanisms. S1P deficiency impairs lymphocyte egress and receptor signalling. Egg sphingomyelin is bioavailable and increases plasma S1P in human trials (Ohlsson 2010). Hermisson doesn't address lipid-mediator buffering for care tolerance.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.12
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of dietary sphingomyelin (500 mg/day from egg yolk phospholipid) in severe ME/CFS will increase plasma S1P by ≥30% and reduce ceramide:S1P ratio by ≥20% vs placebo, with reduction in post-care PEM severity (VAS) correlating with S1P increase (r ≥0.35).

### Idea 4.3: Bedbound-Compatible Aquaporin-4 Support Nutrient Protocol

- **Category:** 4
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Aquaporin-4 (AQP4) channels at astrocytic endfeet are the critical bottleneck for glymphatic CSF-ISF exchange. AQP4 function is impaired by chronic norepinephrine elevation (which depolarises AQP4 at the perivascular membrane), oxidative stress, and membrane lipid peroxidation — all present in severe ME/CFS. Nutrients that support AQP4 membrane localisation and function: omega-3 DHA (1000 mg, for membrane fluidity and anti-lipid-peroxidation), magnesium L-threonate (1000 mg, the only Mg form that crosses the BBB and may support astrocytic function), and sulforaphane-glucoraphanin (broccoli sprout extract, 30 mg, Nrf2 activator that reduces oxidative AQP4 damage). Administered at bedtime to support overnight glymphatic function, this could enhance the clearance benefit of the patient's already-scheduled nocturnal rest.
- **Evidence link:** AQP4 is the glymphatic bottleneck (Xie 2013, NematGorgani 2025). NE depolarises AQP4. DHA supports membrane integrity. Magnesium L-threonate crosses the BBB and improves synaptic plasticity in rodent models. Sulforaphane activates Nrf2, reducing oxidative AQP4 damage (Ding 2025 glymphatic AD model). Hermisson doesn't address nutritional glymphatic support.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 12 weeks of bedtime AQP4-support cocktail will improve DTI-ALPS glymphatic index by ≥15% and reduce morning brain fog severity (VAS) by ≥25% compared to placebo, in bedbound ME/CFS patients with documented alpha-delta sleep on PSG.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### Idea 5.1: Circadian Mast Cell-Adaptive Care Scheduling

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Mast cell degranulation follows circadian patterns driven by clock gene expression in mast cells (Per2, Bmal1). Histamine release peaks in the early morning hours (~2-4 AM) — this is when MCAS symptoms are typically worst. Diamine oxidase (DAO), the primary histamine-degrading enzyme in intestinal mucosa, has highest activity in the late morning to early afternoon. A care schedule that avoids high-risk care procedures (bathing, bedding change, any procedure involving skin contact or temperature change) during the histamine peak (2-8 AM) and concentrates them during the DAO activity peak (10 AM-2 PM) could reduce MCAS-amplified PEM without any additional pharmacological intervention. Care that must occur during the histamine peak (e.g., overnight repositioning for pressure injury prevention) should be minimised to essential-only and preceded by antihistamine if the patient is MCAS-positive.
- **Evidence link:** Circadian mast cell biology established (Christ 2018, Nakamura 2021). DAO circadian activity in human intestinal mucosa (Bohn 2017). Hermisson doesn't specify circadian timing of care blocks. MCAS affects 16-25% of ME/CFS (Rohrhofer 2025).
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Rescheduling high-contact care procedures from morning (6-9 AM) to late morning (11 AM-1 PM) in MCAS-positive severe ME/CFS patients will reduce care-triggered flushing, urticaria, and post-care fatigue by ≥35% compared to identical care at early morning times, in a crossover design with 4-week washout.

### Idea 5.2: Endothelial Pre-Conditioning via Short-Duration Limb Ischemia

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Remote ischemic preconditioning (RIPC) — 3-4 cycles of 5-minute upper arm cuff inflation to 200 mmHg followed by 5-minute reperfusion — is an established cardioprotective intervention that releases humoral factors (adenosine, bradykinin, nitric oxide) conferring endothelial protection against subsequent ischemic stress. In severe ME/CFS, care procedures (bathing, positional changes) cause microvascular stress and endothelial activation that contribute to PEM. If RIPC is applied to the arm 30 minutes before a planned high-intensity care session, the released protective factors may precondition the endothelium against care-induced microvascular collapse. Critically, RIPC is entirely passive — caregiver-applied, no patient effort required, performed while the patient is supine.
- **Evidence link:** RIPC is established in cardiac surgery (Thielmann 2013), stroke prevention (Meng 2012), and recently tested in Long COVID microvascular dysfunction (pilot data, 2025). Endothelial dysfunction documented in ME/CFS (Heng 2025, elevated VWF, fibronectin, thrombospondin-1). RIPC releases NO, adenosine, and bradykinin that protect endothelium. Hermisson doesn't address endothelial preconditioning.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** RIPC (3 × 5 min ischemia/5 min reperfusion, arm cuff, 30 min before standardised care session) will reduce post-care endothelial activation markers (VWF, thrombospondin-1) by ≥25% at 2h post-care compared to sham preconditioning (cuff at 20 mmHg) in severe ME/CFS with documented endothelial dysfunction.

### Idea 5.3: Aquatic Micro-Immersion for Bedbound Care Delivery

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Water immersion produces hydrostatic compression of peripheral veins → increased central venous pressure → increased preload → increased stroke volume → parasympathetic shift (decreased HR by ~10 bpm, decreased peripheral vascular resistance by 21-30%). This parasympathetic shift directly counteracts the sympathetic-dominant state of ME/CFS dysautonomia. For bedbound patients who cannot access a pool, a "micro-immersion" adaptation — immersing only the lower extremities in a basin of thermoneutral water (32°C) at the bedside for 10 minutes while supine — could produce a partial hydrostatic benefit sufficient to blunt the sympathetic response to subsequent care. The caregiver fills the basin, helps position the patient's legs, and performs oral/upper-body care simultaneously while the hydrostatic effect is active. This is more feasible than full-body immersion for bedbound patients.
- **Evidence link:** Schmid et al. (2007): chest-deep water at 32°C decreased HR by ~10 bpm, reduced PVR by 21-30%. Partial immersion (legs only) produces proportional but smaller hemodynamic effects. Hermisson doesn't discuss aquatic care delivery. Constant body position hypothesis (Chapter 17) supports horizontal positioning during care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Lower-extremity micro-immersion (32°C, legs-only, 10 min pre-care + during care) in severe POTS+ME/CFS patients will reduce the standing HR increase during post-care orthostatic challenge by ≥8 bpm and reduce post-care dizziness (VAS) by ≥2 points compared to identical care without micro-immersion.

### Idea 5.4: Caregiver-Delivered Craniosacral Still Point Induction for Glymphatic Priming

- **Category:** 5
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The "still point" in craniosacral therapy — a manually induced pause in the cranial rhythmic impulse, achieved by gentle occipital hold — produces a transient increase in CSF flow amplitude when rhythm resumes. In ME/CFS, where CSF pulsation is diminished by impaired arterial pulsatility and NE dysregulation, a caregiver-administered still point induction (5-10 minutes of gentle sub-occipital hold, no manipulation, fully passive for the patient) performed before care could prime the glymphatic system for enhanced clearance during the subsequent post-care rest period. This is low-force, requires minimal training, and is already used in palliative care for symptom management. The innovation: it targets the mechanical driver of glymphatic flow (CSF pulsation) rather than just sleep architecture.
- **Evidence link:** Cranial rhythmic impulse is measurable via MRI (Greitz 2005). Still point temporarily increases CSF flow amplitude (Upledger 1995, case series). CSF pulsation drives glymphatic clearance (Fultz 2019). ME/CFS has impaired glymphatic flow from multiple causes. Hermisson doesn't address manual glymphatic priming.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.08
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Caregiver-delivered occipital still point induction (10 min, supine, pre-care) will increase post-care DTI-ALPS glymphatic index by ≥10% at 30 min post-care compared to sham hold (same hand position, occipital contact, no intent/pressure), in a single-blind crossover design in severe ME/CFS.

## Category 6: Combinations + Access

### Idea 6.1: Multi-Lock Care Protocol (MLCP) — Domain-Stratified Care Prescription

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The paper's multi-lock model proposes six co-occurring biological domains (autoimmune, mitochondrial, neuroinflammatory, dysautonomia, endothelial, mast cell). Each care intervention has a different metabolic cost and PEM risk profile that depends on which domains are active in that patient. An MCAS-positive patient tolerates bathing poorly (heat, touch → mast cell degranulation) but may tolerate oral care well. A dysautonomia-dominant patient tolerates supine care well but positional changes poorly. The MLCP uses Tier 2 phenotyping results to prescribe a personalised care formulary: for each care activity, a risk score (1-5) is assigned based on the patient's domain profile, and only low-risk (1-2) activities are performed on high-symptom days while medium-risk (3) activities are deferred. This formalises what experienced caregivers do intuitively with no evidence base.
- **Evidence link:** Tier 2 phenotyping framework already in the paper (Chapter 4). Hermisson care guide provides the care activity inventory. Domain-specific care sensitivity is mechanistically plausible but entirely undocumented.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** An MLCP-generated personalised care schedule (care risk stratified by patient domain profile) will produce ≥30% fewer PEM episodes/patient-week than standard Hermisson care (all care delivered in scheduled blocks regardless of domain profile), with equivalent prevention of medical complications (pressure injuries, dehydration, contractures), over 12 weeks in a cluster-randomised home care trial.

### Idea 6.2: Biofeedback-Linked Caregiver Alert System with HRV Fractal Dimension

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Standard HRV metrics (SDNN, RMSSD, LF/HF ratio) capture linear autonomic tone but miss the nonlinear dynamics that predict imminent decompensation. Detrended fluctuation analysis (DFA) $alpha_1$ — the short-term fractal scaling exponent of HRV — decreases before physiological crises (sepsis, hypoglycaemia, vasovagal syncope). A wearable ECG patch (single-lead, 24h monitoring) streaming DFA-$alpha_1$ to a caregiver's phone could provide a 15-30 minute early warning of impending PEM or autonomic decompensation during care — far earlier than visible symptoms or patient stop signals. The caregiver sees a real-time trend line: if DFA-$alpha_1$ drops below 0.75 (indicating loss of fractal organisation → approaching decompensation), the care session is paused, the patient rests supine, and care is deferred.
- **Evidence link:** DFA-$alpha_1$ predicts vasovagal syncope (Virag 2018), sepsis decompensation (Ahmad 2009), and exercise intolerance (Gronwald 2019). Linear HRV is blunted in ME/CFS (Escorihuela 2020). Hermisson stop-signal relies on patient/caregiver detection but PEM onset is delayed.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.15
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A wearable-based DFA-$alpha_1$ alert (threshold <0.75, sustained >5 min) will precede caregiver-observed PEM signals (facial tension, respiratory rate increase, patient discomfort) by ≥15 minutes in ≥60% of care-triggered PEM events in severe ME/CFS, providing actionable pre-PEM lead time.

### Idea 6.3: "Tiered Care Triage" — Severity × Domain × Resource Decision Matrix

- **Category:** 6
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Hermisson assumes multidisciplinary team availability, but most severe ME/CFS patients receive care from a single family caregiver with no specialist backup. A formal triage matrix that maps (severity level) × (domain profile) × (caregiver resources) → (minimum viable care protocol) could reduce the decision burden on untrained caregivers. The matrix has three tiers: Tier A (all care elements possible — full Hermisson protocol), Tier B (compromised — essential care only with domain-stratified risk adjustment per Idea 6.1), Tier C (crisis — only life-sustaining care, caregiver in protective mode). The caregiver answers 3 questions (severity today, how many caregivers available, any new red-flag symptoms) and the matrix outputs the day's care schedule. This is an implementation tool, not a biological insight — but implementation is the bottleneck for care guide uptake.
- **Evidence link:** Caregiver burnout is identified as a direct threat by Hermisson. ME/CFS care access is inequitable (Pendergrast 2021). Triage matrices exist in disaster medicine (START), palliative care (Palliative Performance Scale), and ICU (SOFA score). No triage tool exists for home-based severe ME/CFS care.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Caregivers using the tiered care triage matrix for 8 weeks will report ≥30% lower caregiver strain index scores and ≥20% fewer "care decision uncertainty" events (moments when they didn't know whether to proceed with or defer a care activity) compared to caregivers using Hermisson guidance without a decision matrix, matched for patient severity.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### Idea 7.1: Stochastic Care-PEM Threshold Model with State-Dependent Hazard

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The Hermisson rolling PEM concept can be formalised as a stochastic threshold process. Let $S(t)$ be the patient's "PEM susceptibility state" — a latent variable representing combined metabolic debt, neuroinflammatory activation, and epigenetic stress load. Each care activity $i$ at time $t_i$ has a metabolic cost $c_i$ (measurable via indirect calorimetry or estimated from activity type/duration) and stochastically triggers PEM with probability $P(PEM_i) = 1 - exp(-lambda(S(t_i)) * c_i)$, where $lambda(S)$ is a state-dependent hazard function. The key model feature: $lambda(S)$ increases after each care activity (care sensitises the patient to subsequent care), and decays exponentially with rest time constant $tau$. This creates the observed rolling PEM phenomenon — when care intervals are shorter than the decay constant, $S(t)$ ratchets upward, and PEM probability approaches 1.0 with each successive care session. The model has three patient-specific parameters ($tau$, baseline $lambda_0$, $lambda(S)$ slope) estimable from 2-week care diaries and directly informs optimal care spacing.
- **Evidence link:** Rolling PEM is described by Hermisson. PEM latency is 12-72h (Van Oosterwijck 2010). Stochastic threshold models are used in migraine trigger modelling (Houle 2017) and seizure prediction (Cook 2013). No formal PEM threshold model exists.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The model fitted to individual patient care-PEM diary data (2 weeks) will predict the subsequent 2 weeks of PEM events with AUC ≥0.75, and the fitted $tau$ parameter will correlate with recovery-time-lengthening red-flag status (Tier 3 criterion), with patients in rolling PEM showing $tau$ ≥48h.

### Idea 7.2: Caregiver-Patient Coupled Oscillator Model for Dyad Stability

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The caregiver-patient dyad can be modelled as two coupled oscillators: the patient's physiological stress state $P(t)$ and the caregiver's compassion capacity $C(t)$. $P(t)$ oscillates with care-induced spikes and rest-induced decay (per Idea 7.1). $C(t)$ oscillates with care-delivery depletion (compassion fatigue) and off-duty recovery. The coupling is bidirectional: higher $P(t)$ increases the depletion rate of $C(t)$ (sicker patient → faster caregiver burnout); lower $C(t)$ increases the PEM hazard for the patient (exhausted caregiver → care errors, missed stop signals, protocol non-adherence). The model has a critical coupling strength above which the dyad enters a downward spiral: patient worsening → caregiver exhaustion → worse care → more patient worsening. The model identifies the minimum respite ratio ($C$-recovery time / $C$-depletion time) needed to prevent spiral, as a function of patient severity and caregiver resilience.
- **Evidence link:** Hermisson identifies caregiver burnout as a direct threat to patient stability. Coupled oscillator models exist for therapist-patient alliance dynamics in psychotherapy (Tschacher 2014) and for caregiver-patient dyads in dementia (Reisberg 1999). No formal model exists for ME/CFS caregiver-patient dynamics.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In dyads where the fitted coupling strength exceeds the critical threshold (model-derived), patients will show progressive functional decline (FUNCAP-27 slope) over 6 months, while dyads below the threshold will show stable or improving trajectories, controlling for baseline severity.

### Idea 7.3: HSAT2 EV Propagation Model with Care-Mediated Transmission

- **Category:** 7
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If HSAT2 propagates via exosomes (per Evdokimova 2019), and caregivers have close physical contact with patients, a simple compartmental model (SIR-adapted) can estimate the risk of HSAT2 EV transfer from patient to caregiver. The patient is a chronic "infected" source (constant HSAT2 EV shedding). Each care contact is a transmission event with probability $p$ proportional to contact intensity × duration × barrier protection. Caregivers who "acquire" HSAT2 (elevated plasma EVs) may become secondary transmitters to other household members — or, more speculatively, may themselves develop subclinical ME/CFS-like immune changes (fatigue, MDSC elevation, reduced NK function). The model estimates the steady-state proportion of caregivers with elevated HSAT2 as a function of care intensity and barrier precaution adherence. This directly informs occupational health policy for professional ME/CFS carers — a domain entirely absent from Hermisson.
- **Evidence link:** HSAT2 EV propagation documented in cell culture (Evdokimova 2019). Caregivers handle patient bodily fluids. SIR models adapted for non-infectious transmission exist (obesity as "social contagion" — Christakis 2007). No model exists for HSAT2 transmission in care contexts.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.05
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a compartmental model simulated with plausible parameters ($p=0.001-0.01$ per high-intensity care contact, caregiver HSAT2 clearance half-life = 7 days), the predicted caregiver HSAT2 prevalence will be 5-15% among full-time caregivers (≥40h/week contact), which will be empirically confirmed or refuted by plasma HSAT2 EV qPCR in a caregiver cohort study.

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### Idea 8.1: Locked-In Syndrome Communication Matrix for Very-Severe ME/CFS

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Very-severe ME/CFS patients who cannot speak, move, or tolerate any sensory input share functional communication barriers with locked-in syndrome (LIS) patients — with the critical difference that ME/CFS patients' communication impairment is energy-limited, not motor-neuron-destroyed. LIS communication protocols (eye-tracking, binary "yes/no" eye blinks, brain-computer interfaces using EEG or fNIRS) could be adapted for ME/CFS with modifications: (a) all communication must be caregiver-initiated, not patient-initiated (to avoid the energy cost of spontaneous communication); (b) the communication window is time-limited (e.g., 1-2 minutes maximum); (c) only binary questions are asked (yes/no); (d) the patient's response modality is the one requiring the fewest muscles (single finger twitch > eye blink > EEG-based). This could restore autonomy to very-severe patients who are currently completely dependent.
- **Evidence link:** LIS communication protocols are well-established (Birbaumer 2014, Chaudhary 2022). EEG-based brain-computer interfaces require no motor output. Hermisson addresses non-verbal communication (finger tap, bell) but doesn't stratify by the energy cost of different communication modalities.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Very-severe ME/CFS patients (n≥10) provided with a caregiver-initiated binary communication system (eye-blink yes/no, 2-minute max communication window, caregiver-initiated only) will achieve ≥80% communication success rate (correct answer obtained) with caregiver accuracy ≥90%, with no increase in daily PEM frequency compared to baseline no-communication days.

### Idea 8.2: Pediatric Medical Complexity Care Coordination Model

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Children with medical complexity (CMC) — patients with multi-system chronic conditions requiring >3 specialists, home medical equipment, and daily caregiver-delivered medical interventions — are the closest existing care model to severe ME/CFS. CMC programs provide: a single care coordinator who schedules all appointments, manages equipment, resolves insurance issues, and serves as the family's single point of contact; structured home care protocols with nurse training for family caregivers; respite care funding; and school/homebound education coordination. The CMC infrastructure exists in most children's hospitals and could be adapted for severe ME/CFS adults — the care coordination burden is structurally identical (multiple specialists, home equipment, caregiver training, insurance navigation) but ME/CFS patients have no equivalent program.
- **Evidence link:** CMC care coordination reduces hospitalisation by 40-60% and caregiver stress by 30% (Cohen 2012, Kuo 2016). CMC programs are reimbursed by Medicaid in 18 US states. Hermisson identifies multidisciplinary coordination as essential but provides no implementation model.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Severe ME/CFS patients (adult) enrolled in a CMC-style care coordination program (dedicated coordinator, structured home protocols, respite funding, single point of contact for all specialists) will show ≥40% fewer emergency department visits and ≥30% lower caregiver strain scores over 12 months compared to matched historical controls receiving standard fragmented care.

### Idea 8.3: Chemotherapy-Induced Fatigue Prehabilitation Model

- **Category:** 8
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Cancer prehabilitation — preparing patients physically, nutritionally, and psychologically before major treatment (surgery, chemotherapy) to improve tolerance and recovery — is an evidence-based model with direct relevance to severe ME/CFS care. ME/CFS patients don't have scheduled "treatment," but their care activities are analogous: bathing, medical appointments, and transfers are predictable high-energy-cost events. A prehabilitation protocol before known high-cost events: (1) nutritional pre-loading 2h before (elemental formula, 200-300 kcal, to prevent care-induced hypoglycaemia without requiring digestion); (2) prophylactic rest 30 min before in glymphatic-enhancing conditions (dark, silent, supine); (3) pre-care compression garment application while supine; (4) pre-care medication (e.g., mast cell stabiliser if MCAS-positive). This is already partially in Hermisson (prophylactic rest) but the full prehabilitation model — borrowed from oncology — adds nutritional, hemodynamic, and pharmacological pre-loading.
- **Evidence link:** Cancer prehabilitation improves surgical outcomes, reduces complications, and shortens hospital stay (Carli 2017, Santa Mina 2020). Nutritional pre-loading (clear carbohydrate drink 2h pre-op) is standard in enhanced recovery after surgery (ERAS) protocols. Hermisson includes prophylactic rest but not the full prehabilitation framework.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A 4-element prehabilitation protocol (nutritional pre-load + glymphatic rest + compression + domain-specific pre-medication) applied before all scheduled care events of intensity ≥3/5 will reduce post-care PEM severity by ≥40% compared to Hermisson-standard prophylactic rest alone, in a crossover trial in severe ME/CFS.

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### Idea 9.1: Pre-Care HRV Fractal Dimension as Real-Time Care Tolerance Index

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** DFA-$alpha_1$ of heart rate variability (the short-term fractal scaling exponent, measured from 5-minute ECG segments) quantifies the "complexity" of autonomic regulation. Values near 1.0 indicate healthy fractal organisation; values below ~0.75 indicate loss of physiological complexity and predict imminent decompensation in multiple conditions (sepsis, syncope, exercise intolerance). In severe ME/CFS, DFA-$alpha_1$ may drop before a care session pushes the patient over the PEM threshold — even when linear HRV metrics (SDNN, RMSSD) appear normal. A 5-minute supine ECG before each care block could provide a "care tolerance index": if DFA-$alpha_1$ <0.75 → defer non-essential care; 0.75-0.90 → proceed with caution, micro-pace with activity spoilers; >0.90 → proceed with standard protocol. This is implementable with a single-lead wearable ECG patch and a smartphone app.
- **Evidence link:** DFA-$alpha_1$ predicts exercise intolerance at lactate threshold (Gronwald 2019, Rogers 2021), vasovagal syncope (Virag 2018), and sepsis decompensation (Ahmad 2009). Linear HRV is blunted in ME/CFS but its predictive value for imminent PEM is unknown. Hermisson stop signal is symptom-based, but symptoms are delayed.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.20
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-care DFA-$alpha_1$ <0.75 (5-min supine ECG) will predict care-triggered PEM within 24h with sensitivity ≥60% and specificity ≥75% in severe ME/CFS, outperforming linear HRV metrics (SDNN, RMSSD) for the same prediction (DeLong test p<0.05), in a prospective daily-monitoring study over 4 weeks.

### Idea 9.2: Care-Triggered Exosomal HSAT2 Release as Dynamic PEM Biomarker

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If PEM involves transient amplification of HSAT2 transcription via HSF1 activation (per HSAT2-PEM speculation, Chapter 14), then plasma exosomal HSAT2 RNA should rise after a PEM-triggering event and decline during recovery. In the care context, this means a care session that triggers PEM should produce a measurable spike in circulating HSAT2-containing EVs at 12-48h post-care — not because the care itself releases EVs, but because care-induced metabolic stress activates HSF1 which drives further HSAT2 transcription in cells already epigenetically primed. This would make plasma HSAT2 EV levels a dynamic biomarker of "PEM biology in progress" — distinct from static severity markers. Serial measurement (pre-care, +12h, +24h, +48h, +72h) could identify the HSAT2 peak and its correlation with symptom severity, establishing HSAT2 as the first dynamic molecular correlate of PEM.
- **Evidence link:** HSAT2 is HSF1-inducible (Vourc'h 2022). HSAT2 EVs propagate in cell culture (Evdokimova 2019). PEM is hypothesised to involve HSAT2 amplification (Chapter 14 speculation). No study has measured HSAT2 EVs before/after PEM triggers.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.10
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Plasma exosomal HSAT2 RNA (qPCR) will increase ≥2-fold from baseline at 12-48h after a PEM-triggering care session in ≥60% of post-viral ME/CFS patients (≤3 years onset), with AUC of HSAT2 elevation correlating with PEM severity (VAS) at r ≥0.50 within-subject; healthy controls and non-post-viral ME/CFS patients will show no HSAT2 response to equivalent care.

### Idea 9.3: GPCR Autoantibody Titre as Pre-Care PEM Risk Stratifier

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** $beta_2$-adrenergic autoantibodies correlate with sympathovagal imbalance (Azcue 2026, r=0.45). Care procedures that provoke sympathetic activation (bathing, positional changes, medical examinations) will have a greater physiological cost in patients with high GPCR AAb titres because: (a) the acute sympathetic response to care is partially blocked by $beta_2$-AAbs at vascular and cardiac receptors, requiring higher catecholamine output for the same effect → greater metabolic cost; (b) $beta_2$ receptor signalling regulates mitochondrial biogenesis, so chronic blockade impairs mitochondrial recovery from care-induced ATP depletion; (c) M3/M4 muscarinic AAbs impair parasympathetic recovery (the "brake" that should restore autonomic balance after care). A single GPCR AAb panel (5 receptors: $beta_1$, $beta_2$, M3, M4, $alpha_1$) measured at baseline could stratify patients into high-risk (≥3 AAbs above reference range → predicted poor care tolerance, need for extreme micro-pacing) vs low-risk (0-1 AAbs elevated → standard care protocol).
- **Evidence link:** GPCR AAbs are elevated in 30-60% of ME/CFS (Loebel 2016, Bynke 2020). Azcue 2026: $beta_2$-AAb correlates with autonomic dysfunction. Hermisson does not incorporate biomarker-stratified care risk.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.18
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Patients with ≥3 GPCR AAbs above reference range will show ≥2× higher care-triggered PEM frequency per week compared to patients with 0-1 elevated AAbs, matched for disease severity (FUNCAP-27 score), in a prospective cohort of 60 severe ME/CFS patients receiving standardised Hermisson care protocols over 4 weeks.

### Idea 9.4: Bedside Pupillometry as Real-Time Autonomic Reserve Meter

- **Category:** 9
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The pupillary light reflex is mediated by the autonomic nervous system: parasympathetic (constriction via Edinger-Westphal nucleus) and sympathetic (dilation via superior cervical ganglion). Pupillometry — measuring latency, constriction velocity, and dilation velocity using a handheld infrared pupillometer — is an established ICU neuromonitoring tool. In ME/CFS, where autonomic dysfunction is nearly universal, pupillary parameters may reflect real-time autonomic reserve: slower constriction velocity = parasympathetic insufficiency; slower re-dilation velocity = sympathetic exhaustion. A 30-second pupillometry reading before each care block could serve as a non-invasive, zero-effort, accurate "autonomic fuel gauge" — if constriction velocity is below threshold, the patient's parasympathetic recovery capacity is depleted and care should be deferred. This is far more practical than HRV analysis (requires 5 min of clean ECG) for the chaotic home care environment.
- **Evidence link:** Pupillometry is used in ICU for neurological prognostication (Oddo 2018) and in diabetic autonomic neuropathy (Ferrari 2010). Pupillary abnormalities are documented in fibromyalgia (lower constriction velocity — Bilgen 2019). ME/CFS has no pupillometry data. Autonomic dysfunction is present in 70-90% of ME/CFS.
- **Preliminary certainty:** 0.12
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-care pupillary constriction velocity (measured by handheld pupillometer, 30s, supine) will correlate with same-day PEM probability (r ≥|0.40|) and will decline progressively across successive care blocks on high-PEM-risk days compared to low-PEM-risk days (paired t-test p<0.01), within-subject, over 4 weeks of daily monitoring in severe ME/CFS.
