# Brainstorm: Cold Face Immersion / Diving Reflex for Autonomic Modulation in ME/CFS

**Date:** 2026-06-07
**Context:** 12 papers (GoriniPereira2024, Schlader2016 anchors). CFI = coordinated vagal + sympathetic co-activation (not pure vagal). Vagal activation requires 0°C; ≥7°C = sympathetic-only. 10-20 bpm HR drop, MAP ↑23 mmHg, vasoconstriction 44-72%. Zero ME/CFS/POTS safety data. tVNS (ch14b) is purer vagal but weaker. CFI in ch17 (cert 0.20 after down-revision from 0.30). Other relevant chapters: ch8 (neuro), ch10 (autonomic + vascular).

---

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1.1: Autonomic Paradox Resolution — CFI Sympathetic Co-Activation as Structured Training Signal
**Mechanism:** ME/CFS has unopposed, poorly regulated sympathetic overactivity (high LF/HF, low HRV). CFI produces a qualitatively different sympathetic activation — reflex-mediated, precisely sequenced (vagal surge first → sympathetic plateau → recovery), orchestrated by NTS. This structured autonomic trajectory may retrain appropriate sympathovagal coupling at the NTS level, analogous to how structured exercise benefits ME/CFS despite exercise intolerance. The temporal ordering (vagal first, sympathetic second) may be critical for restoring baroreflex sensitivity.
**Evidence:** Panneton2020 (reflex circuit), Schlader2016 (two-phase time course: vagal surge 0-2min, sympathetic plateau 2-15min), Martinez2025 (Hebbian plasticity at NTS-vagal synapse), ME/CFS sympathetic overactivity literature
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** 4-week daily CFI (0°C, 40s) in ME/CFS reduces baseline LF/HF and improves baroreflex sensitivity (sequence method) despite acute sympathetic activation during each session. The acute CFI-evoked MAP rise attenuates over 4 weeks while vagal response sensitizes.

### H1.2: Vagal Reserve Hypothesis — CFI Response Predicts tVNS Responsiveness
**Mechanism:** CFI activates the full trigeminal→NTS→vagal efferent pathway. The bradycardia magnitude reflects efferent vagal functional capacity ("vagal reserve"). ME/CFS patients with blunted CFI bradycardia (<8 bpm) may have impaired efferent vagal limb (nucleus ambiguus or efferent fibers) and would be poor tVNS responders. Those with preserved response have intact efferent but deficient afferent vagal signaling — better tVNS candidates. CFI = functional test of vagal efferent integrity.
**Evidence:** Heath1990 (large between-subject variability: 10-20 bpm range), Konishi2016 (diurnal modulation), Panneton2020 (efferent pathway via nucleus ambiguus), Martinez2025 (state-dependent plasticity)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients with CFI bradycardia <8 bpm show <20% RMSSD improvement on 4-week tVNS; those with >15 bpm show >40% improvement (r > 0.6). CFI response at baseline predicts tVNS response with AUC >0.80.

### H1.3: NTS Attractor State Hypothesis — CFI Forces Shift from Sympathetic-Dominant Fixed Point
**Mechanism:** Chronic sympathetic overactivity in ME/CFS represents a maladaptive attractor state in the NTS autonomic network — stable, self-reinforcing, resistant to perturbation. CFI provides a strong, patterned input that forces the NTS out of this attractor into a coordinated vagal-sympathetic cycle. Repeated CFI provides the repeated force necessary to shift the attractor landscape, establishing a new, healthier equilibrium (greater vagal tone, appropriate sympathovagal coupling). Analogous to CPG rehabilitation in spinal cord injury — rhythmic patterned input retrains central circuits.
**Evidence:** Martinez2025 (Hebbian plasticity at NTS-vagal synapse), Panneton2020 (NTS as central integrator), Caspers2011 (quantified τ = 10.4s enables precise input timing), general dynamical systems theory of central autonomic networks
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** Rodent model of stress-induced sympathetic hyperactivity (chronic social defeat): 4 weeks daily CFI attenuates NTS c-Fos/FosB expression (neuronal activation marker), normalizes baroreflex gain, and reduces baseline plasma norepinephrine. NTS slice electrophysiology shows altered spontaneous firing patterns after conditioning.

### H1.4: CFI as Provocative Test for Autonomic Decompensation Risk — Rebound Hypotension
**Mechanism:** CFI creates a controlled autonomic stress: vagal surge → sympathetic plateau → vasoconstriction release. Post-CFI vasodilation rebound (44-72% vasoconstriction reversing) creates a sudden BP challenge. ME/CFS patients with impaired baroreflex compensation may fail to counter-regulate → transient hypotension → correlates with clinical orthostatic intolerance. The pattern of hypotension (depth, duration, symptoms) reveals decompensation risk that resting measures miss.
**Evidence:** Schlader2016 (sustained MAP +23 mmHg, then return to baseline — rebound dynamics not measured), Heath1990 (vasoconstriction 44-72% — release dynamics not measured), ME/CFS baroreflex impairment literature
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** Post-CFI MAP drop below pre-CFI baseline >10 mmHg identifies ME/CFS patients with clinical orthostatic intolerance (NASA lean test score ≥4) with sensitivity >80%. Magnitude of rebound hypotension correlates with future syncope risk at 6-month follow-up (r > 0.5).

### H1.5: Trigeminal Cold Hypersensitivity in ME/CFS — TRPM8/TRPA1 Sensitization
**Mechanism:** ME/CFS patients have reported cold intolerance and altered pain processing. If trigeminal TRPM8 (cold) or TRPA1 (noxious cold) channels are sensitized (via inflammation, oxidative stress, or central sensitization), the trigeminal afferent barrage from CFI would be amplified, producing exaggerated diving reflex and/or cold pain disproportionate to stimulus. This could be both a biomarker (quantitative sensory testing of trigeminal cold threshold) and a mechanistic barrier (excessive sympathetic response due to pain pathway overlap).
**Evidence:** GoriniPereira2024 (temperature thresholds), Panneton2020 (trigeminal V1 pathway), TRP channel sensitization in chronic pain literature, ME/CFS cold intolerance (clinical reports)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients with cold allodynia (QST cold pain threshold <10°C on cheek) show ≥50% greater CFI bradycardia and MAP increase than those without, independent of baseline HRV. QST cold pain threshold predicts CFI tolerability (R² > 0.4).

## 2. Research Directions

### R2.1: Single-Session CFI Safety and Tolerability in ME/CFS — Phase 0 (PRIORITY #1)
**Design:** N=20 ME/CFS (10 mild-moderate, 10 severe), 10 healthy controls. Single session: 5 min supine rest, CFI (0°C, 40s, bilateral compress, per Heath1990 optimal parameters), 15 min supine recovery, then 5-min standing orthostatic challenge. Continuous HR, beat-to-beat BP (Finapres/Nexfin), HRV. Symptom VAS q2min (dizziness, nausea, cold discomfort, chest tightness). 24h follow-up for delayed symptoms, PEM.
**Primary:** Proportion completing full 40s CFI. Adverse events (presyncope, syncope, excessive BP response).
**Secondary:** ΔHR, ΔMAP, ΔRMSSD at each phase. Post-CFI standing BP stability vs pre-CFI standing.
**Rationale:** Zero safety data in any dysautonomia population. This is the unavoidable prerequisite for all subsequent ideas.
**Evidence:** All 12 studies (healthy only), Heath1990 (methodological standard)
**Certainty:** 0.75 (highest — the study is needed regardless of outcome)
**Falsifiable:** ≥80% of ME/CFS patients complete full 40s CFI without presyncope. Post-CFI standing BP stable (ΔMAP <10 mmHg from pre-CFI standing). Severe ME/CFS subgroup shows greater BP lability but no syncope.

### R2.2: Temperature Titration — Finding ME/CFS-Specific Optimal Temperature
**Design:** Within-subject, N=15 ME/CFS, 15 controls. 5 temperatures (0, 5, 10, 15, 20°C) × 40s bilateral compress, randomized order, 20-min washout. Continuous HR, BP, HRV. Cold pain VAS after each. Therapeutic index = (ΔRMSSD / ΔMAP) × comfort_score.
**Primary:** Temperature maximizing therapeutic index in each group.
**Rationale:** GoriniPereira2024 showed temperature-dependent vagal-sympathetic dissociation in healthy. ME/CFS may have shifted thresholds — possibly needing colder stimulus due to blunted vagal responsiveness, or warmer stimulus due to cold hypersensitivity.
**Evidence:** GoriniPereira2024 (temperature dose-response: vagal only at 0°C, sympathetic at ≤7°C), Heath1990 (0°C > 5°C > 10°C > 15°C)
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients have a higher optimal temperature (e.g., 5°C TI > 0°C TI) due to cold hypersensitivity reducing comfort, or a lower optimal temperature (e.g., -2°C needed) due to blunted vagal responsiveness.

### R2.3: 4-Week Daily CFI Conditioning Protocol
**Design:** N=30 ME/CFS, randomized: daily CFI (0°C, 40s, bilateral, morning per Konishi2016) vs sham (34°C compress) vs WL. Outcomes at baseline, week 4: resting HRV (5-min supine, 5-min standing), baroreflex sensitivity (sequence method), orthostatic tolerance (NASA lean test), acute CFI challenge response (before/after conditioning — plasticity measure), PEM-VAS daily diary.
**Primary:** ΔRMSSD (resting supine, baseline → week 4).
**Secondary:** CFI-evoked HR drop change from week 1 to week 4 (sensitization vs habituation).
**Rationale:** Martinez2025 proposes Hebbian plasticity at diving reflex circuit. First-ever repeated CFI conditioning study in any population.
**Evidence:** Martinez2025 (plasticity hypothesis), Konishi2016 (diurnal — morning optimal), Caspers2011 (τ = 10.4s informs duration), AlHaddad2010 (single-session post-exercise benefit)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** 4-week daily CFI increases resting RMSSD by ≥30% in ME/CFS vs sham. Acute CFI-evoked HR drop magnitude increases (sensitization: +20% from week 1 to week 4) or decreases (habituation: -20%), indicating plasticity.

### R2.4: CFI + Ambulatory BP Monitoring — 7-Day Home Safety
**Design:** N=15 ME/CFS. CFI daily at home for 7 days with ABPM (24h pre-period, 7 days of CFI, 24h post-period). Logbook: CFI timing, duration, tolerability, symptoms, PEM. Primary: change in 24h BP profile.
**Rationale:** Schlader2016 found 15-min face cooling elevated MAP +23 mmHg sustained. Extrapolation to repeated daily exposure: need to know if CFI causes cumulative BP alteration, nocturnal non-dipping, or orthostatic instability.
**Evidence:** Schlader2016 (sustained BP rise), ABPM standard methods
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** 7-day daily CFI does not significantly alter mean 24h BP, daytime BP, nighttime BP, or nocturnal dipping (all p > 0.10 vs pre-period). No increase in presyncope/syncope during CFI sessions over 7 days.

### R2.5: CFI vs tVNS Head-to-Head Mechanism in ME/CFS
**Design:** N=20 ME/CFS, crossover: (A) single CFI (0°C, 40s), (B) single tVNS (left tragus, 25Hz, 30min), (C) sham (warm compress or sham tVNS). Random order, ≥48h washout. HR, BP, HRV continuously. Plasma catecholamines (NE, Epi) at baseline, 2, 10, 30 min post-stimulus. Pupillometry (sympathetic index).
**Primary:** Δplasma NE (CFI vs tVNS) — tests Panneton2020 circuit prediction that CFI engages RVLM (symp) while tVNS does not.
**Rationale:** Ch14b has tVNS RCT evidence (POTS). Ch17 has CFI (low cert). Head-to-head comparison determines which patients should use which modality.
**Evidence:** Panneton2020 (CFI: trigeminal→RVLM direct; tVNS: ABVN→NTS only), Shamsuzzaman2014 (MSNA during diving), ME/CFS tVNS literature
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Falsifiable:** CFI increases plasma NE ≥50% from baseline; tVNS produces no significant NE change. CFI reduces HR ≥12 bpm with MAP +15 mmHg; tVNS reduces HR ≤5 bpm with MAP unchanged. These differences are consistent across ME/CFS and controls.

### R2.6: CFI Effect on Cerebral Blood Flow (fNIRS/TCD)
**Design:** N=15 ME/CFS (with cognitive dysfunction), 15 controls. CFI (0°C, 40s) with concurrent fNIRS (prefrontal HbO2/HbR) or TCD (MCA velocity). Cognitive battery (N-back) during CFI.
**Primary:** Δ prefrontal HbO2 during CFI, ME/CFS vs controls.
**Rationale:** CFI evokes MAP surge (→ cerebral perfusion increase) with vagal activation (→ possible NVC improvement). ME/CFS has impaired cerebral autoregulation (Badhwar2025). CFI's BP surge could overload impaired autoregulation → paradoxical CBF decrease, or improve NVC via vagal activation.
**Evidence:** Schlader2016 (BP dynamics), ME/CFS cerebral autoregulation literature (vanCampen2024, Badhwar2025)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** During CFI, ME/CFS patients show greater CBFv increase than controls (MAP surge × impaired autoregulation), but post-CFI show greater CBFv drop below baseline (rebound). Cognitive performance during CFI improves in controls but declines in ME/CFS.

### R2.7: CFI for PEM Prevention — Post-CPET Application
**Design:** N=20 ME/CFS. Baseline CPET. Randomized: CFI (0°C, 40s, bilateral) at 30min and 2h post-CPET vs sham (34°C). HR/BP/HRV monitoring 48h. PEM-VAS daily × 3d, actigraphy.
**Primary:** RMSSD recovery trajectory (time to return to 90% of baseline).
**Secondary:** PEM severity at 24h (VAS), PEM duration.
**Rationale:** AlHaddad2010 showed CFI accelerated post-exercise parasympathetic reactivation in healthy adults. PEM involves prolonged autonomic dysfunction. Post-exertion CFI might enhance vagal recovery and reduce PEM severity.
**Evidence:** AlHaddad2010 (post-exercise parasympathetic reactivation), ME/CFS PEM literature
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** CFI post-CPET shortens RMSSD recovery time to 90% of baseline by ≥50% vs sham. PEM-VAS at 24h is ≥30% lower with CFI. Effect driven by patients with higher baseline vagal tone.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### D3.1: Propranolol Pretreatment — Widen CFI Therapeutic Window
**Mechanism:** Low-dose non-selective β-blocker (10-20mg, single dose, 60 min before CFI). Blunts CFI-evoked MAP surge (β1: HR/CO, β2: vascular) while preserving vagal HRV increase (muscarinic, not β-mediated). If CFI therapeutic benefit depends on vagal activation and risk comes from BP surge, β-blockade widens the therapeutic window.
**Dosing:** 10-20mg propranolol, 60 min pre-CFI.
**Risks:** First-dose hypotension, fatigue. Start 10mg.
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** Propranolol (20mg) reduces CFI-evoked MAP increase by ≥50% (+23 → +11 mmHg) while preserving ≥80% of RMSSD increase (≥120 ms) — effectively doubling therapeutic index (ΔRMSSD/ΔMAP). Combined safety + vagal benefit confirmed in N=10 open-label PK/PD.

### D3.2: Nebivolol — β1-Selective + NO-Enhancing for CFI Safety
**Mechanism:** β1-selective blocker that also stimulates eNOS → NO production via β3 agonism. Unlike propranolol, nebivolol spares β2 receptors (which mediate vasodilation) and actively counteracts vasoconstriction via NO. Unique dual mechanism: β1 blockade reduces the CO component of MAP surge, while NO enhancement preserves microvascular perfusion during CFI's vasoconstrictive phase.
**Dosing:** 2.5-5mg daily, 1-week run-in before CFI initiation.
**Risks:** Bradycardia, hypotension. Well-tolerated generally. NO enhancement is unique among β-blockers.
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** Nebivolol (5mg daily × 1 week) reduces CFI-evoked MAP rise by 40% and preserves finger blood flow (laser Doppler ≥80% of baseline) during CFI, vs placebo where finger flow drops to 28% of baseline (Heath1990). The NO effect (measured by flow-mediated dilation) directly correlates with finger flow preservation.

### D3.3: Low-Dose Atropine as Mechanistic Probe (Research Tool, Not Therapeutic)
**Mechanism:** Low-dose IV atropine (0.01 mg/kg) selectively blocks muscarinic M2 receptors on cardiac SA node → blocks vagal efferent. Comparing CFI response with and without atropine would quantify the vagal contribution to bradycardia vs the contribution from β-adrenergic withdrawal (sympathetic reduction).
**Use:** Single-dose probe study, not therapy. Establishes whether CFI bradycardia in ME/CFS is vagal-mediated (atropine-sensitive) or primarily due to sympathetic withdrawal (atropine-resistant). If resistance, CFI's "vagal" claim is misleading.
**Evidence:** Panneton2020 (atropine abolishes diving bradycardia in animals — cervical vagal cold block), human atropine studies
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFS, atropine reduces CFI bradycardia by ≥70% (confirming vagal efferent mediation) — same as animals. If <50% reduction, the bradycardia is not primarily vagal in ME/CFS, challenging the rationale for vagal conditioning via CFI.

### D3.4: Amiloride (Topical, ENaC Blocker) for Cold Sensation Modulation
**Mechanism:** ENaC (epithelial sodium channels) co-mediate cold transduction in trigeminal neurons alongside TRPM8. Topical amiloride (500 µM, as ophthalmic solution or custom gel) may reduce trigeminal cold afferent firing → less intense cold sensation → better CFI tolerability. Unlike anesthetic (which would block the reflex), partial ENaC blockade spares the TRPM8-mediated vagal component.
**Certainty:** 0.20 (weak preclinical evidence for ENaC in cold transduction)
**Falsifiable:** Topical amiloride (500 µM, 10 min pre-CFI) reduces cold pain VAS by ≥30% without attenuating CFI bradycardia by >15%, dissociating cold sensation from reflex vagal activation.

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### S4.1: L-Menthol (Topical, TRPM8 Agonist) — Lower Temperature Threshold for Vagal Activation
**Mechanism:** TRPM8 is the primary cold transduction channel in trigeminal afferents. Topical L-menthol (1-3% cream) applied to trigeminal V1 distribution (forehead, nose, cheeks) 5 min before CFI activates TRPM8 partially, effectively lowering the temperature threshold for vagal reflex activation. This could allow CFI at 5-10°C (warmer, less aversive) to produce the same vagal response normally requiring 0°C.
**Dosing:** 1-3% L-menthol cream/spray (commercially available), 5 min pre-CFI, applied to forehead + nose + cheeks.
**Risks:** Topical, minimal absorption. Cool/skin sensation. Avoid eye contact. Generally well-tolerated.
**Rationale:** Temperature threshold is 0°C for vagal activation (GoriniPereira2024). This is aversive and limits clinical use. TRPM8 agonism could shift the threshold, making CFI practical.
**Evidence:** TRPM8 as primary cold receptor (McKemy2002 Nature), GoriniPereira2024 (0°C threshold for vagal), menthol pharmacology (TRPM8 agonist)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** 3% menthol cream pre-treatment 5 min before face cooling at 5°C produces HR drop ≥80% of the magnitude of 0°C CFI without menthol. Menthol + 0°C combination does not cause excessive bradycardia (HR drop ≤25 bpm). Menthol alone (without cold) does not produce bradycardia.

### S4.2: Oral L-Menthol (Enteric-Coated, Systemic TRPM8)
**Mechanism:** Systemic menthol activates TRPM8 throughout the body, altering thermoregulatory set-point. May produce a "cooling bias" that amplifies or sensitizes trigeminal cold afferent response. More speculative than topical but could provide sustained sensitization rather than pre-treatment window.
**Dosing:** 200-400mg enteric-coated L-menthol, 60 min pre-CFI.
**Risks:** GI upset, menthol odor, rare allergic reactions. Generally recognized as safe (GRAS) in food amounts; higher doses need monitoring.
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** Oral menthol (400mg) increases CFI-evoked HR drop by ≥30% compared to placebo at same water temperature, or enables ≥5°C warmer water to achieve equivalent HR drop.

### S4.3: Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA) — Chronic Vagal Tone Enhancement
**Mechanism:** Omega-3 PUFAs increase vagal tone via GPR120/FFAR4 signaling in brainstem, increasing HRV and reducing resting HR. Chronic supplementation raises baseline vagal tone, potentially amplifying the CFI-evoked vagal surge. Also reduces systemic inflammation, contributing to improved thermoregulation (Schlader2018: heat stress attenuates CFI vagal response — reduced inflammation may improve thermoregulatory control).
**Dosing:** 2-4g EPA+DHA/day, 8-week run-in before assessing CFI response.
**Risks:** Fishy burps, mild anticoagulation. Very safe.
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** 8-week omega-3 (4g/day) increases baseline RMSSD by ≥30% in ME/CFS. CFI-evoked RMSSD absolute increase is larger post-omega-3 (≥50% greater ΔRMSSD), though fold-change may be smaller (ceiling effect). Effect size correlates with baseline omega-3 index (r > 0.4).

### S4.4: Vitamin D Repletion — Restore Vagal Tone and Cold Tolerance
**Mechanism:** VDR expressed in brainstem autonomic nuclei (NTS, DMV). Vitamin D deficiency (prevalent in ME/CFS — limited sun exposure) is associated with lower HRV and higher sympathetic tone. Repletion may raise baseline vagal tone, amplifying CFI vagal response. Also may modulate TRP channel expression (some evidence for VDR regulation of TRPM8).
**Dosing:** 2000-5000 IU/day to target 50-80 ng/mL.
**Risks:** Very safe. Monitor 25(OH)D at 3 months.
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients with vitamin D deficiency (<20 ng/mL) have blunted CFI bradycardia (<10 bpm) vs replete (>30 ng/mL, >15 bpm). 12-week repletion normalizes CFI bradycardia to match naturally replete levels.

### S4.5: Capsaicin (Topical, Low-Dose) — TRPV1 Desensitization for Cold Pain
**Mechanism:** Low-dose capsaicin (0.025%) topically applied to trigeminal distribution initially activates then desensitizes TRPV1 channels. If noxious cold sensation during CFI involves TRPV1 (polymodal, responds to both heat and cold extremes), pre-desensitization could reduce CFI pain without affecting the TRPM8-mediated vagal reflex.
**Dosing:** 0.025% capsaicin cream to face (avoid eyes), 5×/day for 2 weeks before beginning CFI program (desensitization period).
**Risks:** Initial burning sensation (expected). Eye contact painful. Wash hands thoroughly.
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** 2-week topical capsaicin desensitization reduces CFI cold pain VAS by ≥40% without attenuating CFI bradycardia by >10%. The TRPV1-dependent pain component and TRPM8-dependent reflex component are dissociable.

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### N5.1: Graduated Temperature CFI Protocol — Cold Conditioning
**Protocol:** Week 1: 15°C (30s) → Week 2: 10°C (35s) → Week 3: 5°C (40s) → Week 4: 0°C (40s). Morning, seated, bilateral compress. Track cold sensation VAS, tolerability, acute HR/BP response daily.
**Rationale:** Gradual cold conditioning may: (a) improve tolerability and adherence, (b) reduce the startle/shock response that amplifies sympathetic activation, (c) allow TRPM8/TRPA1 adaptation, (d) build patient confidence. By week 4, 0°C feels manageable rather than shocking.
**Evidence:** GoriniPereira2024 (temperature thresholds), cold habituation literature (Janský: repeated cold exposure reduces sympathetic response, increases brown fat activity), Wim Hof method (anecdotal)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** Graduated CFI achieves ≥90% completion rate at 0°C by week 4 vs ≤60% for immediate 0°C CFI. After 4 weeks, resting RMSSD increase is equivalent between groups, but the graduated group has lower cold pain VAS (≤3 vs ≥6/10) and higher adherence.

### N5.2: Short-Duration CFI (10-15s) for Vagal-Predominant Window
**Protocol:** 0°C bilateral compress for 10-15s (not 40s). Rationale: HR decay τ = 10.4s → ~63% of max bradycardia by 10s, ~86% by 21s. Schlader2016: vagal surge peaks within first 2 min, sympathetic dominates after. A 10-15s CFI may capture substantial vagal activation while avoiding the sustained sympathetic vasoconstriction and MAP rise that emerge with longer duration.
**Trade-off:** Accept lower peak vagal activation for minimal sympathetic co-activation.
**Evidence:** Caspers2011 (τ = 10.4s — time constant informs optimal short duration), Schlader2016 (two-phase: vagal early, sympathetic late), Heath1990 (optimal = 40s for max response, but did not test 10-15s as conditioning vs maximal)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** 15s CFI produces 60% of the RMSSD increase of 40s CFI (good) but only 30% of the MAP increase (therapeutic advantage). Post-CFI standing MAP drop with 15s CFI is ≤5 mmHg vs ≥15 mmHg with 40s CFI. ME/CFS patients prefer 15s over 40s (comfort VAS ≥7 vs ≤4/10).

### N5.3: CFI Without Breath-Hold — Slow Breathing During Immersion
**Protocol:** Face immersed in 0°C water OR cold compress applied while maintaining slow nasal breathing (6 breaths/min via snorkel if water-immersed, or nose-breathing with compress). Removes the apnea-induced chemoreflex sympathetic activation (Shamsuzzaman2014 showed apneic component independently drives MSNA).
**Rationale:** Shamsuzzaman2014: simulated diving (cold + apnea) produces synergistic sympathetic activation exceeding sum of individual stimuli. Removing apnea removes this synergy, making CFI safer while preserving trigeminal → vagal activation.
**Evidence:** Shamsuzzaman2014 (cold + apnea synergy), slow breathing HRV literature (ch14a), slow breathing reduces chemoreflex sensitivity
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** CFI without breath-hold (slow nose breathing during 40s cold compress) produces the same RMSSD increase as CFI with breath-hold but only 50% of the MAP increase. Plasma NE is significantly lower without breath-hold. No difference in MSNA surge (peripheral vasoconstriction) between conditions.

### N5.4: Cold Gel Mask (Reusable, Standardized)
**Protocol:** Silicone gel face mask (wraparound, covers V1 distribution: forehead to cheekbones), stored in freezer (-10°C), applied 40s. Reusable, reproducible contact area, controlled temperature decay (gel warms gradually vs ice-bag melt), no water mess, patient-friendly.
**Advantages:** Standardized stimulus (eliminates variability of compress saturation, coverage), better V1 coverage, controlled temperature decay profile (slow warming means sustained cold through 40s), feels less "aggressive" than ice-water.
**Limitation:** Skin-interface temperature may be higher than 0°C water (gel + plastic barrier have lower thermal conductivity than water). Need to validate that achievable cold activates the reflex.
**Evidence:** Heath1990 (ice bag protocol), physics of thermal conductivity (gel ≈ 0.3 W/m·K vs water ≈ 0.6 W/m·K)
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable:** Commercial cold gel mask (freezer -10°C, 40s application) produces ≥80% of the HR drop of 0°C water immersion. Patient preference strongly favors gel mask (≥70% would continue daily vs ≤30% for ice-water). Skin temperature drops to ≥8°C (still within reflex-activating range per GoriniPereira2024).

### N5.5: Face Fan (Cold Air, -5°C Directed Airflow)
**Protocol:** High-velocity fan directing -5°C air at face (trigeminal V1). Avoids water mess, aspiration risk, wet skin. Convenient for repeated home use. Adjustable: flow rate, temperature, duration, oscillation.
**Limitation:** Air has ~25× lower thermal conductivity than water. At 0°C air may not extract heat fast enough to activate the reflex. May require sub-zero air temperature. Frostbite risk at very low temperature.
**Evidence:** Physics: water = 0.6 W/m·K vs air = 0.025 W/m·K. No air-based CFI literature exists.
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** -5°C air fan produces ≥60% of the HR drop of 0°C water immersion (compensating for lower conductivity with higher ΔT gradient and higher flow rate). No frostbite, no discomfort >5/10. 0°C air produces <30% of water response.

### N5.6: Evening CFI Protocol (Exploit ME/CFS Circadian Shift)
**Protocol:** CFI performed at 5-7pm (evening) rather than morning (Konishi2016 optimal in healthy). Rationale: ME/CFS patients have shifted circadian phase ("tired-but-wired," evening symptom exacerbation). Evening vagal activation may be more clinically meaningful for sleep quality and nocturnal autonomic recovery.
**Evidence:** Konishi2016 (morning = maximal in healthy young men — but ME/CFS has circadian disruption), ME/CFS circadian/sleep literature
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients show opposite diurnal pattern: CFI bradycardia is larger in evening than morning (ΔHR evening ≥15 bpm vs morning ≤10 bpm). Evening CFI improves sleep quality (actigraphy SE +5%, WASO -20min) and next-day fatigue more than morning CFI.

## 6. Combinations

### C6.1: CFI + tVNS Sequential Protocol — Priming for NTS Plasticity
**Protocol:** Daily: tVNS (25Hz, left tragus, 30 min) → immediately followed by CFI (0°C, 40s, bilateral). tVNS raises NTS excitability via vagal afferent input (pure vagal, no sympathetic). CFI then delivers the strong trigeminal→NTS→vagal efferent signal. tVNS-primed NTS may show greater Hebbian plasticity from patterned CFI input.
**Rationale:** Shared convergence on NTS (Panneton2020: trigeminal→NTS; tVNS: ABVN→NTS). If NTS excitability is a limiting factor for CFI-induced plasticity (Martinez2025), tVNS pre-activation may remove this bottleneck.
**Evidence:** Panneton2020 (shared NTS convergence), Martinez2025 (Hebbian plasticity), tVNS literature (ch14b)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** tVNS pre-treatment (30 min) increases CFI-evoked HR drop by ≥25% vs CFI alone, indicating NTS facilitation. After 4 weeks daily tVNS+CFI, resting RMSSD increase is ≥50% greater than either alone. Acute CFI response facilitation correlates with resting RMSSD improvement (r > 0.5).

### C6.2: CFI + Slow Breathing Protocol — The "Autonomic Reset"
**Protocol (5 min total):** (1) 2 min slow breathing (6 breaths/min, 5s in/5s out, nose) — raises baseline HRV, primes vagal tone. (2) 40s CFI (0°C compress, exhaling during immersion, no breath-hold) — captures vagal surge. (3) 2.5 min slow breathing recovery — accelerates post-CFI autonomic normalization.
**Rationale:** Slow breathing pre-CFI increases vagal baseline (respiratory sinus arrhythmia) → may lower threshold for CFI vagal activation (ceiling/floor: higher baseline may mean larger absolute response). Slow breathing post-CFI accelerates rebalancing of vagal-sympathetic ratio after the sympathetic plateau, reducing risk of rebound hypotension.
**Evidence:** AlHaddad2010 (post-exercise CFI effect), slow breathing HRV literature (ch14a), Shamsuzzaman2014 (apnea-free protocol)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** CFI+breathing combination produces larger total RMSSD AUC (integrated vagal activation) than CFI alone or breathing alone. Post-CFI MAP returns to baseline within 60s vs >120s with CFI alone. ME/CFS patients rate combination as more comfortable (VAS ≥7/10) than CFI alone (≤5/10).

### C6.3: CFI + Compression Stockings — Protect Against Post-CFI Orthostatic Drop
**Protocol:** Thigh-high graduated compression stockings (20-30 mmHg) worn during CFI session. CFI seated. Remain seated 5 min post-CFI before standing (with stockings still on). Stockings maintain venous return during the post-CFI vasodilation rebound.
**Rationale:** Post-CFI, the 44-72% vasoconstriction releases, creating a sudden drop in peripheral resistance and a relative hypovolemic challenge. Compression stockings counteract this by mechanically supporting venous return, preventing orthostatic BP drop in the vulnerable window.
**Evidence:** Heath1990 (vasoconstriction magnitude 44-72%), Schlader2016 (BP dynamics post-cooling not measured), compression literature in POTS/orthostasis
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** CFI + compression stockings results in ≤5 mmHg post-CFI standing MAP drop vs ≥15 mmHg with CFI alone. Orthostatic symptom scores (dizziness, lightheadedness) during post-CFI standing are ≥50% lower with compression. Patients with pre-existing orthostatic intolerance benefit most.

### C6.4: CFI as PEM Abortive — Rescue Protocol
**Protocol:** At first subjective sign of impending PEM (characteristic mental fog, heaviness, specific pre-PEM sensation), patient performs CFI (0°C, 20s — shorter abortive dose, since the goal is acute vagal surge not conditioning). Compare to standard PEM management (rest, hydration) in a 4-week symptom diary with event-contingent recording.
**Rationale:** PEM involves escalating sympathetic activation, inflammation, and autonomic dysregulation. A well-timed vagal burst may abort this cascade at the earliest phase — analogous to abortive migraine medication (triptan at aura onset).
**Evidence:** AlHaddad2010 (acute vagal restoration), Martinez2025 (acute vagal surge effect on central circuits), anecdotal PEM premonitory symptoms
**Certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable:** In 4-week diary study (N=20 ME/CFS, event-contingent recording), CFI at first PEM symptom reduces progression to full PEM by ≥40% (proportion of episodes where PEM-VAS at 24h ≤ 3) vs no-intervention episodes. Shorter abortive CFI (20s) is as effective as full 40s.

## 7. Mathematical Model

### M7.1: Two-Phase Autonomic Response ODE — Temperature-Dependent Biphasic HR/MAP
**Variables:**
- V(t) = vagal efferent activity (0-1, from NTS → nucleus ambiguus)
- S(t) = sympathetic efferent activity (0-1, from NTS → RVLM)
- T(t) = trigeminal cold afferent input (function of water temperature)
- H(t) = heart rate (deviation from baseline)
- M(t) = MAP (deviation from baseline)

**NTS-level dynamics:**
```
dV/dt = α_v · T(t) · (1 - V) - β_v · V      (vagal: fast activation, τ_v ~10s)
dS/dt = α_s · T(t - δ)^2 · (1 - S) - β_s · S (sympathetic: delayed by δ, quadratic threshold)
```

**Key feature:** Sympathetic activation has quadratic temperature dependence — at T for 7°C, S activates but V does not (GoriniPereira2024 threshold). At T for 0°C, both activate. The delay δ ≈ 20s captures the later onset of sympathetic phase.

**Effector response:**
```
H(t) = H₀ - γ_v · V(t) + γ_s · S(t)     (vagal ↓HR, sympathetic ↑HR, net biphasic)
M(t) = M₀ + μ_s · S(t) - μ_v · V(t) · (1 - B(t))  (baroreflex B dampens vagal depressor effect on MAP)
dB/dt = (M(t) - M₀) / τ_B - G(t) · B    (baroreflex gain, suppressed by diving gate G)
G(t) = g · T(t) / (K_g + T(t))           (cold input opens diving gate, suppressing baroreflex)
```

**Predictions:**
- At 0°C: Rapid HR drop (V fast, τ ~10s), partial HR recovery as S builds (slower, τ ~30-60s), sustained MAP rise (S dominates after ~2 min)
- At 7°C: No HR drop (V below threshold), MAP rise from S-only (smaller than 0°C)
- At 20°C: No response (both below threshold)
- Post-immersion: G decays → baroreflex re-engages with high M set point → transient undershoot (rebound hypotension)

**Evidence:** Caspers2011 (monoexponential τ = 10.4s), Schlader2016 (two-phase time course), GoriniPereira2024 (temperature threshold), Panneton2020 (baroreflex suppression)
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Falsifiable:** Model fits existing HR/MAP trajectories from Heath1990, Schlader2016, and GoriniPereira2024 with R² > 0.90. Model predicts that at temperature 3°C, S activates but V partially activates (~30% of max), producing a unique triphasic HR (drop → partial recovery → plateau). Repeated CFI shifts β_s (sympathetic decay rate) and β_v (vagal decay rate), altering trajectory, testable by re-estimating model parameters from week 1 vs week 4 data.

### M7.2: Baroreflex-CFI Interaction Model — Predicting Rebound Hypotension
**Model extension of M7.1:** Baroreflex normally buffers BP changes with gain g₀. During CFI, diving gate G(t) suppresses baroreflex gain by factor (1 - G):
```
G(t) = g_max · T(t)^n / (K_g^n + T(t)^n)   (sigmoidal gating)
```
When immersion ends (T → 0), G decays with τ_G → baroreflex re-engages at full gain, but M(t) is still elevated from the S-dominated plateau → baroreflex interprets high M as error → drives S down and V up → BP overshoot below baseline. The overshoot magnitude depends on τ_G, the rate of T decay, and baseline baroreflex gain g₀.

**ME/CFS parameter set:** Lower g₀ (impaired baroreflex) → less intra-CFI BP rise (less to suppress) BUT greater post-CFI BP drop (less effective counter-regulation of the simulated "excess" BP).

**Evidence:** Panneton2020 (baroreflex inhibition during diving), ME/CFS baroreflex impairment (literature), Schlader2016 (post-cooling BP not reported — this is a model prediction that fills a data gap)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** Model predicts ME/CFS patients with lowest baroreflex sensitivity (sequence method) have smallest CFI MAP increase (<15 mmHg) but greatest post-CFI MAP drop (>10 mmHg below baseline). Tilt-table BP recovery time correlates with model-derived baroreflex recovery parameter τ_G (r > 0.6). Empirical measurement of post-CFI BP confirms the predicted rebound.

### M7.3: Bayesian Optimal Parameter Search — Temperature × Duration × Frequency Response Surface
**Approach:** Define response surface V = f(T, D, Freq) and S = f(T, D, Freq). Therapeutic index TI = ΔRMSSD / (ΔMAP × Δpain). Use noisy priors from literature (Heath1990: D=40s, T=0°C; GoriniPereira2024: T thresholds; Caspers2011: τ=10.4s) to constrain search. Update posteriors with finite ME/CFS pilot data (N=15 per proposed R2.2).
**Utility:** Identifies optimal operating parameters for ME/CFS before large trials. May reveal that different optimal parameters exist for different ME/CFS subtypes.
**Evidence:** Existing data provides priors. Bayesian optimization framework standard in dose-finding trials.
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** Bayesian-identified optimal parameters in ME/CFS (T=0°C, D=25s, Freq=once daily) differ from literature-based healthy optimal (T=0°C, D=40s, Heath1990). Posterior-predicted TI validated in N=20 crossover: predicted optimal yields TI = predicted value within 95% CrI.

### M7.4: Hebbian Plasticity Model at Trigeminal→NTS→Vagal Synapse
**Plasticity rule:**
```
Δw = η · [f(V_pre, V_post) - θ]
```
where w = synaptic weight, V_pre = trigeminal afferent firing rate (cold input), V_post = vagal efferent firing rate (output), η = learning rate, θ = plasticity threshold.

**STDP-like timing dependence:** f(V_pre, V_post) is maximal when V_pre precedes V_post by ~10-50ms (Hebbian pairing). CFI provides repeated, precisely timed V_pre-V_post pairs (τ = 10.4s time constant ensures consistent activation order).

**Disease modification:** ME/CFS may have higher θ (elevated plasticity threshold due to inflammation, oxidative stress, or impaired NMDAR function) → same CFI input produces less plasticity → requires more sessions or stronger input.

**Prediction:** RMSSD increase over weeks of daily CFI follows sigmoidal time course (slow start → rapid mid-phase → plateau). Time to plateau depends on θ — patients with blunted CFI response at week 0 (proxy for high θ) require more sessions. After washout, weight decays partially but not to baseline (retained plasticity).

**Evidence:** Martinez2025 (Hebbian plasticity at NTS-vagal synapse), STDP literature, ME/CFS inflammation → impaired plasticity (general literature)
**Certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable:** RMSSD over 4-week daily CFI follows sigmoidal trajectory (R² > 0.85 for sigmoid vs linear fit). 2-week washout after 4 weeks shows partial decay (retained gain 40-60%). Patients with lowest week-0 CFI response (<8 bpm HR drop) require ≥6 weeks to reach plateau (vs ≤3 weeks for high responders).

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### B8.1: CFI vs Cold Pressor Test — Comparing Trigeminal vs Spinal Cold Reflexes in ME/CFS
**Bridge:** CPT (hand in 0°C water, 2 min) = spinal cold afferents → spinothalamic tract → sympathetic-only activation (pain → SNS). CFI (face in 0°C water, 40s) = trigeminal cold afferents → trigeminal nerve → NTS → vagal + sympathetic. Comparing responses within the same patient dissociates generalized sympathetic hyperreactivity (CPT) from specific trigeminal-vagal pathway function (CFI).
**Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show exaggerated CPT BP response (≥30 mmHg MAP increase vs ≤20 in controls — existing literature supports exaggerated CPT in ME/CFS) but BLUNTED CFI HR drop (≤8 bpm vs ≥15 bpm in controls). This dissociation identifies a specific trigeminal→vagal pathway deficit, not generalized autonomic hyperreactivity.
**Evidence:** ME/CFS CPT literature (exaggerated BP/pain response), Panneton2020 (separate neuroanatomical pathways), GoriniPereira2024 (CFI temperature threshold)
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable:** Individual patients show CPT↑ + CFI↓ (exaggerated CPT, blunted CFI) as the dominant pattern distinct from controls (both moderate). The CPT↑/CFI↓ ratio predicts vagal symptoms (orthostatic intolerance, GI dysmotility; r > 0.5) better than either test alone.

### B8.2: CFI ↔ Raynaud's Phenomenon — Vasoconstriction Exaggeration as Safety Signal
**Bridge:** Raynaud's involves exaggerated cold-induced vasoconstriction (TRPM8-mediated local reflex + sympathetic drive). ME/CFS has known vasomotor constriction bias (ch10). CFI-evoked finger blood flow reduction (44-72%, Heath1990) may be exaggerated in Raynaud-prone ME/CFS patients → excessive/rebound risk.
**Prediction:** ME/CFS patients with cold extremities (acrocyanosis, low baseline finger temperature, or Raynaud's comorbidity) show ≥85% finger blood flow reduction during CFI (vs ≤72% per Heath1990) and prolonged recovery (>10 min to return to 50% of baseline flow).
**Bridge to therapy:** Raynaud treatments (CCBs: nifedipine; PDE5 inhibitors: sildenafil; topical nitrates) may protect against CFI-induced excessive vasoconstriction. Pre-treatment with topical nitroglycerin (2% ointment to digital area) could permit CFI use in vasoconstriction-prone patients.
**Evidence:** Heath1990 (finger blood flow ↓72%, toe ↓59%), ME/CFS vasomotor constriction bias (ch10), Raynaud's treatment literature
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients with clinical acrocyanosis show finger blood flow reduction ≥85% during CFI (laser Doppler) vs ≤72% in those without. Topical nitroglycerin (2%, 30 min pre-CFI, applied to finger) normalizes flow reduction to ≤72%. The treatment → reduced vasoconstriction → maintained CFI vagal response (HR drop unchanged).

### B8.3: CFI ↔ Migraine — Trigeminal-Vascular Overlap and Headache Risk
**Bridge:** Migraine involves trigeminal V1 activation → CGRP release → vasodilation → pain. CFI activates same trigeminal V1 afferents via cold. In migraine-prone patients (comorbid with ME/CFS), CFI could trigger headache via trigeminal CGRP release. Alternatively, repeated CFI could desensitize trigeminal afferents → reduce migraine frequency (mechanism similar to tVNS for migraine, which activates vagal afferents that inhibit trigeminal nociception via NTS→spinal trigeminal nucleus).
**Prediction:** ME/CFS patients with comorbid migraine have higher rate of headache during/after CFI (≥30%) vs ME/CFS without migraine (≤10%). However, 4-week daily CFI may reduce migraine frequency (trigeminal desensitization).
**Evidence:** Trigeminal V1 → trigeminal nociceptive pathway (migraine literature), tVNS for migraine (vagal→trigeminal inhibition via NTS), ice pack use in acute migraine (symptomatic)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** Acute: ME/CFS+migraine patients report ≥30% headache rate during/within 30 min of CFI vs ≤10% in migraine-free ME/CFS. Chronic: After 4-week daily CFI, migraineur ME/CFS patients show reduced monthly migraine days (≥3 fewer vs baseline) suggesting trigeminal desensitization.

### B8.4: CFI ↔ Long COVID Dysautonomia — Shared Autonomic Phenotype
**Bridge:** Long COVID dysautonomia shares orthostatic intolerance, low HRV, sympathetic overactivity with ME/CFS. Some Long COVID patients have paradoxical water intolerance (showering → presyncope). CFI response may be a cross-condition endophenotype.
**Prediction:** CFI response pattern does not differ between post-COVID ME/CFS, post-EBV ME/CFS, and non-viral ME/CFS (all show blunted vagal, exaggerated sympathetic). This supports a final common autonomic pathway regardless of triggering pathogen.
**Evidence:** Long COVID autonomic literature (overlapping abnormalities), ME/CFS autonomic literature (ch10), GoriniPereira2024 (CFI threshold)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** CFI-evoked HR drop, MAP increase, and RMSSD change do NOT differ significantly between post-COVID (n=30), post-EBV (n=30), and non-viral-onset (n=30) ME/CFS subgroups (all p > 0.20). The CFI response pattern is a trait marker of established ME/CFS, not a trigger-specific marker.

### B8.5: CFI ↔ Vasovagal Syncope — Vagal Hyperreactivity as Contraindication
**Bridge:** VVS involves exaggerated vagal responses to stressors (emotional, orthostatic, pain). CFI's vagal surge could trigger syncope in VVS-prone patients. ME/CFS with comorbid NMH/VVS may be at risk for CFI-induced bradycardia/syncope.
**Prediction:** ME/CFS patients with positive tilt-table test or VVS history show ≥30 bpm HR drop during CFI (vs ≤15 bpm in ME/CFS without VVS) and ≥20% rate of presyncope/syncope during/to post-CFI.
**Implication:** These patients should be excluded from CFI therapy. However, a blunted CFI response (HR drop <8 bpm) in VVS patients would be even more informative — suggesting insufficient vagal reactivity to mount protective counter-regulation → identifies a higher-risk subgroup.
**Evidence:** VVS literature (exaggerated vagal reactivity), Heath1990 (between-subject variability includes some with large HR drops)
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS + VVS comorbidity shows ≥30 bpm CFI HR drop vs ME/CFS alone ≤15 bpm. ≥20% of VVS-comorbid patients experience presyncope during/within 5 min post-CFI. A screening CFI (10s test dose) identifies VVS patients who should not proceed to full 40s protocol (sensitivity >80% using HR drop >20 bpm at 10s as predictor of syncope at 40s).

## 9. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### Dx9.1: CFI Response Pattern as Autonomic Dysfunction Endotype Classifier
**Three predicted endotypes from CFI response cluster analysis (n ≥ 100):**
- **Type A (Blunted vagal):** HR drop <8 bpm, ΔRMSSD <50%, normal MAP. Impaired trigeminal→vagal pathway. → Better candidates for tVNS (direct vagal afferent bypasses trigeminal). Associated with dysautonomia-predominant ME/CFS.
- **Type B (Exaggerated sympathetic):** MAP ↑ >30 mmHg, strong initial vagal but sustained sympathetic, prolonged post-CFI hypotension. Intact reflex but hyperreactive. → Best candidates for CFI conditioning (has capacity for plasticity). Associated with hyperadrenergic POTS overlap.
- **Type C (Exaggerated vagal):** HR drop >25 bpm, MAP ↔ no change (vagal fully compensates). → Contraindication for CFI (syncope risk). Associated with VVS/NMH overlap.
**Evidence:** Heath1990 (large between-subject variability), GoriniPereira2024 (individual variability in thresholds), ME/CFS endotype literature (ch3/4)
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFS (n=100), cluster analysis on CFI parameters (ΔHR, ΔMAP, ΔRMSSD, ΔHRV, post-CFI ΔMAP, recovery τ) identifies ≥3 stable clusters. Clusters correlate with clinical features: Type A has worst orthostatic tolerance, Type B has highest NE and lowest initial HRV, Type C has highest syncope history. Type A shows best tVNS response in prospective sub-study.

### Dx9.2: CFI BNP Response — Cardiac Strain Safety Marker
**Measurement:** NT-proBNP at baseline, 2 min (peak of sympathetic phase), 30 min (recovery) post-CFI. Rationale: CFI increases afterload (MAP +23 mmHg) and may reduce preload (impaired filling from bradycardia + vasoconstriction). Combined effect may transiently increase LV wall stress. BNP rise identifies patients where CFI imposes hemodynamic strain.
**Prediction:** Most ME/CFS patients show no significant BNP change (safe). A subset (PEM-cardiac phenotype, or those with subclinical LV dysfunction) show ≥20% BNP increase — indicating cardiac strain from the combined afterload+preload stress. These patients should use shorter CFI duration or avoid.
**Evidence:** Schlader2016 (hemodynamic load from CFI), BNP physiology (wall stress)
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFS, ≥20% of patients show NT-proBNP increase >20% from baseline at 2 min post-CFI. These patients have lower LV ejection fraction (echo, ≥5% lower) and higher post-CFI PEM rate (≥40% vs ≤10% in BNP-negative group). BNP rise of >30% → contraindication to 40s CFI, but safe with 15s (no BNP rise).

### Dx9.3: CFI + Pupillometry for Autonomic Subtype Dissociation
**Measurement:** Pupillometry during CFI (baseline, 10s intervals, 2 min post). Pupil dilation = sympathetic activation (α1-mediated). Pupil constriction = parasympathetic activation (muscarinic). CFI-evoked pupil changes dissociate vagal (HR, HRV) from sympathetic (pupil, MAP) components of diving reflex.
**Prediction:** Pupil dilation >0.5mm during CFI identifies patients with excessive sympathetic response (MAP rise >30 mmHg, plasma NE increase >100%), independent of HR response. Combined HR+pupil monitoring provides real-time autonomic balance readout.
**Evidence:** Pupillometry-sympathetic correlation (established literature), real-time pupil tracking (commercial devices available)
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable:** CFI-evoked pupil dilation peak correlates with CFI-evoked MAP increase (r > 0.6) and plasma NE (r > 0.5) in ME/CFS. Pupil dilation >0.5mm predicts excessive MAP rise (>30 mmHg) with sensitivity >80%, specificity >75%. Pupil recovery time (to 50% of peak) correlates with post-CFI MAP recovery time (r > 0.6).

### Dx9.4: CFI as Repeated-Measures Autonomic Plasticity Biomarker
**Concept:** Standardized CFI challenge (0°C, 40s, bilateral compress) administered at each clinic visit (weeks 0, 4, 12, 24) during any autonomic intervention (tVNS, CFI training, β-blocker, etc.). Track ΔHR, ΔMAP, ΔRMSSD, and recovery τ over time. Changes in reaction to a fixed stimulus quantify plasticity of the diving reflex circuit.
**Prediction:** Patients whose CFI-evoked HR drop INCREASES over weeks of autonomic therapy (sensitization/improved vagal efferent function) show better clinical outcomes. Those whose CFI response decreases (habituation or stagnation) show worse outcomes.
**Evidence:** Martinez2025 (plasticity), Konishi2016 (state-dependent response), Caspers2011 (quantified response enables tracking)
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable:** Increase in CFI-evoked HR drop from week 0 to week 4 (ΔΔHR ≥ 3 bpm) correlates with ≥30% improvement in resting RMSSD and ≥2-point improvement in fatigue (Chalder) at week 12 (r > 0.5). Non-responders show no change or decreased CFI response. CFI plasticity biomarker outperforms baseline resting HRV in predicting treatment response (AUC 0.80 vs 0.60).
