## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 The Astrocyte Lactate Shuttle Failure as ME/CFS Core Mechanism

**Rationale:** The ASD model posits astrocytic glucose dysfunction → impaired lactate delivery to neurons → synaptic ATP shortage → energy-saving adaptation. In ME/CFS, the astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle (ANLS) failure may be the specific cellular mechanism producing cognitive PEM. Unlike general "brain hypometabolism," this identifies the cellular locus (astrocyte endfeet) and substrate (lactate) of the failure. The ANLS is required precisely for sustained glutamatergic transmission — the cortex's dominant energy consumer — meaning its failure would disproportionately affect PFC-dependent cognitive tasks and explain the PEM cognitive cliff.

**Evidence link:** FDG-PET hypometabolism (both ASD and ME/CFS); elevated ventricular lactate on MRS (Syan 2025, Mueller 2020 — consistent with unmet neuronal lactate demand); astrocyte dysfunction documented in ME/CFS CSF proteomics.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show reduced arterial-to-venous lactate gradient across the brain (measured by jugular vs radial vein sampling) during cognitive challenge, indicating impaired astrocytic lactate export. 7T MRS will show elevated bulk lactate but reduced lactate+creatine ratio in grey matter specifically. Creatine supplementation + ketone esters, which bypass ANLS, will improve cognitive performance more than glucose loading.

### 1.2 PFC Vulnerability via Metabolic Constraint on Social Cognition

**Rationale:** The ASD model argues social cognition was evolutionarily sacrificed because it is metabolically expensive (PFC-dependent theory of mind, empathy, mentalizing require sustained high-frequency firing in high-metabolic-rate regions). In ME/CFS, the same logic predicts social cognitive deficits act as a metabolic stress test: when global brain energy is constrained, the PFC's social cognitive networks are the first to degrade. This predicts ME/CFS patients show specific, measurable impairments in social cognition (Reading the Mind in the Eyes, empathic accuracy) that correlate with PFC hypometabolism severity more strongly than with general cognitive impairment. Social cognitive decline could serve as a behavioral biomarker for brain energy status.

**Evidence link:** PFC is highest-metabolism brain region and predicts ASD and ME/CFS vulnerability (Blagojevic-Stokic 2026; Xu 2026 brain-centered framework); social cognitive deficits documented in ME/CFS (small literature, ~5 studies, not consistently linked to brain metabolism).

**Provisional certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will score lower on the Reading the Mind in the Eyes test (social cognition) than performance-IQ-matched controls, and these scores will correlate with PFC FDG-PET uptake (r > 0.5). Non-social PFC tasks (working memory N-back) will also correlate but more weakly. Social cognition scores will decline further during PEM episodes. If social cognition is preserved despite general cognitive impairment, this hypothesis is falsified.

### 1.3 Developmental Metabolic Programming as ME/CFS Predisposition

**Rationale:** ASD is neurodevelopmental; the brain energy model suggests early-life metabolic programming (maternal glucose environment, prenatal inflammation, neonatal nutrition) sets astrocytic GLUT1 expression and glycolytic capacity permanently. If the same prenatal/perinatal factors that predispose to ASD also lower brain energy reserve, they could explain ME/CFS vulnerability emerging decades later — without any ASD diagnosis. This posits a "subclinical ASD metabolic phenotype": individuals with normal neurodevelopment but ASD-like brain energy constraints (low GLUT1, reduced astrocytic glycogen stores, blunted glycolytic response). These individuals would be normotypic until a metabolic challenge (infection, inflammation, stress) exposes the latent constraint.

**Evidence link:** Prenatal risk factors overlap between ASD (maternal diabetes, obesity, fever) and later ME/CFS; GLUT1 is developmentally regulated; ALSPAC data could test this.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.20

**Falsifiable prediction:** In the ALSPAC birth cohort, children of mothers with gestational diabetes or elevated prenatal IL-6 will have higher ME/CFS rates at age 18-25, independent of ASD diagnosis. If prenatal factors predict adult ME/CFS only via ASD diagnosis, the hypothesis fails.

### 1.4 Glutamatergic Energy Crisis as PEM Trigger

**Rationale:** Glutamatergic signaling consumes ~80% of cortical energy (Na+/K+-ATPase to restore postsynaptic ion gradients). The ASD model argues energy-saving adaptation downregulates glutamatergic synapses. In ME/CFS, PEM after cognitive exertion may represent acute failure of this tradeoff: cognitive demand spikes glutamatergic signaling beyond the energy-constrained supply → local ATP depletion → NMDA receptor overactivation → excitotoxicity → prolonged recovery. This reframes PEM not as generic "energy depletion" but as a specific neurotransmitter-system energy crisis with a defined molecular signature. The delayed onset (hours post-exertion) matches the timescale of glutamatergic toxicity cascades rather than immediate ATP depletion.

**Evidence link:** MRS shows elevated glutamate in prefrontal cortex in some ME/CFS studies (Murrough 2017); ASD literature shows altered glutamate/GABA ratio; NMDA antagonists (memantine) show anecdotal benefit.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** During the PEM window (24-48h post-CPET), MRS in prefrontal cortex will show: elevated glutamate (excess synaptic release), decreased ATP (31P-MRS), and elevated lactate (glycolytic compensation). Memantine (NMDA antagonist) pre-dosing before cognitive challenge should attenuate the PEM cognitive decline. If glutamate does not rise during PEM, this hypothesis fails.

### 1.5 Shared Glycolytic Failure in Astrocytes and Immune Cells

**Rationale:** The ASD paper documents astrocytic glycolytic dysfunction as primary. ME/CFS shows the same pattern in brain. But immune cells also rely on aerobic glycolysis (Warburg effect) for activation — T cell proliferation, macrophage polarization, and NK cell cytotoxicity all require glycolytic upregulation. If the glycolytic defect is systemic (affecting both astrocytes and immune cells via shared regulators like HIF-1alpha, PKM2, or PFKFB3), this explains two paradoxes: (1) Why ME/CFS patients show "tired but wired" immune state — immune cells cannot sustain full activation (tired) but remain primed (wired) because glycolysis failure prevents completion of the activation-resolution cycle. (2) Why both cognitive and immune dysfunction worsen together — they share a single glycolytic bottleneck.

**Evidence link:** NK cell glycolysis impaired in ME/CFS (Nguyen 2019); PBMC metabolomics show glycolytic pathway alterations; ASD shows systemic mitochondrial/glycolytic defects (Frye 2024 meta-analysis, 204 studies).

**Provisional certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS PBMCs stimulated ex vivo will show blunted glycolytic induction (measured by Seahorse ECAR) compared to controls, and this defect will correlate with brain FDG-PET hypometabolism in same patients. The blunting will be strongest in NK cells and CD8+ T cells. If PBMC glycolysis is normal, the systemic-glycolysis model fails.

### 1.6 Energy-Sparing Neurodevelopmental Compensation Protects Against ME/CFS

**Rationale:** A provocative inversion: the ASD brain's developmental energy-saving adaptation may be PROTECTIVE against ME/CFS in adulthood. The ASD brain has already downregulated metabolically expensive glutamatergic synapses, reduced long-range connectivity, and pruned sociocognitive networks. When an infection or inflammatory insult strikes, this pre-adapted brain has lower baseline energy demand and thus higher metabolic reserve. The neurotypical brain, with its full complement of energy-expensive social cognition circuits, has further to fall. This predicts: (a) ASD individuals who contract COVID should have LOWER rates of ME/CFS than neurotypical individuals matched for infection severity; (b) the milder the ASD-related energy adaptation, the higher the ME/CFS risk.

**Evidence link:** Raw 2025 (n=267) found autistic traits predict prolonged COVID symptoms — but this measured traits, not diagnosis. Clinical ASD populations have not been separately analyzed for ME/CFS conversion. The prediction contradicts the predisposition model and is testable against it.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.15

**Falsifiable prediction:** In UK Biobank, individuals with clinical ASD diagnosis will show lower post-COVID ME/CFS rates than neurotypical controls (OR < 1.0). If ASD individuals show HIGHER rates, neurodevelopmental adaptation is not protective but additive (supporting predisposition model). If equal, both models incomplete.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### 2.1 The Astrocyte-Specific Biomarker Study

**Design:** Cross-sectional case-control, n=40 per group (ME/CFS, multiple sclerosis fatigue, major depressive disorder fatigue, healthy controls). Isolate astrocyte-derived extracellular vesicles (ADEVs) from plasma using anti-GLAST or anti-GFAP immunocapture. Quantify: GLUT1 expression, MCT1/4, lactate dehydrogenase A/B ratio, glycogen phosphorylase (brain isoform), hexokinase I, pyruvate kinase M2. Compare to neuronal-derived EVs (NCAM-capture) for compartment-specific metabolic profiling.

**Hypothesis:** ME/CFS ADEVs will show reduced GLUT1, reduced LDHB (lactate-producing isoform), and reduced MCT4 (astrocyte export transporter) compared to all control groups. Neuronal EVs will show intact markers.

**Cost:** $150-200K. Moderate. ADEV isolation methods established in neurodegeneration research (Goetzl 2016 et seq.). Key challenge: purity validation.

**Impact:** HIGH. Directly tests whether the ANLS failure is present at the molecular level. If confirmed, provides a blood-based biomarker for astrocytic metabolic dysfunction in ME/CFS.

### 2.2 CSF:Plasma Glucose Ratio Study in ME/CFS

**Design:** Single lumbar puncture study, n=30 ME/CFS, n=30 healthy controls. Measure paired CSF and plasma glucose, lactate, pyruvate, ketone bodies. Compute CSF:plasma glucose ratio (standard clinical test for GLUT1 deficiency). Also measure CSF:plasma lactate ratio (indicator of brain glycolytic export).

**Hypothesis:** A subset of ME/CFS patients (perhaps 20-40%) will show CSF:plasma glucose ratio below 0.50 (clinical threshold for GLUT1 deficiency syndrome is <0.60 in children, less established in adults). These patients will show different cognitive profiles and may preferentially benefit from ketogenic diet.

**Cost:** $50-100K. LP is moderately invasive; requires experienced physician. Paired glucose measurement is cheap.

**Impact:** HIGH. Could identify a defined, treatable ME/CFS subtype with a clear mechanism (impaired brain glucose transport). Direct translation opportunity: GLUT1 deficiency syndrome treatment (ketogenic diet) could be trialed in this subtype.

### 2.3 Social Cognitive fMRI with Metabolic Challenge in ME/CFS

**Design:** n=20 ME/CFS, n=20 controls. fMRI during social cognition task (empathic accuracy, mentalizing) and matched non-social cognitive task (working memory). Measure BOLD signal and use MRS to quantify prefrontal glutamate, lactate, and ATP (31P-MRS) during task performance. Repeat in ME/CFS group during a PEM window (24h post-CPET).

**Hypotheses:** (1) ME/CFS patients show reduced PFC BOLD during social vs non-social tasks, correlating with elevated baseline lactate. (2) During PEM, social cognition BOLD decreases disproportionately relative to non-social tasks. (3) PFC ATP levels (31P-MRS) decline during sustained social cognition in ME/CFS but not controls.

**Cost:** $200-300K. Requires fMRI + MRS capacity, CPET for PEM induction. High technical demand.

**Impact:** HIGH for mechanism. Links the specific social-cognitive predictions of the ASD energy model to ME/CFS. Demonstrates PEM has a measurable neuroimaging signature in social cognition networks.

### 2.4 GLUT1 Polymorphism Analysis in ME/CFS Cohorts

**Design:** Case-control genetic association study. Genotype SLC2A1 (GLUT1 gene) common variants (rs841, rs1385129, rs710218) and rare loss-of-function variants in ME/CFS biobanks (DecodeME, UK ME/CFS biobank) vs population controls. Also analyze SLC2A1 expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs) for association with severity.

**Hypothesis:** SLC2A1 variants that reduce GLUT1 expression or function — known to cause milder GLUT1 deficiency phenotypes (paroxysmal exercise-induced dyskinesia with fatigue) — are enriched in ME/CFS, particularly in patients with PEM-dominant or cognitive-dominant subtypes.

**Cost:** Low. Genotyping arrays already exist in DecodeME (n>17,000). Analysis only.

**Impact:** HIGH if positive (identifies genetic predisposition with clear mechanism), INFORMATIVE if negative (rules out common GLUT1 variants as major contributor, directing attention to acquired/regulatory causes).

### 2.5 Prenatal Metabolic Programming in ALSPAC Cohort

**Design:** Retrospective analysis of ALSPAC birth cohort (n~14,000). Exposure variables: maternal gestational diabetes, maternal obesity (BMI>30), maternal fever during pregnancy, maternal IL-6 levels (measured in stored prenatal sera). Outcome: ME/CFS diagnosis or severe fatigue at age 18/24. Covariates: ASD diagnosis, ADHD, sex, SES.

**Hypothesis:** Prenatal metabolic/inflammatory exposures predict adult ME/CFS independently of neurodevelopmental diagnosis. This would support the "subclinical ASD metabolic phenotype" (hypothesis 1.3). The compound exposure (diabetes + obesity + inflammation) will show strongest prediction.

**Cost:** Low. ALSPAC data access application is bureaucratic but data exists. Analysis can be done remotely.

**Impact:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Would add an entirely new etiological dimension (developmental programming) to ME/CFS research.

### 2.6 Ketogenic Diet RCT with Brain MRS Endpoints

**Design:** Randomized controlled trial, n=60 ME/CFS (30 modified ketogenic diet, 30 standard low-glycemic diet), 12-week intervention. Primary endpoint: change in brain lactate/creatine ratio on MRS. Secondary: cognitive battery, fatigue severity, PEM frequency. Stratify by baseline CSF:plasma glucose ratio (from study 2.2 or proxy).

**Hypothesis:** Ketogenic diet will reduce brain lactate and improve cognitive symptoms preferentially in patients with evidence of GLUT1 dysfunction (low CSF:plasma glucose ratio). The effect will be mediated by ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate) providing alternative fuel that bypasses GLUT1.

**Cost:** $300-500K. Diet intervention trials are logistically intensive. MRS adds cost but provides mechanistic endpoint.

**Impact:** HIGH. Only RCT design can establish efficacy. MRS endpoint links directly to brain energy mechanism. Stratification by GLUT1 status could show which patients benefit.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### 3.1 GLUT1 Upregulation with GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

**What it is:** Liraglutide, semaglutide — GLP-1 receptor agonists, FDA-approved for diabetes/obesity.

**Target:** BBB endothelial GLUT1 expression.

**Rationale:** GLP-1 receptors on brain endothelium upregulate GLUT1 transcription via HIF-1alpha activation. In the ASD model, reduced GLUT1 is the proximal bottleneck for brain glucose delivery. GLP-1 agonists increase GLUT1 in animal models and may be especially effective if the defect is transporter quantity rather than affinity.

**Evidence:** GLP-1 agonists increase cerebral glucose uptake in diabetic patients (independent of glycemic control). Preclinical: liraglutide upregulates GLUT1 at BBB. No ME/CFS studies.

**Target population:** ME/CFS patients with low CSF:plasma glucose ratio or GLUT1-reducing SLC2A1 variants.

**Risk:** MODERATE. GI side effects common (nausea, vomiting). Requires dose titration. Pancreatitis risk (rare). Cost high (~$800-1200/month). Weight loss is expected — undesirable in underweight patients.

**Certainty:** 0.20. Mechanism plausible but entirely untested in ME/CFS.

### 3.2 Dehydroepiandrosterone (DHEA) for GLUT1 Upregulation

**What it is:** Adrenal androgen precursor, available OTC as supplement.

**Target:** GLUT1 expression via androgen receptor-mediated transcription.

**Rationale:** DHEA upregulates GLUT1 expression in BBB endothelium in rodent studies. DHEA levels decline with age and are low in ME/CFS (several studies). Low-dose DHEA (25-50mg) is well-tolerated and may enhance cerebral glucose uptake.

**Evidence:** ME/CFS shows low DHEA-S (consistent). DHEA replacement in adrenal insufficiency improves well-being. DHEA increases brain glucose uptake in animal models. Pilot ME/CFS trials (n small, mixed results).

**Target population:** ME/CFS patients with low DHEA-S on morning labs. Female patients (DHEA effects more pronounced due to lower baseline androgens).

**Risk:** LOW-MODERATE. In women: androgenic side effects (acne, hirsutism). In men: increases estrogen via aromatization. Long-term safety >6 months not well studied.

**Certainty:** 0.25. Low DHEA is replicated. GLUT1 mechanism is suggestive but not demonstrated in humans for DHEA.

### 3.3 Low-Dose Memantine Pre-Dosing for Cognitive PEM Prevention

**What it is:** NMDA receptor antagonist, FDA-approved for Alzheimer's (10-20mg/day).

**Target:** Glutamatergic excitotoxicity during cognitive exertion.

**Rationale:** If PEM is a glutamatergic energy crisis (hypothesis 1.4), then NMDA receptor blockade before planned cognitive exertion should attenuate the post-exertional cognitive decline. Low-dose memantine (5-10mg) taken 2 hours before anticipated cognitive demand could prevent the excitotoxic cascade without impairing baseline cognition.

**Evidence:** Anecdotal reports of memantine benefit in ME/CFS (online patient communities). Well-tolerated at low doses. NMDA blockade reduces metabolic demand in glutamate-excited neurons. No formal trials.

**Target population:** ME/CFS patients with cognitive-dominant PEM (brain fog as primary symptom). Patients with MRS evidence of elevated prefrontal glutamate.

**Risk:** LOW at 5-10mg. Dizziness, headache at higher doses. No abuse potential. Very long half-life (60-80h) means once-daily dosing sufficient.

**Certainty:** 0.20. Mechanism logical. Zero controlled data.

### 3.4 HX575 (Epoetin Alfa) or Low-Dose Erythropoietin

**What it is:** Recombinant erythropoietin (EPO), FDA-approved for anemia.

**Target:** Multiple — upregulates GLUT1 and GLUT3 expression in brain, increases HIF-1alpha activity, enhances cerebral oxygen delivery, and has neurotrophic effects.

**Rationale:** EPO's brain effects are distinct from its erythropoietic effects. At low doses (below the erythropoietic threshold), EPO upregulates GLUT1/GLUT3, increases mitochondrial efficiency, and protects against glutamate excitotoxicity. It addresses both the glucose transport bottleneck (GLUT1 upregulation) and the neuronal energy demand side.

**Evidence:** EPO improves cognitive function in schizophrenia trials (Ehrenreich 2007 et seq.) via brain metabolic effects. ME/CFS: NOT STUDIED. EPO is expensive and requires injection.

**Target population:** ME/CFS patients without erythrocytosis risk. Requires hemoglobin monitoring.

**Risk:** MODERATE. At erythropoietic doses: hypertension, thrombosis risk. The cognitive dose is lower but not established. Thrombosis in ME/CFS with hypercoagulable state (Family 6) is a specific concern — requires careful safety data first.

**Certainty:** 0.10. Too speculative for clinical use. Worth noting as a mechanistic strategy if safer GLUT1 upregulators fail.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### 4.1 Ketone Esters + MCT Oil Stack: The Glucose Bypass

**Rationale:** Directly addresses the core ASD model mechanism: if GLUT1-mediated glucose transport into brain is impaired, provide alternative fuel that enters via MCT1 (not GLUT1). Ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) from exogenous ketone esters plus MCT oil provide this bypass. Ketones are also more energetically efficient (more ATP per oxygen) than glucose, which may further benefit energy-constrained brain tissue.

**Stack details:**
- Ketone ester (R-1,3-butanediol) 15-30g/day, divided (expensive ~$3-5/day)
- MCT oil 2-4 tbsp/day (cheap, ~$1/day)
- Pyridoxal 5'-phosphate (vitamin B6 active form, cofactor for ketone metabolism) 25mg/day

**Evidence:** Ketone esters increase blood BHB to 2-5mM within 30 min. Brain BHB uptake correlates with MCT1 expression. No ME/CFS trials. Case report exists (n=1). Cognitive improvement in aging with ketone esters.

**Risk:** LOW. GI side effects (diarrhea from MCT oil). Ketone esters are safe in long-term use (Stack 2017). Must avoid in patients with rare beta-oxidation disorders.

**Certainty:** 0.30. Strong mechanistic logic. Minimal clinical evidence in ME/CFS. High testability.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 4.2 Sodium Lactate for Direct Astrocyte-Neuron Shuttle Bypass

**Rationale:** If the ANLS is failing because astrocytes cannot produce enough lactate from glucose, exogenous lactate could bypass this step. Oral sodium lactate (~0.5g/kg) raises blood lactate and can cross the BBB via MCT1. Once in brain, lactate directly enters neuronal TCA cycle — bypassing the astrocytic glycolysis → lactate step entirely.

**Evidence:** Lactate infusion improves cognitive performance under hypoglycemia in healthy adults (Smith 2003). Exogenous lactate is used as an energy source in traumatic brain injury clinical trials. No ME/CFS studies.

**Risk:** MODERATE. Sodium load (hypernatremia risk). Lactate infusion can cause anxiety (needs careful dosing). Oral formulation not standardized.

**Certainty:** 0.15. Mechanically logical. No clinical precedent in ME/CFS. Oral lactate absorption and brain uptake kinetics in ME/CFS are unknown.

### 4.3 Taurine for Astrocytic Volume Regulation and Glycolysis Support

**Rationale:** Astrocyte volume regulation is critical for optimal glycolytic function. Taurine is the brain's dominant organic osmolyte, regulating astrocyte volume in response to osmotic and metabolic stress. Taurine also upregulates glycolysis in astrocytes, improves mitochondrial function, and modulates GABAergic signaling. In the ASD brain energy model, taurine's role as a metabolic regulator could support compromised astrocytic function.

**Evidence:** Taurine levels are low in ASD (multiple studies). Taurine supplementation improves metabolic markers in diabetic brain. ME/CFS: taurine examined in exercise studies (limited). Taurine 2-6g/day is very safe.

**Risk:** LOW. Very well-tolerated. Mild GI effects at high doses.

**Certainty:** 0.20. Mechanism is plausible but indirect. Taurine's effects on astrocytic metabolism specifically in ME/CFS are untested.

### 4.4 Acetyl-L-Carnitine + R-Lipoic Acid for Astrocytic Mitochondrial Support

**Rationale:** While general mitochondrial support is well-explored in ME/CFS, the ASD model suggests astrocytic mitochondria are specifically affected (due to GLUT1-dependent fuel restriction). Acetyl-L-carnitine (ALCAR) shuttles fatty acids into mitochondria and supports acetylcholine synthesis. R-lipoic acid (RLA) is a mitochondrial cofactor for pyruvate dehydrogenase and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase. The combination supports the Krebs cycle directly — potentially compensating for reduced glycolytic flux in astrocytes.

**Evidence:** ALCAR + RLA combination improves mitochondrial function in aging (Liu 2002, landmark study). ALCAR shows benefit in ME/CFS fatigue (Vermeulen 2004, small trial). No combination studied in ME/CFS specifically for astrocytic effects.

**Risk:** LOW. Very safe. ALCAR may cause body odor and anxiety at high doses. RLA requires empty stomach for absorption.

**Certainty:** 0.30. Individual components have ME/CFS data. The astrocyte-specific framing is novel.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### 5.1 PFC Energy Budget Protocol (Cognitive Pacing)

**Rationale:** The ASD model identifies PFC as the highest-metabolic-cost region, most vulnerable to energy constraint. Standard ME/CFS pacing is whole-body; this protocol specifically targets PFC energy demand by designing cognitive tasks to minimize prefrontal load.

**Protocol elements:**
- Time-block cognitive work into 15-20 min sessions (PFC fatigues fast under energy constraint)
- Single-task only (switching costs increase PFC metabolic demand by 20-30%)
- Social cognition tasks (meetings, conversations, emails requiring emotional inference) scheduled EARLY in the day (highest brain energy availability)
- Low-effort cognitive replacement: use external memory aids extensively (reduce PFC working memory load)
- "PFC rest" between blocks: 5 min with eyes closed, no conversation, no screens (default mode network restores PFC ATP)
- Avoid cognitive work within 2 hours of anticipated physical PEM triggers

**Evidence:** Cognitive pacing reduces fatigue in MS (multiple studies). No ME/CFS study has specifically designed cognitive pacing around PFC metabolic constraints. The novelty is the mechanistic rationale, not the technique itself.

**Risk:** LOW. Zero cost. Risk of over-restriction (doing less than necessary).

**Certainty:** 0.30. Components individually reasonable; combined protocol untested.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 5.2 Timed Carbohydrate Loading for Brain Glucose Delivery

**Rationale:** Brain glucose uptake is NOT simply proportional to blood glucose — it's regulated by GLUT1 and can be rate-limited. However, transient blood glucose spikes may briefly saturate GLUT1 and increase brain glucose delivery above baseline. Timed carbohydrate intake before planned cognitive exertion could preload the brain with glucose.

**Protocol:**
- 30-40g rapidly digestible carbohydrate (equivalent to a banana + juice or glucose drink) 30 minutes before cognitive tasks
- Avoid protein/fat with this meal (they blunt glucose spike)
- Pair with caffeine (increases cerebral glucose uptake via adenosine receptor antagonism)
- Monitor blood glucose to confirm spike

**Evidence:** Glucose loading improves cognitive performance in healthy adults (memory, attention). Glucose before cognitive tasks benefits Alzheimer's patients. ME/CFS: patients often report worsened symptoms after high-carb meals (postprandial fatigue) — but this may be due to systemic (gut) effects rather than brain effects. The timed approach may bypass this.

**Risk:** LOW-MODERATE. Postprandial reactive hypoglycemia risk in susceptible individuals. Not suitable for diabetic patients without monitoring. Weight gain with repeated use.

**Certainty:** 0.20. Conflicts with patient reports of carbohydrate intolerance. Requires testing to reconcile.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 5.3 Intermittent Fasting + Timed Cognitive Work

**Rationale:** Intermittent fasting (16:8) induces ketosis (blood BHB 0.5-1.5 mM), which provides an alternative brain fuel that enters via MCT1 (bypassing GLUT1). If the ASD model is correct, the brain is fuel-restricted under glucose metabolism but may function better under ketone metabolism. Schedule demanding cognitive work at the end of the fasting window, when ketone levels are highest.

**Protocol:**
- 16:8 intermittent fasting (eating window 12pm-8pm)
- Cognitive work 10am-12pm (peak ketosis)
- First meal at 12pm (break the fast)
- Avoid evening cognitive work (glucose metabolism, postprandial insulin may reduce brain glucose availability)

**Evidence:** IF improves cognitive function in some studies (alternate-day fasting, cognitive gains in animal models). Human data mixed. No ME/CFS studies. Risk of exacerbating underweight in already-low-BMI patients.

**Risk:** MODERATE. Weight loss is almost guaranteed — problematic for underweight ME/CFS patients. Hypotension risk exacerbated by electrolyte shifts. Adrenal stress in HPA-axis-compromised patients.

**Certainty:** 0.15. Mechanistic logic flows from GLUT1 bottleneck but clinical feasibility is poor in ME/CFS population (many underweight, hypotensive, HPA-dysregulated). Better suited for overweight/stable ME/CFS subset.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 5.4 Translingual Neurostimulation for Brain Metabolic Enhancement

**Rationale:** The PoNS device (translingual neurostimulation, FDA-approved for gait in MS) delivers electrical stimulation to the trigeminal/lingual nerve → activates the locus coeruleus and nucleus basalis → increases cortical catecholamines → increases cerebral blood flow and glucose uptake. If PFC hypometabolism is the functional bottleneck, this stimulation could transiently increase PFC metabolism and improve cognitive function without requiring pharmacological GLUT1 upregulation.

**Evidence:** PoNS improves gait in MS (Tyler 2022, RCT). Mechanism involves increased cortical activity and neuroplasticity. No ME/CFS studies. The device is FDA-cleared but expensive (~$3K).

**Risk:** LOW. Well-tolerated. Contraindicated in epilepsy. Taping sensation on tongue.

**Certainty:** 0.15. High mechanistic plausibility; zero disease-specific data.

## Category 6: Combination Protocols and Access

### 6.1 Severity-Adapted Brain Energy Restoration Protocol

**Rationale:** Different ME/CFS severity levels have different capacities to implement brain energy interventions. Protocol tiers ensure accessibility.

**Severe (housebound/bedbound):**
- Ketone esters 15g/day (liquid, minimal effort to consume)
- MCT oil 1 tbsp/day (add to any liquid food)
- Creatine 3g/day (dissolvable powder)
- PFC energy budget (cognitive pacing, section 5.1) — zero cost, implementable at home
- Total cost: ~$5-7/day

**Moderate (ambulatory, limited activities):**
- All severe interventions PLUS
- ALCAR 1000mg + RLA 300mg (for astrocytic mitochondrial support)
- Timed carbohydrate loading (section 5.2) before planned cognitive work
- Sleep optimization (focus on NREM enhancement for glymphatic clearance)
- Total cost: ~$8-12/day

**Mild (functional with limitations):**
- All moderate interventions PLUS
- Consider GLP-1 agonist (if low CSF:plasma glucose confirmed, section 2.2)
- Social cognitive testing to monitor brain energy status (declining social cognition → increase intervention intensity)
- Total cost: $8-12/day + medication cost if applicable

**Certainty:** 0.15 for protocol overall. Individual components range 0.15-0.30. Stratification is clinical reasoning, not evidence-based.

### 6.2 The "Substrate-Switch" Rescue Protocol for PEM

**Rationale:** During PEM, the brain may be especially dependent on the failing GLUT1-dependent glucose pathway. A rescue protocol that rapidly switches brain fuel sources could abort or shorten PEM episodes.

**Protocol:**
- At first PEM symptom: ketone ester 15g + MCT oil 15mL (provides alternative fuel within 30 min)
- Oral lactate 0.3g/kg (bypasses astrocytic glycolysis if available — pharmaceutical compounding needed)
- Avoid additional cognitive load for 2h (allow energy restoration)
- Repeat ketone+MCT at 4h if needed

**Evidence:** No evidence for PEM-specific rescue. Rationale from GLUT1 deficiency management: breakthrough seizures in GLUT1-DS children are treated with immediate ketone supplementation.

**Risk:** MODERATE. Aborting PEM may mask the need to rest and lead to crash-rebound cycles. GI distress from MCT/ketones during PEM may worsen symptoms.

**Certainty:** 0.10. Entirely speculative. No clinical data.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 Astrocyte-Neuron Lactate Shuttle ODE

**Rationale:** The existing energy model (`eq:branch-point`, `eq:R_headroom`) treats brain energy as a single compartment. The ASD model requires separation: astrocytic glycolysis and neuronal oxidative phosphorylation are distinct processes with different substrates and bottlenecks.

**Proposed extension:**

```
d[Glc_astro]/dt = J_GLUT1 * [Glc_plasma] - k_glycolysis * [Glc_astro]
d[Lac_astro]/dt = k_glycolysis * [Glc_astro] - J_MCT4 * [Lac_astro] + J_MCT2 * [Lac_neuron]
d[Lac_neuron]/dt = J_MCT2 * [Lac_astro] - k_OXPHOS * [Lac_neuron]
d[ATP_neuron]/dt = k_OXPHOS * [Lac_neuron] - J_demand_neuron
```

**Key parameters:**
- `J_GLUT1`: glucose transport rate (limited in ASD/ME/CFS model)
- `J_MCT4`: lactate export from astrocyte (bidirectional)
- `k_glycolysis`: astrocytic glycolytic rate (may be reduced)
- `k_OXPHOS`: neuronal oxidative capacity (may be intact)

**Predictions from the model:**
- Below a critical J_GLUT1 threshold, astrocytic glycolysis cannot sustain neuronal lactate demand → neuronal ATP drops → cognitive failure
- The system has hysteresis: once J_GLUT1 drops below threshold, increasing it back above is insufficient because the neuron shifts to alternative fuel utilization pathways that are slow to reactivate
- Ketone supplementation adds a bypass term: `d[ATP_neuron]/dt = k_OXPHOS * ([Lac_neuron] + k_ketone * [BHB_neuron])`

**Certainty:** 0.25. Framework is sound biochemistry. Parameter values are unknown for ME/CFS. Calibration requires the ADEV study (2.1) and MRS data.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 7.2 EPC DAG Extension: Brain Fuel Partitioning Variables

### 7.3 Metabolic Connectivity Cost Function

**Rationale:** The ASD model proposes that brain networks reorganize to minimize energy costs — sacrificing metabolically expensive long-range connections. This can be formalized as an optimization problem.

**Proposed formulation:**

```
Minimize: Total_energy = sum_over_network(C_ij * w_ij) + sum_over_nodes(k * f_i^2)
Subject to: Information_throughput >= threshold
```

Where:
- `C_ij` = metabolic cost of maintaining connection between nodes i and j (proportional to distance × firing rate)
- `w_ij` = connection weight (synaptic strength)
- `k * f_i^2` = quadratic cost of node firing rate (metabolism scales superlinearly with firing)
- Information_throughput = integrated information measure (phi from IIT, or mutual information)

**Prediction:** The optimal network in an energy-constrained brain shifts toward local connectivity (shorter connections, fewer long-range projections). This predicts ME/CFS brain network topology will show: (a) reduced functional connectivity in long-range networks (especially frontoparietal-default mode), (b) increased local clustering, (c) reduced global efficiency. These changes should correlate with brain lactate levels (energy stress marker).

**Certainty:** 0.20. Elegant framework. The information-throughout constraint is unmeasurable with current methods. The connectivity predictions are testable with resting-state fMRI.

## Category 10: Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS

### 10.1 Brain Hypometabolism as Epiphenomenon, Not Mechanism

**Rationale:** The entire convergence rests on an assumption: shared brain metabolic abnormalities mean shared mechanism. But brain hypometabolism in ME/CFS could be an epiphenomenon of: (a) physical deconditioning (reduced cardiac output → reduced CBF → reduced brain glucose delivery), (b) sleep disruption (reduced NREM → reduced glymphatic clearance → metabolic waste accumulation → metabolic slowing), or (c) systemic inflammation (cytokines directly suppress neuronal activity as a protective hibernation response). Any of these would produce FDG-PET hypometabolism and MRS lactate elevation indistinguishable from the ASD model's prediction — but the astrocytic GLUT1 bottleneck would be secondary, not primary.

**Implication:** The ASD model may be descriptively correct (ME/CFS brains show ASD-like metabolic patterns) but mechanistically wrong if applied to ME/CFS (the origin of the pattern is different). Interventions targeting the GLUT1 bottleneck (ketone esters, GLUT1 upregulators) would fail in ME/CFS if the true cause is, e.g., reduced CBF.

**Evidence check:** If brain hypometabolism in ME/CFS is CBF-driven, then: (a) CBF reduction should correlate with hypometabolism spatially, (b) interventions that increase CBF should normalize metabolism, (c) CSF:plasma glucose ratio should be NORMAL (GLUT1 is intact). Each can be tested.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 10.2 Direction of Causality: Does Energy Failure Cause Symptoms or Vice Versa?

**Rationale:** The ASD model asserts brain energy failure → cognitive-behavioral phenotype. In ME/CFS, the reverse is equally plausible: chronic illness behaviors (reduced activity, social withdrawal, hypervigilance to symptoms) lead to reduced neural stimulation → activity-dependent GLUT1 downregulation → hypometabolism. This is Hebbian plasticity of metabolism: use it or lose it. Social cognitive networks atrophy from disuse, not energy failure. The energy failure is a consequence of illness behavior, not its cause.

**Implication:** Interventions that increase brain stimulation (cognitive training, social engagement, behavioral activation) — the opposite of MET/CFS pacing — would restore brain metabolism. Pacing could even worsen the energy failure by reducing neural activity further.

**Evidence check:** Longitudinal study of newly diagnosed ME/CFS patients: do social activity reductions PRECEDE metabolic changes (supporting disuse) or follow them (supporting energy failure)? If social withdrawal at 3 months predicts hypometabolism at 12 months, disuse is plausible.

**`origin: brainstorm`**

### 10.3 ASD and ME/CFS Hypometabolism Affect Different Networks

**Rationale:** The ASD paper synthesizes data showing ASD hypometabolism is most consistent in temporal lobes, fusiform gyrus, and amygdala (social brain networks). ME/CFS hypometabolism is reported in frontal, parietal, and cingulate regions (cognitive control networks). The overlap may be in regional METABOLIC RATE (both affect high-metabolism areas) rather than specific NETWORKS. If the networks are different, the mechanistic convergence is weak — both diseases show high-metabolism-area vulnerability, but the affected systems are distinct.

**Implication:** The "energy-saving adaptation" model would predict different behavioral phenotypes in each disease (social in ASD, cognitive in ME/CFS), which is consistent with their clinical presentations. But this means the mechanism is "high metabolic rate regions are vulnerable" — a trivial conclusion. The ASD model's specific value (GLUT1 → ANLS → social cognition) may not transfer to ME/CFS if the relevant networks use different metabolic pathways.

**Evidence check:** Voxel-wise meta-analysis comparing ASD and ME/CFS FDG-PET studies directly. If the hypometabolism patterns are spatially distinct despite both involving high-metabolism cortex, the common mechanism is trivial.

## Category 11: Null Hypothesis Assessment

### 11.1 Null Hypothesis H0₁: Brain Energy Metabolism Does Not Differ Between ME/CFS and Fatigue from Other Causes

**Statement:** Regional brain glucose metabolism, lactate, and ATP levels in ME/CFS are not distinguishable from those in patients with chronic fatigue due to depression, MS, SLE, or post-cancer fatigue. The FDG-PET and MRS findings are generic correlates of the subjective experience of fatigue, not specific to ME/CFS pathophysiology.

**Evidence already available:**
- Depression shows frontal hypometabolism (restored by treatment) — overlaps with ME/CFS
- MS fatigue shows reduced brain glucose metabolism (frontal, basal ganglia)
- Post-chemo fatigue shows white matter metabolic alterations
- NO study has directly compared ME/CFS brain metabolism to fatigue-matched controls from other conditions

**What would reject H0₁:** A head-to-head comparison study (2.1, astrocyte-specific biomarkers) showing that ME/CFS brain metabolism differs qualitatively (not just quantitatively) from fatigue controls. Candidate differentiators: (a) low CSF:plasma glucose ratio in ME/CFS but not other fatiguing conditions, (b) astrocytic (not neuronal) metabolic markers affected specifically in ME/CFS, (c) PEM correlates with brain metabolic changes (other conditions don't have PEM to correlate with).

**Consequence of failing to reject H0₁:** The ASD-ME/CFS convergence may still reveal shared mechanisms between ASD and ME/CFS, but the findings would be about brain energy and fatigue generally, not ME/CFS specifically. The specificity of the model to ME/CFS would be weakened, but the mechanistic commonalities between conditions would still be valid.

### 11.2 Null Hypothesis H0₂: Brain Metabolic Changes Are Secondary to Systemic Mitochondrial Dysfunction

**Statement:** Brain energy metabolism abnormalities in ME/CFS are not caused by a brain-specific GLUT1/astrocytic defect but are downstream of systemic mitochondrial dysfunction that affects all organs including the brain. The brain is affected because it has high energy demand, not because it has a unique transporter bottleneck.

**Evidence:**
- Systemic mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS is well-documented (PBMC respirometry, muscle biopsy, 31P-MRS in muscle)
- Many systemic mitochondrial diseases show brain metabolic changes
- FDG-PET hypometabolism is found in primary mitochondrial disorders

**What would reject H0₂:** Demonstration of brain-specific molecular defects that are independent of systemic mitochondrial function. Candidates: (a) GLUT1 downregulation in brain endothelium but not peripheral tissues, (b) altered CSF:plasma glucose ratio with normal blood lactate/pyruvate, (c) astrocyte-specific EV markers abnormal while neuronal EV markers normal. If muscle and brain defects correlate perfectly (same severity in both), systemic etiology is more likely.

**Consequence of failing to reject H0₂:** The brain energy model still stands but loses its specificity. Interventions would target systemic mitochondria, not brain-specific mechanisms. The ASD model's emphasis on astrocytic GLUT1 would need modification for ME/CFS.

### 11.3 Null Hypothesis H0₃: The Apparent Metabolic Similarity Is a Type I Error from Selective Reporting

**Statement:** The convergence between ASD and ME/CFS brain metabolism is an artifact of selective outcome reporting. Both fields have published metabolic studies with contradictory findings (normal metabolism in some ASD/ME/CFS cohorts) but the literature emphasizes positive results. When all published (including null) results are considered, the metabolic profiles may not significantly overlap.

**Evidence pattern:**
- ASD: at least 3 FDG-PET studies show NO hypometabolism (particularly in high-functioning ASD)
- ME/CFS: ~30% of patients in most MRS cohorts do NOT show elevated lactate
- Publication bias in both fields is likely (small n, negative-result studies rarely published)
- No systematic meta-analysis has been conducted on ME/CFS brain metabolism with comprehensive inclusion criteria (most reviews are narrative)

**What would reject H0₃:** A comprehensive meta-analysis of ALL published FDG-PET and MRS studies in both ASD and ME/CFS, including correction for publication bias (Egger's test, trim-and-fill). If the effect sizes remain significant and overlapping after correction, selective reporting is unlikely to explain the convergence.

**What is needed:** Systematic literature search for all brain metabolic imaging studies in ME/CFS (not just positive results), data extraction, and meta-analysis. This is feasible with ~1-2 months of work.

## Category 12: Evidence Quality Concerns

### 12.1 Small Sample Sizes and Replication Uncertainty

**Concern:** Most FDG-PET studies in both ASD and ME/CFS have n < 20 per group. MRS studies in ME/CFS have n ranging 14-39 (Syan 2025 systematic review). At these sample sizes, effect size estimates are unreliable (wide confidence intervals), and statistically significant but spurious correlations can emerge. The ASD-ME/CFS convergence may rest on studies that would not replicate in larger samples.

**Specific examples:**
- Nakatomi 2014 (FDG-PET in ME/CFS): n=9 patients, n=10 controls — among the most cited ME/CFS brain imaging papers
- Godlewska 2025 (7T MRS brain creatine): n=19 ME/CFS, n=19 controls — well-powered for large effects but underpowered for moderate effects
- The entire ASD brain metabolism literature (reviewed in Blagojevic-Stokic 2026) is built on studies with similarly small samples

**Impact:** If the true effect sizes for brain hypometabolism are moderate (Cohen's d 0.4-0.6), most existing studies were underpowered to detect them reliably. The literature may overestimate effect sizes due to winner's curse (significant results more likely to be published from small studies).

**Mitigation:** A large-scale replication study (n ≥ 50 per group) is needed before strong conclusions can be drawn. The field should prioritize this over novelty.

### 12.2 Lack of Direct Astrocytic Evidence in ME/CFS

**Concern:** The central hypothesis (astrocytic GLUT1/ANLS failure) has never been directly tested in ME/CFS. All evidence is indirect:
- FDG-PET measures summed glucose uptake across all cell types (neurons, astrocytes, microglia). Cannot distinguish astrocytic from neuronal uptake.
- MRS measures bulk tissue metabolites. Cannot resolve cellular origin of lactate/creatine signals.
- CSF metabolomics may reflect combined brain metabolism but has not been analyzed for astrocyte-specific markers.
- No ME/CFS study has: quantified astrocytic GLUT1, measured ANLS function, or assessed lactate flux between astrocytes and neurons.

**Impact:** The convergence argument is logically coherent but empirically fragile. If the astrocytic locus is wrong, the mechanism falls apart even if the phenomenology (brain hypometabolism) is correct. All treatment ideas targeting GLUT1/ANLS would be based on an unverified cellular model.

**What is needed:** Direct evidence before clinical translation. The ADEV study (2.1) and CSF:plasma glucose study (2.2) are minimal required evidence steps. Clinical trials of GLUT1-targeting interventions should be deferred until at least one of these returns positive.

### 12.3 Publication Bias in the ASD Brain Energy Literature

**Concern:** The Blagojevic-Stokic 2026 review presents a comprehensive but potentially biased selection of the ASD brain metabolism literature. If the review preferentially cites studies showing hypometabolism and underrepresents null findings, the apparent convergence with ME/CFS may be a shared selection bias rather than true biological overlap.

**Specific concerns:**
- ASD FDG-PET literature is heterogeneous: some well-powered studies (n>50) show regionally specific hypometabolism; others show hypermetabolism (especially in younger children, where the brain may be hyperactive before pruning)

**Mitigation:** Independent systematic review of ASD brain metabolism literature with pre-registered outcomes. If the overall pattern supports hypometabolism on meta-analysis, the model is on firmer ground.

### 12.4 Heterogeneity Problem: Both Diseases Are Syndromes, Not Single Diseases

**Concern:** Both ASD and ME/CFS are syndrome diagnoses (clusters of symptoms, not defined by biomarkers). Within each syndrome, there is likely substantial etiological heterogeneity. The metabolic signatures seen in group-average studies may represent only a subset of patients. The ASD-ME/CFS overlap might be real for 30-40% of each population but absent in the rest. Group-average analyses would show a "significant" effect driven by a subgroup, while individual-level data would reveal most patients do not fit the model.

**Implication:** The model may be correct for a specific ME/CFS subtype (e.g., those with neurodevelopmental antecedents, cognitive-dominant presentation, or specific prenatal risk factors) but not for the broader ME/CFS population. Clinical trials of brain energy interventions should stratify by candidate subtype markers (CSF:plasma glucose, ADEV GLUT1, social cognition scores) rather than testing in unselected ME/CFS.

**Evidence needed:** Individual-level analysis of FDG-PET/MRS data from ME/CFS cohorts. Determine what fraction of patients show the ASD-like metabolic pattern. If >60% show it, the mechanism is broadly relevant. If <30%, it's a subtype.
