## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### Idea 1: The Adaptation Debt Hypothesis — Training Without Supercompensation as Mitochondrial Biogenesis Failure

**Mechanistic rationale:** Normal exercise triggers PGC-1alpha-mediated mitochondrial biogenesis — the supercompensation cycle that makes training "work." In prodromal ME/CFS, this signalling cascade is partially impaired (perhaps via AMPK insensitivity or epigenetic silencing of PGC-1alpha promoters). The result: training volume accumulates metabolic stress WITHOUT the expected adaptive response. Each workout creates damage that repair cannot fully address, producing a slowly-widening gap between training load and fitness. This is NOT deconditioning (they ARE training) — it is failed adaptation, a distinct and testable phenomenon.

**Evidence link:** The overtrained-but-unfit athlete vignette; Architecture C $J_"production,max"$ ceiling; known PGC-1alpha reductions in ME/CFS muscle biopsies (Rutherford 2016).

**Certainty: 0.40**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-illness muscle biopsies (from athletes who later develop ME/CFS) should show reduced PGC-1alpha mRNA response to acute exercise challenge, compared to matched training-volume controls. Alternatively: mitochondrial DNA copy number per muscle fiber should be LOWER than expected for their training volume, despite normal or elevated training stress markers (lactate, CK).

### Idea 2: The Cognitive Energy Cliff — Discrete Phase Transition in Brain ATP Allocation

**Mechanistic rationale:** The late-game fader phenomenon suggests the brain does not degrade gracefully under ATP scarcity but exhibits a PHASE TRANSITION: when available ATP drops below a critical threshold (~70% of baseline?), the brain abruptly deprioritizes executive/attentional functions in favour of motor control and homeostasis. This is not "getting tired" — it is a discrete switching event where prefrontal allocation drops catastrophically. This predicts that the cognitive fade should be sudden (within minutes), not gradual, and that it should correlate with a measurable neurophysiological marker (e.g., prefrontal oxyHb on fNIRS crossing a threshold).

**Evidence link:** Late-game fader vignette; Wirth 2026 (OR 2.93 for cognitive impairment 5 years pre-Dx); Architecture C headroom model; known prefrontal hypoperfusion in ME/CFS (Tanaka 2018).

**Certainty: 0.35**

**Falsifiable prediction:** fNIRS monitoring during sustained cognitive-motor dual task will show that prodromal individuals exhibit an abrupt (not linear) decline in prefrontal oxyHb after a characteristic duration, corresponding to the subjective "fade." Controls will show linear decline without threshold behaviour.

### Idea 3: Tremor as Predictive Oscillation — Motor Control Loop Running Near Instability Boundary

**Mechanistic rationale:** Fine motor control requires continuous error-correction loops (cerebellum -> motor cortex -> muscle -> proprioceptive feedback -> cerebellum). These loops are ENERGY-INTENSIVE because they require high-frequency neural firing and rapid neurotransmitter recycling. When ATP availability is marginal, the loop's gain decreases, causing the control system to approach its oscillatory instability boundary — manifest as physiological tremor with increased amplitude and reduced frequency. This is analogous to control system underdamping. Combined with SFN-mediated proprioceptive degradation (Moak 2024: 53% SFN in pediatric POTS), the control loop has both reduced power AND degraded feedback.

**Evidence link:** Tremor vignette; ch03 mechanism; Moak 2024 SFN; Wyller 2007 sympathetic dysfunction; control theory literature on underdamped systems.

**Certainty: 0.50**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Tremor frequency analysis (accelerometry) in prodromal individuals will show: (a) lower dominant frequency than essential tremor, (b) frequency that DECREASES with fatigue (classical underdamping signature), and (c) correlation with peripheral nerve conduction velocity on quantitative sensory testing.

### Idea 4: The Holiday Paradox — Delayed Crash as Sympathetic Withdrawal Syndrome

**Mechanistic rationale:** The "weekend zombie" and "inexplicably ill on holidays" pattern reflects sympathetic nervous system withdrawal. During high-demand periods (school, training), elevated catecholamines mask underlying energy deficit by maintaining cerebral perfusion, suppressing inflammation, and mobilising glucose. When demand drops (weekends, holidays), catecholamines fall, unmasking: (a) true baseline fatigue, (b) deferred inflammatory responses (dormant infections reactivate), (c) orthostatic intolerance previously compensated by sympathetic tone. This is mechanistically identical to "let-down headaches" in migraineurs and post-exam illness in students, but in prodromal ME/CFS it is more severe and more frequent because the compensatory sympathetic overdrive is greater.

**Evidence link:** Exercise intolerance masked by persistence vignette; Wyller 2007 sympathetic dysfunction; Kato 2006 premorbid stress; catecholamine-energy axis in Architecture C (BH4 cofactor deficiency reducing both catecholamine synthesis and mitochondrial function).

**Certainty: 0.55**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Salivary cortisol and urinary catecholamines measured across weekday-to-weekend transitions in prodromal individuals will show: (a) higher weekday catecholamines than controls matched for activity, (b) steeper weekend decline, and (c) symptom severity on rest days correlating with magnitude of catecholamine drop.

### Idea 5: Two-Hit Timing Model — Puberty as a Reserved-Depleting Event

**Mechanistic rationale:** Puberty is massively energy-expensive: rapid skeletal growth, brain reorganisation, hormonal axis establishment, immune system maturation. In Architecture C terms, puberty represents a sustained increase in $J_"demand,baseline"$ lasting 3-5 years. For individuals with already-reduced $J_"production,max"$ (neurodivergent, hEDS, iron-deficient), puberty itself may consume most remaining headroom — making ANY additional stressor (infection, academic pressure, overtraining) during this window catastrophic. This explains: (a) bimodal early peak at ~16 (puberty + EBV timing), (b) why prodromal signs often emerge 2-5 years before full ME/CFS (during puberty's energy drain), (c) why girls are over-represented (puberty's metabolic cost is higher: menarche, fat redistribution, iron loss).

**Evidence link:** Bimodal onset peak at 16 (ch05); Collin 2018 (sleep disruption at ages 11-13 predicting CFS at 18); sex difference in ME/CFS prevalence emerging at puberty; iron loss from menstruation as $\delta_i$ in Architecture C.

**Certainty: 0.45**

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS onset risk should correlate with: (a) earlier age of menarche (longer overlap between pubertal energy demand and still-developing systems), (b) rapid growth velocity (higher transient energy demand), (c) puberty-onset iron deficiency (measurable $\delta$ increase). Retrospectively testable in ALSPAC data which has pubertal timing variables.

## 2. Research Directions

### Idea 6: The Sibling Stress Test — Prospective Validation in First-Degree Relatives

**Design:** Recruit N=200 asymptomatic siblings of confirmed ME/CFS patients (ages 10-25). Perform at baseline and annually:
- Cardiopulmonary exercise testing (CPET) with 2-day repeat protocol
- HRV analysis (24h Holter)
- Seahorse respirometry on PBMCs (spare respiratory capacity)
- Quantitative sensory testing (small fiber function)
- Sleep architecture (home polysomnography)
- Salivary cortisol diurnal curve
- Serum ferritin, BH4, methylation panel

**Key question:** Do siblings show a GRADIENT of subclinical abnormalities correlating with number of prodromal signs? Can we identify a pre-symptomatic metabolic signature that predicts who will develop ME/CFS within 5 years?

**Certainty: 0.60** (feasibility) / **0.45** (that gradient will exist)

**Falsifiable prediction:** If siblings show NO subclinical metabolic differences from unrelated controls despite sharing genetic background, the genetic-ceiling model of Architecture C is not supported.

Extract features: resting HR trend, HRV trajectory (RMSSD), sleep duration/efficiency, step count variability, recovery time from exercise, respiratory rate during sleep.

Train classifier to identify the "inflection point" — when time-series features transition from subclinical-but-compensated to decompensating.

**Key question:** Is the prodromal-to-disease transition abrupt (supporting phase-transition model) or gradual (supporting linear decline model)?

**Certainty: 0.55** (that detectable signal exists pre-diagnosis)

**Falsifiable prediction:** If no inflection point is detectable in wearable data preceding diagnosis by >3 months, the prodromal decompensation model is not supported. If the transition is abrupt (within 2 weeks), this supports the hysteretic phase-transition model; if gradual (>6 months decline), this supports linear reserve erosion.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### Idea 9: Prophylactic Low-Dose Metformin in At-Risk Adolescents

**Mechanistic rationale:** Metformin activates AMPK, the master sensor of energy deficit that triggers mitochondrial biogenesis via PGC-1alpha. In the prodromal phase, if the problem is FAILED adaptation signalling (Idea 1), then pharmacological AMPK activation could restore the missing biogenesis response. Metformin also reduces Complex I activity slightly — paradoxically beneficial because it forces greater AMPK activation per unit of exercise, potentially "teaching" the system to respond to training stress. Additionally, metformin has anti-inflammatory properties that could reduce the subclinical inflammation that may impair adaptation.

**Evidence link:** Metformin's AMPK activation well-established; known to increase mitochondrial biogenesis in skeletal muscle; protective in some Long COVID cohorts; good safety profile in adolescents (used in PCOS, T2DM).

**Certainty: 0.30** (mechanistically sound but untested; may worsen energy deficit if the ceiling problem is structural rather than signalling-based)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Randomised trial in prodromal individuals (overtrained-but-unfit athletes with family history): 6 months metformin 500mg vs placebo should show improved VO2max gain per training hour AND increased PBMC spare respiratory capacity.

### Idea 10: Ivabradine for Prodromal Sympathetic Overdrive

**Mechanistic rationale:** Prodromal individuals may maintain function through chronic sympathetic overdrive (Idea 4). Ivabradine selectively reduces heart rate (I_f channel blocker) WITHOUT reducing stroke volume or blood pressure — unlike beta-blockers which can worsen fatigue. By reducing the metabolic cost of elevated heart rate (each beat costs ATP), ivabradine could create "energy savings" that increase headroom. In POTS it reduces standing tachycardia; in prodromal ME/CFS it could reduce the sympathetic compensation cost, allowing better energy allocation to brain and muscle.

**Evidence link:** Used in POTS (Taub 2021); heart rate as ATP consumer; sympathetic overdrive documented in adolescent ME/CFS (Wyller 2007); no fatigue-worsening effect unlike beta-blockers.

**Certainty: 0.40**

**Falsifiable prediction:** In prodromal individuals with resting HR >80 bpm, ivabradine 2.5mg BID for 12 weeks should: (a) reduce resting HR by 10-15 bpm, (b) improve self-reported energy levels, (c) NOT worsen exercise capacity (unlike propranolol).

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Idea 11: Creatine as Prodromal Energy Buffer

**Mechanistic rationale:** Creatine phosphate is the immediate ATP buffer — it regenerates ATP within milliseconds, faster than mitochondria can respond. If the prodromal problem is a LOWER CEILING (slower mitochondrial response, reduced max capacity), then increasing the creatine phosphate buffer extends the time before that ceiling is hit. This is like increasing the capacitor bank in an electrical system with a limited generator. For the late-game fader specifically: larger phosphocreatine reserves in brain tissue could extend the duration before the cognitive cliff (Idea 2). Brain creatine supplementation is well-documented (Avgerinos 2018 meta-analysis).

**Evidence link:** Creatine increases brain PCr (MRS-confirmed); low toxicity; used in mitochondrial myopathy (mixed results); Architecture C predicts buffer-extending interventions should delay but not prevent crashes.

**Certainty: 0.50**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Creatine 5g/day for 8 weeks in late-game-fader phenotype adolescents should: (a) increase brain phosphocreatine on 31P-MRS, (b) extend time-to-cognitive-fade on sustained attention tasks, (c) NOT improve absolute ceiling (VO2max unchanged). If VO2max improves, the mechanism is different than predicted.

### Idea 12: Iron + BH4 Cofactor Stack for Puberty-Window Protection

**Mechanistic rationale:** During puberty, iron demand spikes (growth, menstruation in girls, increased blood volume). Iron is required for: mitochondrial Complex I/II/III (iron-sulfur clusters), oxygen transport (haemoglobin), BH4 recycling (dihydropteridine reductase requires iron), and dopamine synthesis (tyrosine hydroxylase). BH4 itself is required for: catecholamine synthesis, serotonin synthesis, NO production (vascular regulation), and phenylalanine metabolism. If Architecture C is correct, puberty-onset iron deficiency in a BH4-vulnerable individual (GCH1 rs841 carrier) could simultaneously reduce MULTIPLE delta_i values, rapidly depleting headroom.

The intervention: aggressive iron repletion (targeting ferritin >100) PLUS indirect BH4 support (folinic acid 15mg + vitamin C 500mg + riboflavin 100mg) during the pubertal growth window (ages 11-16) in at-risk individuals.

**Evidence link:** DelRosso 2026 (iron-neurodevelopmental link); Williams 2025 (GCH1-BH4); Architecture C delta_i table; known pubertal iron depletion epidemiology.

**Certainty: 0.45**

**Falsifiable prediction:** In GCH1 rs841 carriers with prodromal signs, the iron+BH4 stack should: (a) reduce symptom score on a prodromal checklist, (b) improve PBMC spare respiratory capacity, (c) reduce OR for ME/CFS development over 3-year follow-up. If ineffective in GCH1 carriers but effective in non-carriers, the BH4 mechanism is not the correct target.

### Idea 13: CoQ10 + D-Ribose as Prodromal Metabolic Support

**Mechanistic rationale:** CoQ10 (ubiquinone) is the electron carrier between Complex I/II and Complex III. D-Ribose is the precursor for adenine nucleotide synthesis (ATP, ADP, AMP pool replenishment). In the prodromal phase, both may be marginally insufficient: CoQ10 synthesis declines with age and oxidative stress; the total adenine nucleotide pool may slowly deplete if destruction (from repeated energy crises) exceeds synthesis. Unlike creatine (which buffers moment-to-moment), this stack addresses the TOTAL POOL SIZE and ELECTRON TRANSPORT EFFICIENCY.

**Evidence link:** CoQ10 reduced fatigue in ME/CFS RCTs (Castro-Marrero 2015); D-Ribose improved energy in pilot ME/CFS trial (Teitelbaum 2006); both have excellent safety profiles; used in mitochondrial disease.

**Certainty: 0.45**

**Falsifiable prediction:** In prodromal individuals with confirmed reduced spare respiratory capacity on Seahorse, CoQ10 200mg + D-Ribose 5g TID for 12 weeks should improve spare respiratory capacity by >15%. If spare capacity improves but symptoms don't change, the metabolic bottleneck is not the symptom-producing mechanism.

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### Idea 14: The Training Ratio Protocol — Periodization for Energy-Limited Athletes

**Mechanistic rationale:** If the prodromal individual has a LOWER mitochondrial ceiling, conventional training programmes that work for healthy athletes will produce overtraining syndrome in them. The solution is not to stop training (which causes deconditioning AND removes the biogenesis stimulus) but to radically alter the training RATIO: much more recovery time per unit of training stress. Specifically:

- **Intensity cap:** Never exceed 65% of HR max (well below the ceiling)
- **Volume ratio:** For every 1 hour of training, require 3 hours of explicit rest (vs. 1:1 for healthy athletes)
- **Periodization:** 2 days training, 1 day complete rest (vs. 5-6 days for peers)
- **Recovery metrics:** HRV must return to baseline before next session (wearable-gated training)
- **No training during infections:** Zero exercise during any illness, however mild

This keeps the biogenesis stimulus (AMPK activation from sub-threshold exercise) while never exceeding the ceiling (avoiding the damage-without-adaptation cycle).

**Evidence link:** Architecture C predicts that staying below $V_"crit"$ prevents the phase transition; Workwell Foundation pacing principles applied pre-illness; periodization science from sports medicine.

**Certainty: 0.55**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Prodromal athletes randomised to Training Ratio Protocol vs. conventional coaching for 12 months should show: (a) equivalent or better fitness gains (VO2max), (b) fewer illness episodes, (c) lower fatigue scores, (d) no crashes. If TRP athletes show WORSE fitness, the ceiling model's prediction that sub-threshold training still produces adaptation is wrong.

If the prodromal phase is 2-7 years (Wirth 2026, Collin 2018), then education targeting coaches, school nurses, pediatricians, and parents about the prodromal patterns could identify at-risk individuals BEFORE collapse. ## 6. Combinations + Access

**Tier 1 (Any at-risk individual — low cost, no prescription):**
- Iron status check + repletion to ferritin >80 (simple blood test)
- Creatine 3-5g/day (OTC, safe, cheap)
- Vitamin D optimisation (>40 ng/mL)
- Sleep hygiene + HRV-gated activity (wearable monitoring)

**Tier 2 (Confirmed prodromal signs + family history — GP-level):**

**Tier 3 (Actively deteriorating — specialist level):**
- All of Tier 1+2 PLUS
- Low-dose metformin 500mg (AMPK activation)
- Aggressive infection prevention (flu vaccine, hand hygiene education, REST during any illness)
- Formal pacing programme with envelope theory
- Consideration of low-dose naltrexone if immune dysregulation markers present

**Certainty: 0.30** (individual components plausible; no evidence for combined protocol)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Tier 2 intervention in 100 prodromal individuals over 2 years should show <10% conversion to full ME/CFS vs. expected 20-30% based on natural history. If conversion rates are identical, either the prodromal phase is not modifiable or these interventions miss the mechanism.

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### Idea 17: Stochastic Reserve Model — Adding Noise to the Headroom Equation

**Extension:** The current Architecture C model is deterministic: $R_"headroom" = J_"prod,max" - J_"demand"$. But prodromal symptoms are INTERMITTENT — they come and go. This suggests stochastic dynamics:

$$R_"headroom"(t) = J_"prod,max"(t) + \xi_"production"(t) - J_"demand"(t) - \xi_"demand"(t)$$

where $\xi$ terms represent random fluctuations (circadian rhythm, meal timing, sleep quality, infection exposure, menstrual cycle). Symptoms occur whenever $R_"headroom"(t) < 0$ transiently. The FREQUENCY of symptom episodes increases as mean headroom decreases (as reserve erodes), even before mean headroom hits zero permanently.

This predicts: (a) symptoms should be MORE VARIABLE in the prodromal phase (stochastic threshold crossing) vs. established ME/CFS (permanently below threshold); (b) symptom frequency should follow a Poisson-like distribution early, transitioning to constant later; (c) increasing symptom FREQUENCY is a better early warning signal than increasing symptom SEVERITY.

**Certainty: 0.50** (mathematically natural extension; clinically consistent with patient reports)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Time-series analysis of symptom diaries in prodromal individuals should show Poisson-distributed symptom events (random threshold crossings) early, transitioning to constant/daily symptoms as disease establishes. The transition from stochastic to deterministic should be identifiable as a change-point in the time series.

### Idea 18: Adaptation Rate Variable — Modelling Biogenesis Failure

**Extension:** Add a mitochondrial biogenesis rate variable $\beta(t)$ to the ODE system:

$$\frac{dJ_"prod,max"}{dt} = \beta(t) \cdot S_"AMPK"(t) - \gamma \cdot J_"prod,max"(t)$$

where $S_"AMPK"$ is the AMPK activation signal from exercise, $\beta$ is biogenesis efficiency, and $\gamma$ is mitochondrial turnover/degradation rate. In healthy individuals, $\beta$ is high enough that exercise-induced AMPK activation increases $J_"prod,max"$ over time (training effect). In prodromal ME/CFS:

- **Scenario A:** $\beta$ is constitutionally low (genetic — PGC-1alpha variants)
- **Scenario B:** $\gamma$ is elevated (increased mitochondrial damage from ROS)
- **Scenario C:** $S_"AMPK"$ is impaired (AMPK insensitivity)

Each scenario produces the same clinical phenotype (training without adaptation) but has different therapeutic implications: A requires pharmacological biogenesis stimulation; B requires antioxidant protection; C requires AMPK sensitisation (metformin).

**Certainty: 0.45**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Measuring AMPK phosphorylation, PGC-1alpha mRNA, and mitochondrial ROS in PBMCs after a standardised exercise challenge should distinguish scenarios A, B, C in prodromal individuals, pointing to the correct therapeutic target.

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### Idea 19: Overtraining Syndrome as Transient ME/CFS — Same Mechanism, Recoverable

**Mechanistic rationale:** Overtraining syndrome (OTS) in elite athletes shares nearly ALL features of ME/CFS: profound fatigue, performance decline, sleep disruption, mood changes, immune suppression, autonomic dysfunction, elevated resting HR, reduced HRV. The key difference: OTS is RECOVERABLE with rest (weeks to months). In Architecture C terms: OTS occurs when $J_"demand"$ chronically exceeds $J_"prod,max"$ but the system has not crossed the HYSTERETIC threshold — no permanent feed-forward damage loop is established. OTS is prodromal ME/CFS that recovered because the athlete rested BEFORE the phase transition.

**Prediction:** Athletes who develop OTS but DO recover are at elevated ME/CFS risk later (they demonstrated near-threshold dynamics). Athletes who develop OTS and DON'T fully recover (persistent OTS >6 months) should be re-evaluated for ME/CFS.

**Evidence link:** Shared biomarkers (reduced testosterone, elevated cortisol, IL-6 elevation, HRV reduction); OTS prevalence 20-60% in endurance athletes; the ~20% of OTS that becomes "prolonged" and never fully resolves.

**Certainty: 0.50**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Longitudinal follow-up of athletes with documented OTS episodes should show elevated ME/CFS incidence (>5x population rate) over 10 years. If OTS history confers NO excess ME/CFS risk, the shared-mechanism model is wrong.

### Idea 20: Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) as Gender-Specific Prodrome

**Mechanistic rationale:** RED-S (formerly "female athlete triad") describes low energy availability causing: menstrual dysfunction, bone loss, fatigue, impaired performance, mood disturbance, recurrent injury, impaired immunity. The overlap with ME/CFS prodromal signs is striking. In Architecture C terms: RED-S represents a state where $J_"demand" > J_"intake"$ chronically (energy intake too low), PLUS reduced $J_"prod,max"$ from the resulting iron/nutrient depletion. RED-S athletes are simultaneously reducing their ceiling (malnutrition damages mitochondria) AND operating above it (training demands exceed available energy). They are manufacturing the exact conditions the hysteretic phase-transition model predicts should trigger permanent ME/CFS.

**Evidence link:** RED-S athletes show: immune suppression (frequent infections), autonomic dysfunction, cognitive impairment, fatigue — all prodromal ME/CFS signs. RED-S is prevalent in the 15-25 age window overlapping with the early ME/CFS onset peak.

**Certainty: 0.45**

**Falsifiable prediction:** Athletes with documented RED-S should have elevated ME/CFS incidence post-infection compared to energy-sufficient athletes. RED-S severity scores should correlate with ME/CFS risk in a dose-dependent manner.

### Idea 21: Burnout-to-ME/CFS Pipeline — Allostatic Overload as Adult Prodrome

**Mechanistic rationale:** The adult equivalent of the prodromal child athlete may be the adult experiencing occupational burnout. Burnout (chronic workplace stress exceeding recovery capacity) produces: exhaustion, cognitive impairment, depersonalisation, immune dysregulation, sleep disruption, autonomic dysfunction. In Architecture C terms, burnout represents years of $J_"demand" > J_"recovery"$ without the dramatic collapse, maintained by sympathetic overdrive (cortisol, adrenaline). The system is progressively depleting reserves. When a triggering event occurs (infection, surgery, trauma) during burnout, the already-depleted system cannot mount an adequate response and crosses $V_"crit"$.

This predicts the late-onset peak (~age 37): peak career stress, peak burnout prevalence, peak life-complexity, PLUS beginning age-related mitochondrial decline. The "mitochondrial debt" speculation (spec:mitochondrial-reserve-late-peak) could be reframed: it's not just biological ageing but ALLOSTATIC OVERLOAD depleting reserves via sustained HPA axis activation, glucocorticoid-induced mitochondrial dysfunction, and chronic inflammation.

**Evidence link:** Kato 2006 (premorbid stress OR 5.81 for chronic fatigue); burnout prevalence peak ages 35-44; cortisol's mitochondrial toxicity (glucocorticoid receptor on mitochondrial membrane); the late-peak speculation in ch14d.

**Certainty: 0.40**

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a prospective cohort of burnout patients (validated by Maslach Burnout Inventory), post-infection ME/CFS incidence should be >3x population rate. Metabolic markers (PBMC spare respiratory capacity) should be intermediate between healthy controls and established ME/CFS, consistent with partial reserve depletion.

### Idea 23: 2-Day CPET Ratio as Gold-Standard Prodromal Biomarker

**Mechanistic rationale:** The 2-day CPET protocol (exercise test repeated at 24h) is the gold standard for demonstrating PEM in established ME/CFS (Day 2 VO2max drops). In prodromal individuals, we predict a MILDER version: Day 2 decline of 5-10% (vs. >15% in established ME/CFS, vs. 0% in healthy controls). This "subclinical PEM" would be objective, quantitative, and directly measures the phenomenon of interest (failure to recover from exertion).

**Implementation:** For at-risk adolescents (score >8 on prodromal composite), perform 2-day CPET. If Day 2 decline is 5-15%, this confirms prodromal status and justifies Tier 2 intervention. The test also establishes a BASELINE that can be tracked annually.

**Certainty: 0.55** (2-day CPET well-validated in ME/CFS; prodromal application is logical extension)

**Falsifiable prediction:** At-risk individuals with 5-10% Day 2 decline should have >5x ME/CFS conversion rate over 3 years compared to at-risk individuals with 0% decline. If Day 2 decline has no predictive value, the subclinical PEM model is wrong.

### Idea 24: HRV Recovery Kinetics as Wearable Prodromal Marker

**Mechanistic rationale:** After physical exertion, HRV recovery time reflects autonomic re-equilibration speed — which requires energy (re-establishing ion gradients, clearing metabolites, restoring neurotransmitter pools). In the prodromal phase, HRV should recover MORE SLOWLY than expected for fitness level, because the recovery process itself is energy-limited. Critically: this is measurable with consumer wearables (Garmin, Apple Watch, Oura Ring) making it a population-scale screening tool.

**Metric:** "HRV recovery ratio" = (time to return to resting HRV baseline after standardised exercise) / (expected recovery time for that fitness level). Ratio >1.5 = prodromal flag.

**Evidence link:** Wyller 2007 (autonomic dysfunction in adolescent ME/CFS); HRV as established ME/CFS biomarker; consumer wearable data availability.

**Certainty: 0.50**

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a cohort wearing HRV-capable wearables for >12 months, individuals who later develop ME/CFS should show progressive lengthening of HRV recovery time in the 6-12 months preceding diagnosis. The recovery ratio should discriminate pre-ME/CFS from deconditioning (deconditioned people recover slowly but improve with training; pre-ME/CFS people recover slowly and DON'T improve with training).

### Idea 25: Salivary Mitochondrial Stress Markers — Non-Invasive Prodromal Screen

**Mechanistic rationale:** Saliva contains cell-free mitochondrial DNA, lactate, and oxidative stress markers (8-OHdG). If prodromal individuals have subclinical mitochondrial stress, these markers should be detectable in a simple, non-invasive sample. The advantage over blood: no needle (critical for pediatric compliance), can be collected at home, can be repeated frequently to track trajectory.

**Proposed panel:**
- Salivary cell-free mitochondrial DNA (elevated = mitochondrial stress release)
- Salivary lactate (elevated = glycolytic compensation for OxPhos limitation)
- Salivary 8-OHdG (elevated = oxidative DNA damage)
- Salivary cortisol diurnal curve (flattened = HPA axis dysfunction)

Combine into a single "mitochondrial stress index" normalised for age and sex.

**Certainty: 0.30** (individual markers validated in other contexts; salivary mito markers not validated in ME/CFS; feasibility high)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Salivary mitochondrial stress index should be: (a) elevated in prodromal individuals vs. healthy controls, (b) intermediate between controls and established ME/CFS, (c) predictive of ME/CFS conversion. If salivary markers show no elevation despite confirmed prodromal status on CPET, salivary sampling lacks sensitivity for this application.

## Caveats
