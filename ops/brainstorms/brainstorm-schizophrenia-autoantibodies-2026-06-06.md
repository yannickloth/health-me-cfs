### SA-01: REAP/Phage Display in ME/CFS — Specific Experimental Design

- **Category:** 9 — Diagnostic/Biomarker
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.55

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Proteome-wide unbiased autoantibody screening (REAP — Rapid Extracellular Antigen Profiling, as deployed by Nemani 2026 in schizophrenia) uses phage-displayed human extracellular proteins to capture circulating autoantibodies without target preconception. In schizophrenia, this revealed ~2× extracellular autoantibody burden in patients vs controls, identifying novel targets (CNS antigens, neuroactive receptors, ion channels, synaptic proteins, BBB antigens) that targeted ELISAs had missed for decades. ME/CFS GPCR autoantibody research has been entirely target-driven (CellTrend ELISA for β2-AdR, M1/M3/M4, ETA/B, AT1). REAP in ME/CFS would likely discover autoantibody targets beyond this panel — potentially including the same classes Nemani found: ion channels, transporters, adhesion molecules, and other GPCRs not in the standard panel. The Nemani finding that higher autoantibody burden predicted worse risperidone response parallels the ME/CFS observation that autoantibody-positive patients may be immunotherapy-responsive.

**Evidence Link:**
Nemani 2026 (schizophrenia, n=352 SCZ, 971 controls, REAP platform; preprint); Pluvinage 2024 (REAP discovered anti-CD320 causing neurologic B12 deficiency); Freitag 2021, Azcue 2026 (targeted GPCR ELISA only in ME/CFS). Direct parallel: before Nemani, schizophrenia autoantibody research was similarly limited to targeted panels and found only modest signals.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
ME/CFS patients (n ≥ 200) screened by REAP against a human extracellular proteome library (>2,500 targets) will show significantly higher extracellular autoantibody burden than healthy controls (effect size d ≥ 0.3), with ≥ 5 novel autoantibody targets identified that correlate with symptom domains (cognitive, autonomic, pain, fatigue). The novel target set will NOT be limited to the current GPCR panel and will include ≥ 2 ion channel or synaptic protein specificities. A replication cohort (n ≥ 100) will confirm the top 10 hits.

### SA-02: Immunoglobulin Subclass Profiling for Pathogenicity Stratification

- **Category:** 9 — Diagnostic/Biomarker
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.50

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The schizophrenia literature shows a critical IgG subclass problem: among anti-NMDAR antibody-positive patients, the vast majority carry IgA/IgM (non-pathogenic), and only 0.5–1.5% carry IgG (the established pathogenic subclass) (Pollak 2014, Schou 2016). Total autoantibody prevalence of 7.98% collapses to 1.46% IgG. ME/CFS GPCR autoantibody research uses total IgG ELISA (CellTrend), which detects all IgG subclasses combined. If ME/CFS autoantibodies follow the same pattern — mostly IgG4 (non-inflammatory, blocking) rather than IgG1 (inflammatory, complement-fixing) — then total IgG titers may misrepresent true pathogenic burden. The existing paper's speculation on IgG4-to-IgG1 isotype switching (@ch28) needs systematic validation. IgG subclass-specific assays (IgG1, IgG2, IgG3, IgG4) would reveal which subclass drives ME/CFS pathology, enabling: (a) better patient selection for immunoadsorption, (b) correlation of subclass with clinical severity, (c) identification of "pathogenic isotype signature."

**Evidence Link:**
Pollak 2014 (meta-analysis: 7.98% positive any Ig class, only 1.46% IgG); Schou 2016 (n=925, 11.6% anti-neuronal Ab overall, only 0.5% NMDAR IgG); Hartwig 2020 (ME/CFS IgG fails to activate β2-AdR — functional deficit); existing paper ch28 IgG4-IgG1 isotype switch speculation (certainty 0.42). No ME/CFS study has profiled GPCR autoantibodies by IgG subclass.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In n ≥ 100 ME/CFS patients with elevated GPCR autoantibodies (CellTrend-positive), IgG subclass profiling will show: (1) IgG1 and IgG3 (inflammatory, complement-fixing) subclasses correlate more strongly with disease severity (r ≥ 0.4) than total IgG (r ≤ 0.2); (2) IgG4 (non-inflammatory, blocking) autoantibodies are present in ≥ 30% of patients and negatively correlate with symptom severity; (3) the IgG1/IgG4 ratio discriminates immunoadsorption responders from non-responders with AUC ≥ 0.75.

### SA-03: Autoimmune ME/CFS Diagnostic Criteria — Modeled on Autoimmune Psychosis Checklist

- **Category:** 9 — Diagnostic/Biomarker
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.45

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The Tebartz van Elst 2025 Neuropsychiatric Checklist for Autoimmune Psychosis (AP) integrates red-flag symptoms, FDG-PET, CSF, EEG, and MRI findings into a clinical scoring system that estimates the likelihood of an autoimmune etiology in an individual psychosis patient. No equivalent exists for ME/CFS. The schizophrenia experience shows that diagnostic heterogeneity is the single greatest barrier to recognizing autoantibody subgroups (Bartley & Ross 2020). An Autoimmune ME/CFS Checklist would combine: (a) clinical red-flags (specific infection trigger, sudden onset, family history of autoimmunity, multi-system involvement beyond fatigue, steroid-responsiveness in flares), (b) laboratory biomarkers (elevated GPCR autoantibodies, CSF oligoclonal bands, BBB disruption markers, NK cell dysfunction), (c) imaging (brain FDG-PET hypometabolism pattern, neuroinflammation on TSPO-PET). This checklist would enable enrichment of clinical trial populations and guide immunotherapy decisions while definitive assays are still in development.

**Evidence Link:**
Tebartz van Elst 2025 (autoimmune psychosis checklist — narrative review with clinical tool); Endres 2020 (145 AP cases: 87% received immunotherapy, 94% responded — demonstrating clinical utility of systematic autoantibody assessment); existing ME/CFS paper: GPCR autoantibody panel exists but no clinical decision framework; ch14 treatment map flags "GPCR Autoantibody Cascade" as highest-unmet-need Tier 1 target.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
An Autoimmune ME/CFS Diagnostic Checklist (scored 0–20 from clinical + lab + imaging domains) will identify a subgroup of ME/CFS patients (score ≥ 12) who: (1) have ≥ 2× higher GPCR autoantibody titers vs low-scorers; (2) show ≥ 40% response rate to immunoadsorption vs ≤ 10% in low-scorers; (3) show distinct FDG-PET pattern (frontotemporal hypometabolism) consistent with autoimmune encephalitis-like metabolic signature.

### SA-04: Longitudinal Autoantibody Trajectories Over Disease Course

- **Category:** 2 — Research Direction
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.40

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Pearlman 2014 showed that NR2A/NR2B autoantibody titers in first-episode psychosis decline 58% over 8 weeks and 13% over 4 days in acute mania, demonstrating that autoantibody titers fluctuate dynamically and that cross-sectional measurement may misclassify patients. No longitudinal GPCR autoantibody study exists in ME/CFS. Temporal dynamics are critical because: (1) titers may spike during symptom flares (providing a biomarker of flare onset), (2) titers may decline spontaneously in some patients (explaining loss of treatment response), (3) the rate of titer change may differentiate primary autoimmune (rapidly fluctuating) from secondary bystander (stable) autoantibodies, (4) longitudinal IgG subclass shifts (IgG4→IgG1 transition) may mark disease progression from early to chronic stages. The existing paper's reverse cascade prediction (autoantibody normalization over months) and attractor migration model (autoantibody acquisition drives attractor shift) both depend on temporal dynamics that have never been measured.

**Evidence Link:**
Pearlman 2014 (NR2A/NR2B titers decline with treatment in first-episode); existing paper ch33 (autoantibody timescale τ ≈ months, attractor migration driven by autoantibody acquisition); ch28 (plasma cell half-life months–years). No ME/CFS longitudinal autoantibody study exists.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
Serial GPCR autoantibody measurement (monthly for 12 months, n ≥ 60 ME/CFS) will reveal: (1) ≥ 30% of patients show ≥ 2-fold titer fluctuation over the year; (2) titer spikes correlate temporally with PEM/symptom flares (lag 0–7 days, r ≥ 0.3); (3) patients with rising titers over 12 months have worse functional decline (SF-36 PF −10 pts) vs stable or declining titer groups; (4) IgG subclass ratio (IgG1/IgG4) increases with disease duration, consistent with isotype switching hypothesis.

### SA-05: BBB Disruption Permits Pathogenic Autoantibody CNS Access — Combined Mechanism

- **Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.35

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
In schizophrenia, Nemani 2026 found autoantibodies against BBB antigens, suggesting that autoantibody-mediated BBB disruption may be part of the disease mechanism — not just a consequence. In ME/CFS, the existing paper models BBB permeability (P_BBB) as a function of inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β), with cooperative saturation kinetics. However, the model does not include autoantibodies as direct BBB disruptors. The novel hypothesis: ME/CFS autoantibodies target BBB endothelial antigens (tight junction proteins, transporters, adherens junction components) and actively increase BBB permeability, creating a feed-forward loop: autoantibodies → BBB leak → CNS entry of peripheral autoantibodies + cytokines → neuroinflammation → more BBB damage → worse CNS symptom burden. This is distinct from the current model where cytokine-driven BBB leak is purely inflammatory. The schizophrenia parallel suggests autoantibodies against brain endothelial cells exist and are functional. If ME/CFS shares this mechanism, BBB-stabilizing interventions (PEA, luteolin) would need to be combined with autoantibody reduction for efficacy — explaining the high interaction effect of P_BBB in GSA (existing paper ch32: S_T ≈ 0.14, S_T − S_1 ≈ 0.09).

**Evidence Link:**
Nemani 2026 (schizophrenia REAP: autoantibodies against BBB antigens detected); existing paper ch28 (BBB model P_BBB driven by TNF-α, IL-1β — no autoantibody term); ch32 (GSA shows P_BBB has high interaction effect S_T − S_1 ≈ 0.09); ch14a (BBB vulnerability documented); Stein 2023 (immunoadsorption improves post-COVID ME/CFS — mechanism may include BBB antigen autoantibody removal). No study has tested ME/CFS sera for BBB antigen autoantibodies.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
ME/CFS sera (n ≥ 100) screened against BBB endothelial antigen panels (claudin-5, occludin, ZO-1, GLUT1, LRP1) by ELISA and cell-based assay will show: (1) ≥ 15% of ME/CFS patients have autoantibodies against ≥ 1 BBB antigen vs ≤ 5% of controls; (2) BBB autoantibody-positive patients have elevated CSF/serum albumin ratio (Q_Alb > 7.0), indicating objective BBB disruption; (3) these autoantibodies are functional — patient IgG increases endothelial monolayer permeability in vitro (Transwell assay, ≥ 20% increase vs control IgG); (4) BBB autoantibody status predicts preferential CNS symptom burden (cognitive ≥ 3 SD below norm, brain fog severity) independent of peripheral GPCR autoantibody status.

### SA-06: Treatment-Responsive Autoantibody Subtype Identification

- **Category:** 2 — Research Direction
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.45

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The schizophrenia experience provides a clear roadmap for identifying treatment-responsive subtypes: (1) Endres 2020 — 145 case series of autoantibody-associated psychiatric syndromes: 87% received immunotherapy, 94% responded. (2) Methotrexate RCT in schizophrenia (Chaudhry 2020): modest positive symptom improvement but NO biomarker selection — the null result likely reflects unselected patient heterogeneity. (3) Plasmapheresis trials in schizophrenia (Cox 2020): negative — done without autoantibody-based patient selection. ME/CFS is at the same crossroads: RituxME (Fluge 2019) was negative in unselected patients; immunoadsorption pilot (Stein 2023) showed 70% response in autoantibody-selected post-COVID ME/CFS. The key research direction: identify which autoantibody profile predicts response to which immunotherapy. Candidates: (a) IgG1/IgG3-dominant GPCR autoantibodies → immunoadsorption or daratumumab; (b) IgM-dominant tissue-specific autoantibodies → BTK inhibitor or complement inhibition; (c) IgA-dominant or low-titer → symptomatic treatment only. A biomarker-stratified sequential trial design (enrich for autoantibody-positive, then randomize) would parallel the schizophrenia field's evolution from unselected to autoantibody-selected trials.

**Evidence Link:**
Endres 2020 (145 AP cases: 94% immunotherapy response); Chaudhry 2020 (methotrexate RCT: modest effect, no biomarker selection); Cox 2020 (plasmapheresis: negative without patient selection); Fluge 2019 (RituxME: negative in unselected); Stein 2023 (immunoadsorption: 70% in autoantibody-selected post-COVID ME/CFS); existing paper ch18 emerging therapies (BTK inhibition, complement inhibition, BAFF inhibition proposals all contingent on autoantibody stratification); ch28 (B cell model: autoantibody kinetics).

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In a biomarker-stratified trial of immunoadsorption (n ≥ 60 ME/CFS, all GPCR autoantibody-positive), baseline IgG subclass profile will predict response: (1) IgG1/IgG3-dominant patients (≥ 60% of total IgG autoantibody) will show ≥ 50% response rate vs ≤ 20% in IgG4-dominant patients; (2) response correlates with reduction in specific subclass (r ≥ 0.5 for IgG1 titer change vs symptom change); (3) patients with ≥ 3 different GPCR autoantibody specificities (broad repertoire) respond better than single-specificity patients (OR ≥ 2.0).

### SA-07: Passive Transfer Model for ME/CFS IgG Pathogenicity

- **Category:** 2 — Research Direction
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.40

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The gold standard for establishing autoantibody pathogenicity is passive transfer: patient IgG injected into animals produces disease-relevant phenotypes. Dalmau 2008 showed NMDAR antibodies reduce receptor cluster density in vitro and in vivo, establishing causality. Chen 2026 and Mignolet 2026 demonstrated passive transfer models for long COVID IgG producing pain, fatigue, and autonomic changes in mice. Goebel 2021 showed fibromyalgia IgG transfer produces pain hypersensitivity. ME/CFS GPCR autoantibodies have NOT been tested by passive transfer. The key experiment: purified IgG from ME/CFS patients with elevated β2-AdR autoantibodies, injected into mice, should produce: (a) reduced heart rate variability (autonomic dysfunction), (b) reduced voluntary wheel running (fatigue), (c) impaired cognitive performance (novel object recognition), (d) increased pain sensitivity (von Frey). Controls: IgG from autoantibody-negative ME/CFS, IgG from healthy controls, and IgG-depleted ME/CFS serum (to confirm GPCR autoantibodies are causative).

**Evidence Link:**
Dalmau 2008 (NMDAR-Ab → reduced cluster density — in vitro + in vivo); Chen 2026, Mignolet 2026 (long COVID IgG passive transfer — mouse model); Goebel 2021 (fibromyalgia IgG transfer — pain hypersensitivity); Hartwig 2020 (ME/CFS IgG fails to activate β2-AdR in vitro). No ME/CFS passive transfer model exists. Existing paper ch28 discusses passive transfer as a gap (noted for future research).

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
Mice receiving intraperitoneal IgG (200 mg/kg, 3 doses over 5 days) from GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS patients (n ≥ 5 donors, each tested in ≥ 5 mice) will show: (1) significant reduction in HRV (SDNN ↓ ≥ 30% vs control IgG, p < 0.01); (2) reduced voluntary wheel running (↓ ≥ 25%, p < 0.05) on days 3–7 post-transfer; (3) impaired novel object recognition (discrimination index ↓ ≥ 40%, p < 0.05); (4) these effects are abolished by IgG depletion and restored by purified GPCR autoantibody fraction. IgG from autoantibody-negative ME/CFS patients produces no effect.

### SA-08: ME/CFS CSF Autoantibody Profiling — Unbiased Screening

- **Category:** 2 — Research Direction
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.35

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Serum autoantibodies may not reflect the CNS autoantibody repertoire. In autoimmune encephalitis, CSF is often diagnostic when serum is negative. In ME/CFS, all autoantibody research has been serum-based. CSF profiling could reveal: (a) intrathecal autoantibody synthesis (CNS-specific antibodies not present in serum), (b) autoantibodies against CNS-restricted antigens (synaptic proteins, neurotransmitters, myelin components) that are sequestered from the peripheral immune system, (c) evidence of BBB breach (CSF IgG index, oligoclonal bands). The schizophrenia literature shows that a subset of patients have CNS-directed autoantibodies (Nemani 2026 — REAP used serum but found primarily CNS antigens). CSF REAP (applying the same unbiased phage display to CSF) could discover ME/CFS-specific CNS autoantibodies that serum assays miss entirely. The existing paper's neuroinflammation model (microglial activation, BBB disruption) and brain clearance model both predict that CNS autoimmunity may be a significant but undetected component.

**Evidence Link:**
Nemani 2026 (REAP on serum: found CNS antigens as primary targets); Dalmau 2008 (CSF diagnostic superiority in autoimmune encephalitis); existing paper ch28 (BBB model, microglial activation model, neuroinflammation section); ch18 brain clearance models (border immune niche, glymphatic system). No ME/CFS CSF autoantibody REAP study exists.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
CSF from ME/CFS patients (n ≥ 50, paired serum available) screened by REAP against human extracellular proteome will identify: (1) ≥ 3 novel autoantibody targets enriched in CSF vs paired serum (intrathecal synthesis), including ≥ 1 CNS-restricted antigen; (2) ≥ 20% of patients with elevated IgG index (> 0.7) or CSF oligoclonal bands; (3) CSF autoantibody burden correlates with cognitive impairment (COMPASS cognitive score, r ≥ 0.4) independent of serum autoantibody levels; (4) at least one CSF autoantibody target is shared across ≥ 30% of patients (suggesting a common antigenic driver).

### SA-09: Cross-Disease Autoantibody Convergence Analysis

- **Category:** 8 — Cross-Disease Bridge
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.30

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Cullen 2019 showed that schizophrenia risk is elevated across numerous autoimmune disorders (OR 1.26), with highest associations for pernicious anemia (OR 1.91) and pemphigoid (OR 1.90) — conditions with well-characterized autoantibody mechanisms. The rare-to-common paradigm (Casanova & Abel 2022): rare Mendelian autoimmune encephalitis → common autoantibody subgroups in idiopathic psychosis. The same paradigm likely applies to ME/CFS: rare autoimmune conditions (autoimmune encephalitis, narcolepsy, Guillain-Barré, Sjögren's, SLE) → ME/CFS autoantibody subgroups. A systematic cross-disease autoantibody convergence analysis would: (a) compare autoantibody repertoires across ME/CFS, schizophrenia, autoimmune encephalitis, long COVID, fibromyalgia, and post-treatment Lyme disease using the same unbiased platform (REAP or equivalent), (b) identify shared autoantibody targets (convergent autoimmunity) vs disease-specific targets, (c) determine whether autoantibody burden is a general transdiagnostic marker of neuroimmune dysfunction or carries disease-specific information. The existing paper's cross-disease comparison (ch33) and Gulf War Illness discussion would be extended by this analysis.

**Evidence Link:**
Cullen 2019 (meta-analysis: autoimmune risk across psychiatric disorders); Casanova & Abel 2022 (rare-to-common paradigm); existing paper ch33 (cross-disease bridges: GWI, long COVID, autoimmune encephalitis); ch30 integrated systems (multimorbidity framework). No systematic cross-disease autoantibody comparison has been performed using an unbiased platform.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
REAP profiling across ME/CFS (n = 100), schizophrenia (n = 100), long COVID (n = 100), fibromyalgia (n = 100), and healthy controls (n = 100) will show: (1) ≥ 3 autoantibody targets shared across ≥ 3 disease groups (convergent autoimmunity); (2) ≥ 2 autoantibody targets unique to ME/CFS (disease-specific); (3) shared targets are predominantly GPCRs and ion channels, while disease-specific targets are tissue-restricted antigens; (4) autoantibody burden (total hits per patient) correlates with symptom severity across all groups (r ≥ 0.3), suggesting a transdiagnostic neuroimmune severity dimension.

### SA-10: ODE Model of Autoantibody-Receptor Dynamics with IgG Subclass

- **Category:** 7 — Mathematical Model Extension
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.45

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The existing paper's GPCR autoantibody ODE (@ch28 speculation, certainty 0.55) models A_ab(t), R_sens(t), R_desens(t), HRV(t), and C(t). This model can be extended with IgG subclass-resolved variables: A_IgG1(t) and A_IgG4(t) with subclass-specific binding kinetics, receptor effects, and clearance rates. IgG1 has higher FcγR binding affinity (pro-inflammatory, receptor crosslinking, complement activation) and shorter half-life (~21 days). IgG4 has lower FcγR affinity (blocking, non-inflammatory) and can undergo Fab-arm exchange (monovalent binding, reduced crosslinking). The subclass-specific ODE would predict: (a) disease severity depends on IgG1/IgG4 ratio, not total titer; (b) immunoadsorption kinetics differ by subclass (IgG1 clears faster, rebounds faster); (c) subclass-specific treatment response (IgG1-dominant → immunoadsorption; IgG4-dominant → B cell/plasma cell depletion). This extends the existing paper's isotype switch speculation (certainty 0.42) into a quantitative framework.

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper ch28: IgG4-IgG1 isotype switch ODE speculation (certainty 0.42); GPCR AAb ODE speculation (certainty 0.55); ch33 (autoantibody timescale τ ≈ months); Hartwig 2020 (functional IgG assay); Pollak 2014 (IgG subclass prevalence in schizophrenia). General IgG subclass biology (IgG1 half-life 21d, IgG4 Fab-arm exchange, FcγR affinities) is well-established.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
(Mathematical) The subclass-ODE model predicts: (1) a bifurcation in disease severity at IgG1/IgG4 ratio ≈ 1.5 (below this ratio, receptor desensitization is reversible; above it, stable pathological signaling emerges); (2) immunoadsorption produces rapid IgG1 reduction (τ ≈ 3 days) but IgG4 contribution to total AAb rebounds within 14 days due to Fab-arm exchange from tissue pools; (3) daratumumab reduces both subclasses proportionally (plasma cell depletion), while BTK inhibition preferentially reduces IgG4 (extrafollicular → IgG4 more dependent on new B cell activation). These predictions are testable in clinical trial biorepositories.

### SA-11: FcRn Inhibition (Efgartigimod) for Autoantibody Reduction in ME/CFS

- **Category:** 3 — Drug/Medication Idea
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.30

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
FcRn (neonatal Fc receptor) protects IgG from lysosomal degradation, giving IgG its ~21-day half-life. FcRn inhibition (efgartigimod, a human IgG1 Fc fragment engineered for FcRn binding) accelerates IgG clearance, reducing total IgG and pathogenic autoantibodies by ~50–70% within weeks. FDA-approved for generalized myasthenia gravis (anti-AChR antibody-positive). In ME/CFS: if GPCR autoantibodies are pathogenic, FcRn inhibition would rapidly reduce their titers by accelerating catabolism without affecting production. Advantages over immunoadsorption: (a) no apheresis procedure, (b) no hemodynamic stress, (c) weekly IV infusion vs 3–5 apheresis sessions, (d) sustained IgG reduction between doses. Disadvantages: (a) non-specific IgG reduction (infection risk), (b) does not target production (rebound upon discontinuation), (c) IV administration still burdensome. The existing paper's Phase 2 RCT of efgartigimod in ME/CFS was negative (ch14 treatment map), which may reflect patient selection (unselected → no enrichment for autoantibody-positivity) — the same problem as unselected plasmapheresis in schizophrenia (Cox 2020).

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper ch14: "Efgartigimod — FcRn blocker → IgG half-life ↓ — Phase 2 RCT negative"; ch14 treatment map (listed under GPCR Autoantibody Cascade); MG clinical trials (efgartigimod reduces anti-AChR IgG by ~70%); Cox 2020 (unselected plasmapheresis in schizophrenia: negative). The negative ME/CFS trial may have used unselected patients.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In an autoantibody-selected trial (GPCR autoantibody-positive, IgG1-dominant, n ≥ 40), efgartigimod 10 mg/kg IV weekly for 8 weeks will: (1) reduce total IgG by ≥ 50% and GPCR autoantibody titers by ≥ 60%; (2) produce clinical improvement (SF-36 PF + ≥ 15 pts) in ≥ 40% of patients vs ≤ 10% placebo; (3) response correlates with GPCR autoantibody reduction (r ≥ 0.5); (4) 4-week follow-up after last dose shows rebound of ≤ 30% of baseline titer, with clinical worsening tracking titer recovery.

### SA-12: BTK Inhibition for Extrafollicular IgM Autoantibody Pathway

- **Category:** 3 — Drug/Medication Idea
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.35

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Long COVID data (Tatai 2026) show persistent IgM-dominant autoantibodies at 141-day follow-up (60% vs 36% controls), suggesting ongoing extrafollicular B cell activation independent of germinal center reactions. BTK (Bruton's tyrosine kinase) is essential for BCR signal transduction; BTK inhibitors (acalabrutinib, ibrutinib) suppress extrafollicular plasmablast differentiation and reduce IgM autoantibody production. This pathway may be particularly relevant for ME/CFS patients with: (a) IgM-dominant autoantibody profiles, (b) post-infectious onset (where extrafollicular responses dominate early), (c) treatment resistance to B cell depletion (rituximab: spares IgM plasma cells). The existing paper already proposes BTK inhibition for ME/CFS (ch18 emerging therapies, certainty 0.35) with the specific prediction of ≥ 30% IgM reduction. The schizophrenia parallel strengthens this: Liu 2025 showed clozapine + rapamycin synergy in an autoimmune schizophrenia model — both drugs affect B cell signaling pathways (rapamycin through mTOR, clozapine through IL-6 modulation). BTK inhibition is the more targeted approach.

**Evidence Link:**
Tatai 2026 (long COVID: IgM persistence 60% vs 36% controls, 141 days); existing paper ch18 (BTK inhibition speculation, certainty 0.35, falsifiable prediction: acalabrutinib 100 mg daily × 12 weeks → IgM reduction ≥ 30%); Liu 2025 (rapamycin + clozapine in autoimmune schizophrenia model — mTOR and IL-6 pathway modulation affects B cell function); autoimmune disease literature (ibrutinib reduces autoantibodies in RA, lupus models).

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
ME/CFS patients with IgM-dominant GPCR autoantibody profiles (IgM/IgG ratio > 1 for ≥ 2 specificities, n ≥ 30) receiving acalabrutinib 100 mg BID for 12 weeks will show: (1) total IgM reduction ≥ 35% and GPCR-specific IgM reduction ≥ 45%; (2) clinical response in ≥ 35% (SF-36 PF + ≥ 10 pts) vs ≤ 10% in IgG-dominant patients receiving the same treatment; (3) responders show normalization of extrafollicular B cell markers (plasmablast frequency ↓ ≥ 50%, serum BAFF ↓ ≥ 20%); (4) non-responders show compensated survival of IgM plasma cells (persistent IgM despite BCR signal blockade), identifying them as candidates for plasma cell depletion (daratumumab) instead.

### SA-13: Conformational Epitope Hypothesis for Germain 2025 Null

- **Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.40

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Germain 2025 screened 7,542 protein interactions using REAP + Luminex and found NO ME/CFS-specific autoantibody signal. This null result is puzzling given the replicated GPCR autoantibody findings by CellTrend ELISA (Freitag 2021, Azcue 2026). The conformational epitope hypothesis resolves this discrepancy: REAP uses linear peptides expressed on phage (short, ~50–150 amino acid fragments of extracellular domains); GPCRs have complex multi-pass transmembrane topology with highly conformational extracellular loops. The CellTrend ELISA uses full-length GPCRs expressed on HEK cells (native conformation). If ME/CFS GPCR autoantibodies recognize conformational epitopes (dependent on proper 3D folding, glycosylation, and membrane embedding), they would be invisible to REAP/Luminex but detectable by cell-based ELISA. The schizophrenia parallel: NMDAR autoantibodies nearly always recognize conformational epitopes on native GluN1/GluN2 subunits — cell-based assays are the gold standard, not linear peptide ELISAs. This hypothesis explains both: (a) why targeted (conformation-preserving) assays find GPCR autoantibodies in ME/CFS, and (b) why unbiased (linear-epitope) screening missed them. Prediction: REAP/Luminex will be negative for GPCR-specific hits but may find non-GPCR autoantibodies against linear epitopes that have been missed by targeted assays.

**Evidence Link:**
Germain 2025 (REAP + Luminex, 7,542 interactions, no signal); Freitag 2021, Azcue 2026 (CellTrend cell-based ELISA: GPCR autoantibodies found); Dalmau 2008 (NMDAR-Ab: conformational epitopes — cell-based assay required); Nemani 2026 (schizophrenia REAP: found primarily non-GPCR targets — NMDA receptor, ion channels, synaptic proteins). No study has systematically compared linear vs conformational GPCR antigen presentation in ME/CFS.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
Paired testing of ME/CFS sera (n ≥ 100) across three platforms: (1) CellTrend cell-based GPCR ELISA (conformation-preserving); (2) REAP/Luminex linear extracellular domain peptides; (3) in-house full-length GPCR expressed on HEK cells (independent validation). GPCR autoantibodies will be detected by platforms (1) and (3) but NOT by platform (2). Conversely, REAP/Luminex (platform 2) will detect ≥ 3 novel linear-epitope targets (non-GPCR, likely ion channels or transporters) that are missed by both cell-based platforms. A fourth platform using membrane protein arrays (full-length GPCRs in nanodiscs — preserving conformation in a cell-free system) will partially rescue REAP detection for GPCR targets.

### SA-14: Rapamycin + Immunomodulation Parallel from Autoimmune Schizophrenia Model

- **Category:** 3 — Drug/Medication Idea
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.25

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Liu 2025 demonstrated that both clozapine and rapamycin reversed PPI deficits, cognitive impairment, and motor deficits in an autoimmune schizophrenia mouse model (ApoE−/− with anti-SFT2D2 antibodies). Both drugs reduced microglial infiltration and restored dendritic spine density. The synergy of immunosuppression (rapamycin, an mTOR inhibitor) + antipsychotic (clozapine, a dopamine/serotonin antagonist with unique immune effects) suggests a broad principle: autoantibody-driven neuroimmune conditions may require dual targeting of the autoantibody production pathway AND the downstream neural effects. In ME/CFS, this parallel suggests: (a) rapamycin as an mTOR inhibitor could reduce microglial activation and dendritic spine loss, independent of autoantibody production; (b) combining rapamycin with an autoantibody-targeted therapy (immunoadsorption, daratumumab) may produce synergistic CNS recovery beyond either alone. The existing paper already models mTOR pathway involvement in energy metabolism and epigenetic consolidation but does not propose rapamycin specifically. Rapamycin is FDA-approved as an immunosuppressant (sirolimus) and is well-tolerated at low doses (< 5 mg/day).

**Evidence Link:**
Liu 2025 (autoimmune schizophrenia model: clozapine + rapamycin — PPI deficits reversed, microglial infiltration reduced, dendritic spine density restored); existing paper ch18 (no rapamycin proposal currently); ch28 (microglial activation model: sustained μ1 dominance in ME/CFS); ch27 (mTOR pathway in energy metabolism). No rapamycin trial exists in ME/CFS.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
Low-dose rapamycin (sirolimus 2 mg/day, target trough 5–10 ng/mL) for 12 weeks in autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (n ≥ 20) will: (1) reduce neuroinflammatory markers (CSF IL-6 ↓ ≥ 30%, NFL ↓ ≥ 20%); (2) improve cognitive performance (CANTAB spatial working memory, errors ↓ ≥ 25%); (3) NOT reduce GPCR autoantibody titers (rapamycin affects T cell/mTOR, not plasma cells); (4) combining rapamycin with immunoadsorption (sequential, IA first then rapamycin maintenance) produces superior cognitive improvement vs IA alone (+15 pts SF-36 cognitive subscale) — an N-of-1 crossover design is feasible.

### SA-15: Complement C1s Inhibition for IgM-Dominant ME/CFS

- **Category:** 3 — Drug/Medication Idea
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.20

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
IgM is the most potent activator of the classical complement pathway (single IgM molecule can activate C1). In long COVID, Tatai 2026 demonstrated persistent IgM-dominant autoantibodies. If ME/CFS patients with IgM-dominant profiles have complement-mediated tissue injury, C1s inhibition (sutimlimab, approved for cold agglutinin disease — an IgM-mediated autoimmune hemolytic anemia) would block complement activation at the most proximal step while preserving alternative and lectin pathways. This is mechanistically distinct from autoantibody removal (immunoadsorption) or production suppression (BTKi, daratumumab) — it targets the effector mechanism downstream of autoantibody binding. The existing paper (ch18) already proposes sutimlimab for complement-mediated ME/CFS (certainty 0.35) based on the same IgM rationale, but the schizophrenia parallel strengthens the hypothesis: complement dysregulation is documented in both schizophrenia (C4 risk alleles, elevated complement proteins) and ME/CFS (elevated C3a, C5a in some studies).

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper ch18: sutimlimab speculation (certainty 0.35, IgM-mediated complement activation, falsifiable prediction); Tatai 2026 (long COVID IgM persistence); cold agglutinin disease trials (sutimlimab: rapid complement blockade, transfusion independence); schizophrenia literature (C4 risk alleles, complement activation in CSF). No complement inhibition data exist in ME/CFS.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
ME/CFS patients with IgM-dominant autoantibody profiles AND elevated complement activation markers (C3a, C5a, sC5b-9 ≥ 2× normal, n ≥ 10 open-label) receiving sutimlimab (6.5 g IV day 1, then 7.5 g weekly × 4) will show: (1) rapid (within 1 week) normalization of complement activation markers (C3a, C5a, sC5b-9 reduction ≥ 80%); (2) symptom improvement in patients with complement-dependent tissue damage (endothelial dysfunction, microvascular injury identified by nailfold capillaroscopy) within 2–4 weeks; (3) NO improvement in patients whose symptoms are driven by non-complement pathways (direct receptor desensitization by IgG autoantibodies). This identifies complement activation as a modifiable disease amplifier distinct from direct receptor autoantibody effects.

### SA-16: Immunoadsorption + BTK Inhibitor Induction-Maintenance Protocol

- **Category:** 6 — Combination + Access
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.25

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The schizophrenia plasmapheresis experience (Cox 2020) showed that antibody removal alone is ineffective without sustained suppression of production — apheresis trials in the 1970s–80s failed because antibodies rebounded within weeks. The same problem applies to ME/CFS: immunoadsorption provides rapid autoantibody reduction but titers rebound within 4–8 weeks as plasma cells resume production (existing paper B cell model: rebound kinetics determined by plasma cell half-life). A sequential induction-maintenance protocol parallels oncology and autoimmune nephrology: (1) Induction: immunoadsorption (5 sessions over 10 days) → rapid autoantibody clearance; (2) Maintenance: BTK inhibitor (acalabrutinib 100 mg BID) → suppress new autoantibody production by blocking BCR-dependent plasmablast differentiation. This combination addresses both existing autoantibodies (rapid removal) and ongoing production (sustained suppression). The maintenance phase could be time-limited (6–12 months) with monitoring for titer rebound after discontinuation. This is proposed in the existing paper (ch18 emerging therapies: BTK + IA combination, certainty 0.35) but deserves specific emphasis as the most pragmatic path to clinical implementation.

**Evidence Link:**
Cox 2020 (plasmapheresis in schizophrenia: ineffective without production suppression); existing paper ch18 (BTK + IA combination rationale); ch28 (B cell model: plasma cell rebound kinetics predicts titer recovery within 4–8 weeks of IA alone); Stein 2023 (IA monotherapy in post-COVID ME/CFS: 70% response at 2 weeks, durability through 6 months unclear). No BTK inhibitor + IA combination has been tested in any condition.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
In an open-label trial (n ≥ 20 GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS), sequential IA (5 sessions) → acalabrutinib (100 mg BID × 12 weeks) will: (1) show ≥ 80% autoantibody reduction at week 2 (IA effect) with ≤ 30% rebound at week 12 (BTKi maintenance effect); (2) IA-alone historical controls show ≥ 60% rebound at week 12; (3) clinical response rate (≥ 15 pt SF-36 PF improvement) at 12 weeks ≥ 50%; (4) 50% of responders maintain benefit at 6-month follow-off treatment (suggesting the maintenance phase allowed plasma cell pool contraction to establish new steady state). Compare to existing IA-alone cohort (Stein 2023: 70% response at ~2 weeks, durability unknown > 3 months).

### SA-17: Transcutaneous Vagal Stimulation for Autoantibody-Producing Milieu Reduction

- **Category:** 5 — Non-Pharmacological Intervention
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.20

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway (vagus nerve → splenic nerve → norepinephrine → β2-AdR on splenic T cells → ACh → α7nAChR on macrophages → TNF-α suppression) is a well-characterized neural circuit for immune regulation. Transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (tVNS) activates this pathway non-invasively, reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6, IL-1β) in clinical trials (rheumatoid arthritis, Crohn's disease, PTSD). If ME/CFS autoantibody production is driven by an inflammatory milieu (cytokine-driven B cell activation, extrafollicular pathway), then reducing that milieu through vagal stimulation could reduce the drive for autoantibody production. This is a "milieu modulation" strategy: not directly targeting autoantibodies or B cells, but reducing the inflammatory signals that promote their production and persistence. The existing paper's lifestyle section (ch17) already proposes tVNS for autoantibody reduction (certainty 0.15) with acknowledgment that effect size on B cell function is unknown. The schizophrenia parallel offers reassurance that neural-immune circuits are druggable: clozapine's unique immunomodulatory effects are partly mediated through vagal/autonomic pathways.

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper ch17: tVNS for autoantibody Milieu Reduction (certainty 0.15); ch30 integrated systems (vagal anti-inflammatory pathway model); Huerta 2025 (vagal efferent signaling via α7nAChR); RA trials (tVNS reduces TNF-α, IL-6); existing paper ch17: tVNS devices commercially available, parameters not standardized.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
tVNS (30 min daily, left ear tragus, 25 Hz, 0.5–5 mA, adjusted to patient tolerance) for 8 weeks in ME/CFS (n ≥ 30, sham-controlled crossover) will: (1) reduce serum IL-6 by ≥ 20% and TNF-α by ≥ 15% vs sham; (2) reduce GPCR autoantibody titers by ≥ 15% in patients with high baseline inflammatory cytokines (IL-6 > 5 pg/mL) but NOT in patients with low baseline inflammation (suggesting the effect is milieux-mediated, not direct B cell); (3) HRV improvement (RMSSD ↑ ≥ 15%) correlates with cytokine reduction (r ≥ 0.4); (4) HRV destabilization detected by CSD monitoring (existing paper ch33) predicts which patients have larger tVNS-induced cytokine reductions.

### SA-18: Omega-3 + NAC + Vitamin D Stack for GPCR Autoantibody Suppression

- **Category:** 4 — Supplement/Nutraceutical Idea
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.30

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Three mechanistically distinct supplements converge on autoantibody biology: (1) Omega-3 (EPA 2 g + DHA 1 g/day): incorporates into immune cell membranes, alters lipid raft composition, reduces FcγR signaling affinity — dampens FcγR-mediated cytokine production triggered by GPCR-IgG1 autoantibodies (existing paper ch18: certainty 0.45); (2) NAC (600 mg TID): increases glutathione, shifts redox balance, reduces oxidative stress-driven B cell activation — may suppress aberrant B cell activity and reduce GPCR autoantibody production (ch18: certainty 0.38); (3) Vitamin D3 (5,000 IU/day to 25-OH-D target 50–80 ng/mL): induces Tregs and promotes B cell tolerance, potentially reducing autoantibody production through immune regulation (ch18: certainty 0.42). Stack rationale: each targets a different node in the autoantibody production/perpetuation network — omega-3 on effector function, NAC on redox-dependent B cell activation, vitamin D on regulatory tolerance. Combined, they may produce additive or synergistic autoantibody reduction with minimal risk (all three are well-tolerated, low-cost, and already commonly used by ME/CFS patients). The schizophrenia parallel: omega-3 has shown modest benefit in early psychosis prevention (but not replicated in large trials), and vitamin D deficiency is consistently associated with both schizophrenia and ME/CFS.

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper ch18: omega-3 (certainty 0.45), NAC (0.38), vitamin D (0.42) — all proposed for GPCR AAb reduction with individual predictions; ch16 (supplements: NAC, omega-3, vitamin D already discussed for other mechanisms); RA/SLE trials (omega-3 reduces disease activity); lupus models (NAC reduces autoantibodies); vitamin D trials (Treg induction). No combination study in ME/CFS.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
A 24-week open-label trial (n ≥ 30 GPCR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS) of the triple stack (EPA 2g + DHA 1g + NAC 600mg TID + vitamin D3 5000 IU/day, with D-level monitoring) will show: (1) GPCR autoantibody titer reduction ≥ 20% (any subclass) at 24 weeks vs baseline; (2) IgG1 subclass reduction ≥ 25% (the inflammatory subclass); (3) response correlates with omega-3 index increase > 8% (r ≥ 0.5) and Treg expansion (CD4⁺FoxP3⁺ ↑ ≥ 20%, r ≥ 0.4); (4) individual supplements alone would be expected to produce < 10% reduction based on effect size estimates — the combination's effect exceeds the sum of individuals (synergy index > 1.2). This 4-arm trial (each arm n ≥ 15, including combination) would be a feasible pilot.

### SA-19: HSAT2 Retrotransposon Activation Drives Autoantibody Production

- **Category:** 1 — Novel Hypothesis
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.20

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The existing paper's epigenetic consolidation model proposes HSAT2 pericentromeric satellite repeat derepression as a key pathogenic mechanism. HSAT2 RNA can form double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) structures, activating innate immune sensors (MDA5, RIG-I, TLR3). This creates a Type I interferon (IFN-I) response that may promote B cell activation and autoantibody production. The novel hypothesis: HSAT2 reactivation → dsRNA → IFN-I signature → B cell activation → autoantibody production. This would link the two major arms of the existing paper — epigenetic change and autoimmunity — into a unified feed-forward mechanism. The schizophrenia parallel is striking: schizophrenia also shows transposable element activation (LINE-1, HERV-W, HERV-K), and autoantibodies in schizophrenia include anti-retroviral (anti-HERV) specificities. Nemani 2026 found autoantibodies against BBB antigens and synaptic proteins in schizophrenia — some of which may be driven by transposable element-activated innate immunity. The IFN-I signature in ME/CFS (elevated IFN-β, IFN-stimulated genes) may therefore be both a consequence of HSAT2 activation AND a driver of autoantibody production through extrafollicular B cell activation.

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper: HSAT2 model (pericentromeric derepression, ch33, speculation on retrotransposon-mediated immune activation); ch28 (B cell model: T cell-dependent activation); ch31 (IFN-I signature in ME/CFS); schizophrenia literature: transposable element activation (LINE-1, HERV) in psychosis, anti-HERV antibodies; Casanova & Abel 2022 (rare-to-common: IFN-I autoantibodies in critical COVID). The specific link from HSAT2 → dsRNA → IFN-I → B cell activation → autoantibodies has not been proposed.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
ME/CFS patients can be stratified by peripheral blood HSAT2 RNA levels (high vs low, median split). High-HSAT2 patients (n ≥ 30) vs low-HSAT2 patients (n ≥ 30) will show: (1) elevated IFN-I gene signature (IRF7, IFIT1, MX1, OAS1 expression, fold-change ≥ 2, p < 0.01); (2) elevated total autoantibody burden (REAP hits, ≥ 2× high vs low); (3) autoantibody repertoire enriched for nucleic acid-sensing pathway components (anti-dsRNA, anti-IFN-α, anti-MDA5 specificities); (4) in vitro, HSAT2 dsRNA transfection into PBMCs from healthy donors → IFN-I production → B cell activation (CD19⁺CD27⁺CD38⁺ plasmablast expansion ≥ 2-fold) → IgM secretion in culture supernatant. This would close the causal loop from HSAT2 → autoantibody production.

### SA-20: Autoantibody-Driven Attractor Migration in Multi-ODE Model

- **Category:** 7 — Mathematical Model Extension
- **Status:** ⬜
- **Certainty:** 0.35

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The existing paper's multi-attractor ODE model (ch33) defines attractor basins: immune-dominant, metabolic-dominant, neurovascular-dominant, severe/locked. Attractor migration is described qualitatively: patients may move between basins as they acquire locks (autoantibodies, epigenetic consolidation). This can be formalized by adding an autoantibody vector `A(t) = (A_1(t), A_2(t), ..., A_k(t))` representing titers of k different autoantibody specificities, each with its own production/clearance kinetics and receptor targets. The attractor landscape becomes a function of A(t): as autoantibodies develop (e.g., β2-AdR autoantibodies → neurovascular attractor), the landscape's basin boundaries shift. The ODE extension would predict: (a) a critical autoantibody burden threshold for attractor migration (how many specificities or what titer must accumulate before switching attractors); (b) whether attractor migration is reversible (if autoantibodies are removed, does the patient return to the previous attractor or does epigenetic consolidation create a one-way ratchet?); (c) the timescale of attractor migration (months to years, determined by the slowest timescale in the system). This addresses a key clinical question: can early autoantibody removal prevent progression to the severe/locked attractor? The schizophrenia parallel: Luykx 2024 found NMDAR-Ab+ patients had LESS severe symptoms — this may represent a "not yet migrated" attractor state that is still treatment-responsive.

**Evidence Link:**
Existing paper ch33: attractor migration hypothesis (certainty 0.40, directional prediction: immune → metabolic → severe); ch28: B cell model (autoantibody kinetics, plasma cell half-life); ch33: timescale hierarchy (autoantibody τ ≈ months); Luykx 2024 (NMDAR-Ab+ schizophrenia: less negative symptoms, better functioning — possibly a pre-migration attractor state). No quantitative model links autoantibody acquisition to attractor migration in any disease.

**Falsifiable Prediction:**
(Mathematical) The ODE extension predicts: (1) a threshold autoantibody burden θ_A (e.g., ≥ 3 specificities with titer > 75th percentile) that, when exceeded, triggers transition from immune-dominant to neurovascular-dominant attractor; (2) attractor migration is reversible only if the autoantibody burden is reduced below θ_A within τ_epi (epigenetic consolidation timescale, ≈ 6–12 months); after τ_epi, a "lock" is formed (epigenetic consolidation has deepened) and the migration becomes irreversible; (3) this predicts a therapeutic window of ≈ 6 months after autoantibody acquisition during which early immunoadsorption can prevent attractor migration. (Empirical) Retrospective analysis of natural history data (e.g., Nacul 2020, Chu 2019) will show: patients who received early immunomodulatory treatment (within 2 years of autoantibody-positive test) have lower rates of progression to severe disease (OR ≤ 0.4) vs untreated autoantibody-positive patients.

## Key Cross-Cutting Themes

1. **Unbiased screening is the highest-impact single investment.** SA-01 (REAP in ME/CFS) and SA-08 (CSF REAP) would address the most fundamental gap: we do not know the full autoantibody repertoire in ME/CFS. The schizophrenia field was stalled until Nemani 2026 applied REAP; ME/CFS could make the same leap.

2. **IgG subclass specificity resolves contradictory findings.** SA-02 (subclass profiling) and SA-10 (subclass ODE model) together provide a framework for reconciling: (a) CellTrend positives that fail REAP/Luminex detection, (b) the Luykx "better functioning" paradox in schizophrenia (if total IgG includes protective IgG4 subclass), (c) the Germain null (if conformational epitopes are subclass-enriched).

3. **FcRn and BTK inhibition are the two most promising pharmaceutical approaches.** SA-11 (FcRn: accelerate existing IgG clearance) and SA-12 (BTK: suppress new IgM/IgG production) address complementary nodes in the autoantibody lifecycle. The combination (SA-16) with immunoadsorption could produce durable remission.

4. **The HSAT2 → autoantibody link (SA-19) is the most novel but least certain idea.** If correct, it would unify the epigenetic and autoimmune arms of ME/CFS into a single feed-forward mechanism — retrotransposon activation → innate immune activation → autoantibody production → tissue dysfunction → more retrotransposon activation.

5. **The therapeutic window concept (SA-20) has immediate clinical implications.** If early autoantibody acquisition predicts progression to irreversible severe disease, then autoantibody screening in early ME/CFS (< 2 years) and early intervention could be disease-modifying.
