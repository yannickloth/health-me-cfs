## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 Cerebellar Cognitive-Affective Syndrome (CCAS) in ME/CFS — Genetically Grounded

**Certainty:** 0.45
**Clinical impact:** High (would explain cognitive symptoms not captured by frontal-lobe models)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Maccallini 2026 identifies independent replicated signals in cerebellar neuronal populations — the first cell-type resolution of ME/CFS genetics. Cerebellum is not purely motor: the cerebellar cognitive-affective syndrome (CCAS, Schmahmann's syndrome) produces executive dysfunction, impaired working memory, blunted affect, and language difficulties — precisely the cognitive profile of ME/CFS. Cerebellar Purkinje cells are GABAergic and receive massive glutamatergic input from climbing fibers and parallel fibers; glutamatergic synapse enrichment may specifically impact cerebellar circuit function. Cerebellar metabolic demands are exceptionally high (per gram, among highest in brain), making them vulnerable to energy deficit. Convergence: Maccallini cerebellar cell-type signal + Mueller 2020 MRS lactate elevation in cerebellum + glutamate enrichment = cerebellar dysfunction as primary cognitive substrate.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini 2026 cell-type enrichment (cerebellar neurons independent signal); Mueller 2020 elevated lactate-to-creatine in cerebellum; Wirth/Scheibenbogen 2026 E/I model; Schmahmann CCAS literature in other disorders. Ch08 aberrant ponto-cerebellar connections documented.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show CCAS-characteristic deficits on Schmahmann's validated CCAS scale (≥3 domains impaired vs controls), with severity correlating with cerebellar PRS. MRI will show altered cerebellar-cortical functional connectivity during cognitive tasks, and structural imaging will reveal Purkinje cell layer abnormalities on high-resolution protocols.

**Potential to reshape field:** Positions cerebellum as "cognitive epicenter" rather than secondary motor structure in ME/CFS — parallels recent reframing in autism (cerebellar theory).

### 1.2 Depression Paradox Resolution: Shared Polygenic Architecture, Divergent Circuit Implementation

**Certainty:** 0.50
**Clinical impact:** High (resolves confusing study results, destigmatization implications)

**Mechanistic rationale:** DecodeME finds r_g = 0.60 with depression but NO shared causal variants. Maccallini confirms glutamatergic synapse enrichment. Resolution: glutamatergic synaptic genes are shared risk factors for both conditions, but *which circuits* they affect differs based on epistatic context, environmental modifiers, and developmental timing. In depression, glutamatergic variants affect prefrontal-limbic circuits (mood regulation, reward anticipation); in ME/CFS, the same gene classes affect cortico-cerebellar and brainstem circuits (interoception, effort computation, autonomic control). The shared SNPs are at synaptic loci; the divergent expression is circuit-specific. This is analogous to how _CACNA1C_ variants are shared across multiple psychiatric disorders but with divergent phenotypic expression.

**Evidence link:** DecodeME r_g=0.60 depression but no shared causal variants; Maccallini glutamate synapse enrichment; Hirsch 2025 cross-condition GWAS; Steen 2026 twin-sibling shared heritability (non-specific).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Using brain-region-specific eQTLs, the same glutamatergic risk variants will show differential expression effects in cerebellum/brainstem vs prefrontal cortex between ME/CFS and depression datasets. A polygenic risk score for depression will predict depression in ME/CFS co-morbid patients but NOT ME/CFS core symptoms (PEM, OI, pain) — confirming shared variants don't cause ME/CFS-specific features.

### 1.3 Enteric Glutamatergic Dysfunction: Gut-Brain Axis Genetic Link to IBS (r_g = 0.75)

**Certainty:** 0.35
**Clinical impact:** Medium-High (opens new treatment avenue for GI dysfunction in ME/CFS)

**Mechanistic rationale:** The r_g = 0.75 between ME/CFS and IBS is the strongest genetic correlation in DecodeME. The enteric nervous system (ENS) contains ~500 million neurons, of which a substantial fraction are glutamatergic (regulating peristalsis, secretion, visceral sensation). If glutamatergic synapse variants are the shared genetic architecture, ME/CFS patients with high glutamatergic PRS would also have more severe GI dysmotility and visceral hypersensitivity — not because of a separate gut pathology but because the same synaptic risk variants affect enteric glutamatergic neurons. The brain-first model is *not contradicted*: glutamatergic variants affect all glutamatergic neurons, central and enteric. The gut-brain axis in ME/CFS operates through a common genetic vulnerability in glutamatergic signalling, with manifestations in both compartments.

**Evidence link:** DecodeME r_g=0.75 IBS; Steen 2026 shared heritability; Maccallini glutamate synapse enrichment; enteric glutamatergic neuron literature (ENS uses glutamate as primary excitatory transmitter).

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients stratified by high glutamatergic PRS will have significantly higher IBS symptom severity, faster gut transit (or more dysmotility), and greater visceral hypersensitivity on barostat testing than low glutamatergic PRS patients — independent of depression or anxiety comorbidity.

### 1.4 VTA/SN Dopaminergic Secondary Signal: Effort-Motivation Collapse Circuit

**Certainty:** 0.40
**Clinical impact:** High (reframes "fatigue" as effort valuation failure, not muscle exhaustion)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Maccallini identifies secondary signal in dopaminergic midbrain (VTA/substantia nigra) with convergent support from common and rare variant data. VTA projects to nucleus accumbens (reward/motivation) and prefrontal cortex (cognitive control). SN projects to dorsal striatum (motor initiation). The effort-cost computational model already established in ME/CFS (TPJ dysfunction, basal ganglia reward deficits) gains direct genetic grounding: dopaminergic midbrain risk variants may alter dopamine synthesis, release, or receptor density in these projection targets. This would create a genetically-encoded bias toward high effort-cost valuation (everything "feels too hard"), explaining why ME/CFS patients show normal muscle physiology but fail to initiate or sustain effort. Not a primary dopamine deficiency in the classical Parkinsonian sense, but a circuit-level effort-cost miscalibration originating from midbrain genetic variants acting on striatal and prefrontal targets.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini VTA/SN signal + GWAS/WGS convergence; Miller 2014 reduced basal ganglia activation correlating with mental fatigue; Walitt TPJ effort-computation dysfunction; Aregawi 2026 selective NE (not DA) pathway deficiency — together suggesting DA synthesis is intact but midbrain DA neuron circuit function is altered.

**Key distinction from existing model:** Existing model posits effort-cost miscalibration as downstream of metabolic failure; this hypothesis posits it is partially *genetically encoded upstream* — a primary circuit-level vulnerability that precedes and compounds metabolic dysfunction.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Midbrain-striatal functional connectivity during effort-based decision tasks (fMRI) will correlate with DA-PRS in ME/CFS. Patients with high DA-PRS will show greater effort-cost sensitivity despite normal peripheral energy metabolism markers. Dopaminergic agents (L-DOPA/carbidopa, DA agonists) will preferentially benefit high DA-PRS patients.

### 1.5 Pituitary as Neuroimmune Intermediary: Genetic Link from Brain to HPA Axis Dysfunction

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Clinical impact:** Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Maccallini finds pituitary is the only non-brain tissue showing enrichment. Pituitary integrates hypothalamic signals with peripheral endocrine function. Genetic risk variants affecting pituitary function could: (1) alter HPA axis setpoint via altered CRH sensitivity, (2) impair compensatory cortisol response to inflammation (relative adrenal insufficiency), (3) disrupt pituitary-immune crosstalk via shared transcriptional programs. This provides a genetic bridge between brain-first model and the well-documented HPA axis abnormalities (blunted cortisol, altered CRH challenge) in ME/CFS, which have lacked a genetic anchor.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini pituitary enrichment; ch09 HPA axis dysfunction (blunted cortisol, CRH challenge abnormalities); CRH neuron depletion documented; neuroendocrine-immune literature.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Genetic variants driving the pituitary enrichment signal will be enriched in corticotroph function genes (CRHR1, POMC processing, glucocorticoid receptor). Pituitary-specific PRS will predict HPA axis dysfunction (blunted cortisol response to CRH/metyrapone challenge) better than whole-brain PRS.

### 1.6 TRA Locus Non-Replication: Positive Signal for Field Maturity, Not a False Start

**Certainty:** 0.55
**Clinical impact:** Low (methodological, but important for credibility)

**Mechanistic rationale:** Ueland 2022 TRA non-replication is commonly viewed as negative. Alternative framing: the TRA finding's failure to replicate — alongside the emergence of convergent glutamatergic/brain-enriched signals in much larger GWAS — constitutes a *successful field maturation*. The TRA association (a candidate-immune gene) was consistent with an autoimmune model; its non-replication, followed by brain-enriched findings in larger studies, represents model updating. The field should celebrate rather than bury this — it shows ME/CFS genetics can self-correct. This also suggests a systematic bias: candidate gene studies in ME/CFS, most of which were never replicated, should be explicitly catalogued and tested against GWAS data.

**Evidence link:** Ueland 2022; Dibble 2020 review documenting unreplicated candidate gene findings.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Of the >20 candidate gene associations published in ME/CFS prior to DecodeME, no more than 2 will replicate at p < 0.05 in DecodeME GWAS data when formally tested. This falsifiability criterion is a tool for field hygiene, not a clinical hypothesis.

### 1.7 Genetically Anchored Two-Hit Model: Glutamatergic Vulnerability + Infectious Trigger

**Certainty:** 0.50
**Clinical impact:** Medium (predictive model for post-infectious risk)

**Mechanistic rationale:** The glutamatergic synapse genetic enrichment provides the "first hit" — genetically vulnerable glutamatergic circuits. The "second hit" is an infectious trigger (EBV, SARS-CoV-2, _Coxiella burnetii_) that: (1) produces quinolinic acid (kynurenine pathway) acting as NMDA agonist, (2) triggers neuroinflammation elevating extracellular glutamate via impaired astrocytic reuptake, (3) depletes ATP needed for glutamatergic vesicle recycling. In individuals without the genetic vulnerability, these effects are transient. In those with glutamatergic risk variants, the compromised excitation/inhibition balance is pushed past a tipping point, after which maladaptive plasticity locks in the pathological state. This model is testable because it makes distinct predictions about recovery trajectories.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment (hit 1); ch15 kynurenine pathway dysregulation (hit 2); ch08 post-infectious onset literature; Bretherick 2023 DecodeME infection typing showing heterogeneity in triggers.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In prospective post-infectious cohorts (EBV, SARS-CoV-2), development of ME/CFS at 6 months will be predicted by glutamatergic PRS × quinolinic acid levels at baseline (interaction term significant). Low glutamatergic PRS + high quinolinic acid = low ME/CFS risk; high glutamatergic PRS + high quinolinic acid = high ME/CFS risk. Replication in three infection types required.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### 2.1 Cell-Type-Specific PRS for ME/CFS (High Priority, Feasible)

**Certainty:** 0.65
**Impact:** High

**Design:** Using Maccallini cell-type enrichment data, construct three PRS: (1) glutamatergic synapse PRS (genes from significant gene-set), (2) subcortical/cerebellar neuronal PRS (genes from cell-type enrichment), (3) dopaminergic midbrain PRS (genes from VTA/SN signal). Test each against comprehensive clinical phenotyping (PEM severity, cognitive dysfunction, OI, pain, mood) in an existing cohort (DecodeME phenotype data, UK Biobank ME/CFS subset). Hypothesis: distinct PRS subcomponents predict distinct symptom clusters (e.g., DA-PRS → effort/cognition symptoms; glutamate-PRS → sensory hypersensitivity/PEM; cerebellar-PRS → motor/cognitive coordination).

**Falsifiable prediction:** The three PRS subcomponents will show significantly different association patterns with clinical subscales (p for heterogeneity < 0.05). Whole-genome PRS obscures these dissociations.

**Resource requirement:** Existing GWAS data + phenotype data. No new genotyping needed. Can be done computationally within months.

### 2.2 Prospective Post-Infectious Cohort with Genetic Stratification

**Certainty:** 0.50
**Impact:** Very High

**Design:** Enroll n=5,000 infectious mononucleosis (EBV) patients and n=5,000 acute SARS-CoV-2 patients. Collect GWAS array data, symptom surveys, and blood biomarkers (kynurenine, glutamate, cytokines) at baseline (acute infection), 3 months, 6 months, 12 months. Test whether glutamatergic PRS × inflammatory markers predict ME/CFS development. Stratify into high-genetic-risk + high-inflammatory vs other quadrants.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS incidence at 6 months will be >15% in the high-glutamate-PRS + high-quinolinic-acid quadrant vs <3% in the low-glutamate-PRS + low-quinolinic-acid quadrant. The interaction term (PRS × inflammatory marker) will have AUC > 0.75 for ME/CFS prediction.

### 2.3 iPSC-Derived Glutamatergic Neuron Model from High- vs Low-PRS Carriers

**Certainty:** 0.55
**Impact:** Very High

**Design:** Recruit 10 ME/CFS patients with high glutamatergic PRS (top decile) and 10 with low glutamatergic PRS (bottom decile). Generate iPSC-derived cortical glutamatergic neurons and cerebellar granule cells. Measure: mEPSC frequency/amplitude, AMPA/NMDA ratio, vesicle release probability, mitochondrial respiration (Seahorse), response to inflammatory challenge (IFN-γ, TNF-α, quinolinic acid).

**Falsifiable prediction:** High-glutamate-PRS neurons will show altered synaptic properties (increased mEPSC frequency or amplitude, altered AMPA/NMDA ratio) compared to low-glutamate-PRS neurons, even in isogenic backgrounds. Inflammatory challenge will produce exaggerated synaptic dysfunction in high-PRS lines.

### 2.5 Transcriptome-Wide Association Study (TWAS) for ME/CFS Brain Tissue

**Certainty:** 0.40
**Impact:** Very High

**Design:** Use Maccallini/DecodeME GWAS summary statistics with brain eQTL references (GTEx, PsychENCODE, ROSMAP) to perform TWAS — identifying genes whose genetically-predicted expression levels are associated with ME/CFS risk. Compare across brain regions to identify region-specific expression effects. This is computationally feasible using existing tools (FUSION, S-PrediXcan) and would refine the broad tissue enrichment into transcript-level resolution.

These will differ from TWAS hits in depression for the same genes (different direction or different brain region).

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### 3.1 Memantine Stratified by Glutamatergic PRS (High Priority)

**Certainty:** 0.45
**Impact:** Very High

**Mechanistic rationale:** Memantine is an NMDA receptor antagonist (uncompetitive, low-affinity, use-dependent). If ME/CFS involves genetically-driven glutamatergic hyperexcitability, memantine should reduce excitotoxic load and excessive neural activation. Existing trial data show mixed results in ME/CFS, likely because the drug was tested in unstratified populations. Glutamatergic PRS identifies the subgroup most likely to benefit.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate synapse enrichment; Wirth/Scheibenbogen E/I model; memantine mechanism; precedent in other conditions (Alzheimer's, fibromyalgia — modest overall, improved with stratification).

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a randomized trial stratified by glutamatergic PRS (n=40 high, n=40 low), memantine vs placebo will show significant improvement in PEM severity and cognitive function only in the high-PRS subgroup (interaction p < 0.05). Effect size in high-PRS group: d > 0.5. No benefit in low-PRS group.

**Dosing:** 5-20 mg/day, slow titration over 4-6 weeks. CNS-penetrant. Well-tolerated. Generic.

---

### 3.2 Riluzole for Glutamatergic Hyperexcitability

**Certainty:** 0.35
**Impact:** High

**Mechanistic rationale:** Riluzole reduces glutamate release via multiple mechanisms (Na+ channel blockade, inhibition of glutamate release, AMPA receptor modulation). FDA-approved for ALS. In ME/CFS with glutamatergic genetic vulnerability, riluzole could reduce excitotoxic load and modulate E/I balance. Riluzole also activates TRPC6 channels (neuroprotective) and increases BDNF.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; riluzole mechanism (glutamate release inhibitor); ALS precedent for neuroprotection; fibromyalgia pilot data (positive but small).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Riluzole 50 mg BID over 12 weeks improves cognitive function (processing speed, working memory) and reduces sensory hypersensitivity in high-glutamate-PRS ME/CFS patients vs placebo. Liver function monitoring required.

**Risk:** Hepatotoxicity (1-2%), requires monthly LFT monitoring.

---

### 3.3 L-DOPA/Carbidopa for Dopaminergic Midbrain Genotype-Positive Patients

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** Medium-High

**Mechanistic rationale:** Maccallini secondary VTA/SN signal suggests dopaminergic vulnerability. L-DOPA/carbidopa crosses BBB and increases CNS dopamine. If midbrain genetic variants reduce dopamine synthesis capacity, L-DOPA could normalize effort-cost computation, reduce cognitive fatigue, and improve motor initiation. Unlike peripheral DA agents (methylphenidate), carbidopa ensures central conversion. Risk: dyskinesias, nausea, psychiatric effects at high doses.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini VTA/SN signal; ch08 reduced basal ganglia activation; Miller 2014 r²=0.49 effort-cost correlation; Parkinson's literature.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a pilot trial, high DA-PRS ME/CFS patients (but not low DA-PRS) will show significant improvement in effort-based decision-making tasks (fMRI: increased striatal activation) and cognitive fatigue scores on L-DOPA/carbidopa 100/25 mg TID vs placebo. Requires COMT genotyping to control for COMT Val158Met effects on L-DOPA response.

**Critical caution:** Dopamine augmentation in patients with normal (not deficient) dopamine synthesis could worsen symptoms via D2 receptor overstimulation. Requires careful dose titration. Only testable after DA-PRS stratification is validated.

### 3.4 N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) as Glutamate Modulator

**Certainty:** 0.40
**Impact:** Medium-High

**Mechanistic rationale:** NAC modulates glutamatergic transmission via cystine-glutamate antiporter (system x_c^-) in glia, increasing extrasynaptic glutamate which activates presynaptic mGluR2/3 inhibitory autoreceptors, thereby _reducing_ synaptic glutamate release — a counterintuitive but well-documented mechanism. NAC also replenishes glutathione, addressing oxidative stress. In a glutamatergic hyperexcitability model of ME/CFS, NAC has dual benefit: lowering synaptic glutamate + antioxidant.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; NAC mechanism in addiction/OCD (glutamate modulator); NAC in ME/CFS (some patient reports, minimal trial data); precedent in schizophrenia (1g BID effective for negative symptoms, glutamate-mediated).

**Falsifiable prediction:** NAC 1200-2400 mg/day over 8 weeks reduces sensory hypersensitivity and PEM severity in high-glutamate-PRS patients. Effect differs by PRS stratum — larger effect in top tertile. Well-tolerated.

### 3.5 Pregabalin/Gabapentin Stratified by Glutamatergic PRS

**Certainty:** 0.35
**Impact:** Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Gabapentinoids inhibit voltage-gated Ca²+ channels (α2δ-1 subunit), reducing glutamate release from presynaptic terminals. In fibromyalgia, pregabalin shows modest benefit overall but substantial individual variation. If ME/CFS-FM genetic overlap (Steen 2026; Hirsch 2025) reflects shared glutamatergic vulnerability, then gabapentinoid response may be predictable by glutamatergic PRS.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; Steen 2026 FM/ME/CFS shared heritability; Hirsch 2025 comparative GWAS; fibromyalgia pregabalin trial data; gabapentinoid mechanism.

**Falsifiable prediction:** In an observational study of ME/CFS patients taking gabapentinoids clinically, high-glutamate-PRS patients will report significantly greater pain/sleep improvement than low-PRS patients. Response rate difference > 20%.

### 3.6 Lamotrigine for E/I Balance (Mood Stabilizer + Glutamate Modulator)

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Lamotrigine stabilizes glutamate release by inhibiting voltage-gated Na+ channels and reducing pathological glutamatergic transmission without affecting normal transmission. Already used in ME/CFS for mood stability and off-label for pain. The glutamate mechanism suggests potential efficacy for PEM and cognitive dysfunction beyond mood effects. Genetic stratification could identify responders.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; lamotrigine mechanism; ch14 clinical use (mood, some PEM benefit); bipolar precedent (glutamate hypothesis similar to Wirth/Scheibenbogen).

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with high-glutamate-PRS will show greater PEM reduction on lamotrigine than low-PRS patients (retrospective analysis of existing clinical data). Requires slow titration (Stevens-Johnson risk).

### 3.7 Amiloride as Acid-Sensing Ion Channel (ASIC) Blocker for Cerebellar Dysfunction

**Certainty:** 0.20
**Impact:** Low-Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Cerebellar Purkinje cells have exceptionally high ASIC1a expression, which becomes pathologically active during acidosis. Mueller 2020 found elevated lactate and temperature in cerebellum (acidosis). If cerebellar genetic signal + lactate elevation reflects ASIC activation, amiloride (ASIC blocker, FDA-approved diuretic) could reduce cerebellar excitotoxicity. Very speculative — the ASIC-me/CFS link is untested.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini cerebellar cell-type signal; Mueller 2020 cerebellar lactate; ASIC1a cerebellar expression; amiloride neuroprotection literature (stroke models).

**Falsifiable prediction:** If cerebellar lactate is elevated in a subset, those patients will show benefit from amiloride 5-10 mg/day on gait ataxia and cognitive coordination tasks. Requires prior demonstration of cerebellar acidosis in ME/CFS. Low priority.

**Risk:** Electrolyte disturbances, hypotension.

### 4.2 Magnesium Diglycinate or L-Threonate for Glutamate Regulation

**Certainty:** 0.35
**Impact:** Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Magnesium is a physiological NMDA receptor blocker (voltage-dependent, like memantine but weaker). Magnesium glycinate crosses BBB better than oxide. Magnesium L-threonate has highest brain bioavailability. If ME/CFS involves glutamatergic hyperexcitability, magnesium supplementation could raise the threshold for excitotoxic activation. Magnesium is also essential for ATP synthesis (Mg-ATP complex), mitochondrial function, and >300 enzymatic reactions — addressing both glutamate and energy hypotheses.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; NMDA receptor Mg²+ block mechanism; low Mg²+ reported in some ME/CFS cohorts; migraine literature (Mg²+ effective, glutamate implicated); chronic fatigue/exercise literature.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with low RBC magnesium (< 5.0 mg/dL) and high glutamatergic PRS will show the greatest symptom improvement (PEM, sensory sensitivity, sleep) with Mg L-threonate 2000 mg/day (providing 144 mg elemental Mg) over 12 weeks. Double-blind RCT feasible.

### 4.3 Taurine: GABA Agonist and Glutamate Modulator

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Taurine is a partial GABA_A agonist and also acts at glycine receptors, inhibiting overactive glutamatergic circuits. It modulates intracellular Ca²+ and stabilizes mitochondrial membranes. In Wirth/Scheibenbogen E/I imbalance model (glutamate excess, GABA deficit), taurine could partially compensate for GABAergic insufficiency. Literature on taurine in ME/CFS limited but some patient reports.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; Wirth/Scheibenbogen E/I model; taurine GABAergic mechanism; taurine in exercise recovery (reduces muscle damage, oxidative stress); prior ME/CFS brainstorm on taurine.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Taurine 2-4 g/day reduces PEM duration and improves subjective E/I balance (validated questionnaire) in high-glutamate-PRS patients. Blinded cross-over trial.

### 4.4 Glycine: Co-agonist of NMDA Receptor (Context-Dependent)

**Certainty:** 0.20
**Impact:** Low-Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** Glycine is a required co-agonist at NMDA receptors (along with glutamate). At low doses, glycine binding site occupation is necessary for NMDA activation. However, at higher concentrations, glycine can also activate inhibitory glycine receptors in the brainstem/spinal cord. The net effect is context-dependent and unpredictable in ME/CFS without better circuit understanding. Also the key amino acid in glutathione synthesis.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; glycine NMDA co-agonist mechanism; schizophrenia literature (glycine site modulators).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Contraindication warning rather than recommendation: glycine could worsen glutamatergic hyperexcitability if NMDA receptors are already overactive. Not recommended clinically without circuit-specific data.

### 4.5 Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxal-5-Phosphate) for Glutamate-GABA Conversion

**Certainty:** 0.35
**Impact:** Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** B6 (P5P) is essential cofactor for glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD) — the enzyme that converts glutamate to GABA. If GAD activity is suboptimal due to B6 insufficiency (common in chronic inflammation, GI dysfunction), the same glutamate excess that is genetically driven would be compounded by impaired GABA production. Supplementing P5P could increase GABAergic inhibition, restoring E/I balance. B6 is also cofactor for kynureninase (kynurenine pathway, shifted toward quinolinic acid in ME/CFS).

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; Wirth/Scheibenbogen E/I model; GAD-B6 dependency; ch11 GI dysfunction → B6 malabsorption possible; ch15 kynurenine pathway.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with low plasma PLP (< 30 nmol/L) and high glutamatergic PRS will show improvement in sensory sensitivity and subjective hyperarousal with P5P 50-100 mg/day over 8 weeks. Measure GAD activity indirectly via CSF GABA or GABA/glutamate ratio.

### 4.6 Theanine: Glutamate Analog with GABAergic Properties

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** Low-Medium

**Mechanistic rationale:** L-theanine is a glutamate analog that crosses BBB and binds to AMPA, kainate, and NMDA receptors (partial antagonism). Also increases GABA, dopamine, and serotonin levels. Known for promoting relaxation without sedation. In E/I imbalance model, theanine could reduce glutamatergic tone and increase GABA — a natural E/I harmonizer.

**Evidence link:** Maccallini glutamate enrichment; theanine mechanism; green tea literature; anxiety/sleep studies in healthy adults; very safe profile.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 Glutamate-GABA E/I Balance ODE Module

**Certainty:** 0.40
**Impact:** High

**Variable addition to existing ODE models (Watton 2026, Fourel):**

Add state variables: [Glu] (extracellular glutamate concentration), [GABA] (extracellular GABA), and R_EI (E/I balance ratio). Key dynamics:

d[Glu]/dt = α_rel − β_reuptake − γ_diff + δ_QS − ε_GAD × [Glu]
d[GABA]/dt = ε_GAD × [Glu] − ζ_clearance
R_EI = [Glu] / (k_GABA + [GABA])

where α_rel is vesicular glutamate release (modulated by ATP availability for vesicle filling), β_reuptake is astrocytic glutamate uptake (ATP-dependent via EAAT2), γ_diff is diffusion/loss, δ_QS is quinolinic acid contribution (shifts NMDA receptor response), and ε_GAD is GAD activity converting glutamate to GABA (B6-dependent).

**Key prediction:** Below a threshold ATP level, glutamate clearance (β_reuptake) drops faster than glutamate release (α_rel) because EAAT2 is more ATP-demanding than vesicle release (each glutamate transported requires 3 Na+ → Na+/K+ ATPase). This creates a "glutamate trap": once ATP drops below threshold, [Glu] rises self-reinforcingly, shifting R_EI toward excitotoxicity. The genetic PRS parameter shifts the glutamate release setpoint (higher α_rel in high-PRS), lowering the ATP threshold at which the trap activates.

**Falsifiable prediction:** The model predicts a non-linear threshold below which [Glu] accumulates uncontrollably. This predicts that PEM is not a linear function of exertion but has a step-function character — patients can stay below a threshold indefinitely but cross it → disproportionate decline. This matches clinical descriptions.

### 7.2 Cerebellar Purkinje Cell Energy Cost Model

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** Medium

Cerebellar Purkinje cells are among the most energetically demanding neurons (high firing rates, extensive dendritic arbors, continuous high-frequency simple spike firing ~50-100 Hz). Add to ODE:

E_cereb = E_baseline + ∫(P_firing × C_spike) dt over time

where E_cereb is cerebellar energy consumption, P_firing is Purkinje firing rate (driven by glutamatergic input from parallel fibers), C_spike is ATP per action potential (~10⁸ ATP/AP). High glutamatergic PRS → higher P_firing setpoint → higher baseline E_cereb → lower ATP reserve for other functions. PEM occurs when E_cereb + motor/cognitive demands exceed ATP supply.

**Key prediction:** Cerebellar coordination tasks (requiring Purkinje pattern separation) should be disproportionately fatiguing in ME/CFS. This is testable: brief coordination exercises (finger tapping, rhythmic movements) should produce disproportionately long recovery times compared to non-cerebellar cognitive tasks matched for duration and subjective difficulty.

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** Medium

**Rationale:** Cerebellar circuits (vermis, flocculus, paraflocculus) control saccade accuracy, pursuit gain, and VOR adaptation. These can be measured with portable eye-tracking in <10 minutes. If cerebellar genetic signal in Maccallini correspond to measurable cerebellar eye movement dysfunction, eye-tracking becomes a low-cost, rapid, objective phenotype for Cer-PRS stratification.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Cer-PRS correlates specifically with saccadic dysmetria (gain error > 20% in amplitude-matched saccades) and impaired smooth pursuit gain at target velocities >20°/s, but NOT with vestibular-ocular reflex measures (which depend on brainstem more than cerebellum). Eye movement abnormalities are stable over repeated testing (trait, not state).

### 9.9 Combinatorial Risk Score (Sardell) as Clinical Prediction Tool

**Certainty:** 0.45
**Impact:** Very High

**Rationale:** Sardell 2026's combinatorial SNP-SNP interactions produce risk scores with potentially higher OR than additive PRS. Combined with Maccallini meta-GWAS effect sizes (n=19,470 → better parameter estimates), a new combinatorial + additive integrated risk score could be developed.

**Clinical utility:** (1) Identify high-risk individuals (e.g., family members, post-infectious patients) for monitoring or early intervention, (2) stratify clinical trial enrollment to enrich for genetically-defined subgroups, (3) predict disease trajectory (genetic load correlates with severity/outcome).

**Falsifiable prediction:** Integrated (additive + combinatorial) risk score top decile has OR > 3.0 for ME/CFS vs bottom decile (vs OR ~1.5-2.0 for additive PRS alone). Combined score predicts age of onset and severity better than either alone.

### 9.10 Blood-Based Glutamate Receptor Autoantibody Panel + Glu-PRS

**Certainty:** 0.30
**Impact:** High

**Rationale:** Some ME/CFS patients have autoantibodies against neurotransmitter receptors (β2-adrenergic, M3 muscarinic, potentially glutamate receptors — see schizophrenia literature where GluN1 antibodies present in a subset). If Glu-PRS indicates glutamatergic vulnerability + autoantibodies indicate immune attack on glutamate receptors → "double-hit" diagnostic: genetic predisposition + acquired autoimmune targeting.

**Falsifiable prediction:** A subset (~10-20%) of high-Glu-PRS patients will have autoantibodies against NMDA receptor subunits (GluN1, GluN2A) or AMPA receptors (GluA1, GluA2). These patients differ clinically: more sensory hypersensitivity, more PEM triggered by emotional/sensory stress, and may benefit from immunomodulatory therapy (IVIG, rituximab) rather than glutamate modulators.

## Caveats & Limitations

- Maccallini 2026 is a preprint — all analyses contingent on peer-reviewed validation
- PRS subtyping assumes polygenic architecture where subcomponents are dissociable; this has not been demonstrated in any complex disease
- Cell-type enrichment depends on reference single-cell data quality; cerebellar Purkinje cell enrichment may reflect technical factors (high RNA content) as much as biology
- All drug repurposing proposals are speculative — randomized trials required before any clinical recommendation
- Cross-disease parallels (schizophrenia, autism, PD) are analogical reasoning, not mechanistic proof
- Mathematical model extensions require parameter estimation from experimental data not yet collected
- Biomarker proposals need validation in independent cohorts with appropriate blinding
- No proposal here accounts for ancestry-specific genetic architecture (all based on European-ancestry GWAS)
- Enteric glutamatergic hypothesis relies on ENS biology not yet studied in ME/CFS
- All supplement/dietary proposals assume no contraindications; individual medical supervision required
- The "no shared causal variants with depression" finding is specific to the current statistical power of DecodeME; larger sample sizes may reveal shared variants with very small effect sizes
