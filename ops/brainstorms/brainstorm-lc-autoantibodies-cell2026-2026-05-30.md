## NOVEL MECHANISTIC HYPOTHESES (5 ideas)

### H1: Mediator Complex Autoimmunity

**Title:** MED20/MED19 complex disruption → transcriptional dysregulation

**Mechanistic rationale:** MED20 is a subunit of the Mediator complex linking transcription factors to RNA Pol II. Autoantibodies to MED20 (confirmed in array) may trigger microglial activation by disrupting neuronal transcriptional programs controlling energy metabolism and neuroinflammatory signaling, creating a transcriptional "energy crisis" manifesting as central fatigue.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: >21,000 array identified MED20 as confirmed autoantigen; Finding #3: abnormal neuronal activation in fatigue circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65

**Falsifiable prediction:** MED20-specific autoantibody titers correlate with transcriptional dysregulation of neuronal energy metabolism genes in patient-derived iPSC neurons exposed to patient IgG.

---

### H2: USP5-Mediated Ubiquitin Dysregulation

**Title:** USP5 autoantibodies → synaptic protein turnover failure

**Mechanistic rationale:** USP5 is a deubiquitinase regulating synaptic protein recycling. Autoantibody interference may impair ubiquitin-dependent synaptic vesicle replenishment, leading to neurotransmitter depletion and exhaustion after minimal cognitive effort—a mechanistic analogue of the "central fatigue" observed in passive transfer.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: USP5 confirmed as autoantigen; Finding #3: abnormal activation in memory circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** Patient IgG with high USP5 reactivity reduces synaptic vesicle recycling rate in cultured hippocampal neurons measured by FM dye uptake assay.

---

### H3: BBB "Trojan Horse" Transport Amplification

**Title:** FcRn-mediated IgG recycling amplifies CNS access beyond 5%

**Mechanistic rationale:** The 5% IgG BBB crossing estimate may underestimate cumulative CNS exposure. FcRn receptors at the BBB recycle IgG across the endothelium, creating a positive feedback loop where initial IgG entry upregulates FcRn expression, progressively increasing CNS IgG concentration over time despite low single-pass permeability.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: IgG crosses BBB at 5% (day 5); Finding #6: persistence >1 year

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** FcRn blockade reduces CNS IgG accumulation by >80% in passive-transfer mice despite maintaining 5% initial BBB permeability.

---

### H4: Symptom-Symptom Autoantibody Networks

**Title:** Autoantibody co-occurrence clusters define clinical endotypes

**Mechanistic rationale:** The 85% symptom-symptom correspondence suggests patients have unique autoantibody "fingerprints" targeting specific CNS circuits. Rather than single-antibody causality, disease arises from convergent autoantibody networks targeting complementary neuronal populations (e.g., MED20 + USP5 + unknown cluster X) that collectively produce distinct clinical profiles.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #4: 85% pain-phenotype mice from pain-LC donors; Finding #5: patient subgroups by neurocognitive profiles

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.70

**Falsifiable prediction:** Unsupervised clustering of >70 autoantigen reactivity profiles in n>100 LC patients reveals 3-5 clusters that map to distinct clinical symptom clusters with >75% accuracy.

---

### H5: Autoantibody-Induced Neuronal Metabolic Exhaustion

**Title:** Chronic IgG binding → persistent neuronal hypoactivation

**Mechanistic rationale:** Persistent autoantibody binding (>1 year) may induce epigenetic reprogramming of neurons into a hypoexcitable state. Chronic engagement of IgG-bound surface receptors (e.g., USP5-associated or other surface targets) triggers sustained calcium signaling, leading to mitochondrial downregulation and transcriptional silencing of excitability genes—a "metabolic hibernation" that persists even after IgG clearance.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #6: autoantibodies persist >1 year; Finding #2: fatigue-like behavior in mice; Finding #3: abnormal neuronal activation patterns

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Neurons exposed to patient IgG for 14 days maintain reduced firing rates for >7 days after IgG washout, accompanied by sustained epigenetic marks at mitochondrial biogenesis gene promoters.

## RESEARCH DIRECTIONS (5 ideas)

### R1: Large-Scale Autoantigen Mapping Validation

**Title:** Validate >70 CNS/PNS targets across independent cohorts

**Mechanistic rationale:** The 21,000-protein array identified >70 CNS/PNS targets, but only MED20 and USP5 were confirmed. Systematic validation of all targets across 3 independent cohorts (Chen 2026, Wilhelm 2025, and a new cohort) will establish the core autoantigen landscape and prioritize therapeutic targets.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: >21,000 array → >70 CNS/PNS autoantigens; Finding #8: three independent replications

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.80

**Falsifiable prediction:** At least 20 of the >70 targets replicate in all three cohorts with FDR <0.05; >10 show symptom-specific correlations.

---

### R2: Symptom-Symptom Transfer Specificity Test

**Title:** Multi-phenotype passive transfer with patient-matched IgG

**Mechanistic rationale:** The 85% symptom-symptom correspondence suggests highly specific autoantibody-phenotype mapping. Systematically testing IgG from LC patients stratified by clinical phenotype (pain-dominant, fatigue-dominant, cognitive-dominant, autonomic-dominant) in passive transfer to mice will validate the specificity and identify phenotype-specific autoantibody signatures.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #4: 85% pain-phenotype mice from pain-LC donors; Finding #5: patient subgroups by neurocognitive profiles

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.75

**Falsifiable prediction:** IgG from fatigue-dominant LC patients produces fatigue behavior in >75% of recipient mice with <10% developing pain phenotypes, and vice versa.

---

### R3: CNS Circuit-Specific IgG Delivery

**Title:** Circuit-targeted IgG microinjection maps symptom localization

**Mechanistic rationale:** Passive transfer delivers IgG systemically. Direct microinjection of patient IgG into specific CNS circuits (e.g., ventral tegmental area for fatigue, somatosensory cortex for pain, hippocampus for memory) will map which circuits are sufficient for symptom induction and clarify whether systemic IgG acts via convergent circuit disruption.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: abnormal activation in pain, fatigue, memory, emotion circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** IgG injection into VTA produces fatigue behavior without thermal hyperalgesia; injection into S1 produces thermal hyperalgesia without fatigue.

---

### R4: Autoantibody Persistence Mechanism

**Title:** Plasma cell sanctuary sites in CNS enable >1-year persistence

**Mechanistic rationale:** Peripheral autoantibodies typically decay within months. >1-year persistence suggests CNS-localized plasma cells or long-lived plasma cells in protected niches (bone marrow, lymph nodes) continuously producing anti-MED20/USP5 antibodies. Identifying these sanctuary sites is critical for targeting chronic autoantibody production.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #6: autoantibodies persist >1 year

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Long-lived plasma cells (CD138+ CD38++ CD19+) producing anti-MED20 or anti-USP5 IgG are detectable in meningeal lymphatics or cervical lymph nodes of LC patients with >1-year symptom duration.

---

### R5: IENF Damage Progression Tracking

**Title:** Serial skin biopsy tracks autoantibody-driven neuropathy

**Mechanistic rationale:** IENF damage was observed in passive-transfer mice, linking autoantibodies to small fiber neuropathy. Serial skin biopsies in LC patients with known autoantibody profiles will determine whether IENF density loss correlates with specific autoantibody clusters (e.g., USP5 vs other targets) and whether damage progresses or stabilizes over time.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #2: IENF damage in passive-transfer mice

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.70

**Falsifiable prediction:** Patients with high USP5 reactivity show 2x faster IENF density loss over 12 months compared to low-reactivity controls (p<0.01).

## DRUG/MEDICATION IDEAS (4 ideas)

### D1: FcRn Blockade for CNS Protection

**Title:** Rozanolixizumab reduces CNS IgG accumulation

**Mechanistic rationale:** FcRn receptors recycle IgG at the BBB, potentially amplifying CNS exposure beyond the 5% initial crossing. Blocking FcRn (rozanolixizumab, efgartigimod) should reduce IgG recycling and limit CNS accumulation, protecting fatigue/memory circuits from autoantibody-mediated dysfunction.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: IgG crosses BBB at 5%; Finding #6: >1-year persistence

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65

**Falsifiable prediction:** Rozanolixizumab treatment reduces CNS IgG levels by >70% and improves fatigue scores by ≥30% in LC patients with confirmed CNS autoantibodies (MED20/USP5).

---

### D2: Proteasome Activators Counteract USP5 Blockade

**Title:** PA28γ agonists restore synaptic protein turnover

**Mechanistic rationale:** If USP5 autoantibodies impair deubiquitinase function, reducing ubiquitin availability for synaptic vesicle recycling, enhancing proteasome activity (via PA28γ agonists) could compensate by increasing ubiquitin regeneration and restoring normal synaptic protein turnover, potentially improving cognitive symptoms.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: USP5 confirmed as autoantigen; Finding #3: abnormal activation in memory circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** PA28γ agonist treatment rescues synaptic vesicle recycling deficits in neurons exposed to high-USP5-reactivity IgG, measured by electrophysiology and FM dye assays.

---

### D3: Receptor-Targeted Immunoadsorption

**Title:** MED20/USP5-specific immunoadsorption columns

**Mechanistic rationale:** Current immunoadsorption uses generic anti-IgG columns. Developing columns functionalized with recombinant MED20 or USP5 protein would selectively remove pathogenic CNS autoantibodies while sparing protective antibodies, increasing specificity and reducing infection risk compared to broad IgG depletion.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: MED20, USP5 confirmed as autoantigens

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.70

**Falsifiable prediction:** MED20-specific immunoadsorption reduces anti-MED20 titers by >95% with <10% reduction in anti-viral IgG titers, compared to >80% reduction in anti-viral titers with generic anti-IgG columns.

---

### D4: B-Cell Subset-Specific Depletion

**Title:** Autoreactive memory B-cell targeting spares plasma cells

**Mechanistic rationale:** Persistent autoantibodies (>1 year) may arise from long-lived plasma cells resistant to rituximab (which targets CD20+ B cells). Targeting specific memory B-cell subsets (e.g., CD19+CD27+CD38-) or plasma cell markers (CD138) with anti-CD138 (e.g., antibody-drug conjugates) could eliminate the source of chronic anti-MED20/USP5 production.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #6: autoantibodies persist >1 year; Plasma cell sanctuary (prior work)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** Anti-CD138 ADC treatment reduces anti-MED20 titers by >80% in LC patients with >1-year symptom duration, whereas rituximab produces <20% reduction.

## SUPPLEMENT/NUTRACEUTICAL IDEAS (3 ideas)

### N1: Flavonoid-Mediated BBB Protection

**Title:** Luteolin strengthens BBB against IgG crossing

**Mechanistic rationale:** Flavonoids like luteolin upregulate tight junction proteins (claudin-5, occludin) and reduce endothelial permeability. If CNS autoantibodies require BBB crossing (5% at day 5), enhancing barrier integrity could reduce CNS exposure below the pathogenic threshold, potentially limiting fatigue and cognitive symptoms.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: IgG crosses BBB at 5%; Finding #2: CNS symptom production in mice

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** Luteolin pretreatment reduces CNS IgG levels by >60% and prevents fatigue behavior in passive-transfer mice despite identical peripheral IgG levels.

---

### N2: Nrf2 Activators Counteract Neuronal Oxidative Stress

**Title:** Sulforaphane mitigates IgG-induced neuronal hyperactivation

**Mechanistic rationale:** Abnormal neuronal activation in fatigue/memory circuits suggests excitotoxic stress. Nrf2 activators (sulforaphane, bardoxolone) upregulate antioxidant defenses (HO-1, NQO1) and may protect neurons from IgG-induced hyperactivation, potentially stabilizing cognitive function in autoantibody-positive LC patients.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: abnormal neuronal activation in pain, fatigue, memory, emotion circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** Sulforaphane treatment normalizes neuronal activation patterns (measured by fMRI) in passive-transfer mice and reduces fatigue behavior by ≥40%.

---

### N3: Magnesium Glycinate Supports Synaptic Function

**Title:** Magnesium restores synaptic plasticity impaired by USP5 autoantibodies

**Mechanistic rationale:** USP5 autoantibodies may disrupt synaptic vesicle recycling, leading to neurotransmitter depletion. Magnesium is a cofactor for synaptic vesicle fusion and regulates NMDA receptor function. High-bioavailability magnesium (glycinate) may support residual synaptic function and improve cognitive resilience in USP5-positive patients.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: USP5 confirmed as autoantigen; Finding #3: abnormal activation in memory circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** Magnesium glycinate supplementation improves working memory scores by ≥15% in LC patients with high USP5 reactivity compared to placebo (p<0.05).

## NON-PHARMACOLOGICAL INTERVENTIONS (3 ideas)

### NP1: Aerobic Exercise Modulates BBB Permeability

**Title:** Low-intensity aerobic conditioning reduces IgG CNS access

**Mechanistic rationale:** Exercise upregulates tight junction proteins and reduces systemic inflammation, potentially lowering BBB permeability. A structured low-intensity aerobic protocol (aiming for heart rate <60% max) may reduce CNS autoantibody exposure below pathogenic levels while avoiding post-exertional malaise in LC patients.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: IgG crosses BBB at 5%

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** 12-week low-intensity aerobic protocol reduces CNS IgG levels by ≥40% (measured by CSF:serum ratio) without worsening fatigue scores.

---

### NP2: Vagus Nerve Stimulation Modulates CNS IgG Effects

**Title:** Transcutaneous VNS normalizes neuronal hyperactivation

**Mechanistic rationale:** VNS activates cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathways and modulates limbic circuit excitability. Transcutaneous VNS (tVNS) may counteract abnormal neuronal activation in fatigue/memory circuits induced by CNS autoantibodies, potentially reducing symptom severity without altering autoantibody titers.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: abnormal neuronal activation in pain, fatigue, memory, emotion circuits

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** Daily tVNS for 8 weeks normalizes fMRI activation patterns in the default mode network and improves fatigue scores by ≥25% in LC patients with confirmed CNS autoantibodies.

---

### NP3: Environmental Light Therapy Stabilizes Circadian Dysregulation

**Title:** Timed bright light exposure mitigates fatigue circuit dysregulation

**Mechanistic rationale:** Fatigue circuit dysregulation may involve hypothalamic nuclei controlling circadian rhythms. Timed bright light therapy (10,000 lux, morning 30 min) may stabilize circadian signaling and partially compensate for autoantibody-induced hypothalamic dysfunction, improving daytime fatigue and sleep quality.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #3: abnormal neuronal activation in fatigue circuits; Finding #5: patient subgroups by neurocognitive profiles

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** Morning bright light therapy for 6 weeks improves fatigue severity scores by ≥30% in LC patients with fatigue-dominant phenotypes and confirmed MED20 autoantibodies.

## CROSS-DISEASE BRIDGES (2 ideas)

### X1: CNS Autoantibodies in Post-Infectious Myalgic Encephalomyelitis

**Title:** ME/CFS CNS autoantibody profile parallels Long COVID

**Mechanistic rationale:** The >70 CNS/PNS autoantigens identified in LC may overlap with autoantibody targets in post-infectious ME/CFS (e.g., post-EBV, post-Q fever). Testing ME/CFS patients for MED20, USP5, and other CNS targets will determine whether a shared CNS autoantibody mechanism underlies both conditions, supporting a unified "post-infectious autoantibody encephalopathy" diagnosis.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #1: >21,000 array → >70 CNS/PNS autoantigens; Finding #6: persistence >1 year

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65

**Falsifiable prediction:** ≥30% of ME/CFS patients test positive for MED20 or USP5 autoantibodies, and positivity correlates with neurocognitive symptom severity (r≥0.5).

### X2: Autoantibody Subsets Define Psychiatric vs Neurological Presentations

**Title:** CNS vs peripheral autoantibody profiles differentiate POTS vs depression

**Mechanistic rationale:** Post-infectious dysautonomia (POTS) and depression can both present with fatigue but have distinct mechanisms. CNS autoantibodies (MED20, USP5) may define a "neurological" fatigue subtype (POTS + cognitive dysfunction), whereas peripheral autoantibodies (GPCRs) or no autoantibodies define a "psychiatric" subtype, improving diagnostic precision.

**Evidence anchor:** Finding #5: patient subgroups by neurocognitive vs other profiles; Finding #1: >70 CNS/PNS targets

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Patients with CNS autoantibody positivity have 3x higher odds of meeting POTS criteria (95% CI 2.0-4.5) vs patients with only peripheral autoantibodies.

## CONCLUSION

The Cell 2026 paper's unique contributions—large-scale CNS autoantigen discovery, fatigue transfer, symptom-symptom correspondence, and quantitative BBB penetration—open multiple novel research directions. Priority ideas focus on validating the >70 targets, translating autoantibody profiles into personalized treatment algorithms, and developing targeted CNS autoantibody therapies (FcRn blockade, specific immunoadsorption). Cross-disease bridges with ME/CFS and dysautonomia may unify post-infectious autoantibody mechanisms into a single diagnostic and therapeutic framework.
