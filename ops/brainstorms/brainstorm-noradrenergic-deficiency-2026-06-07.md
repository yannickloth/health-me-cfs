## Tier 1: Highest Novelty & Impact

### H1: P2X7–ATP–Calcium–VMAT2 Axis — A Mechanistic Convergence Point for NE Deficiency and PEM
**Category:** 1. Novel hypothesis
**Mechanism:** VMAT2-mediated vesicular fusion and NE release requires calcium-triggered exocytosis. Calcium entry into noradrenergic terminals is gated partly by P2X7 purinergic receptors — ATP-gated ion channels permeable to Ca²⁺. Low intracellular ATP → impaired P2X7 gating → reduced activity-dependent Ca²⁺ influx → impaired VMAT2 vesicle fusion → reduced NE release per action potential. This creates a **triple ATP vulnerability**: (1) ATP for proton pump (already in paper), (2) ATP for P2X7 Ca²⁺ gating (new), (3) ATP for vesicle trafficking (general). The P2X7 link also bridges NE deficiency to neuroinflammation: activated P2X7 on microglia → IL-1β release → further LC dysfunction.
**Evidence:** P2X7-KO mice show reduced stimulated NE release in hippocampus (Miranda-Morales 2019). P2X7 expression on microglia upregulated in neuroinflammation. P2X7 gating requires ≥1 mM ATP at binding site; intracellular ATP depletion drops below this threshold. P2X7 polymorphisms (rs3751143, rs17525809) associated with chronic pain and fatigue.
**Certainty:** 0.25 (novel synthesis; no direct ME/CFS data)
**Falsifiable:** P2X7 antagonist (Brilliant Blue G, A-438079) reduces NE release from patient-derived sympathetic neurons in vitro; P2X7 SNP rs3751143 predicts CSF NE Pathway status in ME/CFS cohort; P2X7 expression on PBMCs correlates negatively with NE Pathway index.
**Already in paper:** No. P2X7 axis not addressed in ch08, ch14h, or ch14f.

### T1: Guanfacine — The "Wrong Autoreceptor" Approach (α2A-Selective Over Clonidine)
**Category:** 3. Drug/medication idea
**Mechanism:** The clonidine null trial (Sulheim 2014) is often cited as "α2 agonism doesn't work." However, clonidine is a **non-selective** α2A/α2B/α2C agonist — it suppresses LC firing globally (presynaptic α2A autoinhibition) while also binding peripheral α2B (vasoconstriction) and α2C (CNS effects). **Guanfacine** is α2A-selective with >50× selectivity over α2B. At low doses (1–3 mg), guanfacine preferentially binds **postsynaptic** α2A receptors in prefrontal cortex — improving working memory, attention, and cognitive efficiency — without suppressing LC firing rate. The postsynaptic α2A effect enhances PFC NE signaling exactly where it's needed for executive function and effort-based decision-making.
**Evidence:** Guanfacine improves working memory in ADHD (Biederman 2008), PFC cognitive function in TBI, and age-related cognitive decline (MCI trials). Postsynaptic α2A density is highest in PFC — the region most affected in ME/CFS brain fog. Guanfacine does **not** reduce CSF NE or MHPG at clinical doses (unlike clonidine). The ADHD ME/CFS overlap literature (Saez-Francas 2012) supports dual benefit.
**Certainty:** 0.35 (mechanism-based; no ME/CFS data)
**Falsifiable:** Guanfacine XR 1–4 mg over 8 weeks improves PFC-dependent cognitive tasks (n-back, Stroop, effort-based decision-making) and reduces subjective mental fatigue in ME/CFS, while clonidine-equivalent doses worsen fatigue. CSF NE Pathway does NOT decrease (confirming no presynaptic suppression). Guanfacine benefits are largest in patients with lowest PFC-related functional connectivity.
**Rationale for novelty:** Only clonidine has been tested; guanfacine is pharmacodynamically distinct. The α2A-selectivity is the critical difference that makes it potentially therapeutic where clonidine is null or harmful. This is NOT in the paper.
**Already in paper:** No. Ch14h discusses clonidine null and α2 antagonism (yohimbine), but not α2A-selective agonism.

### H2: Structural vs Functional VMAT2 Deficiency — PTPRN2 Hypomethylation as Parallel Hit
**Category:** 1. Novel hypothesis
**Mechanism:** The ATP-vesicular hypothesis (in paper) assumes functional VMAT2 impairment due to ATP shortage. But PTPRN2 hypomethylation (documented in ch12) alters secretory vesicle biogenesis — reducing the **number** of functional vesicles available for NE synthesis, not just their filling capacity. This creates two mechanistically distinct NE deficiency subtypes: (1) **metabolic** — normal VMAT2 number, impaired filling due to ATP depletion; (2) **structural** — reduced VMAT2/vesicle number due to PTPRN2 dysregulation, normal ATP per vesicle. They converge on low CSF NE but require different treatments: metabolic responds to ATP support (creatine, ketones, CoQ10); structural requires transcriptional/epigenetic intervention (PTPRN2 targeting, possibly lithium, HDAC inhibitors).
**Evidence:** PTPRN2 hypomethylation in ME/CFF CD4+ T-cells (ch12:324). PTPRN2 regulates secretory vesicle accumulation and is expressed in brain. Reduced vesicle number → reduced VMAT2 protein → lower NE storage capacity even with normal ATP. PTPRN2-KO mice show altered neurotransmitter release.
**Certainty:** 0.25 (extrapolation; PTPRN2 in brain NE vesicles unstudied in ME/CFS)
**Falsifiable:** CSF NE Pathway in ME/CFS patients with PTPRN2 hypomethylation correlates with VMAT2 protein in CSF exosomes (lower VMAT2 = structural subtype). ATP supplementation (creatine + CoQ10) improves NE Pathway only in PTPRN2-normal (metabolic) but not PTPRN2-hypomethylated (structural) patients. PTPRN2 expression correlates with DBH activity in CSF.
**Already in paper:** PTPRN2 is in ch12 but the convergence with NE deficiency is mentioned only in the compatibility audit, not in ch14h. The structural vs metabolic subtype distinction is entirely novel.

### T2: Droxidopa (L-DOPS) — Direct Bypass of the ATP-Dependent DBH Bottleneck
**Category:** 3. Drug/medication idea
**Mechanism:** Droxidopa (L-threo-3,4-dihydroxyphenylserine) is a synthetic NE precursor that bypasses the rate-limiting DBH step entirely. It is converted directly to NE by L-amino acid decarboxylase (LAAAD), which does NOT require vesicular ATP or DBH. A single oral dose (100–600 mg TID) elevates CNS and peripheral NE without relying on VMAT2 filling, DBH activity, or ATP availability. This is the most direct pharmacological bypass of the putative metabolic bottleneck. Currently FDA-approved for nOH; could be repurposed for ME/CFS.
**Evidence:** Droxidopa improves standing BP in nOH (Kaufmann 2014; NCT00633880). Animal models: enhances PFC, noradrenergic transmission, improves cognition in DBH-KO mice (Thomas 1998). Crosses BBB (~30% of plasma levels). nOH safety data well-established. No ME/CFS trial.
**Certainty:** 0.30 (mechanism-based; nOH data; no ME/CFS data)
**Falsifiable:** Droxidopa 200–600 mg TID over 4 weeks increases CSF NE Pathway (NE+DHPG+MHPG) in ME/CFS patients measurable by lumbar puncture or CSF exosome. Fatigue severity (MFI physical) and handgrip duration improve in patients with lowest baseline NE Pathway. No tachyphylaxis over 8 weeks. Contraindicated in patients with elevated plasma NE (>600 pg/mL standing).
**Key distinction from NRI:** NRI extends dwell time of whatever NE is released; droxidopa increases the absolute amount of NE available for release. These are complementary — combination may be synergistic.
**Already in paper:** No. Not mentioned anywhere in ch08, ch14h, ch15, or ch14f.

### H3: NE Deficiency × TRPM3 Channelopathy — Two-Hit Calcium Dysregulation
**Category:** 1. Novel hypothesis
**Mechanism:** TRPM3 channelopathy (ch14h) impairs calcium influx into NK cells — but TRPM3 is also expressed in noradrenergic neurons. TRPM3 mediates calcium entry required for activity-dependent vesicle fusion. If TRPM3 function is impaired in LC neurons (same biochemical lesion as in NK cells), this reduces calcium-triggered exocytosis of NE vesicles. Combined with the ATP-vesicular filling defect, this creates a **two-hit** deficit: (1) ATP shortage → underfilled vesicles (in paper), (2) TRPM3 dysfunction → impaired fusion/empty vesicles fail to dock/release. Even if ATP-VMAT2 function were normal, impaired calcium signaling would still reduce NE release. This could explain why some patients with normal DBH activity or ATP still have NE deficiency — their bottleneck is at the exocytosis step.
**Evidence:** TRPM3 expressed in brain including LC. Functional TRPM3 impairment in ME/CFS NK cells (Cabanas 2018, 2025). TRPM3 mediates Ca²⁺ influx in neurons that triggers vesicle fusion. TRPM3 antagonists reduce neurotransmitter release in preclinical models.
**Certainty:** 0.20 (highly speculative; TRPM3 function in ME/CFS LC neurons unmeasured)
**Falsifiable:** TRPM3 functional and expression levels in ME/CFS iPSC-derived LC neurons correlate with CSF NE Pathway activity. Calcium imaging in patient-derived LC neurons shows reduced depolarization-evoked Ca²⁺ influx vs controls. TRPM3 agonist (pregnenolone sulfate) increases NE release from patient LC neurons in vitro; TRPM3 antagonist reduces it.
**Already in paper:** No. TRPM3 is discussed in ch14h but not linked to NE deficiency. This is a novel cross-hypothesis bridge.

### B1: CSF NE Pathway / Plasma NE Ratio as Diagnostic Index
**Category:** 9. Diagnostic/biomarker
**Mechanism:** Ratio of CSF NE Pathway (central) to plasma NE (peripheral) captures the central-peripheral mismatch in a single index. Healthy: CSF NE is 20-40% of plasma NE. ME/CFS with central deficiency but peripheral elevation → ratio drops to <10%. This index may discriminate ME/CFS from POTS (where both CSF and plasma NE are normal), depression (both low), and PD (both low).
**Evidence:** Aregawi 2026 shows CSF NE low; Winkler 2016 shows plasma NE high at rest. No study has measured CSF and plasma NE simultaneously in ME/CFS. Ratio approach validated in PD (Goldstein 2016).
**Certainty:** 0.40 (components measured independently; ratio not validated)
**Falsifiable:** Simultaneous CSF + plasma NE measurement in ME/CFS shows CSF/plasma NE ratio < 0.15 in >80% of patients vs <0.10 not seen in HVs (ratio > 0.20). Ratio correlates with PEM severity (r > 0.5). Ratio predicts atomoxetine response: lower ratio → larger benefit. Ratio improves upon CSF-alone discrimination AUC.
**Novelty point:** The **ratio** is diagnostic, not either measure alone. This captures the mismatch physiology. Not in paper.
**Already in paper:** No. Ch14h mentions the mismatch but not as a calculable diagnostic index.

### M1: ATP-Dependent NE Synthesis ODE with P2X7 Calcium Gating
**Category:** 7. Mathematical model extension
**Structure:** ODE system coupling three pools:
```
d[NE_ves]/dt = k_VMAT2 * f([ATP], [Ca²⁺]) - k_release * f([Ca²⁺]) - k_deg * [NE_ves]
d[ATP]/dt = k_prod - k_cons * u(t) - k_VMAT2 * [NE_ves]  (ATP consumption by VMAT2)
d[Ca²⁺]/dt = k_P2X7 * [ATP]/(Km + [ATP]) * I(t) - k_pump * [Ca²⁺]
```
Where u(t) = exertion signal, I(t) = neural activity drive. The key novelty: P2X7 term makes Ca²⁺ influx a function of ATP availability, linking energy state directly to exocytosis trigger. Below ATP threshold [ATP] < [P2X7_Km], Ca²⁺ influx drops → vesicle release fraction collapses → NE output crashes even if VMAT2 filling is normal. This produces a **biphasic response**: moderate ATP drop → compensated (slower filling, maintained release); severe ATP drop → decompensation (collapse of both filling AND release).
**Parameters measured from:** VMAT2 Km~μM (well-established), P2X7 EC50~1 mM ATP, NE turnover kinetics from Aregawi CSF data.
**Earlier model status:** ch14f has ODE for autonomic-mitochondrial loop. ch29 has POTS ODE. ch30 has 64-variable model. This extends the NE subsystem with P2X7-Ca²⁺ coupling not present in any existing model.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The model predicts a critical ATP threshold below which NE release collapses nonlinearly — this could correspond to the PEM threshold. If verified, would explain why graded exertion produces nonlinear PEM severity.

## Tier 2: Strong Novelty, Moderate-High Evidence Support

### H4: NE Circadian Oscillation Collapse — Loss of Diurnal LC Patterning
**Category:** 1. Novel hypothesis
**Mechanism:** LC firing follows a circadian pattern: high during wakefulness (phasic bursts for attention), low during NREM (tonic background for glymphatic vasomotion), silent during REM. Low CSF NE may reflect not just reduced mean but **flattened amplitude** — the sleep-wake transition is absent, harming both daytime arousal (no morning NE surge) and nighttime clearance (no vasomotion oscillation). This explains unrefreshing sleep despite normal sleep duration: the LC-NE oscillation that drives glymphatic clearance never engages. DORAs (lemborexant) improve glymphatic by restoring NE oscillation but do not increase mean daytime NE.
**Evidence:** Hauglund 2025: NE oscillation, not mean, drives vasomotion. Zhu 2025: NE-glymphatic coupling. Aregawi 2026: CSF NE low, but no circadian sampling. Maksoud 2021: near-normal PSG but unrefreshing sleep.
**Certainty:** 0.30 (CSF circadian data lacking in ME/CFS; NE oscillation amplitude never measured)
**Falsifiable:** CSF NE sampled via indwelling catheter every 2h over 24h shows reduced amplitude (ΔNE < 50% of healthy controls) in ME/CFS vs controls, not just reduced mean. DORA (lemborexant) at night increases nocturnal NE oscillation amplitude (measured by pupil diameter or CSF after bedtime) but does NOT increase daytime mean NE — improving sleep refreshingness without fatigue reduction. Combination: DORA night + atomoxetine day improves both sleep quality and daytime fatigue.
**Already in paper:** NE-glymphatic coupling in ch08; DORAs in ch15. But the circadian oscillation collapse is a distinct hypothesis not explicitly stated — ch15 discusses NE oscillations for vasomotion but not the diurnal LC pattern loss.

### T3: Atomoxetine + Guanfacine Fixed-Dose Combination — Parallel NRI + Postsynaptic α2A
**Category:** 3. Drug/medication idea; 6. Combination
**Mechanism:** Atomoxetine increases synaptic NE by blocking reuptake; low-dose guanfacine enhances postsynaptic α2A signaling in PFC without suppressing LC firing. The combination targets both increased NE availability (atomoxetine) and enhanced receptor-level signal transduction (guanfacine) — a dual mechanism that no single agent provides. Guanfacine's PFC-specific effects may also counter atomoxetine's potential anxiety/excitation side effects via α2A-mediated PFC regulation of amygdala reactivity.
**Evidence:** Atomoxetine + guanfacine used clinically in ADHD (combination safe, well-tolerated, synergistic for attention). Guanfacine reduces atomoxetine-associated sympathetic activation in some patients. No ME/CFS data.
**Certainty:** 0.30 (ADHD safety data; no ME/CFS data; mechanistic plausibility)
**Falsifiable:** Atomoxetine (40 mg) + guanfacine XR (2 mg) over 8 weeks produces greater improvement in MFI physical fatigue and cognitive efficiency (Stroop, n-back) than either drug alone, with lower rates of atomoxetine-induced tachycardia and anxiety. Subgroup analysis: largest benefit in patients with comorbid ADHD traits (ASRS > 24). Combination is safe in ME/CFS: no more tachycardia or orthostatic worsening than atomoxetine alone.
**Already in paper:** Atomoxetine alone in ch14h; guanfacine not discussed. The combination is novel.

### H5: LC Dysfunction as Peripheral Immune Gate — Splenic NE-Hematopoietic Axis
**Category:** 1. Novel hypothesis; 8. Cross-disease bridge
**Mechanism:** The LC projects to spinal intermediolateral column → sympathetic chain → splenic nerve → β2-AR on splenocytes → cytokine gene regulation. Central NE deficiency reduces splenic sympathetic tone → altered cytokine balance (IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ dysregulation). This provides a direct neuroanatomical pathway from CSF NE deficiency to the peripheral immune dysfunction documented in ME/CFS (low NK function, Th17 skewing, elevated IL-6). The spleen is the "immunological lymph node" where the NE-immune link is strongest. If splenic NE is low, the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway may also fail (splenic α7nAChR is downstream of NE).
**Evidence:** Splenic nerve denervation in animals alters cytokine responses and antibody production (Rosas-Ballina 2008). β2-AR agonists modulate NK cell cytotoxicity, Th17 differentiation. ME/CFS: low NK function, Th17 skewing, elevated IL-6. Elevated plasma NE (Winkler 2016) may reflect adrenal medullary response rather than splenic sympathetic tone.
**Certainty:** 0.20 (no direct splenic NE measurement in ME/CFS; multiple inferential steps)
**Falsifiable:** ME/CFS patients show lower splenic nerve activity (measured by splenic doppler or ¹²³I-MIBG SPECT splenic signal intensity) correlated with CSF NE Pathway. β2-AR agonist (terbutaline) acutely enhances NK cell cytotoxicity in ME/CFS PBMCs in vitro. Low CSF NE predicts worse NK function in vivo.
**Already in paper:** No. Immune dysfunction (ch14b) and NE deficiency (ch08) are discussed separately; no explicit splenic NE-immune bridge.

### B2: Pupillometry-Based LC Function Test — Non-Invasive NE Proxy
**Category:** 9. Diagnostic/biomarker; 5. Non-pharmacological
**Mechanism:** Pupil diameter is modulated by LC-NE activity: phasic LC bursts → pupil dilates; tonic LC firing → baseline pupil size. The pupillary light reflex (PLR) latency is mediated by LC-NE → superior cervical ganglion → iris. Measuring baseline pupil diameter, PLR latency, and task-evoked pupil dilation provides a non-invasive readout of LC-NE functional integrity. Key parameters: (1) resting pupil diameter (tonic LC), (2) phasic dilation during cognitive task, (3) PLR constriction velocity and latency, (4) post-illumination pupil response.
**Evidence:** Pupil diameter correlates with LC firing rate in non-human primates (Joshi 2016). PLR latency increases in LC-degenerative disorders (PD, MSA). Pupillometry discriminates POTS from controls (Del Pozzi 2021). No ME/CFS pupillometry study with CSF NE correlation exists.
**Certainty:** 0.35 (validated LC correlate; no ME/CFS + CSF validation)
**Falsifiable:** Baseline pupil diameter and PLR latency correlate with CSF NE Pathway index (r > 0.5) in ME/CFS patients undergoing LP. Pupillometry parameters discriminate PI-ME/CFS from HV (AUC > 0.75). Task-evoked pupil dilation correlates with handgrip duration (r > 0.5). Pupillometry predicts atomoxetine response: blunted phasic response → larger NRI benefit.
**Already in paper:** Mentioned in passing in ch14h:901 as one of several potential surrogates. The full protocol with specific parameters and validation strategy is novel.

### T4: Pregnenolone Sulfate as TRPM3-Mediated NE Release Enhancer
**Category:** 3. Drug/medication idea; 4. Supplement/nutraceutical
**Mechanism:** Pregnenolone sulfate (a neurosteroid) is a positive allosteric modulator of TRPM3. If TRPM3 dysfunction impairs calcium-mediated NE vesicle fusion in ME/CFS (H3), low-dose pregnenolone sulfate may enhance residual TRPM3 function → improved Ca²⁺ influx → restored NE release. This targets the **exocytosis** step, not the **filling** step — complementary to ATP-vesicular support. Pregnenolone also modulates NMDA, GABA-A, and sigma-1 receptors, providing additional neurosteroid benefits for cognition and mood.
**Evidence:** Pregnenolone sulfate potently activates TRPM3 (Wagner 2008). Oral pregnenolone 50–200 mg is available as supplement, crosses BBB. Pregnenolone improves cognition in schizophrenia (Marx 2009) and sleep in TBI. Pregnenolone levels are reduced under chronic stress, possibly in ME/CFS (HPA axis attenuation). No ME/CFS data.
**Certainty:** 0.15 (very preliminary; TRPM3 mechanism in LC neurons unconfirmed)
**Falsifiable:** Pregnenolone 50 mg increases CSF NE Pathway in ME/CFS (sub-chronic, 2 weeks) vs placebo. TRPM3 p.Arg1092Gln or other functional variants predict large response. Effect is blocked by TRPM3 antagonist (ononetin) in patient-derived LC neurons in vitro. Cognitive improvement correlates with NE elevation.
**Already in paper:** TRPM3 discussed in ch14h but not linked to NE or treatment. Pregnenolone not mentioned.

### T5: L-DOPS + Low-Dose Atomoxetine — Bypass + Dwell Extension Dual Strategy
**Category:** 6. Combination; 3. Drug/medication
**Mechanism:** Droxidopa (L-DOPS) increases total NE synthesis by bypassing DBH; atomoxetine extends synaptic dwell time of released NE. Together they target the two independent bottlenecks: (1) insufficient NE production (L-DOPS), (2) insufficient synaptic persistence (atomoxetine). This is the most aggressive NE augmentation strategy and would only be considered after failure of monotherapy. The combination may produce synergy: L-DOPS provides substrate → atomoxetine prevents its premature clearance → each NE molecule has more functional impact.
**Evidence:** Both drugs approved by FDA for nOH. L-DOPS + atomoxetine used in refractory nOH (case reports, no RCT). No ME/CFS data. Safety concern: hypertension risk requires BP monitoring; tachycardia limit.
**Certainty:** 0.15 (nOH combination data; no ME/CFS data; safety concern)
**Falsifiable:** L-DOPS 300 mg TID + atomoxetine 40 mg improves MFI physical fatigue by ≥2 SMD (additive over either alone) in ME/CFS patients with both (a) low CSF NE (<50% of HV) and (b) normal plasma NE (<400 pg/mL supine). Combination safe without HTN crisis in 12-week monitoring. Requires baseline plasma NE ≤ 400 pg/mL (excludes hyperadrenergic POTS).
**Already in paper:** Atomoxetine in ch14h; droxidopa not mentioned. Combination not addressed.

### H6: NE Deficiency as Fibromyalgia Pain Bridge — Impaired Descending Analgesia
**Category:** 8. Cross-disease bridge
**Mechanism:** The PAG-RVM (periaqueductal gray-rostral ventromedial medulla) descending pain modulation system depends on NE: LC → spinal cord NE release → α2-AR → inhibition of nociceptive transmission. Low CSF NE → impaired descending analgesia → central sensitization → pain amplification. This provides a direct mechanism for the pain-fatigue overlap in ME/CFS and FM: the same NE deficiency that causes fatigue also disinhibits pain processing. FM patients have normal CSF NE but exhibit α2-AR desensitization; ME/CFS patients have low CSF NE with normal receptors. Both converge on impaired NE-mediated pain inhibition but through different mechanisms — explaining why SNRIs work in both conditions.
**Evidence:** FM: impaired descending analgesia (Julien 2005), α2-AR desensitization. ME/CFS: low CSF NE (Aregawi 2026). SNRI efficacy in both (duloxetine in FM [Arnold 2004], no ME/CFS trial). Conditioned pain modulation (CPM) impaired in both FM and ME/CFS. PAG-RVM function measurable by fMRI.
**Certainty:** 0.35 (mechanism coherent; direct test feasible)
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFS patients, CSF NE Pathway correlates with CPM magnitude (r > 0.4): lower NE → weaker endogenous analgesia. Duloxetine increases NE-dependent pain thresholds (quantitative sensory testing) better in patients with low vs normal CSF NE. ME/CFS with comorbid pain (widespread > 3 months) have lower CSF NE than pain-free ME/CFS.
**Already in paper:** Pain mechanisms in ch14a, ch15. NE deficiency in ch08. But the explicit bridge linking NE deficiency to impaired descending analgesia is not drawn.

### B3: Urinary VMA/HVA Ratio as Peripheral Catecholamine Balance Index
**Category:** 9. Diagnostic/biomarker
**Mechanism:** Vanillylmandelic acid (VMA) is the primary NE metabolite; homovanillic acid (HVA) is the primary DA metabolite. 24h urinary VMA/HVA ratio reflects systemic NE:DA turnover. Since NE is derived from DA (via DBH), a low VMA/HVA ratio indicates impaired DBH function or selective NE deficiency — the same biochemical pattern as CSF NE Pathway. If peripheral DBH mirrors central DBH, urine VMA/HVA could serve as a non-invasive surrogate for CSF NE Pathway status.
**Evidence:** Low VMA in PD (correlates with CSF NE). Urine VMA/HVA ratio used in neuroblastoma diagnosis. Cheap, standardized, non-invasive. No ME/CFS study has measured 24h urine VMA/HVA. DBH is present in both CNS and peripheral sympathetic nerves, so peripheral VMA may partially reflect central DBH function.
**Certainty:** 0.25 (plausible but unvalidated against CSF)
**Falsifiable:** 24h urinary VMA/HVA ratio correlates with CSF NE Pathway (NE+DHPG+MHPG) in ME/CFS patients undergoing LP (r > 0.5). VMA/HVA < 2.0 (or age/sex-adjusted) discriminates PI-ME/CFS from HV (AUC > 0.70). Ratio normalizes after droxidopa treatment.
**Already in paper:** No. Urinary catecholamine metabolites not discussed.

## Tier 3: Speculative but Worth Recording

### H7: NE Deficiency × POTS Bidirectional Causality — Low CNS NE Drives Hyperadrenergic POTS
**Category:** 8. Cross-disease bridge
**Mechanism:** Central NE deficiency → impaired baroreflex integration at NTS → loss of inhibitory modulation of peripheral sympathetic outflow → compensatory peripheral NE elevation → POTS. This reverses the causal direction usually assumed: instead of "POTS causes brain fog via reduced CBF," low central NE causes both brain fog AND disinhibited peripheral sympathetic outflow (POTS). Hyperadrenergic POTS may be a **consequence** of low central NE, not an independent comorbidity. This predicts that treating central NE deficiency (atomoxetine, droxidopa) would improve POTS symptoms — the opposite of the current clinical concern that NRIs worsen tachycardia.
**Evidence:** Aregawi 2026: no correlation between NE Pathway and orthostatic tachycardia — i.e., low NE patients can have normal or elevated HR. Wyller 2016: SAM enhancement + HPA attenuation. Clonidine null: if peripheral sympathetic overactivity were independent, suppressing it should help.
**Certainty:** 0.15 (causal direction speculative; NRIs in POTS could worsen)
**Falsifiable:** Acute atomoxetine challenge (40 mg) in ME/CFS+POTS patients with documented low CSF NE shows improved orthostatic HR and BP (not worsened) at 2h post-dose. Standing plasma NE at 2h post-dose shows blunted increase (less sympathetic activation) — because central NE improves baroreflex gain. A CSF NE-guided trial of atomoxetine in POTS patients without ME/CFS shows improvement only in those with low CSF NE.
**Already in paper:** No. POTS is discussed separately (ch10). The bidirectional causality hypothesis is novel.

### H8: DBH Promoter Polymorphism (rs1611115) Predicts CSF NE Pathway and PEM Severity
**Category:** 9. Diagnostic/biomarker
**Mechanism:** DBH transcription is controlled by rs1611115 (−1021C>T), a promoter SNP accounting for ~50% of plasma DBH variation. The T allele reduces DBH expression and activity. In healthy HVs, TT homozygotes have 10-fold lower DBH activity than CC. If this polymorphism interacts with ATP-sensitive VMAT2 impairment, TT carriers with ME/CFS would have the lowest CSF NE Pathway and most severe PEM. This is a genetic susceptibility factor that amplifies metabolic NE deficiency.
**Evidence:** rs1611115 strongly predicts plasma DBH (Zabetian 2001). DBH activity in CSF and plasma are correlated. TT homozygotes have reduced NE synthesis capacity. No ME/CFS DBH genetics study exists.
**Certainty:** 0.20 (well-established in healthy/PD; no ME/CFS data)
**Falsifiable:** rs1611115 TT homozygotes among ME/CFS patients have lower CSF NE Pathway than CC/CT carriers (genotype effect > 1 SD). TT carriers with PEM fulfill more severe PEM criteria (DSQ-PEM score, longer recovery time). DBH activity in serum correlates with NE Pathway and predicts handgrip duration.
**Already in paper:** No. DBH genetics not discussed.

### H9: ME/CFS as Functional Pre-PD — Reversible LC Failure vs Irreversible LC Degeneration
**Category:** 8. Cross-disease bridge
**Mechanism:** In PD, LC degenerates years before substantia nigra, and CSF NE drops before DA. ME/CFS shares the early LC dysfunction signature (low CSF NE) but without structural degeneration (no α-synuclein aggregates). ME/CFS may represent "functional PD" — the same LC failure but caused by energy deficit/inflammation rather than proteinopathy. This would predict that (a) ME/CFS patients do NOT develop PD at elevated rates (LC death does not progress), (b) ME/CFS CSF NE is reversible with ATP restoration (unlike PD where NE is structurally gone), (c) the mechanisms protecting against LC degeneration (heat shock proteins, mitophagy) are intact in ME/CFS.
**Evidence:** Aregawi 2026 included PD comparator: PD showed dual NE+DA deficiency, ME/CFS showed selective NE deficiency. LC degeneration earliest PD pathology (Braak stage 1). No epidemiological ME/CFS→PD link. 7T MRI LC imaging in ME/CFS would distinguish structural vs functional.
**Certainty:** 0.15 (speculative; no direct LC imaging in ME/CFS)
**Falsifiable:** 7T LC neuromelanin imaging shows normal LC volume in ME/CFS (vs reduced in PD) despite low CSF NE. ME/CFS patients followed over 10 years do NOT develop PD at elevated rates. CSF NE in ME/CFS normalizes after ATP support (ketogenic diet, CoQ10) — PD CSF NE does not improve with same intervention.
**Already in paper:** LC dysfunction in ch08. PD comparator in ch08 (Aregawi data). The "functional pre-PD" concept is not in paper.

### T6: Melatonin Day 3 mg + Atomoxetine AM — Chronobiotic NE-Fatigue Protocol
**Category:** 6. Combination; 3. Drug/medication
**Mechanism:** All ME/CFS medications should consider circadian phase. High-dose melatonin (>3 mg) during the day is typically contraindicated (phase shift), BUT in ME/CFS with potential LC-NE circadian collapse, daytime melatonin may paradoxically help by reducing the wake-promoting drive that cannot be met (LC-NE insufficient to maintain alertness). The body struggles to be awake because LC cannot generate the NE surge — daytime melatonin reduces the mismatch by reducing the wakefulness demand. Combined with atomoxetine (which increases NE availability), this allows the NE system to operate within its reduced capacity. Evening DORA + morning atomoxetine + optional low-dose daytime melatonin for refractory cases.
**Evidence:** Melatonin reduces LC firing in animal models via MT1 receptors. ME/CFS patients report daytime sedation paradoxically relieved by small melatonin doses (patient reports). No controlled data.
**Certainty:** 0.10 (controversial; phase-shifting risk; no data)
**Falsifiable:** DORA (lemborexant 5 mg) at bedtime + atomoxetine 40 mg at breakfast improves both sleep quality (PSG, subjective) and daytime fatigue (MFI) more than either alone in ME/CFS. Adding melatonin 1 mg daytime in non-responders does NOT worsen phase shift and may improve fatigue in those with lowest baseline pupil diameter.
**Already in paper:** DORAs in ch15. Atomoxetine in ch14h. Combination not discussed. Melatonin discussed separately (sleep section).

### B4: CSF Exosome VMAT2 and DBH Protein — Direct Vesicular Enzyme Readout Without LP
**Category:** 9. Diagnostic/biomarker
**Mechanism:** Neuronal exosomes cross the BBB into blood and carry CNS-specific proteins including VMAT2 and DBH. Isolating L1CAM-positive exosomes from plasma and measuring VMAT2 and DBH protein levels by ELISA or SIMOA provides a direct readout of the vesicular NE synthesis machinery WITHOUT LP. If VMAT2 in CSF exosomes is low, the structural deficit (PTPRN2) is confirmed; if VMAT2 is normal but activity is ATP-dependent, the metabolic subtype is confirmed.
**Evidence:** L1CAM exosome isolation validated in AD (Aβ, tau), PD (α-synuclein). VMAT2 and DBH detectable in CSF. Plasma neuronal exosomes reflect CNS protein turnover. No ME/CFS exosome VMAT2/DBH study exists.
**Certainty:** 0.20 (validated in neurodegeneration; no ME/CFS data)
**Falsifiable:** L1CAM+ exosomal VMAT2 and DBH protein levels correlate with CSF NE Pathway in ME/CFS patients undergoing LP (r > 0.5). Exosomal VMAT2 distinguishes structural from metabolic NE deficiency subtypes. Serial exosome VMAT2/DBH tracks treatment response to atomoxetine and droxidopa.
**Already in paper:** No. CNS exosomes not discussed in NE context.

### H10: Pacing + Atomoxetine as PEM-Prevention Protocol — Medication-Enabled Pacing
**Category:** 5. Non-pharmacological; 6. Combination
**Mechanism:** Atomoxetine increases synaptic NE without increasing NE synthesis demand. This enables the brain to maintain effort capacity within a smaller NE envelope. Combined with a strict pacing protocol (HR-based, e.g., 15 bpm below AT), the medication reduces the NE crash after activity without enabling overexertion. The key distinction from stimulants: atomoxetine does not mask fatigue perception as strongly (no DA release), so the patient retains awareness of their energy limits while having more functional capacity within those limits. This is "medication-enabled pacing" not "medication-enabled overexertion."
**Evidence:** Young 2025 solriamfetol: did NOT measure PEM — critical gap. Atomoxetine does not produce euphoria or reward-activation (no DA component) — lower abuse risk and less fatigue-perception masking. Mwesigwa 2024: atomoxetine safe in nOH with BP improvement.
**Certainty:** 0.25 (mechanism-based; no PEM-specific atomoxetine data)
**Falsifiable:** Atomoxetine 40 mg + HR-monitored pacing (PEM prevention using HR < anaerobic threshold) reduces PEM frequency and severity (DSQ-PEM) compared to pacing alone over 8 weeks. Unlike modafinil or methylphenidate, atomoxetine does NOT increase daily step counts (no activity-enabling) while improving fatigue (MFI physical). Atomoxetine + pacing is superior to atomoxetine alone (pacing prevents PEM).
**Already in paper:** Ch15 discusses stimulant pacing interaction. Atomoxetine in ch14h. But the specific combination protocol is not detailed.

### T7: Low-Dose Prazosin (α1 Antagonist) Nightly for NE-Mediated Vasomotion Preservation
**Category:** 3. Drug/medication
**Mechanism:** NE drives vasomotion via α1 receptors on cerebral arterioles: NE release → vasoconstriction → CSF displacement → glymphatic flow. In ME/CFS, low NE means vasomotion is weak. However, chronic low NE may also lead to α1 receptor upregulation → when NE IS released (during REM transition, partial arousals), it causes excessive vasoconstriction → microvascular ischemia → further damage. Low-dose prazosin (1–2 mg at night) selectively blocks α1 receptors in cerebral vasculature, dampening the exaggerated response and preserving residual vasomotion amplitude. This is paradoxical: α1 blockade in the setting of low NE may improve vascular function by preventing excessive NE responses when they do occur.
**Evidence:** Prazosin effective for PTSD nightmares — central α1 blockade reduces autonomic arousal. Used at 1–5 mg at night. Cerebral blood flow effects in ME/CFS unstudied. No safety concern (hypotension risk but typically well-tolerated at low dose).
**Certainty:** 0.10 (mechanism complex; α1 in ME/CFS cerebral vasculature unstudied)
**Falsifiable:** Low-dose prazosin (1 mg qHS) over 4 weeks improves sleep refreshingness (PROMIS sleep disturbance) and morning cognitive function (Stroop) in ME/CFS. DTI-ALPS (glymphatic index) increases after prazosin treatment. No worsening of orthostatic hypotension (monitored). Effect is larger in patients with highest standing plasma NE (most α1 upregulation).
**Already in paper:** No. Prazosin not discussed in NE context.

### H11: NE Deficiency × Adenosine Receptor Cross-Talk — Sleep Pressure Mismatch
**Category:** 1. Novel hypothesis
**Mechanism:** Adenosine A1 receptors and α1-adrenergic receptors form heteromers in the brain that cross-inhibit each other. When NE is low (ME/CFS), α1-A1 heteromer signaling may be shifted toward adenosine dominance → increased sleep pressure perception during wakefulness → the subjective "wired but tired" feeling. Atomoxetine increases NE → shifts heteromer balance back toward α1 → reduces excessive adenosine-mediated sleep pressure. This explains why NRIs often improve subjective wakefulness in the absence of objective sleep deprivation. The heteromer mechanism links NE deficiency directly to the unrefreshing sleep phenomenon via a receptor-level interaction.
**Evidence:** A1-α1 heteromers demonstrated in HEK cells and rat brain (Ciruela 2012), cross-inhibition confirmed. Adenosine suggests sleep homeostasis during wake. CSF NE deficiency shifts balance toward A1. Antagonists: caffeine (A1 antagonist) provides temporary relief.
**Certainty:** 0.10 (receptor heterobiology in ME/CFS unstudied; mechanism plausible)
**Falsifiable:** In ME/CFF patient-derived cells expressing A1-α1 heteromers, NE deprivation increases A1-mediated cAMP inhibition; NE restoration (atomoxetine) reverses this. Caffeine response in ME/CFS correlates with CSF NE: lower NE → larger caffeine benefit.
**Already in paper:** No. Adenosine-NE heterodimer not discussed.

### H12: Dabigatran/Voraxapar — Thrombin Inhibition for NE Vascular Coupling
**Category:** 3. Drug/medication — cross-topic connection
**Mechanism:** Thrombin activates PAR1 receptors on cerebral endothelium → reduces cerebral blood flow reactivity and impairs neurovascular coupling. Chronic thrombin/PAR1 activation may impair NE-mediated vasomotion by desensitizing the vascular response to NE — the vessel cannot respond to the NE signal even when present. Low-dose dabigatran (thrombin inhibitor) or vorapaxar (PAR1 antagonist) could restore NE-vascular coupling by reversing PAR1-mediated desensitization. Thrombin/PAR1 activation is linked to BBB disruption in neuroinflammation (documented in ME/CFS).
**Evidence:** Vorapaxar (PAR1 antagonist) FDA-approved for CVD. Dabigatran used in cerebrovascular disease prevention. PAR1 mediates neurovascular coupling impairment in AD models. No ME/CFS data.
**Certainty:** 0.05 (highly speculative; multiple inferential steps; bleeding risk)
**Falsifiable:** CSF thrombin activity or PAR1 expression on CSF exosomes is elevated in ME/CFS patients with low DTI-ALPS (glymphatic index). PAR1 antagonist restores NE-mediated vasomotion in patient-derived cerebral microvascular endothelial cells in vitro. Only worth pursuing if glymphatic-NE coupling confirmed impaired.
**Already in paper:** No. Completely novel direction.
