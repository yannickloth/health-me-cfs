## Executive Summary

TSPO PET imaging in ME/CFS presents a critical paradox: the only published study found NO neuroinflammation signal, while preliminary conference data (unpublished Michelle James Stanford study) suggests TSPO elevation in peripheral tissues (muscle, bone marrow) with DECREASED signal in brain. This "peripheral-up, central-down" pattern may reflect either (1) true absence of central neuroinflammation with peripheral pathology driving symptoms, or (2) technical confounding where BBB transport limitations mask central inflammation.

**Key literature findings:**
- Only ONE published ME/CFS TSPO PET study (Raijmakers 2021, n=9) - negative finding, first-generation tracer
- GWI shows WIDESPREAD cortical TSPO elevation (Alshelh 2020) - positive finding, second-generation tracer
- Barzon 2026: Peripheral inflammation REDUCES TSPO tracer brain influx (K1) - BBB transport confounding
- TREM1 is MORE SPECIFIC than TSPO for infiltrating myeloid cells (James 2022, 2023)
- No whole-body TSPO PET studies in ME/CFS published in PubMed
- Michelle James Stanford ME/CFS study NOT in PubMed (conference-only?)

**Critical knowledge gaps:**
- No ME/CFS studies using second- or third-generation TSPO tracers
- No whole-body TSPO PET in ME/CFS
- No BBB permeability assessment (K1 influx rate) in ME/CFS TSPO studies
- No TREM1-PET studies in ME/CFS
- Michelle James study status unknown (conference abstract vs. preprint vs. published)

## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### H1: The "Peripheral-Inflation, Central-Starvation" Hypothesis

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
The paradoxical pattern suggested by preliminary Michelle James data (elevated peripheral TSPO, decreased brain TSPO) may reflect a resource reallocation mechanism: systemic inflammation drives massive TSPO upregulation in peripheral immune cells (monocytes in bone marrow, muscle macrophages), while mitochondrial dysfunction in brain microglia prevents compensatory TSPO upregulation. The brain's high energy demand (20-25% of resting metabolism) combined with ME/CFS mitochondrial impairment may exhaust the capacity for microglial activation, leaving the CNS "immune-quiet" despite peripheral inflammation driving symptoms via vagal and humoral signaling.

**Evidence Link:**
- Direct: Michelle James preliminary data (conference-only) shows peripheral TSPO elevation with brain signal decrease
- Direct: Barzon 2026 shows peripheral inflammation reduces TSPO tracer brain influx (BBB transport confounding)
- Direct: Heng 2025 shows immune cell energy deficits (reduced ATP/ADP ratio)
- Supporting: Van Campen 2020-2024 series shows 91% of ME/CFS patients have CBF decline during orthostasis
- Supporting: Walitt 2024 shows central catecholamine deficiency in ME/CFS

**Certainty:** 0.25 (Mechanistically plausible; Michelle James data not peer-reviewed; alternative explanations exist)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. ME/CFS patients should show elevated TSPO signal in bone marrow, spleen, and postural muscles on whole-body TSPO PET, with brain signal equal to or below healthy controls
2. TSPO expression in ME/CFS peripheral monocytes should be elevated (flow cytometry), while brain microglia from post-mortem tissue (if available) should show normal or reduced TSPO
3. Peripheral inflammation markers (CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha) should correlate positively with peripheral TSPO signal but negatively with brain TSPO signal (supporting the trade-off hypothesis)
4. If peripheral TSPO elevation drives symptoms, treatments that reduce peripheral immune activation (immunomodulators, senolytics) should improve symptoms without changing brain TSPO signal

**Alternative Interpretation:** The observed brain signal decrease could be BBB transport limitation (Barzon 2026 mechanism) rather than true absence of microglial activation. This can be distinguished by measuring tracer influx rate K1 directly.

### H2: TSPO as Mitochondrial Energy Crisis Marker Rather Than Neuroinflammation Marker

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
TSPO is located on the outer mitochondrial membrane and regulates cholesterol transport for steroidogenesis, in addition to being upregulated during inflammation. In ME/CFS, the primary defect may be mitochondrial dysfunction rather than inflammation. The negative Raijmakers 2021 TSPO PET finding (no neuroinflammation) could reflect that microglia are energy-depleted and CANNOT upregulate TSPO even if they are "activated" in a functional sense. The Michelle James peripheral TSPO elevation may reflect compensatory upregulation in tissues that still have mitochondrial capacity, while brain microglia are too energy-deficient to respond.

**Evidence Link:**
- Direct: TSPO biology - located on outer mitochondrial membrane, dual role in inflammation and mitochondrial function
- Direct: Heng 2025 shows reduced ATP/ADP ratio in ME/CFS immune cells
- Supporting: Syed 2025 mitochondrial review documents widespread mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS
- Supporting: Nakatomi 2014 showed neuroinflammatory marker elevation in ME/CFS brain regions (contradicts TSPO-negative finding, suggesting marker mismatch)
- Supporting: Yu 2026 NII neuroinflammation imaging suggests white matter pathology consistent with ischemia rather than pure inflammation

**Certainty:** 0.30 (TSPO's mitochondrial role is established; interpretation as primary marker in ME/CFS is speculative)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Direct measurement of brain microglial mitochondrial function (e.g., mitochondrial membrane potential, ATP production) in ME/CFS should show deficits even when TSPO PET is negative
2. Correlation between mitochondrial function markers in CSF or PBMCs and symptom severity should be stronger than correlation between TSPO PET signal and symptoms
3. Treatments that improve mitochondrial function (CoQ10, NAD+ precursors) should increase TSPO PET signal in brain microglia if the baseline low signal reflects energy deficit rather than absence of activation
4. Post-mortem brain tissue from ME/CFS patients should show microglial activation markers (Iba1, CD68) elevated despite negative TSPO PET findings during life

**Research Implication:** This hypothesis suggests TSPO PET is the WRONG biomarker for ME/CFS neuroinflammation, and mitochondrial tracers (e.g., FDG-PET for metabolism) would be more informative.

### H3: The "BBB-Shield" Hypothesis: Neuroinflammation Present but Inaccessible to TSPO Tracers

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Barzon et al. (2026) demonstrated that peripheral inflammation reduces blood-to-brain transport of TSPO tracers across three different tracers (PK11195, DPA714, PBR28). If ME/CFS involves significant systemic inflammation (as documented by plasma proteomics and cytokine studies), TSPO tracers may be systematically under-delivered to the brain, creating false-negative findings. The Michelle James observation of decreased brain TSPO signal in ME/CFS could reflect this BBB transport limitation rather than true absence of neuroinflammation. The brain may have activated microglia expressing TSPO, but the tracer simply cannot reach them at sufficient concentration.

**Evidence Link:**
- Direct: Barzon 2026 - negative correlation between CRP and TSPO tracer brain influx rate (K1) across 358 scans
- Direct: Raijmakers 2021 - negative ME/CFS TSPO PET finding (n=9, first-generation tracer)
- Supporting: Van Campen 2024 - 91% of ME/CFS patients show CBF decline during orthostasis, suggesting cerebrovascular dysfunction
- Supporting: McAlpine 2026 - Long COVID vascular biomarkers show endothelial dysfunction without overt coagulopathy, similar pattern expected in ME/CFS
- Supporting: Heng 2025 - vascular dysfunction biomarkers in ME/CFS

**Certainty:** 0.45 (Mechanism well-established in other conditions; direct ME/CFS application requires K1 measurement to confirm)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. ME/CFS patients should have reduced TSPO tracer brain influx rate (K1) compared to healthy controls when measured with kinetic modeling
2. Patients with highest peripheral inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, IL-1beta) should show lowest K1 values (Barzon 2026 finding should replicate in ME/CFS)
3. If K1 reduction is the primary confound, then adjusting TSPO binding potential for K1 should reveal significant differences between ME/CFS and controls
4. BBB permeability measured by alternative methods (e.g., dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI with gadolinium) should show reduced permeability in ME/CFS during periods of elevated peripheral inflammation
5. Treatments that reduce peripheral inflammation (anti-inflammatories, senolytics, immunomodulators) should increase K1 and allow better tracer delivery, potentially revealing previously masked neuroinflammation

**Clinical Implication:** This hypothesis suggests that ME/CFS neuroinflammation may have been MISSED rather than ABSENT in the Raijmakers 2021 study due to methodological limitation (no K1 measurement).

### H4: The "Reservoir-Shift" Hypothesis: Peripheral Monocytes as Primary TSPO Source

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
James et al. (2022, 2023) demonstrated that TSPO is expressed in both central microglia AND peripheral myeloid cells (monocytes, macrophages). The Michelle James finding of elevated peripheral TSPO with decreased brain signal may indicate that ME/CFS involves a SHIFT of TSPO-expressing cells from central to peripheral compartments. Specifically, bone marrow may produce and release more monocytes into circulation (compensating for systemic immune challenges), while brain microglia are relatively quiescent due to CNS-specific regulatory mechanisms or energy limitation.

**Evidence Link:**
- Direct: James 2022 - TSPO tracers detect inflammation in brain AND peripheral tissues (blood, spleen)
- Direct: James 2023 - TREM1-PET shows higher specificity for infiltrating peripheral myeloid cells than TSPO-PET
- Direct: Michelle James preliminary data - elevated TSPO in bone marrow, postural muscles; decreased in brain
- Supporting: Hornig 2015 - cytokine signatures in ME/CFS show timing-dependent immune patterns (early stage pro-inflammatory)
- Supporting: Che 2025 - NK cell exhaustion in ME/CFS with elevated IL-2 (immune dysregulation)
- Supporting: Van Campen 2020 - severity-dependent patterns suggesting immune involvement

**Certainty:** 0.35 (Reservoir shift concept is novel but plausible; limited direct ME/CFS data)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Flow cytometry of peripheral blood should show elevated monocyte TSPO expression in ME/CFS compared to controls
2. Whole-body TSPO PET should show bone marrow signal that correlates with monocyte TSPO expression (ex vivo flow cytometry validation)
3. The ratio of peripheral-to-central TSPO signal (bone marrow/brain, muscle/brain) should be higher in ME/CFS than healthy controls or other fatigue conditions (e.g., GWI, which shows cortical elevation)
4. Treatments that reduce monocyte activation or production (e.g., low-dose immunosuppressants, immunomodulators) should reduce peripheral TSPO signal more than brain signal
5. Longitudinal tracking during PEM flare should show peripheral TSPO signal INCREASE (monocyte activation) while brain signal DECREASES further (BBB transport limitation worsens with inflammation)

**Research Implication:** This hypothesis suggests that peripheral immune modulation may be more effective than CNS-targeted therapies for ME/CFS, even though symptoms are predominantly neurocognitive.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### R1: Replication of Raijmakers 2021 with Second-Generation Tracer + K1 Measurement

**Rationale:**
The single published ME/CFS TSPO PET study used first-generation tracer PK11195, which has poor signal-to-noise ratio, and did not measure BBB transport (K1). Replication with modern methodology is essential before concluding whether ME/CFS involves neuroinflammation.

**Proposed Design:**
- Participants: n=60 ME/CFS (stratified by severity), n=30 healthy controls
- Tracer: [11C]-PBR28 or [11C]-DPA-713 (second-generation) OR [18F]-LW223 (third-generation, polymorphism-independent)
- Imaging: Dynamic TSPO PET for 90 minutes post-injection to calculate kinetic parameters
- Key measurements:
  - TSPO binding potential (BPND) - standard outcome from Raijmakers 2021
  - Brain influx rate (K1) - to assess BBB transport (Barzon 2026 methodology)
  - Whole-body imaging (if using [18F]-LW223) to assess peripheral TSPO distribution
- Additional measures: Plasma CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha at scan time; rs6971 genotyping for second-generation tracers
- Primary outcomes: (1) BPND difference ME/CFS vs. controls, (2) K1 difference ME/CFS vs. controls, (3) Correlation of BPND with K1 (confounding assessment)

**Evidence Link:**
- Raijmakers 2021: Provides baseline methodology to replicate and improve
- Barzon 2026: Provides K1 measurement methodology
- Alshelh 2020 (GWI): Shows second-generation tracer can detect neuroinflammation in fatigue syndrome
- Khaing 2026: Demonstrates whole-body TSPO PET feasibility with third-generation tracer

**Certainty:** 0.85 (Study design is methodologically rigorous; addresses documented gaps)

**Testability:** High - All required techniques are established; requires access to PET facility and tracer synthesis

### R2: Whole-Body TSPO PET in ME/CFS to Quantify Peripheral Inflammation

**Rationale:**
No whole-body TSPO PET studies exist in ME/CFS. The Michelle James preliminary data suggests peripheral TSPO elevation in muscle, bone marrow, and other tissues. Systematic whole-body imaging would quantify the extent and pattern of peripheral TSPO expression, distinguishing ME/CFS from GWI (which shows cortical elevation) and from healthy controls.

**Proposed Design:**
- Participants: n=40 ME/CFS (20 mild-moderate, 20 severe), n=20 healthy controls
- Tracer: [18F]-LW223 (third-generation, polymorphism-independent, longer half-life for whole-body imaging)
- Imaging: Whole-body PET/CT from head to mid-thigh at multiple time points (e.g., 60 min, 90 min, 120 min post-injection)
- Regions of interest: Brain, bone marrow (sternum, vertebrae), spleen, liver, heart, lungs, kidneys, postural muscles (shoulders, neck, glutes), skeletal muscle (thigh, calf)
- Quantification: Standardized uptake values (SUVs) normalized to each tissue, comparison across groups
- Additional measures: Symptom severity, fatigue scales, orthostatic intolerance measures, inflammatory biomarkers

**Evidence Link:**
- Khaing 2026: First-in-human biodistribution of [18F]-LW223 shows feasibility of whole-body imaging
- Michelle James preliminary data: Suggests specific pattern of peripheral TSPO elevation (muscle, bone marrow)
- Heng 2025: Vascular dysfunction biomarkers suggest systemic inflammation

**Certainty:** 0.70 (Methodology established; Michelle James data provides specific pattern to test)

**Testability:** High - Requires third-generation tracer access and longer scan time; otherwise straightforward

### R3: TREM1-PET vs. TSPO-PET Head-to-Head Comparison in ME/CFS

**Rationale:**
James et al. (2022, 2023) showed that TREM1 is more specific for infiltrating peripheral myeloid cells, while TSPO detects both central microglia and peripheral immune cells. Comparing both tracers in ME/CFS could distinguish (1) microglial activation vs. (2) peripheral immune cell infiltration, resolving ambiguity in interpretation of TSPO PET findings.

**Proposed Design:**
- Participants: n=30 ME/CFS, n=15 healthy controls
- Tracers: (1) TSPO tracer (PBR28 or DPA-713) for microglial + peripheral, (2) TREM1-PET (if human-validated tracer available; currently preclinical only)
- Imaging: Separate scans for each tracer, at least 48 hours apart to avoid interference
- Analysis: Compare spatial distribution patterns - TREM1 signal should localize to infiltrating cells, TSPO to both infiltrating and resident cells
- Additional measures: CSF analysis for neuroinflammatory markers, blood flow cytometry for myeloid cell subsets

**Evidence Link:**
- James 2023 (MS mouse model): TREM1-PET showed 14-17-fold higher sensitivity than TSPO-PET for active disease
- James 2022 (PD mouse model): Both tracers showed elevated signal, but TREM1 was more specific for infiltrating myeloid cells
- VanElzakker 2019: Critically reviews TSPO PET limitations in ME/CFS

**Certainty:** 0.40 (Conceptually strong; TREM1-PET may not be available for human use yet)

**Testability:** Medium - Requires human-validated TREM1 tracer; currently preclinical

### R4: TSPO PET During Active PEM vs. Baseline

**Rationale:**
Peripheral inflammation is expected to be highest during PEM flare-ups. If BBB transport limitation (H3) is real, K1 should be lowest during PEM, potentially masking neuroinflammation. Scanning patients during both baseline and active PEM states could reveal dynamic changes in TSPO signal and BBB transport.

**Proposed Design:**
- Participants: n=30 ME/CFS patients with reliable PEM triggers
- Protocol: Baseline TSPO PET scan during stable period, then standardized PEM trigger (submaximal exercise or cognitive challenge), followed by TSPO PET scan 24-48 hours post-trigger (during peak PEM)
- Measurements: TSPO binding potential (BPND), brain influx rate (K1), inflammatory biomarkers at both time points
- Analysis: Compare baseline vs. PEM for both BPND and K1; correlate changes with PEM severity

**Evidence Link:**
- Barzon 2026: Shows CRP correlates with K1 reduction (inflammation affects BBB transport)
- Literature on exercise-recovery: Keller 2024, Van Campen 2020 show objective PEM measures via 2-day CPET
- Clinical observation: PEM flares are accompanied by symptom worsening consistent with systemic inflammation

**Certainty:** 0.60 (Mechanistically grounded; PEM triggering methodology established)

**Testability:** Medium - Requires careful patient selection (reliable PEM triggers) and ethical consideration of causing PEM for research

### R5: Longitudinal TSPO PET to Track Neuroinflammation Evolution

**Rationale:**
Hornig 2015 showed cytokine signatures vary by disease duration (early vs. late stage). TSPO PET may show similar temporal evolution. Tracking patients over 2-3 years could reveal whether neuroinflammation develops over time or remains absent.

**Proposed Design:**
- Participants: n=40 newly diagnosed ME/CFS (<2 years duration), n=40 long-duration ME/CFS (>7 years), n=20 controls
- Protocol: Baseline TSPO PET with K1 measurement, then follow-up scans at 12 months and 24 months
- Analysis: Compare cross-sectional (new vs. long-duration) and longitudinal (changes over time) patterns
- Additional measures: Disease duration, severity progression, treatment changes

**Evidence Link:**
- Hornig 2015: Early-stage ME/CFS shows pro-inflammatory cytokines; late-stage shows immunosenescence pattern
- Montoya 2017: Cytokine patterns correlate with severity, suggesting temporal evolution
- Yu 2026 NII imaging: White matter abnormalities correlate with clinical measures

**Certainty:** 0.50 (Conceptually aligned with cytokine literature; longitudinal design resource-intensive)

**Testability:** Medium-High - Methodologically straightforward; requires long-term commitment and funding

## Category 3: Medication Ideas

### M1: Low-Dose Naltrexone (LDN) for Microglial Modulation

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
LDN acts as a glial cell modulator, reducing microglial activation and neuroinflammation. If ME/CFS involves masked neuroinflammation (H3: BBB-shield hypothesis), LDN might reduce central inflammation, potentially improving BBB transport (less peripheral inflammation = higher K1) and allowing better tracer delivery for detection, in addition to symptomatic benefit.

**Evidence Link:**
- Document context: LDN discussed in ch01-introduction as emerging from Long COVID experience
- Literature: Bolton 2020 shows LDN benefit in inflammatory conditions via microglial modulation
- Indirect: Walitt 2024 shows central catecholamine deficiency; microglial modulation could affect catecholamine signaling

**Rationale for Use in ME/CFS:**
- Dose: 1.5-4.5 mg/day, titrated slowly
- Duration: 12-week trial with serial TSPO PET scans (if available) to assess neuroinflammation changes
- Monitoring: Baseline and serial inflammatory biomarkers, symptom scales

**Certainty:** 0.35 (LDN has clinical use in ME/CFS; specific effect on neuroinflammation in ME/CFS unproven)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. LDN treatment should reduce neuroinflammatory markers in CSF (if repeat lumbar puncture feasible)
2. TSPO PET binding potential should decrease if neuroinflammation is present, or K1 should increase if BBB transport improves
3. Symptom improvement should correlate with reduction in inflammatory biomarkers
4. Patients with higher baseline inflammation (CRP, IL-6) should show greater LDN response (Barzon 2026 suggests these patients have more BBB transport limitation to overcome)

**Safety Note:** LDN has good safety profile at low doses; main contraindication is concurrent opioid use.

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
TSPO ligands (e.g., emapunil/XBD173, PK11195 derivatives) have been investigated for anti-anxiety and anti-inflammatory effects in preclinical models. If TSPO elevation reflects mitochondrial dysfunction rather than pure inflammation (H2), TSPO ligands might modulate mitochondrial function and provide symptomatic benefit.

**Evidence Link:**

- Indirect: Heng 2025 shows mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS immune cells
- Indirect: Syed 2025 mitochondrial review supports targeting mitochondrial pathways

**Rationale for Use in ME/CFS:**
- Compounds: Emapunil (XBD173), emapunil derivatives, or investigational TSPO ligands
- Mechanism: Modulate TSPO function on mitochondrial outer membrane, potentially improving energy production
- Status: Investigational; not approved for clinical use; clinical trial required

**Certainty:** 0.20 (Highly speculative; TSPO ligands primarily preclinical)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. TSPO ligand treatment should improve mitochondrial function markers (ATP production, membrane potential) in PBMCs
2. Should improve symptom scores if mitochondrial dysfunction contributes to fatigue
3. Should not worsen inflammatory markers (TSPO modulation can be pro- or anti-inflammatory depending on context)
4. Neuroimaging (TSPO PET if available) should show reduced binding if treatment normalizes TSPO expression

**Safety Note:** TSPO ligands are investigational; full clinical trial required before any clinical use.

### M3: Anti-Inflammatory Agents to Improve BBB Transport

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
If BBB transport limitation masks neuroinflammation (H3), reducing peripheral inflammation should increase K1 (tracer brain influx rate), potentially improving symptom delivery of endogenous therapeutic factors in addition to aiding diagnostic detection. Anti-inflammatory agents that reduce CRP and cytokines might achieve this.

**Evidence Link:**
- Direct: Barzon 2026 shows CRP correlates with reduced K1
- Indirect: Hornig 2015, Montoya 2017 show cytokine elevation in ME/CFS subgroups
- Indirect: Heng 2025 shows vascular dysfunction biomarkers suggesting systemic inflammation

**Potential Agents:**
1. Low-dose prednisone (short-term, for acute flares)
2. Anti-IL-6 agents (e.g., tocilizumab) - experimental, high-risk
3. TNF-alpha inhibitors (e.g., etanercept) - experimental, off-label
4. Senolytics (dasatinib + quercetin) - address SASP-driven inflammation (see OQ in ch14k)

**Rationale for ME/CFS-Specific Approach:**
- Target: Patients with elevated CRP or cytokines (biomarker-stratified)
- Outcome: Improvement in K1 (if TSPO PET available), symptom scores, inflammatory markers
- Monitoring: Autoimmune markers, infection risk (if biologics used)

**Certainty:** 0.25 (Mechanistically plausible based on Barzon 2026; clinical application untested)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Anti-inflammatory treatment should increase K1 (tracer brain influx rate) when measured with TSPO PET
2. Patients with higher baseline CRP should show greater K1 improvement with treatment
3. Symptom improvement should correlate with K1 increase (suggesting BBB transport limitation was limiting factor)
4. Anti-inflammatory treatment without concomitant BBB permeability improvement should NOT improve central symptoms if BBB transport is primary limiting factor

**Safety Note:** Most anti-inflammatory agents carry significant risks; biologic agents require specialist prescribing.

## Category 4: Supplement and Nutraceutical Ideas

### S1: Curcumin as TSPO Modulator and Anti-Inflammatory

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
Curcumin has been shown to bind to TSPO and modulate its function in preclinical models, exhibiting anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective effects. It also reduces peripheral inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha), which could improve BBB transport (Barzon 2026 mechanism) and reduce systemic inflammation driving ME/CFS symptoms.

**Evidence Link:**
- Literature: Curcumin binds TSPO and exhibits anti-inflammatory effects in animal models
- Indirect: Barzon 2026 - reducing peripheral inflammation should improve BBB transport
- Indirect: Heng 2025 - vascular dysfunction and inflammation in ME/CFS

**Proposed Protocol:**
- Formulation: Bioavailable curcumin (e.g., Meriva, BCM-95) for enhanced absorption
- Dose: 500-1000 mg bioavailable curcumin daily
- Duration: 12-week trial
- Outcomes: Inflammatory markers, symptom scores, TSPO PET if available

**Certainty:** 0.40 (Mechanistically sound; human ME/CFS data limited)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Curcumin should reduce peripheral inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha)
2. Should improve symptom scores, particularly neurocognitive symptoms if neuroinflammation contributes
3. If TSPO PET available, should show increased K1 (improved BBB transport) due to reduced peripheral inflammation
4. Flow cytometry should show reduced monocyte activation (if whole-body TSPO PET also available)

**Safety Note:** Curcumin is generally well-tolerated; caution with blood thinners and gallbladder issues.

### S2: Omega-3 Fatty Acids for Neuroinflammation Modulation

**Mechanistic Rationale:**
EPA and DHA have well-established anti-inflammatory effects and can modulate microglial activation states. High-dose omega-3 supplementation may shift microglia from pro-inflammatory (M1) to anti-inflammatory (M2) phenotype, potentially reducing neuroinflammation if present, while also reducing peripheral inflammation and improving BBB transport.

**Evidence Link:**
- Literature: Omega-3s modulate microglial activation and reduce neuroinflammation in animal models
- Indirect: VanElzakker 2019 discusses neuroinflammation in ME/CFS
- Indirect: Heng 2025 shows immune dysfunction in ME/CFS

**Proposed Protocol:**
- Formulation: High-purity fish oil with EPA:DHA ratio ~2:1
- Dose: 2-4 grams EPA+DHA daily
- Duration: 12-24 week trial
- Outcomes: Inflammatory markers, symptom scales, omega-3 index (target >8%), TSPO PET if available

**Certainty:** 0.35 (Good safety profile; ME/CFS-specific evidence limited)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. High-dose omega-3 should increase omega-3 index and reduce inflammatory markers
2. Should improve symptom scores, particularly fatigue and cognitive symptoms
3. If neuroinflammation present, TSPO PET binding potential should decrease
4. Omega-3 index should correlate with symptom improvement (dose-response relationship)

**Safety Note:** Omega-3s are generally safe; caution with blood thinners at high doses.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

The vagus nerve mediates the cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway, reducing microglial activation and neuroinflammation. Non-invasive transcutaneous VNS (taVNS) or implanted VNS could reduce central inflammation in ME/CFS while also modulating peripheral immune function. This approach addresses both central and peripheral components of ME/CFS pathology.

**Evidence Link:**
- Document context: VNS mentioned in ch01-introduction for reducing neuroinflammation
- Literature: VNS reduces neuroinflammation in animal models and has shown benefit in depression and inflammatory conditions
- Indirect: VanElzakker 2019 discusses neuroinflammation in ME/CFS
- Indirect: Ch14f brainstorming discusses VNS for recovery

**Proposed Protocol:**
- Device: taVNS (transcutaneous auricular VNS) for accessibility
- Parameters: 20-30 Hz, 30 minutes daily
- Duration: 12-week trial
- Outcomes: Symptom scores, HRV (vagal tone marker), inflammatory biomarkers, TSPO PET if available

**Certainty:** 0.35 (Established safety; ME/CFS-specific neuroinflammation effect unproven)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. taVNS should increase HRV (RMSSD) indicating improved vagal tone
2. Should reduce peripheral inflammatory markers (IL-6, TNF-alpha)
3. Should improve symptom scores, particularly fatigue and cognitive symptoms
4. If neuroinflammation present, TSPO PET binding potential should decrease

**Safety Note:** taVNS is non-invasive with good safety profile; contraindicated with certain cardiac devices.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### M1: TSPO PET Kinetic Model with BBB Transport Parameter

**Extension to Existing Framework:**
The standard TSPO PET quantification uses binding potential (BPND) but may miss BBB transport effects. An extended model incorporating K1 (brain influx rate) as a separate parameter would allow distinguishing between (1) no TSPO expression, (2) TSPO present but poor delivery, (3) normal TSPO with normal delivery.

**Proposed Extension:**
Add K1 to the existing temporal evolution model as a state-dependent variable:
K1(t) = K1_base - alpha * inflammation(t)

Where:
- K1_base is baseline BBB transport in absence of inflammation
- alpha is sensitivity of BBB transport to inflammation
- inflammation(t) is inflammatory marker level at time t

This creates a dynamic model where BBB transport varies with inflammation state, potentially explaining variability in TSPO PET findings.

**Evidence Link:**
- Barzon 2026: Quantifies CRP-K1 relationship across 358 scans
- Document context: Temporal evolution model (ch31) already has state variables for damage, recovery
- Cross-reference: Ratchet model (ch31) shows damage accumulation with exertion

**Certainty:** 0.55 (Mathematically straightforward; requires empirical parameter estimation)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Fitting TSPO PET data with extended model should improve goodness-of-fit vs. standard model
2. Alpha parameter should be higher in ME/CFS than healthy controls (greater sensitivity of BBB to inflammation)
3. Model should predict K1 reduction during PEM flares (when inflammation peaks)
4. Adjusting BPND for K1 should reveal group differences not apparent in standard analysis

### M2: Neuroinflammation-PEM Coupling Model

**Extension to Existing Framework:**
Integrate neuroinflammation into the temporal evolution model of PEM. Neuroinflammation could act as both (1) amplification factor (worsening PEM severity when elevated) and (2) persistence factor (prolonging recovery when slow to resolve).

**Proposed Extension:**
Add neuroinflammation state N(t) to model:
dN/dt = gamma * damage(t) - delta * N(t)

Where:
- gamma is neuroinflammation activation rate from tissue damage
- delta is neuroinflammation resolution rate
- damage(t) is damage state from exertion

PEM severity S(t) becomes function of both damage and neuroinflammation:
S(t) = beta * damage(t) + epsilon * N(t)

Where beta and epsilon are weighting factors.

**Evidence Link:**
- Document context: Temporal evolution model (ch31) with damage, recovery variables
- Indirect: Keller 2024 - 13-day recovery suggests slow processes
- Indirect: Yu 2026 NII - white matter pathology correlates with clinical measures

**Certainty:** 0.45 (Conceptually plausible; requires empirical validation)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Model should capture observed PEM trajectories better than damage-only model
2. Patients with higher baseline neuroinflammation (if TSPO PET available) should have longer PEM duration (lower delta)
3. Neuroinflammation resolution rate (delta) should correlate with PEM recovery time
4. Treatments that reduce neuroinflammation should increase delta and shorten PEM

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### CD1: ME/CFS vs. Gulf War Illness: Divergent TSPO PET Patterns

**Rationale:**
Alshelh 2020 found WIDESPREAD cortical TSPO elevation in GWI, while Raijmakers 2021 found NO neuroinflammation in ME/CFS. This suggests distinct neuroinflammatory mechanisms despite symptom overlap. Understanding this difference may reveal disease-specific pathophysiology.

**Evidence Link:**
- Alshelh 2020 (GWI): Widespread cortical TSPO elevation, n=15
- Raijmakers 2021 (ME/CFS): No neuroinflammation, n=9
- Clinical overlap: Both conditions share fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, pain
- Trigger difference: GWI (toxic exposures, stress) vs. ME/CFS (infection, other triggers)

**Hypothesis:**
The different TSPO PET patterns reflect distinct pathophysiologies:
- GWI: Direct toxic injury to brain triggers microglial activation (neuroinflammation-dominant)
- ME/CFS: Peripheral pathology (immune, vascular) drives symptoms via non-neuroinflammatory mechanisms (peripheral-dominant)

**Certainty:** 0.50 (Based on direct comparative data; mechanisms inferred)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. GWI patients should show cortical TSPO elevation on replication studies
2. ME/CFS patients should show peripheral TSPO elevation (muscle, bone marrow) with normal/reduced brain signal on whole-body imaging
3. Biomarker profiles should differ: GWI shows stronger central markers, ME/CFS shows stronger peripheral markers
4. Treatment responses should differ: Neuroinflammation-targeted treatments (LDN, microglial modulators) more effective in GWI; peripheral-targeted treatments (immune modulators, vascular support) more effective in ME/CFS

### CD2: ME/CFS vs. Long COVID: Vascular vs. Neuroinflammatory Dominance

**Rationale:**
Long COVID research (McAlpine 2026) shows vascular dysfunction without overt coagulopathy, with vascular biomarkers correlating with cognitive symptoms. ME/CFS may share this vascular pathology pattern, but the extent of neuroinflammation may differ. TSPO PET comparison could distinguish the balance.

**Evidence Link:**
- McAlpine 2026: Vascular biomarkers correlate with cognitive symptoms in Long COVID
- Heng 2025: Vascular biomarkers in ME/CFS
- Symptom overlap: Fatigue, cognitive dysfunction ("brain fog"), post-exertional malaise
- Trigger: Both post-infectious (SARS-CoV-2 vs. various viruses in ME/CFS)

**Hypothesis:**
Both ME/CFS and Long COVID involve vascular dysfunction, but:
- Long COVID: Primarily vascular with minimal neuroinflammation (brain fog driven by hypoperfusion, not microglial activation)
- ME/CFS: Vascular dysfunction PLUS potential neuroinflammation in subsets

**Certainty:** 0.45 (Vascular overlap established; neuroinflammation difference speculative)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Both ME/CFS and Long COVID should show vascular dysfunction biomarkers (sP-selectin, sICAM-1, etc.)
2. Long COVID should show minimal TSPO PET signal elevation (vascular-dominant)
3. ME/CFS should show more variable TSPO PET patterns (some elevated, some not, reflecting heterogeneity)
4. Treatment responses should differ: Vascular-targeted treatments (endothelial support) beneficial in both; neuroinflammation-targeted treatments beneficial only in ME/CFS subsets with elevated TSPO

## Category 9: Diagnostic and Biomarker Ideas

### B1: Multi-Tracer Imaging Strategy: TSPO + Perfusion + Metabolism

**Rationale:**
Single-modality imaging (e.g., TSPO PET alone) provides limited information. A multi-tracer approach could distinguish (1) neuroinflammation (TSPO PET), (2) hypoperfusion (perfusion PET or arterial spin labeling MRI), (3) metabolic dysfunction (FDG-PET). This integrated approach would provide comprehensive picture of CNS pathology in ME/CFS.

**Proposed Protocol:**
- Tracer 1: TSPO PET (PBR28, DPA-713, or LW223) for neuroinflammation
- Tracer 2: Perfusion measurement (ASL-MRI or O-15 water PET) for blood flow
- Tracer 3: FDG-PET for glucose metabolism
- Integration: Coregistered imaging to correlate patterns across modalities

**Evidence Link:**
- Document context: Walitt 2024 NIH deep phenotyping used multiple modalities
- Indirect: Van Campen 2020-2024 shows CBF abnormalities
- Indirect: Chaudhuri 2004 shows PET hypometabolism in ME/CFS

**Certainty:** 0.60 (Methodologically sound; resource-intensive)

**Falsifiable Predictions:**
1. Different ME/CFS subtypes should show distinct imaging patterns:
   - Type A: Elevated TSPO (neuroinflammation-dominant)
   - Type B: Reduced perfusion (vascular-dominant)
   - Type C: Hypometabolism (mitochondrial-dominant)
   - Type D: Mixed pattern
2. Imaging subtypes should correlate with clinical presentations and treatment responses
3. Multi-tracer approach should have higher diagnostic accuracy than any single tracer
4. Treatment responses should align with dominant pathology (e.g., LDN benefits Type A, vascular treatments benefit Type B)

**Clinical Translation Potential:**
This approach could enable precision medicine in ME/CFS by identifying dominant pathology in individual patients and guiding targeted treatment selection.

## Caveats and Limitations

1. **Michelle James Study Status Unknown:** The unpublished Stanford study mentioned in briefing is NOT in PubMed as of 2026-05-07. Its status (conference abstract, preprint, published elsewhere) must be verified before incorporating findings as evidence.

2. **Single ME/CFS TSPO PET Study:** Only Raijmakers 2021 (n=9) is published. All inferences are based on this single study plus cross-disease extrapolation (GWI, AD, MS).

3. **BBB Transport Confounding:** Barzon 2026 finding (peripheral inflammation reduces TSPO tracer brain influx) was in depression and schizophrenia patients, not ME/CFS. Requires validation in ME/CFS cohort.

4. **Tracer Limitations:** First-generation tracers (PK11195) have poor signal-to-noise; second-generation tracers (PBR28, DPA-714) have polymorphism issues; third-generation tracers (LW223) are new and expensive.

5. **Sample Size Limitations:** All cited studies have small to moderate samples (n=9-358). Large-scale replication is needed.

6. **Treatment Suggestions are Research-Stage:** All medication, supplement, and non-pharmacological interventions are research-stage and require clinical trials. NOT clinical recommendations for individual patients.

7. **Cross-Disease Extrapolation:** Many ideas import from related conditions (GWI, AD, MS, Long COVID). ME/CFS-specific mechanisms may differ.

8. **TSPO Biology Complexity:** TSPO has dual roles (inflammation marker + mitochondrial function regulator). Interpretation of TSPO PET signal is inherently ambiguous.
