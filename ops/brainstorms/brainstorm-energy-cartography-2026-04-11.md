## Certainty legend

- **0.70-0.85** Strong mechanistic + multi-study support, replication present
- **0.50-0.69** Plausible mechanism + single-study support or strong indirect evidence

## PART A: NOVEL HYPOTHESES

### H1. The "FAD bottleneck" hypothesis (Riboflavin as master rate-limiter)

**Rationale.** FAD is a covalent prosthetic group or obligate cofactor at >=8 energy-chain steps: PDC (E3 lipoamide dehydrogenase), KGDHC (E3, shared), branched-chain alpha-ketoacid DH, every acyl-CoA dehydrogenase in beta-oxidation (VLCAD, LCAD, MCAD, SCAD), ETF and ETF-QO that shuttle beta-oxidation electrons to Complex III, and succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II) itself. A single cofactor thus gates both the carbohydrate-to-acetyl-CoA arm AND the fatty-acid-to-acetyl-CoA arm AND one direct ETC entry point. Marginal riboflavin status (common in Western diets, worsened by GI malabsorption, mast cell activation, bile acid dysbiosis) would produce a metabolomic signature nearly indistinguishable from PDK overactivation plus beta-oxidation failure plus Complex II dysfunction — exactly what Fluge 2016, Armstrong 2015, and Tomas 2020 report.

**Evidence link.** FAD dependency is textbook biochemistry. Riboflavin-responsive Complex II / MADD / ETF-QO disease phenocopies ME/CFS exercise intolerance. No formal riboflavin status study in ME/CFS beyond small observational cohorts.

**Certainty.** 0.42 (plausible, untested, strong mechanistic case).

**Falsifiable prediction.** In a cohort of 100 ME/CFS patients: (a) erythrocyte glutathione reductase activation coefficient (EGRAC, the gold-standard riboflavin status biomarker) will be >=1.3 in >=30% of patients vs <10% of age/sex-matched controls; (b) urinary acylcarnitine profile will show elevated C4-C18 acylcarnitines (the MADD/ETF-QO signature) in EGRAC-high patients; (c) 400 mg/day riboflavin for 12 weeks will normalize EGRAC and reduce plasma lactate/pyruvate ratio in responders. If EGRAC is normal in the majority, the hypothesis is refuted as a primary driver.

### H2. The ISCU bottleneck hypothesis (upstream-most treatable failure)

**Rationale.** ISCU (the iron-sulfur cluster scaffold protein) is required for assembly of Fe-S clusters in: Complex I (8 clusters), Complex II (3 clusters), Complex III (1 cluster), aconitase (1 cluster), ETF-QO (1 cluster), ferrochelatase, xanthine oxidase, and DNA repair enzymes. A single upstream failure at ISCU or the ISC machinery (NFS1, FXN/frataxin, HSC20, HSCB) would produce simultaneous Complex I/II/aconitase/ETF-QO deficit — which is exactly the pattern reported in ME/CFS muscle biopsies. ISCU myopathy (OMIM 255125) causes exercise intolerance, myoglobinuria, and lactic acidosis on exertion — indistinguishable symptom-wise from severe ME/CFS with PEM. Frataxin loss (Friedreich's ataxia) causes fatigue and cardiomyopathy via the same pathway.

**Evidence link.** ISCU has never been measured in ME/CFS. Morris & Maes 2014 list it as "needs study." Friedreich's ataxia and ISCU myopathy provide disease-precedent.

**Certainty.** 0.35 (creative, disease-precedent, untested).

**Falsifiable prediction.** Aconitase/fumarase activity ratio (a classic Fe-S cluster biomarker — aconitase is Fe-S-dependent, fumarase is not) will be <0.6 in severe ME/CFS muscle biopsies vs ~1.0 in controls. Serum ISCU mRNA in PBMCs will be reduced. Frataxin levels will be low-normal. If aconitase/fumarase ratio is normal, Fe-S cluster assembly is intact and ISCU is not the upstream failure.

### H3. ANT autoantibody hypothesis: "stealth energy starvation"

**Rationale.** ANT (SLC25A4/5/6) is the most abundant inner membrane protein and the obligate exporter of matrix ATP. Autoantibodies against ANT have been documented in dilated cardiomyopathy, Chagas disease, and hepatitis C — they block ATP/ADP exchange without affecting oxygen consumption measured in permeabilized fibers (because oxygen can still be consumed driving proton pumping; the ATP just cannot leave the matrix). Luschnat 2025 found autoantibodies against multiple mitochondrial proteins in ME/CFS. If ANT is a target, the patient would show: normal Seahorse OCR (misleadingly), elevated matrix ATP, depleted cytosolic ATP, secondary glycolytic upregulation (via AMPK), and PEM-like exertion intolerance. This would be invisible to every standard mitochondrial assay that measures oxygen consumption.

**Evidence link.** Anti-ANT antibodies documented in DCM/Chagas. Multi-target mitochondrial autoantibodies found in ME/CFS (Luschnat 2025). Direct ANT-antibody test in ME/CFS: never done.

**Certainty.** 0.30 (specific, testable, no direct evidence yet).

**Falsifiable prediction.** In ME/CFS serum: (a) ELISA against recombinant ANT1 will show higher titers than matched controls; (b) IgG purified from ANT-positive ME/CFS patients will inhibit ATP/ADP exchange in isolated heart mitochondria in vitro; (c) cytosolic/matrix ATP ratio (measured by FRET biosensors in fibroblasts cultured with patient IgG) will be reduced; (d) patient fibroblasts will show normal OCR but impaired ECAR-to-ATP coupling. Negative ELISA refutes the hypothesis.

### H4. The "PDK as a thrift program" reinterpretation

**Rationale.** PDK upregulation is usually framed as a pathological lesion. Alternative reinterpretation: PDK upregulation is a *protective* response to a downstream lesion — the cell is restricting pyruvate entry into the TCA because downstream ROS handling (PRDX3/MnSOD/GPX4) is compromised. Pushing more substrate into a leaky ETC would generate more superoxide. PDK is the cellular "dimmer switch" preventing catastrophic ROS. If true, inhibiting PDK alone (dichloroacetate, DCA) without simultaneously repairing the antioxidant system would *worsen* ME/CFS by flooding a vulnerable ETC. This predicts DCA monotherapy fails; DCA + NAC + CoQ10 + riboflavin succeeds.

**Evidence link.** Shankar 2025 (PNAS) — MnSOD decreased. PRDX3 not measured. DCA never formally trialled in ME/CFS at scale.

**Certainty.** 0.45 (explains several puzzles simultaneously; strong theoretical case).

**Falsifiable prediction.** In a pilot DCA trial: patients with *low* baseline MnSOD and GPX4 will worsen (more PEM, higher F2-isoprostanes); patients with *normal* antioxidant status will improve (lactate/pyruvate normalization, reduced PEM). If DCA effect is uncorrelated with baseline antioxidant status, the hypothesis is wrong.

### H5. The cardiolipin peroxidation cascade hypothesis

**Rationale.** Cardiolipin (CL) is the only phospholipid required for Complex I, III, IV, ANT, and PiC structural stability. Oxidized CL (CLox) triggers cytochrome c release, inflammasome activation, and Complex I disassembly. CL is uniquely vulnerable because (a) it has 4 PUFA chains (vs 2 for normal phospholipids) and (b) it sits directly adjacent to the ETC superoxide generators. If MnSOD is decreased (Shankar 2025) and PRDX3 is impaired, CL peroxidation would be rampant. Elamipretide (SS-31) selectively binds CL and prevents peroxidation — it is approved/trialled in Barth syndrome and primary mitochondrial myopathy. Predicts: serum CLox is elevated in ME/CFS; elamipretide reverses PEM in responders.

**Evidence link.** Molnar et al. highlight elamipretide for ME/CFS theoretical rationale. Never formally trialled. Barth syndrome / MELAS trials show exercise improvement.

**Certainty.** 0.40 (strong mechanistic, disease-precedent, ready for trial).

**Falsifiable prediction.** Serum oxidized cardiolipin (LC-MS) will be >2x control in moderate-severe ME/CFS. A 12-week open-label elamipretide trial will produce: reduced PEM frequency, improved 6-minute walk distance, reduced plasma 4-HNE and F2-isoprostanes in responders. Non-response with normal baseline CLox refutes.

### H6. Malonyl-CoA/CPT1 lock: the "fatty acid blockade" hypothesis

**Rationale.** Substrate switching between carbohydrate and fat oxidation is governed by the AMPK -> ACC -> malonyl-CoA -> CPT1 axis. High malonyl-CoA blocks CPT1 and prevents fatty acid entry into mitochondria. If PDC is blocked by PDK overactivation AND CPT1 is blocked by high malonyl-CoA, the mitochondrion is substrate-starved from both sides. The cell must then rely on glutamine/BCAA anaplerosis — exactly the "amino acid cannibalism" signature Fluge 2016 reported. Nobody has measured malonyl-CoA or ACC phosphorylation in ME/CFS. AMPK itself is downregulated by chronic inflammation and glucocorticoid imbalance.

**Evidence link.** Fluge 2016 amino acid cannibalism. AMPK-ACC-CPT1 axis is textbook. Never measured in ME/CFS.

**Certainty.** 0.35.

**Falsifiable prediction.** Plasma malonyl-CoA (or surrogate: acetylcarnitine C2 / palmitoylcarnitine C16 ratio) will be elevated in ME/CFS. Phospho-ACC/total-ACC ratio in PBMC lysate will be decreased. CPT1 activity in muscle biopsy will be normal (enzyme fine, just inhibited). Activation of AMPK with metformin (mild) or ZLN024 (specific) should de-repress CPT1 and improve fat oxidation — testable via stable-isotope palmitate tracer.

### H7. "Functional riboflavin deficiency from BCRP/ABCG2 transporter downregulation"

**Rationale.** Riboflavin uptake at the gut and transport into mitochondria requires SLC52A1-3 (riboflavin transporters) and the ABC transporter ABCG2 (BCRP). Chronic inflammation and TNF-alpha downregulate ABCG2. Some ME/CFS patients may have *adequate dietary riboflavin* but fail to load mitochondria — appearing normal on serum B2 but deficient at the site of action. This would explain why standard 10-20 mg B2 supplementation fails while high-dose (400 mg) sometimes works (mass-action override).

**Certainty.** 0.25.

**Falsifiable prediction.** PBMC mitochondrial FAD content (HPLC) will be lower than serum riboflavin predicts in ME/CFS. SLC52A2 mRNA will be reduced. Only high-dose (>=200 mg) riboflavin will normalize intramitochondrial FAD.

### H8. PGC-1alpha "locked-off" state hypothesis

**Rationale.** PGC-1alpha activation requires deacetylation by SIRT1 (NAD+-dependent) and phosphorylation by AMPK. ME/CFS shows: reduced NAD+ (documented), suppressed AMPK (hypothesized), chronic glucocorticoid dysregulation (documented), and elevated TNF-alpha (documented in subgroups). All four converge on *blocking* PGC-1alpha activation. The cell cannot build new mitochondria even when it needs to. Standard exercise rehabilitation fails because exercise normally works via AMPK -> PGC-1alpha -> biogenesis, and that pathway is broken upstream. This predicts: nicotinamide riboside + AMPK activator + exercise mimetic (together) is required to unlock biogenesis.

**Certainty.** 0.45 (convergent, explains graded exercise failure).

**Falsifiable prediction.** PGC-1alpha protein in muscle biopsy will be <60% control. Post-exercise biopsy in ME/CFS will show BLUNTED PGC-1alpha induction (normally 3-5x) vs controls. NR + metformin + very gentle exercise protocol will restore post-exercise PGC-1alpha induction measurable in 8 weeks.

### H9. "PEM = delayed ISR activation from misfolded ETC proteins"

**Rationale.** The integrated stress response (ISR) is activated by OMA1/DELE1/HRI when inner-membrane proteostasis fails. ISR activation takes 24-72 hours and drives ATF4-mediated metabolic reprogramming including transient shutdown of protein synthesis, amino acid reprioritization, and the mtUPR. The characteristic 24-48 hour delay of PEM matches the ISR kinetics. If exertion causes mitochondrial proteostasis stress (CL oxidation, Fe-S cluster damage), the ISR would produce: delayed-onset fatigue, muscle pain (from amino acid reprioritization), cognitive dysfunction (from ATF4-driven 1C metabolism in brain), and recovery over 1-3 days matching ISR attenuation kinetics.

**Evidence link.** ISR/mtUPR is documented in mitochondrial diseases. OMA1-DELE1-HRI axis is 2020+ science. Never studied in ME/CFS.

**Certainty.** 0.40 (novel, explains PEM timing).

**Falsifiable prediction.** Plasma GDF15 and FGF21 (canonical ISR/mtUPR biomarkers) will rise 24-48 h after exertion in ME/CFS, peaking with PEM symptoms, not during or immediately after exercise. ATF4 target genes (CHAC1, ASNS, DDIT3) will be upregulated in PBMC during PEM. ISRIB (an ISR inhibitor, used experimentally) should prevent or attenuate PEM if this is correct. Absent GDF15 rise refutes.

---

### H10. Thiamine functional deficiency via impaired TPP conversion

**Rationale.** Thiamine is clinically normal in many ME/CFS patients, yet PDC, KGDHC, transketolase, and BCKDH all require thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) — the active form. Conversion thiamine -> TPP requires thiamine pyrophosphokinase (TPK1), which is Mg2+-dependent and inhibited by oxidative stress. Low intracellular Mg2+ (common in ME/CFS) plus oxidative stress may produce *functional* TPP deficiency despite normal serum thiamine — explaining anecdotal responses to high-dose thiamine or benfotiamine (lipid-soluble, bypasses transport limitations).

**Certainty.** 0.40.

**Falsifiable prediction.** Erythrocyte transketolase activation coefficient (ETKAC) will be >1.25 in >=25% of ME/CFS vs <5% controls, even when serum thiamine is normal. Response to benfotiamine (300 mg/day, 12 weeks) will correlate with baseline ETKAC, not with serum thiamine.

## PART B: RESEARCH DIRECTIONS

### R1. Multi-cofactor status screen ("the B-vitamin functional panel")

Run in 200 ME/CFS + 200 matched controls: EGRAC (riboflavin), ETKAC (thiamine), methylmalonic acid (B12), homocysteine (folate/B6/B12), plasma pyridoxal-5-phosphate (B6 active), whole-blood NAD+, RBC magnesium, intracellular lipoic acid (rare assay). Cost: ~200 EUR/patient. Would identify *which* cofactor deficits are real vs hypothetical. **Priority: HIGHEST** (cheap, feasible, directly actionable).

### R2. Aconitase/fumarase activity ratio as a field test for Fe-S cluster health

Muscle biopsy or PBMC lysate. Aconitase is Fe-S dependent and redox-labile; fumarase is not. Ratio <0.6 indicates Fe-S/ROS damage. Requires only spectrophotometer. Screens ISCU hypothesis rapidly.

### R3. Stable isotope substrate oxidation profiling

Administer 13C-glucose, 13C-palmitate, 13C-leucine on separate days; measure 13CO2 exhalation kinetics and plasma 13C-labeled TCA intermediates. Directly quantifies the PDC block, beta-oxidation capacity, and BCAA cannibalism. Gold standard for substrate flux. Expensive but definitive.

### R4. Post-exertion ISR biomarker time course

GDF15, FGF21, ATF4 target genes at t=0, 6, 12, 24, 48, 72 h after a standardized CPET. Matches PEM timing to molecular kinetics. Cheap (ELISA).

### R5. Direct mitochondrial autoantibody panel

Indirect immunofluorescence on isolated mitochondria + western blot against: ANT1/4, VDAC1/2, PiC, UCP2/3, PDC-E2, KGDHC-E2, ATP synthase alpha/beta. Follow up positives with functional inhibition assays (ATP/ADP exchange, oxygen uptake).

### R6. ISRIB + riboflavin + elamipretide small-molecule rescue trial in ME/CFS patient fibroblasts

Before any clinical trial: culture patient fibroblasts under galactose (forces oxidative metabolism), measure OCR/ECAR/growth. Rescue with each molecule alone and in combination. Identifies responders by ex vivo phenotype.

### R7. Mitochondrial proteomics of PEM (pre/post exertion muscle biopsy)

Gold-standard n=10 study: biopsy at baseline and 24 h post-CPET; TMT-labeled quantitative proteomics of mitochondrial fraction; look for changes in CL remodeling enzymes (tafazzin), Fe-S assembly proteins, PGC-1alpha targets, ISR/mtUPR markers, antioxidant enzymes.

### R8. Cardiolipinomics by LC-MS

CL species and oxidized CL in plasma, PBMC, and (ideally) muscle. ME/CFS vs controls vs Long COVID vs Barth syndrome (positive control).

### R9. Mendelian randomization study using UKBiobank

Use SNPs in ACADVL, ETFDH, ISCU, SLC52A2, SLC25A4 (ANT1), PGC-1alpha as instrumental variables for fatigue phenotype and ME/CFS diagnosis. Cheap, uses existing data. Could triage which of H1-H10 are prioritized.

### R10. PDK inhibitor dose-ranging trial (DCA) stratified by baseline antioxidant status

Small n=40: stratify by baseline MnSOD + GPX4 + F2-isoprostanes. Randomize within strata to DCA vs DCA+NAC+CoQ10 vs placebo. Tests H4.

## PART C: PHARMACOLOGICAL (REPURPOSED) INTERVENTIONS

### Rx1. Dichloroacetate (DCA) -- PDK inhibitor
- **Mechanism:** Inhibits PDK, restores PDH flux, reverses pyruvate->lactate shunt.
- **Precedent:** Used in PDH deficiency, MELAS, congenital lactic acidosis, brain cancer trials.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** Direct reversal of Fluge 2016 finding.
- **Risk:** Reversible peripheral neuropathy (dose-dependent), hepatotoxicity.
- **Certainty it helps:** 0.35. Caveat: may fail or worsen if H4 is right.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 12.5 mg/kg/day DCA for 12 weeks will reduce lactate/pyruvate ratio in all patients but clinical improvement (fatigue score) will stratify by baseline antioxidant status.

### Rx2. Elamipretide (SS-31, bendavia) -- cardiolipin stabilizer
- **Mechanism:** Binds CL, prevents peroxidation, restores cristae architecture.
- **Precedent:** Phase 3 in Barth syndrome (TAZPOWER), primary mitochondrial myopathy, dry AMD.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** H5 (cardiolipin peroxidation cascade).
- **Risk:** Injection-site reactions, rare hypersensitivity.
- **Certainty:** 0.40.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 40 mg/day subcutaneous for 12 weeks reduces F2-isoprostanes, improves 6MWT >30 m in responders with elevated baseline CLox.

### Rx3. Metformin (low dose, 500 mg bid) -- AMPK activator
- **Mechanism:** Mild Complex I inhibition -> AMP rise -> AMPK activation -> PGC-1alpha -> biogenesis. Also reduces malonyl-CoA -> CPT1 disinhibition.
- **Precedent:** Anti-aging trials (TAME), Long COVID trial positive (Bramante 2023).
- **ME/CFS rationale:** H6, H8.
- **Risk:** GI intolerance, B12 depletion (monitor), rare lactic acidosis — CAUTION in already-high-lactate patients.
- **Certainty:** 0.45.
- **Safety flag:** Start very low (250 mg); monitor lactate; contraindicated if renal function impaired. H4 concern: AMPK activation + ETC inhibition could worsen PEM in some.

### Rx4. Sulforaphane / DMF (dimethyl fumarate) -- NRF2 activators
- **Mechanism:** Activate NRF2 -> induces GCLC, NQO1, HO-1, antioxidant enzymes, PRDX3.
- **Precedent:** DMF approved for MS, psoriasis. Sulforaphane in autism, schizophrenia trials.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** Address the NRF2 gap directly.
- **Risk:** DMF: lymphopenia, PML (rare). Sulforaphane: GI.
- **Certainty:** 0.40.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** DMF 240 mg bid for 12 weeks -- urinary 8-OHdG and F2-isoprostanes will fall >30%; PGC-1alpha mRNA in PBMCs will rise; subset with high baseline oxidative stress will show fatigue improvement.

### Rx5. Idebenone -- CoQ10 analog
- **Mechanism:** Short-chain synthetic CoQ10, crosses BBB better than CoQ10.
- **Precedent:** Approved in Europe for LHON. Trialled in Friedreich's ataxia (mixed).
- **ME/CFS rationale:** Brain energy metabolism deficit; CoQ10 deficit documented.
- **Risk:** GI, rare hepatic.
- **Certainty:** 0.35.

### Rx6. Low-dose rapamycin (sirolimus) -- mTOR inhibitor
- **Mechanism:** Induces autophagy/mitophagy, clears damaged mitochondria, allows biogenesis.
- **Precedent:** Anti-aging trials, ongoing Long COVID interest.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** If damaged mitochondria accumulate (H8), mitophagy induction could help.
- **Risk:** Immunosuppression, wound healing, mouth ulcers. Weekly low-dose may mitigate.
- **Certainty:** 0.25. Caveat: could worsen infection susceptibility.

### Rx7. Bezafibrate -- PPAR pan-agonist, PGC-1alpha inducer
- **Mechanism:** PPAR-alpha/gamma/delta agonism induces PGC-1alpha, fatty acid oxidation enzymes, and biogenesis.
- **Precedent:** Trialled in CPT2 deficiency, VLCAD deficiency, mitochondrial myopathies.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** H6, H8.
- **Risk:** Rhabdomyolysis especially with statins, hepatotoxicity.
- **Certainty:** 0.30.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 400 mg/day 12 weeks raises post-exercise PGC-1alpha induction; improves fat-oxidation capacity by indirect calorimetry.

### Rx8. Mitoquinone (MitoQ) -- mitochondria-targeted ubiquinone
- **Mechanism:** CoQ10 tethered to triphenylphosphonium cation; concentrates in matrix.
- **Precedent:** Trials in PD, MS, CKD, aging.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** If CoQ10 deficit is real, MitoQ delivers it to the correct compartment.
- **Certainty:** 0.35.

### Rx9. ISRIB -- integrated stress response inhibitor
- **Mechanism:** Small-molecule inhibitor of eIF2alpha-P downstream signaling, blocks ISR.
- **Precedent:** Preclinical only. TBI, Down syndrome cognition.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** H9 (PEM as ISR activation).
- **Risk:** UNKNOWN in humans long-term. Would require careful phase 1.
- **Certainty:** 0.20. Experimental only.

### Rx10. Tafamidis / pyridoxine for TTR stabilization -- not relevant, skip.

### Rx11. Deferiprone -- iron chelator (for Fe-S cluster paradox)
- **Mechanism:** In Friedreich's, labile iron accumulates while Fe-S clusters are deficient; chelators redistribute. Deferiprone has mitochondrial access.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** If H2 (ISCU) is right, mitochondrial iron redistribution may help.
- **Risk:** Neutropenia, agranulocytosis.
- **Certainty:** 0.15. Research only.

### Rx12. Omaveloxolone -- NRF2 activator (approved for Friedreich's ataxia, 2023)
- **Mechanism:** Potent NRF2 activator, approved for Friedreich's.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** Friedreich's shares the Fe-S/ROS axis with hypothesized ME/CFS mechanism.
- **Certainty:** 0.35. Interesting cross-disease bridge.

## PART D: SUPPLEMENTS AND NUTRACEUTICALS

### S1. Riboflavin high-dose (400 mg/day)
- **Evidence precedent:** Proven in MADD (riboflavin-responsive ETF-QO deficiency), migraine prophylaxis, Complex II deficiency.
- **Safety:** Excellent; orange urine, no known toxicity.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** H1. Single most underexplored high-impact intervention.
- **Certainty of benefit:** 0.40.
- **Actionability:** HIGH. Cheap, OTC, 12-week trial is feasible today.
- **Responder prediction:** Elevated C4-C18 acylcarnitines, EGRAC >1.3, history of exercise intolerance since youth.

### S2. Benfotiamine (300-600 mg/day) -- lipid-soluble thiamine
- **Precedent:** Diabetic neuropathy trials, PDH deficiency.
- **Safety:** Excellent.
- **Rationale:** H10. Bypasses transport.
- **Certainty:** 0.35.

### S3. R-alpha-lipoic acid (600 mg/day)
- **Mechanism:** Essential lipoamide cofactor for PDC-E2, KGDHC-E2, BCKDH-E2; potent antioxidant; regenerates glutathione and CoQ10.
- **Precedent:** Diabetic neuropathy (approved in Germany), mitochondrial myopathies.
- **Rationale:** All four cofactor-dependent dehydrogenases converge on lipoate.
- **Certainty:** 0.45.

### S4. Nicotinamide riboside (NR) or NMN (300-1000 mg/day)
- **Mechanism:** NAD+ precursor. Fuels SIRT1, SIRT3, PARPs.
- **Precedent:** Multiple trials in aging, cardiovascular, ME/CFS pilot (Guarneri 2019).
- **Rationale:** NAD+ deficit documented; unlocks PGC-1alpha via SIRT1.
- **Certainty:** 0.40.
- **Combine with:** exercise mimetic or very gentle movement for biogenesis.

### S5. Creatine monohydrate (3-5 g/day)
- **Evidence:** Direct ME/CFS feasibility study positive (cited in chapter); brain creatine deficit documented.
- **Safety:** Excellent; most-studied sports supplement.
- **Certainty:** 0.55. (Highest of the supplements list, already has ME/CFS data.)
- **Actionability:** IMMEDIATE.

### S6. Coenzyme Q10 ubiquinol (200-400 mg/day)
- **Evidence:** Deficit documented in ME/CFS. Several small positive trials.
- **Safety:** Excellent.
- **Certainty:** 0.50.
- **Caveat:** Ubiquinol > ubiquinone absorption; still poor brain penetration — consider MitoQ for CNS.

### S7. Magnesium glycinate / malate (300-400 mg elemental)
- **Mechanism:** Cofactor for ~300 enzymes including TPK1 (thiamine activation), ATP synthase (ATP-Mg complex), creatine kinase.
- **Precedent:** Small ME/CFS trial of Mg sulfate IM (Cox 1991, positive).
- **Certainty:** 0.45.

### S8. L-carnitine / acetyl-L-carnitine (1-3 g/day)
- **Evidence:** Carnitine deficiency documented in ME/CFS; several small positive trials.
- **Certainty:** 0.45.
- **Caveat:** TMAO concern — use sparingly or with garlic/fish oil context.

### S9. PQQ (pyrroloquinoline quinone, 10-20 mg/day)
- **Mechanism:** PGC-1alpha induction (mouse data); mitochondrial biogenesis.
- **Precedent:** Small human biogenesis studies.
- **Certainty:** 0.20. Weak.

### S10. Sulforaphane (from broccoli sprouts, or BroccoMax 100 mg glucoraphanin)
- **Mechanism:** NRF2 activation.
- **Certainty:** 0.35. Food-as-medicine pathway.

### S11. Methylene blue (1-4 mg/kg, low dose)
- **Mechanism:** Alternative electron acceptor bypassing Complex I/III; accepts electrons from NADH and donates to cytochrome c.
- **Precedent:** Used in methemoglobinemia, ifosfamide encephalopathy, some cognitive studies.
- **Rationale:** Bypasses Complex I/III defects entirely.
- **Safety:** Serotonin syndrome with SSRIs (CONTRAINDICATED with SSRIs!), G6PD hemolysis.
- **Certainty:** 0.30. Interesting but risk-flagged.

### S12. Urolithin A (Mitopure, 500-1000 mg/day)
- **Mechanism:** Induces mitophagy via selective mitochondrial turnover.
- **Precedent:** Muscle function trials in older adults positive.
- **Rationale:** Clear damaged mitochondria to allow biogenesis.
- **Certainty:** 0.25.

### S13. Citrulline malate (6-8 g pre-activity)
- **Mechanism:** Arginine/NO precursor; supports PGC-1alpha signaling via NO; ammonia buffer.
- **Precedent:** Fibromyalgia and exercise trials.
- **Certainty:** 0.30.

### S14. Oxaloacetate (benaGene, 100-500 mg/day)
- **Mechanism:** Direct TCA intermediate; may bypass aconitase step.
- **Precedent:** Small ME/CFS open-label trial (Cash 2022) -- positive signal.
- **Rationale:** If aconitase is Fe-S-damaged (H2), provide downstream TCA substrate.
- **Certainty:** 0.35.

### S15. D-ribose (5 g tid) -- classically marketed for ME/CFS
- **Mechanism:** Bypasses pentose phosphate rate-limit for ATP salvage.
- **Evidence:** One positive open-label trial, never RCT-replicated.
- **Certainty:** 0.20.

## PART E: NON-PHARMACOLOGICAL INTERVENTIONS

### N1. Ketogenic diet (therapeutic ketosis)
- **Mechanism:** Ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) are converted to acetyl-CoA by BDH1 and SCOT, **bypassing PDC entirely**. Directly fuels the TCA cycle without requiring pyruvate -> acetyl-CoA conversion. This is the most rational intervention if PDK overactivation is the primary lesion.
- **Precedent:** Proven in pyruvate dehydrogenase deficiency (PDHD) as standard of care. Effective in GLUT1 deficiency. Trialled in mitochondrial disease with mixed results.
- **ME/CFS rationale:** Directly bypasses the Fluge 2016 lesion.
- **Risk:** PEM may worsen transiently during keto-adaptation (2-4 weeks); requires careful caloric intake; electrolyte needs.
- **Certainty:** 0.50. Highest conviction non-pharma intervention.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Exogenous ketone esters (BHB salts, 10-20 g pre-activity) will acutely improve exercise tolerance in ME/CFS patients with elevated lactate/pyruvate, without requiring dietary adaptation. If exogenous BHB acutely helps but keto diet long-term harms, this exposes a PEM-vs-fuel trade-off.

### N2. Exogenous ketone esters as acute PEM prophylaxis
- **Mechanism:** Same as N1 but without chronic diet.
- **Protocol:** 10-20 g ketone ester 30 min before any predictable exertion.
- **Evidence precedent:** Elite endurance athletes; Alzheimer's cognition trials.
- **Certainty:** 0.40.

### N3. Pacing by heart-rate ceiling (anaerobic threshold-1)
- **Mechanism:** Keep exertion below the lactate inflection point — avoids forcing PDC-blocked cells into further lactate production.
- **Precedent:** Workwell Foundation, Bateman-Horne protocol.
- **Certainty:** 0.70 (established).

### N4. Heat acclimation (mild sauna, 30-40 C, 15-20 min)
- **Mechanism:** Heat shock protein induction including HSP60, HSP70 -- mitochondrial proteostasis protection; may activate mtUPR adaptively.
- **Risk:** Orthostatic intolerance (common in ME/CFS) worsened by heat. Must be done carefully; infrared may be better tolerated.
- **Certainty:** 0.25.

### N5. Cold exposure / norepinephrine-driven UCP activation
- **Mechanism:** Cold activates UCP1/3, norepinephrine surges -- may train mitochondrial biogenesis via PGC-1alpha.
- **Risk:** POTS/autonomic flare.
- **Certainty:** 0.20.

### N6. Hyperbaric oxygen (1.5-2.5 ATA)
- **Mechanism:** Dissolved oxygen rises, may push past diffusion-limited mitochondrial uptake; NRF2 activation.
- **Precedent:** Long COVID trials (Zilberman-Itskovich 2022 positive); fibromyalgia.
- **Certainty:** 0.30.

### N7. Photobiomodulation (red/near-infrared, 630-810 nm)
- **Mechanism:** Cytochrome c oxidase (Complex IV) absorbs at 810 nm, may accelerate electron transport; modulates NO.
- **Precedent:** Long COVID pilot trials.
- **Certainty:** 0.25.

### N8. Intermittent fasting (12-16 h overnight, NOT prolonged)
- **Mechanism:** AMPK activation + autophagy/mitophagy + mild ketosis.
- **Risk:** Hypoglycemia sensitivity in ME/CFS; PEM from caloric deficit.
- **Certainty:** 0.25. Individualize.

### N9. Nutrient timing: protein + creatine + carnitine pre-exertion, glucose + ketones intra-exertion
- **Mechanism:** Provide every substrate arm simultaneously; load cofactors before demand.
- **Certainty:** 0.35 empirical/logical.

## PART F: COMBINATION PROTOCOLS

### C1. "Cofactor saturation" protocol (low-risk, ready-to-trial)
**Components:** Riboflavin 400 mg + Benfotiamine 300 mg + R-ALA 600 mg + Mg glycinate 400 mg + Ubiquinol CoQ10 200 mg + NR 300 mg + Creatine 5 g. All OTC, all safe, hits every known cofactor gap simultaneously.
**Rationale:** If ME/CFS is primarily a multi-cofactor functional deficiency, this protocol would reveal it.
**Certainty of benefit:** 0.45.
**Trial design:** n=60 open-label crossover, 12-week intervention, 4-week washout, symptom diary + lactate/pyruvate + acylcarnitine profile.

### C2. "PDC rescue" protocol
**Components:** DCA (low dose, 6.25 mg/kg) + R-ALA 600 mg + Benfotiamine 300 mg + NAC 1200 mg + CoQ10 200 mg.
**Rationale:** Releases PDK block (DCA) + provides downstream cofactors (lipoate, TPP) + pre-treats antioxidant risk (NAC, CoQ10) — addresses H4 concern.
**Risk:** DCA neuropathy; monitor closely.
**Certainty:** 0.35.

### C3. "Biogenesis unlock" protocol
**Components:** NR 300 mg + Metformin 500 mg bid + Bezafibrate 400 mg + very gentle graded movement (under HR ceiling) + sulforaphane.
**Rationale:** NAD+ + AMPK + PPAR + NRF2 -> PGC-1alpha from four directions simultaneously.
**Risk:** Rhabdo risk from bezafibrate; monitor CK.
**Certainty:** 0.35.

### C4. "Fuel switch" protocol (ketone arm)
**Components:** Modified ketogenic diet (70% fat) + MCT oil 15 mL tid + exogenous BHB 10 g before exertion + carnitine 1.5 g.
**Rationale:** Bypass PDC (N1, N2).
**Certainty:** 0.45.

### C5. "Antioxidant reconstruction" protocol
**Components:** DMF or sulforaphane + NAC + R-ALA + vitamin E (mixed tocopherols) + selenium 100 mcg + CoQ10 ubiquinol + MitoQ.
**Rationale:** Restores GSH, NRF2 targets, mitochondrial-targeted antioxidants.
**Certainty:** 0.40.

### C6. "Proteostasis" protocol
**Components:** Low-dose rapamycin (weekly) + urolithin A + trehalose (mitophagy inducer) + riboflavin + R-ALA.
**Rationale:** Clear damaged mitochondria, rebuild with cofactors.
**Certainty:** 0.20. Most speculative.

## PART G: MATHEMATICAL MODEL EXTENSIONS

### M1. Add PDK activity as a dynamic state variable
- New variable: PDK_active(t)
- dPDK/dt driven by: inflammation (IL-6, TNF), glucocorticoid tone, acetyl-CoA/CoA ratio (feedback)
- Inhibited by: DCA, pyruvate, ADP/ATP ratio
- Output: PDH_flux = V_max * [pyruvate] * (1 - PDK_active / (PDK_active + K))

### M2. FAD/FADH2 pool as shared constraint across beta-ox, SDH, KGDHC, PDC
- Mass balance: d[FAD]/dt + d[FADH2]/dt = 0
- Ratio [FAD]/[FADH2] gates 8 enzymes simultaneously
- Riboflavin intake shifts total pool size
- Predicts: marginal riboflavin + high demand = catastrophic multi-step failure

### M3. Cardiolipin peroxidation as ETC degradation driver
- State: [CL_ox]/[CL_total]
- d[CL_ox]/dt = k_ox * [ROS] * [CL] - k_repair * [tafazzin] * [CL_ox]
- Couples to Complex I/III/IV activity via CL-dependence factor
- Elamipretide modeled as reducing k_ox

### M4. Integrated stress response (ISR) module for PEM modeling
- States: [eIF2a-P], [ATF4], [GDF15/FGF21]
- Driven by: mitochondrial proteostasis stress signal S(t)
- S(t) integrates ROS + CL_ox + Fe-S damage
- Output: protein synthesis inhibition, amino acid reprogramming, delayed fatigue at t+24h
- **This is the most important addition — it models the PEM delay mechanically.**

### M5. ANT flux as separate state from ETC flux
- J_ANT = V_ANT * (ATP_m - ATP_c*K_ANT)
- Autoantibody titer reduces V_ANT
- Distinguishes "ETC failure" (OCR low) from "ANT failure" (OCR normal, cytosolic ATP low)
- Provides a direct signature that Seahorse misses.

### M6. PGC-1alpha biogenesis ODE
- d[PGC-1a_active]/dt = k_phos * [AMPK] * [NAD+]/[NAD+ + Km] - k_deg * [PGC-1a]
- Couples mitochondrial biomass to AMPK and NAD+ status
- Predicts: both must be restored for biogenesis to occur

### M7. Two-compartment Fe-S model
- [Fe_labile], [Fe-S cluster]
- Assembly rate depends on ISCU, frataxin, NFS1
- Degradation by ROS
- Deferiprone reduces [Fe_labile], ISRIB irrelevant here

## PART H: CROSS-DISEASE BRIDGES

### X1. Friedreich's ataxia (FXN/frataxin loss -> Fe-S cluster deficiency)
- **Shared:** Fe-S cluster deficit, aconitase/SDH failure, mitochondrial iron dysregulation, fatigue, cardiomyopathy.
- **Imported tools:** Omaveloxolone (approved 2023, Reata), idebenone, deferiprone.
- **Imported biomarker:** Aconitase/fumarase ratio.
- **Difference:** FA has clear genetic trigger; ME/CFS may be acquired via ROS damage to existing Fe-S clusters.
- **Actionable research:** Run omaveloxolone in ME/CFS cohort with low aconitase/fumarase ratio.

### X2. Barth syndrome (TAZ loss -> cardiolipin remodeling failure)
- **Shared:** Cardiolipin abnormality, exercise intolerance, lactic acidosis.
- **Imported tools:** Elamipretide (TAZPOWER trial).
- **Imported biomarker:** MLCL/L4-CL ratio (monolyso-cardiolipin to tetralinoleoyl-cardiolipin ratio — gold-standard Barth biomarker; worth measuring in ME/CFS).
- **Difference:** Barth is X-linked genetic; ME/CFS CL abnormality may be oxidative.

### X3. MADD / Multiple Acyl-CoA Dehydrogenase Deficiency (ETFDH, ETFA, ETFB mutations)
- **Shared:** Exercise intolerance, elevated acylcarnitines C4-C18, riboflavin-responsive phenotype.
- **Imported tools:** High-dose riboflavin (established standard of care), CoQ10.
- **Imported biomarker:** Acylcarnitine profile (bloodspot).
- **ME/CFS correlate:** H1, H7. Run acylcarnitine panel on ME/CFS cohort.

### X4. MELAS / Leigh / primary mitochondrial disease
- **Shared:** Lactic acidosis, exercise intolerance, PEM-like crashes.
- **Imported tools:** Arginine/citrulline (MELAS stroke-like episodes), idebenone, elamipretide, cysteamine bitartrate, EPI-743.
- **Difference:** Genetic ETC mutations; ME/CFS has no such primary mutations documented.

### X5. Long COVID
- **Shared:** Mitochondrial dysfunction, PEM, cognitive symptoms. Substantial overlap in lactate, CoQ10 deficit, and biogenesis suppression literature.
- **Imported tools:** Metformin (Bramante 2023), HBOT (Zilberman-Itskovich 2022), coenzyme Q10, NAD+ precursors.
- **Strategic value:** Long COVID trials provide a large, well-funded pipeline. ME/CFS should systematically adopt/adapt Long COVID positive findings given pathophysiological overlap.

### X6. Sepsis-induced mitochondrial dysfunction
- **Shared:** Post-infectious onset, TCA enzyme inhibition, NAD+ depletion, impaired biogenesis.
- **Imported concept:** "Mitochondrial hibernation" — adaptive downregulation may become maladaptively locked.
- **Imported tools:** NR, CoQ10, alpha-ketoglutarate.

### X7. GLUT1 deficiency syndrome
- **Shared:** Fatigue, exertion intolerance, episodic worsening.
- **Imported tool:** Ketogenic diet (standard of care).
- **Relevance:** Supports N1/C4 ketogenic hypothesis.

### X8. Pyruvate dehydrogenase deficiency (genetic PDHD)
- **Shared:** PDC hypofunction (though mechanism differs: PDK overactivation vs genetic PDH loss).
- **Imported tool:** Ketogenic diet (standard of care!) — This is the closest mechanistic cousin to the Fluge 2016 finding.
- **Key insight:** Ketogenic diet is the *established* treatment for PDC failure. ME/CFS PDC failure has never been treated this way in a trial.

### X9. Chronic fatigue in inflammatory bowel disease / cancer cachexia
- **Shared:** Inflammation-driven PGC-1alpha suppression.
- **Imported biomarkers:** GDF15, FGF21.

### X10. Statin myopathy
- **Shared:** CoQ10 depletion, exercise intolerance, mitochondrial dysfunction, post-exertional muscle pain.
- **Imported insight:** CoQ10 supplementation partially effective.
- **ME/CFS subtype question:** Is there a "post-statin" ME/CFS phenotype? Worth studying.

## SPECIAL FOCUS: THE FOUR PRIORITY QUESTIONS

### Q1. What does PDK overactivation predict for demand vs rest states?
At rest, PDK block is tolerable: low flux, acetyl-CoA demand is modest, glycolysis to lactate can buffer. During demand (physical/cognitive exertion), pyruvate production surges, but PDC remains blocked -> pyruvate dumps into lactate, sarcoplasmic pH falls, acidosis triggers premature muscle fatigue, AND acetyl-CoA supply to TCA is insufficient to meet ATP demand -> local energy crisis. The crisis may not manifest as immediate failure (glycolytic ATP buffers briefly) but as *delayed* proteostasis stress (ISR, H9) once the acidosis + ROS damage accumulates -> PEM 24-48 h later.

**What would restore PDC without triggering PEM?**
1. Ketone esters acutely (bypass the lesion, no ROS penalty, no acidosis).
2. Gentle demand (sub-threshold pacing) to avoid saturating the block.
3. Multi-cofactor loading so that when PDC does fire, it runs at maximal capacity per unit of flux.
4. DCA + antioxidant cover (to prevent ROS backfire when PDC reopens, H4).
5. **NOT:** raw DCA (H4 concern), glucose loading, or graded exercise therapy (all push through the block).

### Q2. What clinical tests distinguish ANT failure from ETC failure?
- **ETC failure:** low OCR, low ATP production rate, high lactate, normal ANT flux.
- **ANT failure (H3):** normal OCR (misleading!), low ATP production rate, matrix ATP elevated, cytosolic ATP depleted, normal Complex I-V individual assays, positive anti-ANT antibodies.

**Differential tests:**
1. Seahorse OCR (permeabilized vs intact cells): ANT block shows discrepancy — permeabilized cells (ANT bypassed) have normal respiration, intact cells are impaired.
2. Atractyloside sensitivity (ANT inhibitor): ANT-blocked cells will not show added inhibition.
3. Plasma creatine kinase / lactate behavior during exertion: ANT block produces profound ATP starvation despite normal lactate if oxidative metabolism proceeds but ATP cannot be exported.
4. Direct: anti-ANT ELISA + functional IgG-blockade assay on isolated mitochondria.

### Q3. Riboflavin response trial design and responders
**Design:** n=100 ME/CFS, open-label 400 mg/day for 12 weeks with crossover placebo arm.
**Responder characteristics predicted:**
- Post-viral onset (inflammation -> riboflavin mobilization stress)
- Elevated C4/C5/C14/C16 acylcarnitines
- EGRAC >=1.3
- High plasma lactate on exertion
- History of migraine (riboflavin response overlap)
- BMI low or high (marginal dietary intake)
- GI symptoms (malabsorption)

**Primary endpoint:** 30% reduction in PEM frequency over final 4 weeks.
**Biomarker endpoint:** EGRAC normalization + acylcarnitine reduction.
**Key falsifier:** If non-responders have identical baseline EGRAC to responders, the mechanism is wrong.

### Q4. ISCU as upstream-most treatable failure
If ISCU/ISC assembly is impaired, a single upstream fix would cascade to improve Complex I, II, III, aconitase, ETF-QO simultaneously. Interventions:
- **Frataxin upregulation:** TAT-frataxin (preclinical), omaveloxolone (NRF2 -> frataxin induction).
- **Iron redistribution:** deferiprone (cautious).
- **Cysteine/sulfur donation:** NAC, taurine.
- **Lipoate restoration:** R-ALA (lipoate synthesis is itself Fe-S dependent — so lipoate deficiency is downstream of ISC failure and supplementation bypasses this).

**The killer experiment:** Measure aconitase/fumarase ratio + frataxin + mitochondrial iron in 50 ME/CFS vs 50 controls. If ratio <0.6 in >20% of patients, H2 is strongly supported and omaveloxolone trial becomes the natural next step.

## META-CONCLUSIONS

1. **The riboflavin gap (H1) is the single highest expected-value near-term research target.** Low cost, OTC supplement, strong disease precedent, never tested, explains multiple findings simultaneously.

2. **The ketogenic/ketone-ester pathway (N1, N2) is the most defensible immediate clinical experiment** — it is the established treatment for PDHD, the closest genetic cousin to the Fluge 2016 finding, and has acute testability via exogenous BHB.

3. **Creatine (S5) has the strongest existing ME/CFS evidence** and should be standard-of-care adjunct while other investigations proceed.

4. **The ISR/PEM-timing hypothesis (H9)** deserves a dedicated research program — if PEM is ISR-mediated, GDF15/FGF21 become objective PEM biomarkers and ISRIB becomes a mechanistically rational future drug.

5. **ANT autoantibodies (H3)** are the most clinically "undetectable" hypothesis — worth testing precisely because negative Seahorse/OCR results in ME/CFS research would miss this entirely.

6. **H4 is a safety warning:** any PDK inhibitor trial must stratify or pre-treat for antioxidant capacity.

7. **Cross-disease import from Friedreich's ataxia (omaveloxolone), Barth (elamipretide), and MADD (riboflavin)** provides an immediate pipeline of tools with existing safety data. ME/CFS research should systematically screen every approved mitochondrial disease drug for relevance.

8. **The combination protocol C1 ("cofactor saturation")** is the lowest-risk, highest-actionability immediate intervention that could be run today in interested patients under medical supervision.

## Caveats

- Every hypothesis here is speculative. Mechanistic plausibility does not entail clinical benefit.
- Treatment suggestions require physician review; several (DCA, bezafibrate, rapamycin, DMF, methylene blue) carry non-trivial risks.
- Certainty scores are subjective estimates, not formal Bayesian posteriors.
- None of this replaces pacing and symptom-aware clinical care.
- All proposed trials require IRB approval and should prioritize safety endpoints in severely affected patients.
