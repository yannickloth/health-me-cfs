# Creative Brainstorming: eMSN, Striatum, and ME/CFS Pathophysiology

**Date:** 2026-05-27
**Based on:** Literature review of eMSN discovery, DecodeME GWAS, Maccallini network analysis, CRH depletion findings
**Purpose:** Generate testable hypotheses, treatment ideas, and research directions

---

## Evidence Baseline

### Well-Established Facts (High Certainty)
- **eMSN exist:** Confirmed in primates (He 2021) and humans (Siletti 2023)
- **eMSN markers:** RXFP1 (primate), GAD1/GAD2/CXCL14/DRD1/ADARB2 (human)
- **eMSN characteristics:** D1/D2 hybrid MSNs, ~10% of striatal MSNs, uniformly distributed
- **ME/CFS genetics:** DecodeME GWAS (n=15,579) found brain tissue enrichment but NO eMSN-specific associations
- **Metabolic module:** Maccallini identified 272-gene module (sphingolipids, energy, heme)
- **CRH depletion:** Preliminary autopsy shows hypothalamic CRH neuron depletion (n=7-10, low certainty)

### Critical Evidence Gaps (Acknowledged)
- **No direct genetic evidence:** Neither DecodeME nor Maccallini identified eMSN-specific associations
- **Cell-type specific analysis NOT performed:** Brain enrichment was tissue-level, not cell-type level
- **eMSN function unknown:** Hybrid D1/D2 signaling properties not understood
- **CRH-eMSN relationship:** Unknown if eMSN produce or respond to CRH
- **Striatum-fatigue mechanism:** No direct studies linking striatal dysfunction to ME/CFS fatigue

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1.1: eMSN "Conflict Resolution" Dysfunction Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.25 (Low-Moderate)
**Evidence Link:** eMSN hybrid D1/D2 properties suggest role in complex behavior/conflict; ME/CFS patients show difficulty with "mind-body" integration, conflicting physiological demands
**Mechanism:** eMSN integrate opposing direct (D1/go) and indirect (D2/no-go) basal ganglia pathways. eMSN dysfunction may create "decision paralysis" at the motor-cognitive interface, where simultaneous competing signals (rest vs. activity, mental vs. physical effort) cannot be resolved efficiently.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show abnormal activation patterns in striatal regions during tasks requiring rapid switching between mental and physical effort, quantifiable via fMRI tasks with simultaneous cognitive-motor dual demands.
**Treatment Implication:** Therapies targeting conflict resolution circuitry (cognitive-behavioral strategies targeting dual-task performance) may improve function.

### H1.2: RXFP1 Signaling Dysregulation Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.30 (Low-Moderate)
**Evidence Link:** RXFP1 is highly specific eMSN marker; relaxin signaling modulates cAMP, NO, and inflammation; ME/CFS involves immune-metabolic dysregulation
**Mechanism:** RXFP1 (relaxin receptor) on eMSN may serve as integrative node linking neuroimmune signaling to basal ganglia output. Aberrant RXFP1 signaling could disrupt the balance between direct/indirect pathways during physiological stress (exercise, infection), manifesting as PEM and autonomic instability.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients will have altered RXFP1 expression in peripheral blood mononuclear cells (as eMSN surrogate) or altered relaxin levels in plasma/CSF. RXFP1 agonists will acutely improve exercise tolerance in a subset of patients.
**Treatment Implication:** RXFP1 agonists (e.g., INSL3 analogs in development) or relaxin pathway modulators.

### H1.3: CRH-Striatal Disconnection Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.20 (Low)
**Evidence Link:** Dutch autopsy shows hypothalamic CRH depletion; CRH is present in striatum; CRH and dopamine systems interact
**Mechanism:** Hypothalamic CRH depletion reduces CRH signaling to striatum. eMSN (if CRH-responsive) may lose critical modulatory input that normally tunes basal ganglia output during stress. CRH-dopamine crosstalk disruption could explain why ME/CFS patients have abnormal autonomic responses and exaggerated post-exertional collapse.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show reduced CRH receptor expression in striatum (post-mortem studies) or abnormal CRH-induced fMRI activation patterns. CRH agonist administration will produce blunted striatal responses in ME/CFS vs. controls.
**Treatment Implication:** CRH receptor agonists or indirect CRH pathway enhancers (investigational).

### H1.4: eMSN Metabolic Vulnerability Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Moderate)
**Evidence Link:** Maccallini module shows metabolic dysfunction (energy, sphingolipids); eMSN are GABAergic neurons with high energy demands; hybrid receptor expression may increase metabolic load
**Mechanism:** eMSN's hybrid D1/D2 expression requires simultaneous maintenance of both signaling pathways, creating unique metabolic demands. In ME/CFS with systemic metabolic dysfunction (ATP depletion, sphingolipid alterations), eMSN may be preferentially vulnerable to energy failure, leading to striatal dysfunction manifesting as impaired motivation, effort initiation, and motor planning.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** eMSN density will correlate with severity of metabolic abnormalities (ATP depletion, sphingolipid profiles) in ME/CFS. Post-exertion metabolomics will reveal unique signatures in patients with prominent striatal symptoms.
**Treatment Implication:** Metabolic support targeting neuron-specific energetics (see supplement section H4.1).

### H1.5: CXCL14 Neuroimmune Striatal Dysregulation Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.25 (Low-Moderate)
**Evidence Link:** CXCL14 is eMSN marker; chemokines modulate neuroimmune function; ME/CFS involves chronic immune activation
**Mechanism:** CXCL14 expression on eMSN suggests these cells participate in neuroimmune signaling. Aberrant CXCL14 production or signaling could create a maladaptive neuroimmune environment in striatum, altering microglial activation patterns and contributing to sickness behavior maintenance in ME/CFS.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** CXCL14 levels will be elevated in ME/CFS CSF and correlate with disease severity. Single-cell analysis of post-mortem striatum will show altered microglial states specifically adjacent to CXCL14+ eMSN.
**Treatment Implication:** CXCL14 pathway modulators (investigational) or downstream anti-inflammatory strategies.

### H1.6: Basal Ganglia "Effort Cost" Miscalibration Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Moderate)
**Evidence Link:** Basal ganglia dysfunction correlates with mental fatigue (r²=0.49); striatum encodes effort-cost calculations; ME/CFS patients experience exercise as abnormally costly
**Mechanism:** The striatum assigns effort cost to planned actions. In ME/CFS, basal ganglia circuits (potentially involving eMSN integration) may overestimate physiological cost, leading to pre-emptive inhibition of motor activity and exaggerated subjective fatigue perception. This is not "laziness" but a miscalibrated cost-benefit computation at the neural circuit level.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show abnormal striatal activation during effort-cost decision tasks (fMRI), overvaluing physiological costs relative to rewards. Computational modeling will reveal elevated cost-sensitivity parameters.
**Treatment Implication:** Cognitive approaches targeting effort perception; dopaminergic agents recalibrating cost signals.

### H1.7: Striatal Autonomic Integration Failure Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Moderate)
**Evidence Link:** Keller 2024 shows autonomic dysfunction is primary PEM driver; striatum modulates autonomic output via basal ganglia-thalamus-cortex loops; ME/CFS shows orthostatic intolerance and autonomic dysregulation
**Mechanism:** Striatum integrates autonomic status with motor planning. eMSN dysfunction or broader striatal pathology may create a disconnect between autonomic state and motor command generation, leading to inappropriate activity initiation despite inadequate autonomic preparation (causing crashes) or excessive inhibition despite sufficient resources (contributing to disability).
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show abnormal striatal-heart rate variability coupling during postural changes. Striatal activity will predict autonomic collapse events before they occur.
**Treatment Implication:** Autonomic biofeedback combined with striatal-targeted cognitive training.

### H1.8: "Neurochemical Hybridity Collapse" Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.20 (Low)
**Evidence Link:** eMSN hybrid D1/D2 receptor expression; Walitt 2024 shows catecholamine pathway alterations; hybrid systems have more failure modes
**Mechanism:** eMSN's dual receptor expression creates a delicate balancing act between direct and indirect pathway activation. In ME/CFS with catecholamine dysregulation, this balance may be destabilized, leading to "flip-flop" behavior where minor perturbations cause dramatic shifts in striatal output (abnormal over-activation followed by pathological inhibition). This could explain the "boom-crash" cycling of ME/CFS.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show greater variability in striatal activation patterns during repeated trials. Spectral analysis of basal ganglia activity will show abnormal oscillatory patterns indicative of unstable hybrid signaling.
**Treatment Implication:** Stabilizing agents targeting receptor balance (atypical dopaminergics with D1/D2 balanced activity).

## 2. Research Directions

### R2.1: Cell-Type Specific GWAS Analysis of DecodeME Data
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.90 (High)
**Rationale:** DecodeME showed brain enrichment but performed tissue-level, not cell-type analysis. MAGMA or similar tools can be applied with cell-type specific gene sets (eMSN markers: RXFP1, GAD1, GAD2, CXCL14, DRD1, ADARB2).
**Method:** Download DecodeME summary statistics; extract cell-type specific gene signatures from Siletti 2023 atlas; run MAGMA cell-type enrichment analysis specifically for striatal subtypes.
**Expected Outcome:** Either (a) identify eMSN-specific genetic enrichment (supporting hypothesis) or (b) definitively rule out strong genetic contribution (clarifying null result).
**Timeline:** 3-6 months (bioinformatics analysis).

### R2.2: Post-Mortem eMSN Quantification Study
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.60 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** Direct histological examination of eMSN in ME/CFS brain tissue is definitive test. Brain banks exist; eMSN markers are well-characterized.
**Method:** Obtain ME/CFS and control post-mortem brain tissue; immunohistochemistry for eMSN markers (RXFP1, DRD1, DRD2, CXCL14); quantify eMSN density and distribution; assess CRH receptor colocalization.
**Expected Outcome:** Determine whether eMSN density, morphology, or protein expression differs in ME/CFS.
**Timeline:** 12-24 months (tissue acquisition, processing, analysis).
**Limitation:** Small sample sizes inherent to post-mortem ME/CFS studies.

### R2.3: CRH-eMRI: CRH Challenge with Striatal fMRI
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.50 (Moderate-Low)
**Rationale:** Test H1.3 (CRH-striatal disconnection) by probing striatal response to CRH modulation.
**Method:** Administer CRH receptor agonist (or placebo) to ME/CFS patients and controls; perform fMRI focusing on striatum; measure activation patterns and connectivity.
**Expected Outcome:** Identify abnormal CRH-striatal signaling in ME/CFS if hypothesis holds.
**Timeline:** 24-36 months (safety studies, trial design, execution).
**Limitation:** CRH agonists may have significant side effects; small sample sizes due to invasiveness.

### R2.4: Striatal Effort-Cost fMRI Task Development
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.80 (High)
**Rationale:** Test H1.6 (effort cost miscalibration) using established cognitive neuroscience paradigms.
**Method:** Develop fMRI task where subjects choose between options varying in effort requirement and reward; model computational parameters (cost-sensitivity, reward-sensitivity); compare ME/CFS vs. controls.
**Expected Outcome:** Quantify abnormal effort-cost computation in ME/CFS; identify striatal activation correlates.
**Timeline:** 12-18 months (task development, validation, data collection).
**Advantage:** Non-invasive, can scale to larger cohorts.

### R2.5: Peripheral RXFP1 Expression as eMSN Surrogate Marker
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.70 (Moderate-High)
**Rationale:** Direct brain access impractical; need accessible biomarker.
**Method:** Quantify RXFP1 mRNA expression in PBMCs (or immune cell subsets) in ME/CFS vs. controls; correlate with symptoms, CPET parameters, and autonomic measures.
**Expected Outcome:** Identify RXFP1 expression patterns correlating with ME/CFS severity or subtype.
**Timeline:** 6-12 months (blood collection, qPCR analysis).
**Limitation:** Peripheral expression may not reflect brain expression; correlation does not prove causation.

### R2.6: CXCL14 and Chemokine Profiling in CSF
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.65 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** Test H1.5 (neuroimmune striatal dysregulation).
**Method:** Collect CSF from ME/CFS patients undergoing lumbar puncture (existing cohorts); multiplex chemokine profiling focusing on CXCL14 and related chemokines; correlate with clinical measures.
**Expected Outcome:** Identify chemokine signatures associated with ME/CFS severity; prioritize therapeutic targets.
**Timeline:** 6-12 months (existing samples, assay execution).
**Limitation:** Lumbar puncture is invasive; samples limited.

### R2.7: Two-Day CPET with Striatal MR Spectroscopy
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.55 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** Test metabolic vulnerability hypothesis (H1.4) by combining functional challenge (2-day CPET) with metabolic imaging.
**Method:** Perform 2-day CPET on ME/CFS and controls; before and after each CPET, acquire striatal 1H-MRS; measure metabolic changes (NAA, lactate, glutamate, energy metabolites).
**Expected Outcome:** Identify abnormal striatal metabolic responses to exercise in ME/CFS; test whether eMSN vulnerability is related to metabolic collapse.
**Timeline:** 24-30 months (protocol design, recruitment, data collection).
**Advantage:** Directly connects PEM phenomenon to striatal metabolism.

### R2.8: Computational Modeling of Basal Ganglia Autonomic Coupling
**Certainty of Feasibility:** 0.85 (High)
**Rationale:** Test H1.7 (striatal autonomic integration failure).
**Method:** Record simultaneous fMRI (striatum) and autonomic measures (HRV, blood pressure) during postural challenge; use dynamic causal modeling or Granger causality to quantify coupling; compare ME/CFS vs. controls.
**Expected Outcome:** Quantify abnormal striatal-autonomic coupling in ME/CFS; identify predictive biomarkers of autonomic collapse.
**Timeline:** 12-18 months (data collection, computational analysis).
**Advantage:** Non-invasive, generates mechanistic insights.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### D3.1: Solriamfetol Revisited with Striatal Hypothesis
**Certainty:** 0.60 (Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Solriamfetol (dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor) showed benefit in 2025 ME/CFS trial. Under eMSN hypothesis, benefit may come from rebalancing D1/D2 signaling in striatum, improving effort-cost computation and motor initiation. Unlike amphetamines (which force neurotransmitter release and deplete stores), solriamfetol is a pure reuptake inhibitor with cleaner safety profile.
**Evidence Link:** Young 2025 RCT showed fatigue improvement; Walitt 2024 identified catecholamine pathway alterations; striatal dysfunction correlates with fatigue.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Patients with prominent striatal symptoms (effort initiation difficulty, motor planning problems) will show preferential response to solriamfetol. fMRI will show normalized striatal activation patterns after treatment.
**Risk Profile:** Low-moderate. Generally well-tolerated; may cause insomnia, anxiety, increased heart rate.
**Clinical Trial Design:** Double-blind crossover, n=50 ME/CFS patients stratified by striatal symptom score; 12-week treatment period; primary outcomes: fatigue scales, 2-day CPET, fMRI effort-cost task.
**Caution:** Trial did not measure PEM (cardinal symptom). Efficacy for PEM prevention untested.

### D3.2: Atypical Dopaminergics with D1/D2 Balanced Activity
**Certainty:** 0.30 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** If eMSN dysfunction involves D1/D2 imbalance (H1.8), agents with balanced activity on both receptors may restore hybrid signaling stability better than selective agonists.
**Candidates:**
- **Aripiprazole:** Partial agonist at D2 (and D1 at higher doses), may stabilize D1/D2 balance
- **Cariprazine:** D3-preferring with D2 activity; modulates striatal circuits
- **Bromocriptine:** D2 agonist with complex dose-dependent effects
**Evidence Link:** Some ME/CFS patients report benefit from aripiprazole (LDN + abilify combinations); dystonia research shows D1/D2 balance importance in striatal function.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients with D1/D2 imbalance (measured via peripheral gene expression or receptor binding assays) will respond preferentially to balanced agents. Side effects will correlate with degree of initial imbalance.
**Risk Profile:** Moderate. Dopaminergics can cause dyskinesia, psychiatric effects, metabolic changes.
**Clinical Trial Design:** Pilot study n=30, comparing aripiprazole vs. placebo; 8-week treatment; outcomes: fatigue, PEM frequency, actigraphy (motor activity), neuropsychiatric side effects.

### D3.3: RXFP1 Agonists (Investigational)
**Certainty:** 0.20 (Low)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Direct targeting of eMSN-specific receptor (H1.2). RXFP1 activation increases cAMP, NO production, and has anti-inflammatory effects—potentially addressing multiple ME/CFS pathways.
**Candidates:**
- **INSL3 analogs:** In development for reproductive applications; RXFP1 agonists
- **Relaxin-2 analogs:** FDA-approved (serelaxin) for acute heart failure (failed phase 3, but safety data exists)
**Evidence Link:** RXFP1 is eMSN-specific marker; relaxin signaling modulates inflammation and vascular function; ME/CFS involves neuroimmune and vascular dysfunction.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** RXFP1 agonist administration will acutely improve exercise tolerance in ME/CFS patients with low baseline RXFP1 signaling (measured via expression biomarkers).
**Risk Profile:** Unknown for chronic use. Acute heart failure trials showed hypotension; not suitable for patients with orthostatic intolerance.
**Clinical Trial Design:** Very early-stage; would require extensive safety profiling before ME/CFS trials.

### D3.4: CRH Receptor Modulators
**Certainty:** 0.15 (Low)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address CRH-striatal disconnection (H1.3) by modulating CRH receptor activity in brain.
**Candidates:**
- **CRH-R1 agonists:** Could restore hypothalamic-striatal signaling
- **CRH-R1 antagonists:** Counteract excessive CRH effects if CRH signaling is abnormal rather than depleted
**Evidence Link:** Dutch autopsy suggests CRH depletion; CRH receptor modulators in development for anxiety/depression.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** CRH-R1 agonists will normalize striatal fMRI responses in ME/CFS patients. CRH levels will correlate with treatment response.
**Risk Profile:** High. CRH system modulates HPA axis broadly; off-target effects likely.
**Clinical Trial Design:** Not ready for clinical translation; would require animal studies and extensive safety profiling first.

### D3.5: CXCL14 Pathway Modulators
**Certainty:** 0.20 (Low)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address neuroimmune striatal dysregulation (H1.5) by targeting eMSN-associated chemokine signaling.
**Candidates:**
- **CXCR4 antagonists:** CXCL14 may signal through CXCR4; existing drugs (plerixafor) used clinically
- **Broad chemokine inhibitors:** Non-specific but could normalize chemokine environment
**Evidence Link:** CXCL14 is eMSN marker; ME/CFS involves chronic immune activation; chemokine modulation works in other neuroinflammatory conditions.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** CXCL14 levels will correlate with treatment response to CXCR4 antagonists. CSF chemokine profiles will normalize after treatment.
**Risk Profile:** Moderate. Plerixafor generally well-tolerated but designed for stem cell mobilization; chronic use unknown.
**Clinical Trial Design:** Exploratory study n=20, measuring CSF chemokines before/after plerixafor infusion; correlate with symptom changes.

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### S4.1: Neuron-Specific Mitochondrial Support Stack
**Certainty:** 0.45 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address eMSN metabolic vulnerability (H1.4) by providing neuron-specific mitochondrial cofactors. eMSN are GABAergic neurons with high energy demands; general mitochondrial support may preferentially benefit high-demand neurons.
**Components:**
- **MitoQ (10-20 mg/day):** Mitochondria-targeted antioxidant; protects neuronal mitochondria from ROS
- **PQQ (10-20 mg/day):** Stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis; crosses blood-brain barrier
- **Acetyl-L-carnitine (500-1000 mg twice daily):** Acetyl groups for acetylcholine synthesis; transports fatty acids into mitochondria; benefits cognitive function
- **R-lipoic acid (100-200 mg twice daily):** Regenerates antioxidants; improves mitochondrial function
**Evidence Link:** Maccallini module shows metabolic dysfunction; Keller 2024 shows mitochondrial dysfunction downstream of autonomic failure; eMSN high energy demands make them vulnerable.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** This stack will improve 2-day CPET performance in ME/CFS patients with prominent cognitive/motor symptoms. NIRS monitoring will show improved brain oxygenation.
**Safety Profile:** Generally well-tolerated. PQQ may cause insomnia in some; take morning. Acetyl-L-carnitine can be stimulating.
**Protocol:** Start low dose; titrate over 4 weeks; assess response at 12 weeks.
**Severity Considerations:** Start with lower doses in severe patients; may worsen initially (detoxification) before improving.

### S4.2: Dopamine Precursor + Cofactor Protocol
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Support catecholamine synthesis for eMSN signaling, addressing Walitt 2024 catecholamine pathway findings.
**Components:**
- **L-tyrosine (500-1500 mg morning):** Dopamine precursor; crosses blood-brain barrier
- **Vitamin B6 (P-5-P form, 25-50 mg/day):** Cofactor for L-DOPA decarboxylase
- **Vitamin C (500-1000 mg with tyrosine):** Cofactor for dopamine beta-hydroxylase (dopamine to norepinephrine conversion)
- **Iron (if deficient):** Cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase (rate-limiting step)
**Evidence Link:** Walitt 2024 shows catecholamine pathway alterations; striatal dysfunction requires adequate dopamine signaling; Keller 2024 suggests catecholamine-PEM connection.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Patients with low baseline catecholamines (measured via plasma or urine) will show improvement in effort-cost computation and motor initiation. HRV will improve.
**Safety Profile:** Generally safe. L-tyrosine can cause insomnia; take morning. Iron should not be supplemented without confirming deficiency.
**Protocol:** Test baseline tyrosine, dopamine metabolites; start L-tyrosine 500 mg morning; increase to 1500 mg if tolerated; reassess at 8 weeks.
**Contraindications:** MAO inhibitors, thyroid conditions (tyrosine is thyroid hormone precursor), phenylketonuria.

### S4.3: Sphingolipid Modulation Protocol
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Maccallini module shows sphingolipid metabolism enrichment; sphingolipids are critical for neuronal membranes and signaling; modulating sphingolipid balance may support neuronal function.
**Components:**
- **Serine (500-1000 mg/day):** Precursor for sphingolipid synthesis; improves sphingolipid ratios
- **Myo-inositol (500-1000 mg twice daily):** Influences sphingolipid metabolism; supports membrane function
- **Vitamin D (adequate levels):** Modulates sphingolipid metabolism
**Evidence Link:** Maccallini 2025 identified sphingolipid pathway enrichment in ME/CFS; sphingolipids critical for neuronal membranes; eMSN signaling requires healthy membranes.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Sphingolipid profiles in blood will normalize after supplementation. Cognitive symptoms will correlate with sphingolipid ratio improvements.
**Safety Profile:** Generally safe. Serine may cause digestive upset at high doses; split doses. Myo-inositol generally well-tolerated.
**Protocol:** Test baseline sphingolipid panel if available; start serine 500 mg; titrate to 1000 mg; assess at 12 weeks.
**Note:** Research-stage approach; evidence limited.

### S4.4: Neuroprotective Anti-Inflammatory Stack
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address neuroimmune striatal dysregulation (H1.5) by reducing neuroinflammation that may affect eMSN function.
**Components:**
- **Curcumin (500-1000 mg with piperine twice daily):** Anti-inflammatory, crosses blood-brain barrier
- **Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA 2-3 g/day):** Anti-inflammatory, supports neuronal membranes
- **Resveratrol (200-500 mg/day):** Anti-inflammatory, supports mitochondrial function
- **Quercetin (500 mg twice daily):** Anti-inflammatory, may reduce microglial activation
**Evidence Link:** ME/CFS involves chronic immune activation; CXCL14 suggests neuroimmune component; neuroinflammation impairs basal ganglia function.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Inflammatory markers (CRP, cytokines) will decrease after supplementation. fMRI will show improved striatal connectivity.
**Safety Profile:** Generally safe. Curcumin may cause digestive upset; take with food. Omega-3 may increase bleeding risk with anticoagulants. Resveratrol may interact with blood thinners.
**Protocol:** Start with curcumin + omega-3; add resveratrol if tolerated; assess at 12 weeks.

### S4.5: GABAergic Support
**Certainty:** 0.30 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** eMSN are GABAergic neurons; supporting GABA synthesis and function may improve striatal output.
**Components:**
- **L-theanine (200-400 mg twice daily):** Increases GABA, reduces anxiety, improves attention
- **Magnesium (glycinate or threonate, 200-400 mg/day):** GABA receptor cofactor; magnesium threonate crosses BBB
- **Vitamin B6 (P-5-P, 25-50 mg/day):** Cofactor for GAD enzymes (GABA synthesis)
**Evidence Link:** eMSN are GABAergic (express GAD1/GAD2); ME/CFS involves anxiety and hyperarousal; GABA dysfunction implicated in fatigue disorders.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** GABA levels (measured via MRS) will increase after supplementation. Anxiety symptoms will decrease; motor initiation may improve.
**Safety Profile:** Generally safe. L-theanine may cause drowsiness; can take evening. Magnesium may cause diarrhea at high doses.
**Protocol:** Start magnesium at night; add L-theanine morning/evening; assess at 8 weeks.
**Note:** Theoretical approach; direct eMSN-GABA connection untested.

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### N5.1: Effort-Cost Retraining Protocol
**Certainty:** 0.50 (Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address basal ganglia effort-cost miscalibration (H1.6) by systematically recalibrating perceived effort through graded exposure.
**Protocol:**
1. **Baseline assessment:** Effort-cost fMRI task + daily activity logs + perceived effort scales
2. **Graded exposure:** Activities started well below perceived capacity; effort ratings recorded objectively (heart rate, activity counts) and subjectively
3. **Feedback integration:** Compare objective vs. subjective effort; identify overestimation patterns
4. **Gradual titration:** Increase activity as effort perception becomes more accurate
5. **Metacognitive strategies:** Explicit training on recognizing accurate vs. inflated effort signals
**Evidence Link:** Basal ganglia dysfunction correlates with fatigue (r²=0.49); effort-cost computation is striatal function; cognitive behavioral principles can reshape perception.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Patients undergoing retraining will show normalized striatal activation on effort-cost fMRI tasks. Perceived effort will more closely match objective measures. PEM episodes will decrease.
**Implementation Considerations:** Requires specialized therapist training; may not be suitable for severe patients initially.
**Timeline:** 12-16 week program.

### N5.2: Dual-Task Training for Motor-Cognitive Integration
**Certertainty:** 0.45 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address eMSN conflict resolution dysfunction (H1.1) by improving integration of competing motor and cognitive demands.
**Protocol:**
1. **Simple dual-tasks:** Walking while counting backwards, balance while naming objects
2. **Progressive complexity:** Increase cognitive load, increase motor complexity
3. **Strategic training:** Explicit instruction on prioritization strategies, task-switching
4. **Physiological monitoring:** Heart rate, autonomic measures during training
5. **Recovery integration:** Training only within "energy envelope"; sufficient recovery between sessions
**Evidence Link:** eMSN hypothesized role in conflict resolution; ME/CFS patients struggle with mental-physical multitasking; dual-task training improves function in other neurological conditions.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Dual-task performance will improve after training. fMRI will show improved striatal activation patterns. PEM incidence will not increase if training stays within envelope.
**Safety Considerations:** MUST stay below PEM threshold; start very simple; extensive recovery required.
**Timeline:** 8-12 week program.

### N5.3: Vagal Tone Enhancement for Striatal Modulation
**Certainty:** 0.55 (Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Vagus nerve projects to basal ganglia indirectly; vagal activation modulates neuroinflammation and autonomic balance; may improve striatal-autonomic coupling (H1.7).
**Protocol:**
1. **Transcutaneous vagus nerve stimulation (tVNS):** Daily sessions (15-30 min), ear-clip device
2. **Vagal breathing exercises:** Resonance frequency breathing (typically 5-6 breaths/minute), 10-20 min twice daily
3. **Cold water face immersion:** Quick vagal activation (30-60 seconds), for acute PEM management
4. **HRV biofeedback:** Monitor vagal tone during activities, optimize pacing
**Evidence Link:** Keller 2024 shows autonomic dysfunction; vagal stimulation has anti-inflammatory effects; vagus-basal ganglia connections documented in neuroscience literature.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** HRV will increase with training. Autonomic collapse episodes will decrease. fMRI will show improved striatal-autonomic coupling.
**Safety Profile:** Generally safe. tVNS devices generally well-tolerated; contraindicated with certain cardiac conditions.
**Accessibility:** tVNS devices available OTC in some countries; breathing exercises free; cold water free.

### N5.4: Cognitive-Motor Task-Switching Training
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Address eMSN hybrid signaling stability (H1.8) by training flexible switching between mental and physical states.
**Protocol:**
1. **Scheduled state transitions:** Regularly alternate between rest, light mental activity, light physical activity
2. **Explicit transition cues:** Use timers, alarms, environmental cues to signal state changes
3. **Transition difficulty reduction:** Plan transitions in advance, minimize decision load during transitions
4. **Gradual complexity increase:** Start with simple transitions, add complexity as function improves
5. **Transition recovery optimization:** Identify optimal recovery periods between transitions
**Evidence Link:** ME/CFS patients struggle with state changes; basal ganglia involved in task switching; eMSN hybridity may affect transition ability.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Transition difficulty will decrease with training. State-change crashes will reduce. Wearable sensors will show smoother autonomic transitions.
**Implementation:** Requires careful planning within energy envelope; may be challenging for severe patients.

### N5.5: Sensory Integration Therapy for Striatal Function
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Low-Moderate)
**Mechanistic Rationale:** Striatum processes multisensory information; sensory overload common in ME/CFS may reflect striatal dysfunction. Sensory integration therapy may normalize striatal processing.
**Protocol:**
1. **Sensory profiling:** Identify hypersensitivities (light, sound, touch, movement)
2. **Graded exposure:** Controlled, gradual exposure to sensory stimuli, starting below threshold
3. **Sensory diet:** Scheduled sensory activities throughout day (vestibular, proprioceptive, tactile input)
4. **Environmental modification:** Optimize home/ work environments to reduce sensory load
5. **Crisis strategies:** Techniques for managing sensory overload episodes
**Evidence Link:** Striatum involved in sensory integration; sensory symptoms common in ME/CFS; sensory integration therapy effective in other neurological conditions.
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Sensory thresholds will improve with therapy. fMRI will show normalized striatal sensory processing. PEM triggered by sensory exposure will decrease.
**Implementation:** Typically requires occupational therapy with sensory integration training.

## 6. Combinations + Accessibility by Severity

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### M7.1: Incorporate Striatal Effort-Cost Computation
**Certainty:** 0.50 (Moderate)
**Extension to Existing Models:** Add striatal effort-cost module to energy management models.
**New Variables:**
- `E_striatal`: Striatal energy reserve (distinct from systemic)
- `C_effort`: Computed effort cost for proposed action
- `R_reward`: Perceived reward value for action
- `D_decision`: Decision variable (action if R_reward > C_effort * S_striatal)
- `S_striatal`: Striatal sensitivity factor (elevated in ME/CFS)
**New Parameters:**
- `alpha_striatal`: Striatal energy depletion rate
- `beta_striatal`: Striatal recovery rate
- `gamma_effort`: Effort cost scaling factor
- `delta_reward`: Reward processing efficiency
**Model Structure:**
```
E_striatal(t+1) = E_striatal(t) - alpha_striatal * Activity(t) + beta_striatal * Recovery(t)
C_effort = gamma_effort * Systemic_Cost * S_striatal
D_decision = sign(R_reward - C_effort)
Action = D_decision * Capacity(E_striatal, E_systemic)
```
**Predictions:**
- Elevated `S_striatal` in ME/CFS explains excessive effort perception
- Striatal energy depletion creates pre-emptive inhibition even when systemic reserves adequate
- Model predicts "decision paralysis" phenomenon where patients feel unable to initiate action despite objective capacity
**Validation Requirements:** Fit to behavioral data (effort-cost tasks), validate against fMRI striatal activation, predict PEM thresholds.

### M7.2: Hybrid D1/D2 Signaling Model
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Low-Moderate)
**Extension to Existing Models:** Add eMSN-specific hybrid signaling module to dopamine models.
**New Variables:**
- `D1_signal`: Direct pathway activation
- `D2_signal`: Indirect pathway activation
- `D_hybrid`: eMSN hybrid output (function of D1, D2, and cross-talk)
- `S_balance`: D1/D2 balance factor
- `I_instability`: Instability index (propensity for flip-flop behavior)
**New Parameters:**
- `k1`: D1 activation coefficient
- `k2`: D2 activation coefficient
- `k_cross`: Cross-talk coefficient (D1-D2 interaction)
- `threshold_hybrid`: eMSN activation threshold
**Model Structure:**
```
D1_signal = k1 * Dopamine * D1_receptor_density
D2_signal = k2 * Dopamine * D2_receptor_density
D_hybrid = f(D1_signal, D2_signal, k_cross, S_balance)
I_instability = |D1_signal - D2_signal| / (D1_signal + D2_signal)
Flip-flop = 1 if I_instability > threshold_hybrid else 0
Striatal_Output = (D1_signal - D2_signal) + D_hybrid - Flip-flop*Penalty
```
**Predictions:**
- Elevated `I_instability` in ME/CFS explains boom-crash cycling
- `k_cross` modulation could stabilize hybrid signaling
- Model predicts that small dopamine fluctuations cause large output changes when balance disrupted
**Validation Requirements:** Correlate with treatment response to dopaminergics, validate against peripheral biomarkers, predict symptom cycling patterns.

### M7.3: Striatum-Autonomic Coupling Model
**Certainty:** 0.45 (Low-Moderate)
**Extension to Existing Models:** Add bidirectional striatum-autonomic coupling module.
**New Variables:**
- `A_autonomic`: Autonomic state (sympathetic-parasympathetic balance)
- `S_striatal_autonomic`: Striatum-to-autonomic coupling strength
- `A_autonomic_striatal`: Autonomic-to-striatal coupling strength
- `F_feedback`: Feedback delay factor
**New Parameters:**
- `t_lag`: Delay between striatal command and autonomic response
- `gain_autonomic`: Autonomic response gain
- `gain_striatal`: Striatal response to autonomic feedback
**Model Structure:**
```
A_autonomic(t) = A_autonomic(t-1) + S_striatal_autonomic * Striatal_Command(t - F_feedback)
Striatal_Response(t) = A_autonomic_striatal * A_autonomic(t - t_lag)
Coupling_Error = |Striatal_Command - Autonomic_Response|
Collapse_Predictor = Cumulative_Coupling_Error > Threshold
```
**Predictions:**
- Reduced `S_striatal_autonomic` in ME/CFS explains autonomic collapse
- Elevated `t_lag` explains delayed autonomic compensation
- Model predicts that collapse events are preceded by accumulating coupling errors
**Validation Requirements:** Fit to simultaneous fMRI-autonomic data, validate against PEM event prediction, test coupling improvement with interventions.

### M7.4: Multi-System Integration Model
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Low-Moderate)
**Extension to Existing Models:** Integrate striatal, autonomic, metabolic, and immune modules into unified framework.
**New Variables:**
- `E_systemic`: Systemic energy reserve
- `E_striatal`: Striatal energy reserve
- `A_autonomic`: Autonomic state
- `I_immune`: Immune activation state
- `M_metabolic`: Metabolic function state
- `G_global`: Global homeostatic set-point
**Coupling Parameters:**
- `c_striatal_autonomic`: Striatum-autonomic coupling
- `c_autonomic_metabolic`: Autonomic-metabolic coupling
- `c_metabolic_immune`: Metabolic-immune coupling
- `c_immune_striatal`: Immune-striatal coupling
**Model Structure:**
```
E_systemic(t+1) = E_systemic(t) - Activity(t) + Recovery(t) * f(A_autonomic, M_metabolic)
E_striatal(t+1) = E_striatal(t) - Neural_Activity(t) + Neural_Recovery(t) * f(A_autonomic, I_immune)
A_autonomic(t+1) = A_autonomic(t) + c_striatal_autonomic * E_striatal + c_autonomic_metabolic * M_metabolic
M_metabolic(t+1) = M_metabolic(t) + c_autonomic_metabolic * A_autonomic + c_metabolic_immune * I_immune
I_immune(t+1) = I_immune(t) + c_metabolic_immune * M_metabolic + c_immune_striatal * E_striatal
G_global(t+1) = w1*E_systemic + w2*E_striatal + w3*A_autonomic + w4*M_metabolic + w5*I_immune
```
**Predictions:**
- System will show "tipping points" where coupling failures cascade
- Different patient subtypes will have different dominant coupling failures
- Model predicts that interventions must target multiple couplings simultaneously
**Validation Requirements:** Fit to comprehensive multi-modal data, identify patient subtypes via clustering, predict treatment response.

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### B8.1: ME/CFS-Parkinson's Striatal Connection
**Certainty:** 0.45 (Low-Moderate)
**Rationale:** Both conditions involve striatal dysfunction; Maccallini module shows overlap with Parkinson's; both have fatigue and motor planning issues.
**Testable ME/CFS Predictions from Parkinson's Research:**
- ME/CFS patients will show mild abnormalities on Parkinson's motor tests (gait analysis, finger tapping, tremor assessment)
- Alpha-synuclein aggregation markers may be elevated in a subset of ME/CFS patients
- Parkinson's therapeutics (e.g., amantadine, MAO-B inhibitors) may benefit ME/CFS fatigue
- Striatal dopamine transporter imaging (DAT-SPECT) will show abnormalities in ME/CFS
**Reverse Predictions (Parkinson's from ME/CFS):**
- Parkinson's patients with prominent fatigue may share ME/CFS metabolic signatures
- PEM-like phenomena may be present in early Parkinson's (post-exertional worsening)
- Autonomic dysfunction profiles may overlap
**Shared Mechanisms:**
- Striatal mitochondrial dysfunction
- Neuroinflammation (microglial activation)
- Dopamine signaling abnormalities
- Autonomic dysregulation
**Research Bridge:** Collaborative studies comparing ME/CFS and Parkinson's cohorts using striatal imaging, metabolomics, and autonomic testing.

### B8.2: ME/CFS-Schizophrenia Striatal Connection
**Certainty:** 0.30 (Low-Moderate)
**Rationale:** Schizophrenia involves profound striatal dopamine dysregulation; both conditions have cognitive dysfunction and motivation issues; eMSN hybridity may relate to schizophrenia pathology.
**Testable ME/CFS Predictions from Schizophrenia Research:**
- ME/CFS patients will show abnormal striatal connectivity patterns (similar to but less severe than schizophrenia)
- Genetic risk variants for schizophrenia may be enriched in ME/CFS striatal pathways
- Atypical antipsychotics (e.g., aripiprazole, cariprazine) may improve ME/CFS fatigue via striatal modulation
- Negative symptoms (avolition, anhedonia) may overlap with ME/CFS energy regulation issues
**Reverse Predictions (Schizophrenia from ME/CFS):**
- Schizophrenia patients with prominent fatigue may share ME/CFS autonomic signatures
- Metabolic dysfunction may be more prevalent in schizophrenia than currently recognized
**Shared Mechanisms:**
- Striatal dopamine dysregulation
- Cognitive-motor integration deficits
- Motivation/effort-cost computation abnormalities
**Research Bridge:** Comparative neuroimaging studies; repurposing schizophrenia striatal-targeted therapeutics.

### B8.3: ME/CFS-ADHD Striatal Connection
**Certainty:** 0.50 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** ADHD involves striatal dysfunction and dopamine dysregulation; both conditions have executive dysfunction; stimulants help some ME/CFS patients.
**Testable ME/CFS Predictions from ADHD Research:**
- ME/CFS patients will show ADHD-like patterns on effort-cost tasks (delay discounting, temporal discounting)
- ADHD genetic risk variants may be enriched in ME/CFS cohorts
- ADHD medications (stimulants, atomoxetine) may benefit ME/CFS patients with ADHD-like features
- Executive function profiles will overlap (working memory, attention, task-switching)
**Reverse Predictions (ADHD from ME/CFS):**
- ADHD patients may have subtle autonomic dysfunction similar to ME/CFS
- ADHD medications may worsen ME/CFS PEM in non-responders
**Shared Mechanisms:**
- Striatal dopamine dysfunction
- Executive dysfunction
- Motivation/effort regulation abnormalities
- Neural network connectivity issues
**Research Bridge:** Comparative cognitive testing; stratified clinical trials of ADHD medications in ME/CFS.

### B8.4: ME/CFS-Long COVID Striatal Connection
**Certainty:** 0.60 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** Both are post-viral conditions with overlapping symptoms; both involve neuroinflammation; both show striatal abnormalities in imaging studies.
**Testable ME/CFS Predictions from Long COVID Research:**
- ME/CFS and Long COVID will share striatal imaging abnormalities
- Post-viral ME/CFS and Long COVID will have overlapping CSF cytokine/chemokine profiles
- Anti-inflammatory approaches that help Long COVID may also help ME/CFS
- Viral persistence mechanisms may be shared
**Reverse Predictions (Long COVID from ME/CFS):**
- Long COVID patients will show ME/CFS-like metabolic signatures
- ME/CFS therapeutics may benefit Long COVID fatigue
**Shared Mechanisms:**
- Post-viral neuroinflammation
- Striatal dysfunction
- Autonomic dysregulation

- Immune dysregulation
**Research Bridge:** Multi-condition studies comparing ME/CFS, Long COVID, and controls across imaging, metabolomics, immunology.

### B8.5: ME/CFS-Fibromyalgia Striatal Connection

## 9. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### D9.1: RXFP1 Expression as Peripheral eMSN Surrogate
**Certainty:** 0.35 (Low-Moderate)
**Rationale:** Direct brain access impractical; need accessible biomarker for eMSN function.
**Measurement Approach:** Quantify RXFP1 mRNA expression in PBMCs via qPCR. Optionally, measure protein expression via flow cytometry.
**Stratification Potential:** Patients with low RXFP1 expression may have eMSN dysfunction subtype.
**Treatment Response Prediction:** Low RXFP1 patients may respond preferentially to RXFP1 agonists or dopaminergics.
**Validation Requirements:** Correlate with brain imaging, test-retest reliability, predict clinical outcomes.
**Advantages:** Relatively simple, inexpensive, scalable.
**Limitations:** Peripheral expression may not reflect brain expression; environmental factors affect expression.

### D9.2: Striatal Effort-Cost fMRI Biomarker
**Certainty:** 0.50 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** Direct measurement of striatal function implicated in effort-cost miscalibration (H1.6).
**Measurement Approach:** fMRI task where subjects choose between options varying in effort requirement and reward. Extract striatal activation parameters and model effort-cost sensitivity.
**Stratification Potential:** Patients with abnormal striatal activation patterns form "striatal subtype."
**Treatment Response Prediction:** Striatal subtype patients may respond preferentially to dopaminergics, effort-cost retraining.
**Validation Requirements:** Standardize task, establish normative data, test-retest reliability, predict treatment response.
**Advantages:** Direct brain measurement, mechanistically relevant.
**Limitations:** Expensive, limited accessibility, contraindicated for some patients (e.g., severe, claustrophobic).

### D9.3: CXCL14 Chemokine Panel
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Low-Moderate)
**Rationale:** CXCL14 is eMSN marker; may reflect neuroimmune striatal dysfunction (H1.5).
**Measurement Approach:** Multiplex chemokine assay in CSF or plasma. Focus on CXCL14 and related chemokines.
**Stratification Potential:** Patients with elevated CXCL14 may have "neuroimmune striatal subtype."
**Treatment Response Prediction:** High CXCL14 patients may respond to anti-inflammatory approaches or CXCR4 antagonists.
**Validation Requirements:** Correlate with symptoms and imaging, test-retest reliability, predict treatment response.
**Advantages:** Accessible (plasma), mechanistically relevant.
**Limitations:** CSF more invasive but more brain-relevant; plasma may not reflect CNS levels.

### D9.4: Striatum-Autonomic Coupling Index
**Certainty:** 0.45 (Low-Moderate)
**Rationale:** Striatum-autonomic disconnection may be core ME/CFS mechanism (H1.7, M7.3).
**Measurement Approach:** Simultaneous fMRI (striatum) and autonomic monitoring (HRV, blood pressure) during postural challenge. Compute coupling metrics (Granger causality, coherence).
**Stratification Potential:** Patients with low coupling form "autonomic-striatal subtype."
**Treatment Response Prediction:** Low coupling patients may respond to vagal stimulation, effort-cost retraining.
**Validation Requirements:** Establish coupling metrics, test-retest reliability, predict treatment response and PEM events.
**Advantages:** Mechanistically relevant, predictive of crashes.
**Limitations:** Complex measurement, requires specialized equipment.

### D9.5: Metabolic-Striatal Signature
**Certainty:** 0.40 (Low-Moderate)
**Rationale:** Metabolic module overlaps with striatal vulnerability (H1.4, Maccallini).
**Measurement Approach:** Blood metabolomics focusing on Maccallini module genes (sphingolipids, energy metabolites, heme degradation products). Combine with 2-day CPET.
**Stratification Potential:** Patients with specific metabolic signatures form "metabolic-striatal subtype."
**Treatment Response Prediction:** Metabolic-striatal patients may respond to neuron-specific mitochondrial support, metabolic interventions.
**Validation Requirements:** Establish metabolic signatures, correlate with striatal imaging, predict treatment response.
**Advantages:** Mechanistically relevant, complementary to existing CPET.
**Limitations:** Metabolomics complex and variable; requires specialized analysis.

### D9.6: Composite Multi-Modal ME/CFS Subtyping
**Certainty:** 0.55 (Moderate)
**Rationale:** ME/CFS likely involves multiple mechanisms; single biomarker insufficient.
**Measurement Approach:** Combine multiple modalities:
- fMRI (striatal activation)
- Autonomic measures (HRV, tilt-table)
- Metabolomics (blood/CSF)
- Immune markers (cytokines, chemokines)
- CPET outcomes
- Symptom profiles
**Stratification Potential:** Cluster analysis to identify distinct subtypes (e.g., "striatal-autonomic," "metabolic-immune," "neuroinflammatory").
**Treatment Response Prediction:** Different subtypes respond preferentially to different interventions (personalized medicine).
**Validation Requirements:** Large cohort, comprehensive testing, machine learning analysis, prospective validation.
**Advantages:** Comprehensive, personalized, maximizes therapeutic matching.
**Limitations:** Expensive, complex, requires significant infrastructure.

## Critical Caveats

1. **Evidence Gap Acknowledgment:** No direct genetic evidence linking eMSN to ME/CFS. All hypotheses are speculative.
2. **Tissue-Level vs. Cell-Type:** DecodeME brain enrichment is tissue-level, not cell-type specific. Cell-type analysis required.
3. **CRH Depletion Certainty:** Dutch autopsy findings are preliminary (low certainty, secondary source, not peer-reviewed).
4. **eMSN Function Unknown:** Basic biology of eMSN not established; role in disease entirely speculative.
5. **Safety First:** Many ideas are research-stage. All interventions require medical supervision.
6. **Patient Heterogeneity:** ME/CFS likely involves multiple mechanisms; not all patients will have striatal involvement.
7. **Risk of Harm:** Inappropriate application of interventions (especially activity-based) can worsen ME/CFS. Pacing remains foundational.
8. **No Clinical Recommendations:** This document is brainstorming, not clinical guidance. All ideas require validation.
