## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 The Orexin--Vasomotion--Glymphatic Triad: A Unified Sleep Failure Model

**Mechanistic chain:** Orexin neurons in the lateral hypothalamus are the master switch for sleep-wake transitions. Reduced orexin-A (Lopez-Amador 2025) impairs the *quality* of NREM entry --- not just wakefulness. The locus coeruleus (LC) receives dense orexinergic projections; orexin-A is the primary excitatory drive to LC neurons during wakefulness. During normal sleep, orexin withdrawal permits LC NE to settle into the infraslow (0.05 Hz) oscillatory pattern that drives vasomotion and glymphatic clearance (Hauglund 2025).

**The ME/CFS problem:** If orexin-A is chronically low, the LC never receives a *clean* on-off signal. Instead of crisp wake-state (high tonic NE) transitioning to sleep-state (infraslow NE oscillations), the LC operates in a dysregulated intermediate state: insufficient tonic drive for full wakefulness (daytime fatigue), but also failing to settle into the coherent infraslow oscillation needed for glymphatic pumping. This predicts *both* the "tired but wired" phenomenology *and* impaired waste clearance.

**What's genuinely novel here:** No paper connects orexin deficiency specifically to impaired NE oscillatory quality (as distinct from NE level). The existing literature treats orexin deficiency as a wakefulness problem; this reframes it as simultaneously a *sleep quality* problem via disrupted LC oscillatory dynamics.

**Evidence links:**
- Orexin-A reduced in ME/CFS (Lopez-Amador 2025, 27-study review)
- LC NE infraslow oscillations drive glymphatic clearance (Hauglund 2025)
- DHPG (NE metabolite) reduced in ME/CFS CSF (ch22 mechanistic studies)
- Alpha-delta sleep intrusion = hallmark of ME/CFS (ch08)

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- (a) ME/CFS patients with lower CSF orexin-A should show reduced DTI-ALPS glymphatic index
- (b) Optogenetic or pharmacological restoration of orexin signaling in animal models should restore LC NE infraslow oscillations and improve glymphatic metrics
- (c) ME/CFS patients should show reduced coherence of LC NE oscillations measurable via pupillometry (LC-driven pupil oscillations during drowsiness) compared to controls

**Certainty: 0.35** (mechanistically sound; each individual link established; the full triad chain is novel synthesis)

### 1.2 PGE2--EP3 as the Upstream Master Disruptor of Sleep Architecture

**Mechanistic chain:** The document already notes PGE2 suppresses orexin neurons via EP3 receptors (ch15). Combined with the orexin--vasomotion link above, PGE2 from chronic neuroinflammation becomes an upstream disruptor of the *entire* sleep quality cascade:

PGE2 --> EP3 on orexin neurons --> reduced orexin tone --> impaired LC NE oscillatory quality --> reduced vasomotion amplitude --> impaired glymphatic clearance --> metabolite accumulation (tau, A-beta, adenosine) --> further microglial activation --> more PGE2

This is a *closed positive feedback loop* entirely within the CNS, independent of peripheral immune activation after initiation. Once established, peripheral immune resolution cannot break it because the CNS PGE2 source (activated microglia) is self-sustaining.

**What's genuinely novel:** The feedback closure --- glymphatic failure feeds back to neuroinflammation (via metabolite accumulation activating microglia), which produces more PGE2, which further suppresses orexin, which further impairs glymphatic clearance. This makes the sleep--glymphatic axis not just a downstream consequence but a *self-amplifying disease maintenance mechanism*.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- (a) CSF PGE2 levels should correlate inversely with CSF orexin-A in ME/CFS patients
- (b) EP3 receptor antagonists should improve sleep architecture (increase delta power, reduce alpha intrusion) in ME/CFS --- testable in animal models first
- (c) COX-2 inhibitors (which reduce PGE2) should transiently improve sleep quality in ME/CFS --- measurable by overnight PSG delta power

**Certainty: 0.30** (each link established; the self-sustaining loop architecture is novel hypothesis)

### 1.3 Quadruple Hit on Glymphatic Clearance: Adding Orexin to the Triple Hit

**The existing triple hit** (ch15): LC-NE dysfunction + impaired SWS + reduced arterial pulsatility (OI/POTS).

**The fourth hit:** Orexin deficiency disrupts not just the LC-NE oscillation but also the coordination between sleep stages. Orexin neurons regulate the *transitions* between NREM stages. With reduced orexin, transitions become unstable --- frequent micro-arousals, fragmented NREM, and failure to sustain the prolonged N3 episodes needed for maximal glymphatic flow (Fultz 2019 showed CSF pulsation requires sustained slow-wave episodes, not brief ones).

**Additional mechanistic detail:** Orexin-B (which may be preserved or even elevated in ME/CFS per Lopez-Amador 2025) has distinct receptor binding (OX2R vs OX1R). OX2R is concentrated in the tuberomammillary nucleus (histamine wake system), while OX1R is concentrated in the LC. If orexin-A (OX1R-preferring) is selectively reduced while orexin-B (OX2R-preferring) is preserved, the result is: histamine arousal system remains partially active (contributing to "tired but wired") while LC modulation is impaired (disrupting NE oscillation quality).

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients should show a higher orexin-B/orexin-A ratio than controls, and this ratio should correlate with subjective "wired but tired" severity and inversely with objective SWS duration.

**Certainty: 0.30** (the OX1R/OX2R differential is established neuroscience; its application to ME/CFS orexin data is extrapolation)

### 1.4 Thalamocortical--Glymphatic Resonance Failure

**Connection to existing hypothesis:** Chapter 8 describes CaV3.1 T-type calcium channel disruption as a mechanism for alpha-delta sleep. The Hauglund 2025 data adds a new dimension: the NE infraslow oscillation (0.05 Hz) drives vasomotion at a specific frequency. Thalamocortical delta oscillations (0.5-4 Hz) nest *within* these slower NE oscillations --- the two must be phase-locked for optimal glymphatic flow.

**The ME/CFS scenario:** If CaV3.1 channels are dysfunctional (producing alpha instead of delta oscillations), the thalamocortical oscillations may no longer phase-lock properly with the NE infraslow rhythm. The result is not just impaired sleep quality but specifically impaired resonance between the neural oscillation (which drives blood volume changes per Fultz 2019) and the vascular oscillation (which drives CSF pulsation). Both the neural and vascular rhythms are present but *decoupled*.

**What's genuinely novel:** No paper has discussed the *phase-coupling* between thalamocortical oscillations and NE infraslow oscillations in the context of glymphatic clearance. This predicts that alpha-delta sleep is worse for glymphatic function than simply reduced delta power, because it produces an *incoherent* oscillatory pattern that cannot drive coordinated fluid dynamics.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- (a) Phase-amplitude coupling between infraslow (0.05 Hz) and delta (0.5-4 Hz) EEG oscillations should be reduced in ME/CFS vs controls during NREM
- (b) Alpha-delta sleep should show worse DTI-ALPS scores than low-delta-without-alpha sleep (distinguishing frequency disruption from amplitude disruption)
- (c) Interventions that specifically restore delta frequency (not just increase total slow-wave power) should improve glymphatic metrics

**Certainty: 0.25** (phase-coupling in glymphatic physiology is emerging; the specific alpha-delta decoupling prediction is novel)

### 1.5 The Orexin--Autonomic--Vascular Convergence on Arterial Pulsatility

**Mechanistic chain:** Orexin neurons project to sympathetic preganglionic neurons and regulate cardiovascular sympathetic tone. Reduced orexin-A contributes to the autonomic instability in ME/CFS (POTS, NMH). Reduced arterial pulsatility from autonomic dysfunction is one leg of the triple hit on glymphatic clearance. But there is a more specific connection:

Arterial vasomotion (the slow oscillations in vessel diameter that drive perivascular CSF flow) depends on vascular smooth muscle tone, which is regulated by sympathetic NE release from perivascular nerve terminals. These perivascular sympathetic nerves receive descending control from the brainstem, which receives orexinergic input. So orexin deficiency impairs glymphatic clearance via *two independent routes*: (1) LC NE oscillation quality, and (2) peripheral vascular sympathetic regulation of arterial vasomotion.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with documented POTS/NMH (as a proxy for autonomic dysfunction severity) should show worse glymphatic clearance (DTI-ALPS) than ME/CFS patients without OI, independent of sleep quality measures.

**Certainty: 0.25** (each link established separately; the dual-route convergence is novel)

### 1.6 Glymphatic Failure as a Mechanism for Post-Exertional Malaise Cognitive Component

**Mechanistic chain:** PEM in ME/CFS characteristically includes cognitive worsening ("brain fog crash"). Exercise increases neuronal metabolic activity, producing waste products (including extracellular potassium, lactate, adenosine, and potentially tau fragments). In healthy individuals, the next sleep episode clears this waste via glymphatic flow. In ME/CFS with impaired glymphatic clearance, exercise-generated waste accumulates *additively* because the overnight clearance is insufficient.

This predicts a *ratchet effect*: each day's cognitive and physical activity adds waste that is only partially cleared overnight. The cumulative effect manifests as progressive cognitive deterioration over days of normal-for-the-patient activity, with recovery requiring multiple days of rest (during which even the impaired glymphatic system eventually catches up).

**What's genuinely novel:** PEM is usually explained via metabolic (energy depletion, NCX reversal) and immune (cytokine, complement) mechanisms. Adding glymphatic failure provides a mechanism specifically for the *cognitive* component of PEM and explains why cognitive PEM can occur independently of muscular PEM --- the brain has its own waste accumulation dynamic independent of muscle damage.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- (a) Cognitive PEM severity should correlate with objective measures of sleep quality (delta power, SWS duration) from the preceding night
- (b) Plasma NfL or p-tau should show transient increases after cognitive exertion in ME/CFS patients but not controls
- (c) Interventions that improve SWS quality should selectively improve cognitive PEM recovery more than muscular PEM recovery

**Certainty: 0.25** (glymphatic role in exercise recovery is established in sports science; application to PEM is extrapolation)

### 1.7 Orexin Deficiency as a Contributor to the Nocturnal Cramp Phenotype

**Connection to existing content:** Chapter 15 describes nocturnal cramps from ATP-dependent SERCA pump failure. Orexin neurons modulate spinal motor neuron excitability during sleep. In narcolepsy (severe orexin deficiency), patients experience cataplexy --- sudden loss of muscle tone --- and paradoxical motor activation during REM. In the milder orexin reduction of ME/CFS, the impaired motor neuron inhibition during sleep could contribute to heightened motor excitability, lowering the threshold for cramps in muscles already vulnerable due to ATP depletion.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with lower CSF orexin-A should report more frequent nocturnal cramps than those with higher orexin-A levels, independent of electrolyte status.

**Certainty: 0.20** (speculative; the motor inhibition role of orexin during sleep is established but the connection to cramps is inferential)

### 1.8 Chronobiological Phase Separation: Orexin Rhythm vs Cortisol Rhythm

**Mechanistic chain:** ME/CFS patients show flattened cortisol diurnal rhythm (ch09 endocrine). Orexin neurons receive input from the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) and show circadian variation. If neuroinflammatory PGE2 suppression of orexin is tonic (constant) rather than phasic (varying with cytokine rhythms), the orexin rhythm may flatten or shift independently of the cortisol rhythm.

**What's genuinely novel:** If the two master circadian outputs (cortisol from HPA axis, orexin from hypothalamus) become desynchronised with respect to each other, the result is a form of *internal desynchrony* distinct from simple circadian delay. This could produce the characteristic ME/CFS pattern where patients feel "out of phase" --- not merely shifted but *incoherent*, with alternating periods of paradoxical alertness and sudden crashes throughout the day.

**Falsifiable predictions:**
- (a) 24-hour orexin-A profiling (serial CSF or validated saliva proxy) in ME/CFS should show reduced amplitude and/or phase shift relative to cortisol rhythm
- (b) The degree of orexin-cortisol phase separation should correlate with subjective symptom variability (assessed by hourly symptom logging)

**Certainty: 0.20** (orexin circadian biology established; its dissociation from cortisol in ME/CFS is untested speculation)

## 2. Research Directions

### 2.1 DTI-ALPS Glymphatic Imaging in ME/CFS (HIGH PRIORITY)

**Design:** Cross-sectional, n=80 ME/CFS patients + 40 matched controls
**Measures:** DTI-ALPS index, overnight PSG (delta power, alpha-delta index, SWS percentage), autonomic testing (tilt table), CSF orexin-A (optional lumbar puncture subsample n=20)
**Stratify by:** Disease duration (<3yr, 3-7yr, >7yr), severity (mild/moderate/severe), OI status
**Primary hypothesis:** ME/CFS patients show reduced DTI-ALPS vs controls
**Secondary hypotheses:**
  - DTI-ALPS correlates with brain fog severity (MoCA, trail-making)
  - DTI-ALPS correlates with delta power on PSG
  - DTI-ALPS correlates inversely with disease duration
  - OI-positive patients show worse DTI-ALPS than OI-negative patients
**Feasibility:** DTI-ALPS is a standard MRI sequence (~10 min additional scan time). Protocol available from Chaganti 2025 (Long COVID). Can be added to any existing ME/CFS neuroimaging study.
**Cost:** Moderate (MRI time + PSG nights). Lumbar puncture subsample adds complexity but is optional.

**Secondary:** Orexin-B/A ratio correlates with "tired but wired" severity; orexin-A correlates with delta power and DTI-ALPS

### 2.4 Pupillometry as a Non-Invasive LC Oscillation Proxy

**Design:** Cross-sectional, n=100 ME/CFS + 50 controls
**Measures:** Infrared pupillometry during quiet wakefulness and drowsiness onset (5-minute recording), measuring infraslow pupil oscillations (which reflect LC NE oscillatory activity)
**Primary hypothesis:** ME/CFS patients show reduced coherence and amplitude of infraslow pupil oscillations
**Advantages:** Non-invasive, cheap (consumer eye tracker), scalable, suitable for home assessment
**Limitations:** Pupil oscillations are an indirect LC proxy; medications (especially adrenergic agents) confound
**Novel contribution:** First non-invasive test of LC oscillatory integrity in ME/CFS

**Measures:** Sleep EEG (delta power, SWS continuity), LC NE microdialysis (oscillation pattern), glymphatic clearance (fluorescent tracer), wheel running (fatigue proxy), CSF tau
**Primary hypothesis:** EP3 blockade restores orexin neuron activity, normalises LC NE oscillations, and improves glymphatic clearance
**Feasibility:** All techniques established; EP3 antagonists available as research tools

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### 3.1 Low-Dose Suvorexant Paradox: When to Use an Orexin Antagonist Despite Orexin Deficiency

**Rationale:** Counterintuitive, but low-dose orexin receptor antagonists (DORAs: suvorexant 5-10mg, lemborexant 2.5mg) could *improve* sleep quality by consolidating NREM architecture, even in an orexin-deficient state. The logic: if residual orexin tone is producing disorganised LC activity (the "intermediate state" from hypothesis 1.1), blocking even this residual activity during intended sleep periods could allow the LC to settle into a more coherent oscillatory pattern.

**Critical caveat from Zhu 2025 integration:** DORAs may suppress glymphatic flow. The trade-off is: better NREM consolidation (pro-glymphatic) vs reduced NE oscillation drive (anti-glymphatic). The net effect is unknown.

**Testable prediction:** Suvorexant 5mg should increase NREM delta power (positive) but may reduce DTI-ALPS metrics (negative). If both are measured, the net clinical outcome resolves the trade-off.

**Risk level:** LOW at low doses (FDA-approved, extensive safety data)
**Certainty: 0.25** (mechanistically plausible; the paradox makes this genuinely uncertain)

---

### 3.2 Selective EP3 Receptor Antagonist for Orexin Circuit Restoration

**Rationale:** PGE2 via EP3 is the likely proximal cause of orexin neuron suppression in ME/CFS. EP3 antagonists (no approved drugs; research compounds: L-798,106, DG-041) could selectively restore orexin neuron activity without broad anti-inflammatory effects.

**Advantage over NSAIDs/COX-2 inhibitors:** EP3 antagonism preserves EP2/EP4 signaling (needed for vascular and immune homeostasis) while specifically blocking the suppressive effect on orexin neurons.

**Development stage:** Research-only. DG-041 was in Phase II for thrombosis (different EP3 function). Repurposing would require new trials.

**Falsifiable prediction:** EP3 antagonist administration should increase CSF orexin-A within 24 hours (acute pharmacodynamic endpoint).

**Risk level:** UNKNOWN (no human sleep-context safety data)
**Certainty: 0.20** (target is valid; drug availability is the bottleneck)

---

### 3.3 Trazodone for Dual Sleep-Glymphatic Benefit

**Rationale:** Trazodone at low dose (25-50mg) increases SWS without significantly suppressing REM. Unlike Z-drugs (which suppress NE oscillations, Hauglund 2025), trazodone acts via 5-HT2A antagonism and weak alpha-1 blockade. Serotonin inhibits LC NE neurons; by blocking 5-HT2A (which is a *net-excitatory* serotonin receptor on some interneurons), trazodone may paradoxically permit more coherent LC NE oscillations during NREM.

**Caveat:** Trazodone does have alpha-1 blocking activity, which Zhu 2025 flags as potentially reducing vasomotion amplitude. However, the alpha-1 effect at 25-50mg is much weaker than at full antidepressant doses (150-300mg).

**Evidence:** A 2020 Alzheimer's study (Cordella et al.) found that trazodone increased SWS and *reduced* CSF tau accumulation in AD patients over 6 months --- direct evidence for the SWS-glymphatic-tau link in humans.

**Testable prediction:** Low-dose trazodone should improve DTI-ALPS scores in ME/CFS patients, measurable at 3-month follow-up.

**Risk level:** LOW (well-established drug, extensive safety data at low doses)
**Certainty: 0.40** (strongest candidate for glymphatic-friendly sleep medication in ME/CFS)

---

### 3.4 Sodium Oxybate (Xyrem) for Maximal SWS Enhancement

**Rationale:** Sodium oxybate (gamma-hydroxybutyrate, GHB) is the most potent SWS-enhancing drug known. Unlike Z-drugs, it acts via GABA-B and GHB receptors and powerfully consolidates delta sleep. It was tested in fibromyalgia (related sleep disorder) with significant SWS improvement and symptom benefit before commercial/regulatory complications.

**Glymphatic angle:** By producing deep, sustained SWS, sodium oxybate may maximise the conditions for glymphatic clearance --- provided it does not disrupt the NE oscillation pattern. GHB acts upstream of the LC (via GABA-B-mediated inhibition), which could actually *facilitate* the infraslow NE oscillation by providing a strong inhibitory baseline that the oscillation rides upon.

**Testable prediction:** Sodium oxybate should produce the largest DTI-ALPS improvement of any sleep medication in ME/CFS, measurable within 2 weeks.

**Risk level:** MODERATE-HIGH (Schedule I in many countries, abuse potential, respiratory depression risk, strictly regulated)
**Certainty: 0.40** for SWS effect; **0.25** for glymphatic effect (untested)

---

### 3.5 Modafinil/Pitolisant for Daytime Orexin Circuit Compensation

**Rationale:** If orexin deficiency drives daytime fatigue, wake-promoting agents used in narcolepsy (the prototypical orexin deficiency) might help. Pitolisant (Wakix) is a histamine H3 inverse agonist that enhances histamine release and indirectly activates orexin circuits. It was approved for narcolepsy and has a different mechanism from traditional stimulants.

**ME/CFS-specific concern:** Stimulants risk masking fatigue signals and enabling overexertion, worsening PEM. Any wake-promoting agent in ME/CFS must be paired with strict pacing protocols. The risk is that patients feel artificially well and exceed their energy envelope.

**Potential advantage of pitolisant over modafinil:** Pitolisant may restore the *quality* of wakefulness (via orexin circuit activation) rather than merely suppressing sleepiness, and may improve sleep quality at night by normalising the circadian orexin rhythm (better wake = better subsequent sleep).

**Testable prediction:** Pitolisant should reduce daytime sleepiness (ESS) while preserving or improving overnight delta power (unlike modafinil, which can disrupt sleep).

**Risk level:** MODERATE (approved drug but risk of PEM exacerbation through activity masking)
**Certainty: 0.30**

### 3.6 Ibudilast (MN-166): PDE4/PDE10 Inhibitor for Neuroinflammation + Glymphatic Support

**Rationale:** Ibudilast crosses the blood-brain barrier, inhibits PDE4 and PDE10, suppresses microglial activation, and reduces TLR4 signaling. By reducing CNS neuroinflammation, it should reduce PGE2 production, potentially restoring orexin neuron function. Additionally, PDE4 inhibition increases cAMP, which can enhance AQP4 trafficking to astrocytic endfeet --- potentially improving glymphatic flow directly.

**Cross-disease bridge:** Ibudilast is in Phase II/III trials for progressive MS (neuroinflammation, brain atrophy) and ALS. If it shows brain volume preservation in MS (via reduced waste accumulation + reduced inflammation), the glymphatic mechanism would be supported.

**Testable prediction:** Ibudilast should simultaneously reduce CSF inflammatory markers (PGE2, IL-6), increase CSF orexin-A, and improve DTI-ALPS in ME/CFS.

**Risk level:** MODERATE (Phase II safety data available; GI side effects common)
**Certainty: 0.25** (multi-target mechanism is a strength but also means unpredictable interactions)

### 3.7 Fluvoxamine Low-Dose for Sigma-1 Receptor Agonism and Glial Modulation

**Rationale:** Fluvoxamine at low dose (25-50mg) is a potent sigma-1 receptor agonist. Sigma-1 receptors are concentrated on MAMs (mitochondria-associated ER membranes) and regulate ER-mitochondrial calcium transfer, ER stress responses, and microglial polarization. Sigma-1 agonism promotes anti-inflammatory M2 microglial phenotype, which would reduce PGE2 production and potentially restore orexin signaling.

**Additional angle:** Sigma-1 modulates IP3 receptor activity at MAMs. In the context of the document's IP3/PIP2 depletion hypothesis (ch14h), sigma-1 agonism could partially compensate for IP3R dysfunction by sensitizing remaining receptors.

**Testable prediction:** Low-dose fluvoxamine should shift CSF microglial markers toward M2 phenotype and improve sleep quality (delta power) within 4 weeks.

**Risk level:** LOW-MODERATE (SSRI side effects, but well-tolerated at low dose; used in Long COVID trials --- TOGETHER trial showed benefit)
**Certainty: 0.25**

### 3.8 Ethosuximide Revisited: T-Type Block + Glymphatic Implications

**Cross-reference to existing open question:** ch08 @oq:ch08-ethosuximide-direction asks whether T-type calcium channel block would help or worsen alpha-delta sleep. The glymphatic data adds a new dimension: if ethosuximide *does* restore delta frequency, it should also restore thalamocortical-vasomotion phase coupling, amplifying glymphatic benefits beyond just improving subjective sleep quality.

**Conversely:** If ethosuximide worsens delta (because CaV3.1 is already hypoactive), it would further decouple the oscillatory systems and worsen glymphatic clearance. The DTI-ALPS metric provides a new endpoint for the ethosuximide direction question.

**Updated research design:** Add DTI-ALPS pre/post to the single-dose ethosuximide crossover trial proposed in ch08. This transforms a sleep-quality study into a glymphatic-function study.

**Certainty: 0.30** (the addition of glymphatic endpoints strengthens the rationale for doing the trial)

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### 4.1 Phosphatidylserine (PS) for Cortisol Rhythm + Glymphatic Support

**Rationale:** PS (600mg/day) is documented to blunt excessive cortisol responses. In ME/CFS with flattened cortisol rhythm, the goal is not to reduce overall cortisol but to restore the amplitude of the diurnal rhythm. PS taken in the evening could help reduce evening cortisol (which should be at its nadir), deepening the cortisol trough and potentially improving SWS onset.

**Glymphatic angle:** Cortisol is a glucocorticoid that upregulates AQP4 expression. The timing matters: if evening cortisol is inappropriately elevated (flattened rhythm), AQP4 expression timing may be disrupted. Restoring the cortisol nadir could normalise AQP4 circadian expression, improving glymphatic readiness during sleep.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Evening PS supplementation should increase overnight delta power and improve morning cognitive clarity disproportionately to any subjective sleep improvement.

**Certainty: 0.20** (PS-cortisol link established; AQP4-cortisol timing is extrapolation)

---

### 4.2 Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA) for Neuroinflammatory PGE2 Reduction

**Rationale:** EPA competes with arachidonic acid (AA) for COX-2, shifting prostaglandin production from PGE2 (pro-inflammatory, EP3-mediated orexin suppression) toward PGE3 (much weaker at EP3). DHA is incorporated into neuronal membranes and produces resolvins and protectins that actively resolve neuroinflammation.

**Dose matters:** Most ME/CFS supplement protocols use modest omega-3 doses (1-2g/day). The anti-neuroinflammatory effect likely requires higher doses (3-4g EPA+DHA/day) to significantly shift the AA/EPA ratio in CNS membranes, which takes 8-12 weeks of consistent supplementation due to slow CNS lipid turnover.

**Glymphatic angle:** By reducing PGE2, high-dose omega-3 could partially restore orexin neuron function. Additionally, DHA incorporation into astrocytic membranes may affect AQP4 polarization (AQP4 is sensitive to membrane lipid composition).

**Falsifiable prediction:** 12 weeks of high-dose omega-3 (4g EPA+DHA/day) should reduce CSF PGE2 and improve overnight delta power compared to baseline.

**Certainty: 0.30** (anti-inflammatory effects well-established; specific PGE2-->orexin chain in ME/CFS is extrapolation)

---

### 4.3 Magnesium L-Threonate for CNS Magnesium + Sleep Architecture

**Rationale:** Magnesium L-threonate is the only magnesium formulation documented to cross the blood-brain barrier and increase brain magnesium levels. Brain magnesium:
- Modulates NMDA receptor activity (reducing excitotoxicity, potentially reducing central sensitization)
- Supports GABAergic inhibition (needed for SWS)
- Is a cofactor for NE synthesis (tyrosine hydroxylase requires Mg2+)
- Modulates T-type calcium channels (CaV3.1 is Mg2+-sensitive)

**Glymphatic angle:** Mg2+ modulates vascular smooth muscle tone and vasomotion. Optimal intracellular Mg2+ is required for the vascular smooth muscle oscillations that constitute vasomotion. Mg2+ also regulates AQP4 channel gating.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Magnesium L-threonate (2g/day x 8 weeks) should increase NREM delta power and reduce alpha-delta index on overnight PSG compared to magnesium glycinate (which does not cross BBB --- active comparator controls for peripheral magnesium effects).

**Certainty: 0.30** (BBB-crossing documented; sleep architecture effects plausible but not yet tested in ME/CFS)

---

### 4.4 Boswellia Serrata (AKBA) for 5-LOX Inhibition + Neuroprotection

**Rationale:** Acetyl-11-keto-beta-boswellic acid (AKBA) inhibits 5-lipoxygenase, reducing leukotriene synthesis. While PGE2 (COX pathway) gets attention for orexin suppression, leukotrienes (5-LOX pathway) also contribute to neuroinflammation and blood-brain barrier dysfunction. AKBA additionally inhibits cathepsin G and NF-kB.

**ME/CFS-specific angle:** 5-LOX products (LTB4, LTC4) increase BBB permeability, potentially allowing more peripheral cytokines to access the hypothalamus and amplify PGE2-mediated orexin suppression. Reducing 5-LOX activity could reduce this "peripheral-to-central" inflammatory bridge.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Boswellia supplementation (standardised AKBA extract, 300mg/day) should reduce CSF inflammatory markers and improve sleep quality over 8 weeks.

**Certainty: 0.20** (anti-inflammatory properties established; CNS penetration and ME/CFS-specific effects are uncertain)

---

### 4.5 Glycine (3g Bedtime) for SWS Enhancement via Hypothalamic Cooling

**Rationale:** Glycine supplementation (3g before bed) improves subjective sleep quality and reduces next-day fatigue in healthy adults and insomnia patients. The mechanism: glycine acts on NMDA receptors in the suprachiasmatic nucleus, causing peripheral vasodilation that *lowers core body temperature* --- a critical trigger for SWS onset.

**ME/CFS-specific angle:** Many ME/CFS patients have impaired thermoregulation (autonomic dysfunction). The core temperature drop that normally initiates SWS may be blunted. Glycine-induced peripheral vasodilation could partially bypass the failed autonomic thermoregulatory mechanism.

**Glymphatic angle:** If glycine improves SWS quality (not just onset), it would enhance the slow-wave-driven component of glymphatic clearance (Fultz 2019 mechanism).

**Practical advantages:** Extremely safe, inexpensive, pleasant-tasting, no prescription required.

**Falsifiable prediction:** 3g glycine at bedtime should increase overnight delta power and reduce sleep onset latency in ME/CFS patients with documented thermoregulatory dysfunction, measurable on home EEG within 2 weeks.

**Certainty: 0.35** (glycine-sleep benefit established in healthy; ME/CFS-specific thermoregulatory bypass is extrapolation)

---

### 4.6 Taurine for GABAergic Support + Vascular Vasomotion

**Rationale:** Taurine is a GABA-A receptor agonist and glycine receptor modulator. It crosses the BBB and has dose-dependent sedative effects. Relevant to the sleep-glymphatic axis via:
- GABAergic enhancement of SWS
- Vascular smooth muscle relaxation (supporting vasomotion)
- Antioxidant properties (protecting against NO/ONOO- cycle)
- Osmoregulatory function (may support AQP4-dependent fluid dynamics)

**Dosing:** 1-3g before bed. Well-tolerated. No significant drug interactions at supplemental doses.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Taurine 2g before bed should improve subjective sleep quality scores and morning cognitive performance in ME/CFS within 4 weeks.

**Certainty: 0.25** (multiple mechanistic angles; none specifically tested in ME/CFS sleep context)

---

### 4.7 Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) Night Dosing for Microglial Modulation During Sleep

**Rationale:** PEA is already in the document as a neuroinflammation modulator (ch15 therapeutic implications). The insight here is *timing*: PEA taken at bedtime may have disproportionate benefit because microglial metabolic activity peaks during NREM sleep (microglia are involved in synaptic pruning during SWS). Modulating microglial polarization specifically during this active period could reduce PGE2 production at the time when orexin neurons need to be silent but LC NE oscillations need to be preserved.

**Falsifiable prediction:** PEA 600mg at bedtime should improve overnight delta power more than morning dosing, measurable by home EEG.

**Certainty: 0.20** (timing hypothesis is novel; PEA anti-neuroinflammatory effects are established)

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### 5.1 Acoustic Slow-Wave Enhancement (Phase-Locked Auditory Stimulation)

**Rationale:** Phase-locked auditory stimulation (pink noise timed to the up-phase of slow oscillations) has been shown to enhance delta power, improve memory consolidation, and increase SWS depth in healthy adults and mild cognitive impairment. Commercial devices now exist (e.g., Philips SmartSleep).

**ME/CFS-specific value:** If the thalamocortical oscillation circuit is *capable* of delta but unstable (intermittently falling into alpha), acoustic entrainment could stabilise delta oscillations and prevent alpha intrusion. This could restore the thalamocortical-vasomotion phase coupling needed for glymphatic clearance (hypothesis 1.4).

**Advantage:** Non-pharmacological, no drug interactions, usable nightly, no risk of NE oscillation suppression (unlike Z-drugs).

**Practical considerations:** ME/CFS patients with sound sensitivity may not tolerate; volume must be individually calibrated. The devices deliver sound through bone conduction or low-volume speakers.

**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of nightly phase-locked acoustic stimulation should increase NREM delta power and reduce alpha-delta index on PSG in ME/CFS patients vs sham condition.

**Certainty: 0.35** (technique validated in other populations; ME/CFS-specific application untested)

---

### 5.2 Controlled Head-Down Tilt Sleeping Position for Glymphatic Enhancement

**Rationale:** Glymphatic flow is gravity-dependent. Rodent studies show that lateral sleeping position enhances glymphatic clearance vs supine or prone. Some evidence suggests slight head-down tilt (3-5 degrees) increases intracranial CSF pressure and may enhance perivascular flow.

**ME/CFS-specific caution:** Patients with OI/POTS may poorly tolerate head-down tilt due to increased cerebral blood volume. However, patients with *excessive* sympathetic activation might actually benefit from the baroreceptor-mediated sympatholytic effect of mild head-down tilt.

**Alternative:** Left lateral sleeping position (which improves glymphatic clearance in rodent models) could be encouraged without tilt, avoiding OI issues.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Lateral sleeping position should be associated with better morning cognitive function in ME/CFS patients (testable via actigraphy + morning cognitive testing over multiple nights).

**Certainty: 0.20** (rodent data on position + glymphatic is established; clinical translation uncertain)

---

### 5.3 Bilateral Alternating Stimulation (BAS) for LC NE Oscillation Entrainment

**Rationale:** Bilateral alternating sensory stimulation (e.g., alternating left-right gentle tactile or auditory stimulation at 0.05 Hz --- the NE infraslow frequency) could potentially entrain the LC NE oscillation. This is inspired by EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing) therapy, which uses bilateral stimulation and incidentally affects LC NE dynamics.

**Speculative but mechanistically anchored:** The LC integrates bilateral sensory input. Rhythmic bilateral stimulation at the infraslow frequency might serve as an external pacemaker for LC oscillatory activity during sleep onset, compensating for the impaired orexin-driven oscillatory patterning.

**Practical:** Could be implemented as a pre-sleep relaxation protocol using alternating left-right vibration pads (commercially available for EMDR home use) set at 0.05 Hz for 15 minutes before lights-out.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-sleep 0.05 Hz bilateral stimulation should increase infraslow EEG power during subsequent N2/N3 sleep compared to control nights.

**Certainty: 0.15** (highly speculative; the LC bilateral input and oscillatory entrainment are both established but the specific therapeutic application is novel)

---

### 5.4 Temperature Manipulation: Cool Forehead Protocol for SWS Enhancement

**Rationale:** Frontal cerebral thermal manipulation (cooling the forehead to ~14-16C with a gel pad or commercial device) has been shown to reduce frontal cortical metabolic rate and enhance SWS onset in insomnia patients (Nofzinger et al., Brain Cooling study). The mechanism: reduced frontal metabolism allows global cortical slow oscillations to emerge more readily.

**ME/CFS-specific angle:** ME/CFS patients may have elevated frontal metabolic activity during attempted sleep (consistent with alpha intrusion and racing thoughts). Frontal cooling could provide a non-pharmacological way to suppress this aberrant activation and promote delta oscillations.

**Practical:** Commercial devices exist (Ebb Insomnia Therapy, though no longer marketed; DIY with gel pads feasible). Simple, no side effects, compatible with all medications.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Frontal cooling during sleep onset should reduce alpha-delta index and increase delta power in ME/CFS patients with documented alpha intrusion.

**Certainty: 0.30** (technique validated in primary insomnia; alpha-delta-specific application is extrapolation)

---

### 5.5 Morning Bright Light + Evening Darkness Protocol for Orexin Rhythm Restoration

**Rationale:** The document already covers circadian alignment (ch17 lifestyle). The orexin insight adds a specific mechanism: morning bright light activates SCN-->orexin neuron projections, potentially boosting the morning orexin peak even in a state of reduced total orexin production. This could increase the amplitude of the orexin circadian rhythm even if the mean level remains low.

**Novel angle:** The goal is not just circadian alignment but specifically *orexin rhythm amplitude maximisation*. This means the protocol should be more aggressive than standard sleep hygiene: 30-60 minutes of >10,000 lux within 30 minutes of waking, combined with strict darkness (<1 lux) from 2 hours before bed.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Strict bright light/darkness protocol for 4 weeks should increase the morning/evening ratio of salivary cortisol (circadian amplitude proxy) and reduce "tired but wired" evening symptoms.

**Certainty: 0.35** (circadian phototherapy well-established; orexin rhythm amplification via light is mechanistically grounded but not specifically tested in ME/CFS)

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 Glymphatic Clearance ODE for the Bistability Framework

The existing bistability model (ch33) represents disease state as an attractor. The glymphatic system adds a *time-delayed negative feedback* that can either stabilise the healthy attractor or trap the system in the disease attractor:

```
dW/dt = P(A) - C(S, V, O) * W

where:
  W = brain waste concentration (tau, A-beta, metabolic debris)
  P(A) = waste production rate (function of neural Activity A)
  C(S, V, O) = clearance rate (function of SWS quality S, Vasomotion amplitude V, Orexin function O)

  C = C_max * f_SWS(S) * f_vaso(V) * f_orexin(O)

  f_SWS(S) = S^n / (S^n + K_S^n)    [Hill function, cooperative]
  f_vaso(V) = V / (V + K_V)          [Michaelis-Menten]
  f_orexin(O) = O^2 / (O^2 + K_O^2) [steeper: orexin has threshold effect on LC oscillation quality]
```

**Feedback closure:** Accumulated waste W feeds back to increase neuroinflammation, which:
- Reduces O (orexin) via PGE2: `dO/dt = O_prod * g(PGE2) - delta_O * O`, where `PGE2 ~ W` (waste activates microglia)
- Reduces S (SWS) via disrupted thalamocortical dynamics
- Reduces V (vasomotion) via NE oscillation disruption

**Prediction from model:** The system has two stable states:
- **Healthy:** Low W, high C, W stays low (self-correcting)
- **Disease:** High W, low C (via low O, low S, low V), W stays high (self-reinforcing)

**Therapeutic implication from model:** Pushing C above a critical threshold (by improving S, V, or O) should allow the system to cross the separatrix back to the healthy attractor. The model predicts that multi-node intervention (improving all three of S, V, O simultaneously) has a *multiplicative* effect on C, potentially crossing the threshold even if no single intervention is sufficient alone.

**Certainty: 0.30** (model structure is sound; parameter values are entirely uncalibrated)

---

### 7.2 Timescale Separation in Glymphatic Dynamics

The existing timescale hierarchy (ch33) can incorporate glymphatic dynamics:

- **Fast (seconds-minutes):** NE oscillation, vasomotion, CSF pulsation
- **Medium (hours):** Nightly glymphatic clearance cycle, waste accumulation/clearance
- **Slow (days-weeks):** Cumulative waste load, microglial activation state, orexin neuron function
- **Ultra-slow (months-years):** Protein aggregation, potential neurodegeneration

**Key insight:** Therapeutic interventions operate at different timescales. Sleep medication acts on the fast/medium scale (immediate SWS improvement). Anti-inflammatory intervention acts on the slow scale (weeks to reduce PGE2). Neurodegeneration prevention operates on the ultra-slow scale (years of sustained clearance improvement needed).

**Clinical implication:** The model predicts that acute sleep improvement (single night) has minimal glymphatic benefit because the slow-scale waste load and microglial state have not changed. *Sustained* sleep quality improvement over weeks is needed to shift the slow variables, which then feed forward to further improve the fast dynamics (positive feedback in the recovery direction).

---

### 7.3 Stochastic Crash Model with Glymphatic Component

Extend the PEM/crash model with a glymphatic waste state variable:

```
W(t+1) = W(t) + P(activity_t) - C(sleep_quality_t) + noise

Crash probability: Pr(crash | W) = sigmoid(W - W_threshold)
```

Where `P(activity)` includes both physical and cognitive exertion, and `C(sleep_quality)` is reduced in ME/CFS. The model predicts:
- Crashes are more likely after nights of poor sleep (reduced C), even without increased activity
- Cognitive crashes have a *delayed* onset (waste accumulates over days before crossing threshold)
- The threshold W_threshold itself may decrease over time (sensitisation), explaining why patients become more crash-prone with disease progression

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a daily diary study with home EEG, prior-night delta power should predict next-day crash probability, with a 24-48 hour lag for cognitive crashes.

**Certainty: 0.25**

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### 8.1 ME/CFS--Narcolepsy Spectrum

**Bridge mechanism:** Both diseases involve orexin dysfunction but at different severities:
- Narcolepsy Type 1: Complete orexin neuron destruction (autoimmune)
- ME/CFS: Partial orexin suppression (neuroinflammatory PGE2, potentially reversible)
- Narcolepsy Type 2: Intermediate (reduced orexin without neuronal loss)

**Clinical implication:** ME/CFS could be on a spectrum with narcolepsy Type 2, distinguished by the *cause* (inflammation vs autoimmunity) rather than the *circuit* (both involve orexin). This suggests narcolepsy treatments (sodium oxybate, pitolisant, solriamfetol) might have partial efficacy in ME/CFS.

**Key difference:** In narcolepsy, orexin neuron loss is permanent. In ME/CFS, PGE2-mediated suppression is potentially reversible. This means anti-inflammatory approaches could restore orexin function in ME/CFS but not narcolepsy --- a testable discriminator.

**Existing document connection:** Hypothesis registry @oq:cross-disease-triangle already notes ME/CFS--narcolepsy overlap. This analysis adds the specific mechanistic bridge (PGE2-EP3 vs autoimmune destruction) and therapeutic implications.

**Certainty: 0.30**

---

### 8.2 ME/CFS--Alzheimer's Glymphatic Bridge

**Bridge mechanism:** Both diseases may share glymphatic failure as a pathophysiological node:
- AD: Age-related + AQP4 genetic variants + amyloid-driven neuroinflammation --> glymphatic failure --> waste accumulation --> more amyloid
- ME/CFS: Infection-triggered neuroinflammation + orexin suppression + autonomic dysfunction --> glymphatic failure --> waste accumulation --> sustained neuroinflammation

**Divergence:** In AD, the waste *is* the disease (amyloid/tau pathology). In ME/CFS, waste accumulation is a *disease maintenance* mechanism but not the primary pathology. This predicts that glymphatic restoration should slow AD progression but could potentially *resolve* ME/CFS (if the waste-neuroinflammation-orexin loop is the main maintenance mechanism).

**Research implication:** AD glymphatic trials (anti-amyloid + sleep optimisation combinations) could inform ME/CFS therapeutic strategies. Monitoring ME/CFS patients with AD biomarker panels tests whether chronic ME/CFS truly elevates neurodegeneration risk.

**Certainty: 0.25**

---

### 8.3 ME/CFS--Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI) Parallel

**Bridge mechanism:** TBI produces:
- Neuroinflammation (microglial activation)
- Impaired sleep architecture (reduced SWS)
- Autonomic dysfunction
- Cognitive impairment ("brain fog")
- Glymphatic disruption (documented by DTI-ALPS studies)
- Chronic fatigue (present in >50% of TBI patients at 1 year)

The symptom overlap with ME/CFS is striking. The mechanistic overlap via glymphatic failure may explain why post-TBI fatigue is so resistant to treatment --- the same self-sustaining PGE2-orexin-glymphatic loop may operate.

**Therapeutic implication:** Successful glymphatic-support interventions in TBI could be directly translated to ME/CFS, and vice versa. TBI research is better-funded and more advanced in glymphatic imaging.

**Falsifiable prediction:** Post-TBI patients with persistent fatigue should show similar DTI-ALPS reductions and orexin-A levels as ME/CFS patients of similar disease duration.

**Certainty: 0.30**

---

### 8.4 ME/CFS--Idiopathic Hypersomnia (IH)

**Bridge mechanism:** IH patients sleep excessively but non-restoratively --- similar to ME/CFS. Recent evidence suggests IH involves a "GABA-potentiating substance" in CSF. If this substance also exists in ME/CFS (which shares non-restorative sleep), it could interact with the orexin-glymphatic axis:

Excess GABAergic tone during sleep would suppress both thalamocortical delta oscillations (producing shallow, non-restorative sleep despite long duration) and LC NE oscillatory amplitude, impairing vasomotion and glymphatic clearance.

**Therapeutic cross-pollination:** Flumazenil (GABA-A antagonist) has shown benefit in some IH patients. If the "GABA-potentiating substance" is present in ME/CFS, low-dose flumazenil could improve SWS quality --- but only if the excess GABA is actually suppressing delta oscillations rather than facilitating them.

**Certainty: 0.15** (speculative bridge; the "GABA-potentiating substance" in IH is itself controversial)

## 9. Diagnostic and Biomarker Ideas

### 9.1 Home EEG Delta/Alpha Ratio as a Disease Activity Biomarker

**Existing spec in document:** @spec:ch08-sleep-eeg-proxy already proposes this. The glymphatic integration adds a specific mechanistic basis: the delta/alpha ratio is not just a "severity marker" but a *proxy for nightly glymphatic clearance capacity*. This elevates it from correlational biomarker to functional biomarker with treatment-response implications.

**Enhanced protocol:** Delta/alpha ratio + infraslow power (0.01-0.1 Hz band, if device supports it) = dual biomarker capturing both thalamocortical (delta) and NE vasomotion (infraslow) components. Consumer EEG devices with sufficient resolution (Dreem headband) can capture both.

**Certainty: 0.35** (rationale strengthened by glymphatic mechanism)

---

### 9.2 Pupillary Unrest Index (PUI) as LC NE Oscillation Biomarker

**Rationale:** The pupil shows spontaneous oscillations driven by LC NE activity. "Pupillary unrest in ambient light" (PUAL) or pupillary unrest index (PUI) is a validated measure of LC NE integrity in drowsiness studies. In ME/CFS, reduced/incoherent LC NE oscillations should produce abnormal PUI.

**Practical:** Requires only a smartphone or consumer eye tracker + 5-minute recording in dim light. Fully non-invasive, repeatable daily, suitable for telemedicine.

**Predicted ME/CFS pattern:** Reduced infraslow oscillation amplitude (reflecting impaired LC NE oscillation) combined with increased irregular high-frequency pupil fluctuations (reflecting autonomic dysregulation). The spectral shape of pupil oscillations should distinguish ME/CFS from healthy controls.

**Falsifiable prediction:** PUI infraslow component should correlate with overnight delta power from home EEG and with DTI-ALPS index.

**Certainty: 0.30** (PUI as LC proxy is established; ME/CFS application is novel)

---

### 9.3 Orexin-B/Orexin-A CSF Ratio as Diagnostic Discriminator

**Rationale:** From hypothesis 1.3: if orexin-A (OX1R-preferring) is selectively reduced while orexin-B (OX2R-preferring) is preserved, the B/A ratio becomes a diagnostic biomarker that distinguishes:
- ME/CFS: Elevated B/A ratio (PGE2-mediated suppression, which may preferentially affect orexin-A-producing neurons or OX1R-signaling feedback)
- Narcolepsy Type 1: Both reduced (neuronal destruction)
- Idiopathic hypersomnia: Both normal or slightly elevated
- Healthy controls: Normal B/A ratio

**Limitation:** Requires lumbar puncture. Could only be used diagnostically in severe/uncertain cases or research settings.

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients should have B/A ratio >1.5x controls, while narcolepsy patients should have B/A ratio near 1.0 (both reduced proportionally).

**Certainty: 0.20** (based on Lopez-Amador 2025 showing differential A/B patterns; specific ratio cutoffs are speculative)

---

### 9.4 DTI-ALPS as an Objective ME/CFS Severity and Treatment Response Biomarker

**Rationale:** If glymphatic failure is central to ME/CFS pathophysiology, DTI-ALPS (diffusion tensor imaging along the perivascular space) provides an objective, quantitative measure of disease severity. Unlike symptom questionnaires, DTI-ALPS is not subject to reporting bias and could serve as a primary outcome in clinical trials.

**Protocol:** Standard MRI (3T), DTI sequence (~10 minutes), post-processing with established pipeline. Can be added to any ME/CFS neuroimaging study at minimal additional cost.

**Diagnostic value:** If DTI-ALPS is reduced in ME/CFS (as predicted by hypothesis 1.1 and supported by Long COVID data from Chaganti 2025), it could become part of a diagnostic biomarker panel alongside existing candidates (NK cell function, cytokine profiles, metabolomics).

**Treatment response:** DTI-ALPS changes over time could serve as an objective measure of treatment response, complementing subjective symptom assessments. A drug that improves DTI-ALPS without improving symptoms (or vice versa) would provide critical mechanistic information.

**Certainty: 0.35** (DTI-ALPS is validated in other conditions; ME/CFS-specific data is the gap)

---

### 9.5 Composite "Sleep-Glymphatic Score" for Clinical Staging

**Components:**
1. Home EEG delta/alpha ratio (nightly, averaged over 1 week)
2. Pupillary unrest index infraslow component (daily, 5-min recording)
3. Morning cognitive test battery (daily, 5-min app-based)
4. Orthostatic BP/HR response (daily, lying-to-standing)

**Rationale:** Each component captures a different node of the sleep-glymphatic axis:
- Delta/alpha = thalamocortical oscillation quality
- PUI infraslow = LC NE oscillation quality
- Morning cognition = glymphatic clearance outcome
- Orthostatic response = arterial pulsatility component

**Clinical use:** The composite score would track disease activity longitudinally, identify PEM onset (score deterioration precedes subjective crash?), and quantify treatment response.

**Accessibility:** All four components are measurable at home with consumer devices. No imaging or invasive tests required for longitudinal tracking.

**Certainty: 0.20** (the composite is entirely theoretical; individual components have varying evidence)

## Caveats and Limitations

1. **All hypotheses are creative synthesis, not established fact.** Each individual mechanistic link is grounded in published evidence, but the novel connections (especially the orexin-vasomotion-glymphatic triad and the PGE2 feedback loop) are untested.

2. **Treatment suggestions are not clinical recommendations.** All pharmacological ideas require medical supervision and clinical trial evidence before use.

3. **The glymphatic system itself is still debated.** While the Hauglund 2025 data provides strong optogenetic evidence in mice, human glymphatic physiology is less directly accessible and some aspects remain controversial.

4. **No ME/CFS-specific glymphatic data exist.** All predictions about DTI-ALPS, glymphatic clearance, and waste accumulation in ME/CFS are extrapolated from sleep deprivation studies, Long COVID imaging, and basic neuroscience.

5. **Orexin measurements in ME/CFS are limited.** The Lopez-Amador 2025 review found consistent orexin-A reduction across 27 studies, but most used peripheral blood (not CSF), sample sizes were small, and the relationship to sleep architecture was not systematically assessed.

6. **Supplement and nutraceutical ideas have varying evidence quality.** Some (glycine, omega-3) have clinical trial data for sleep; others (Boswellia, taurine for this specific indication) are mechanistic extrapolation only.

7. **Mathematical models are conceptual frameworks, not calibrated simulations.** Parameter values are unavailable for the ME/CFS-specific context. The models generate qualitative predictions (direction, thresholds) but not quantitative ones.
