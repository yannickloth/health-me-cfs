## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses — Mechanistic Connections Not Yet in Literature

### Idea 1.1: DPP-4/CD26 as a Molecular Bridge Between GLP-1 Biology and T Cell Exhaustion in ME/CFS (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** DPP-4 (CD26) is the primary enzyme that degrades GLP-1, and it is also a key co-stimulatory molecule on T cells. Elevated DPP-4 activity would simultaneously (a) reduce endogenous GLP-1 availability, (b) impair T cell co-stimulation by cleaving CXCL12/CXCL10 chemokines, and (c) contribute to the T cell exhaustion phenotype documented in ME/CFS. This creates a single-node hypothesis where a dysregulated protease explains both metabolic and immune dysfunction — a parsimonious mechanism that does not require separate GLP-1 and T cell pathologies.

**Evidence:** Serum DPP-4 activity is modifiable by diet, inflammation, and DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin). DPP-4 is elevated in inflammatory states and cleaves GLP-1 within minutes. In ME/CFS, T cell exhaustion markers (PD-1, TIM-3) are replicated; DPP-4 sheddase activity on T cells has not been measured. GLP-1 RAs bypass DPP-4 degradation, which is why they are long-acting — but if DPP-4 activity itself is pathogenic, then GLP-1 RAs would treat symptoms while DPP-4 inhibitors would target the root protease dysregulation.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (DPP-4 biology in ME/CFS entirely unexamined; mechanism is parsimonious but has zero direct evidence)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show elevated serum DPP-4 activity compared to healthy controls, and DPP-4 activity will correlate with T cell exhaustion markers (PD-1 expression, IL-10 production) in repeated measures.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If validated, creates a unified metabolic-immune mechanism and opens two parallel drug strategies: GLP-1 RAs (bypass degradation) vs DPP-4 inhibitors (reduce degradation + modulate immune function). The latter are cheaper, generic (sitagliptin), and better tolerated — potentially more accessible for ME/CFS.

### Idea 1.2: GLP-1 Resistance as a Driver of Cerebral Metabolic Starvation in ME/CFS (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** GLP-1 receptors on neurons and glia regulate cerebral glucose uptake, mitochondrial biogenesis, and insulin sensitivity. If ME/CFS involves central GLP-1 resistance (analogous to central insulin resistance in neurodegeneration), then neurons would be metabolically starved despite normal or elevated blood glucose. This would explain reduced cerebral glucose metabolism documented in ME/CFS PET studies (if they exist), and why patients experience cognitive dysfunction (brain fog) that feels like "brain not getting enough fuel." GLP-1 RAs at supraphysiological doses might overcome resistance, improving neuronal energy availability independent of peripheral effects.

**Evidence:** Central insulin resistance is well-documented in Alzheimer's (Type 3 diabetes). GLP-1 RAs improve cerebral glucose metabolism in T2D and PD patients in FDG-PET studies. Reduced cerebral glucose metabolism has been reported in ME/CFS in some PET studies. No study has measured central GLP-1 receptor sensitivity in ME/CFS. The Spezani 2026 review explicitly outlines the metabolic dysfunction → neuroinflammation vicious cycle that GLP-1 RAs can disrupt, but the "resistance" framing is unexplored.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (zero ME/CFS-specific data; extrapolation from AD/PD literature; central GLP-1 resistance not established in any disease)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Acute IV GLP-1 (7-36) infusion in ME/CFS will show blunted cerebral FDG uptake response compared to healthy controls (measured by FDG-PET), indicating central GLP-1 resistance. Chronic GLP-1 RA treatment will increase resting cerebral glucose metabolism in frontal cortex.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Brain fog and cognitive dysfunction are among the most disabling symptoms. If rooted in central metabolic starvation, GLP-1 RAs could provide symptomatic relief through direct neuronal energization — a mechanism entirely distinct from anti-inflammatory effects.

### Idea 1.3: GLP-1 Receptor-Mediated Stabilisation of the Endothelial Glycocalyx as a Mechanism for PEM Reduction (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** Endothelial dysfunction in ME/CFS includes glycocalyx degradation, impaired flow-mediated dilation, and microvascular permeability. GLP-1 receptors on endothelial cells activate eNOS, increase NO bioavailability, and preserve glycocalyx integrity. By stabilising the glycocalyx, GLP-1 RAs could reduce exercise-induced microvascular leakage, oedema, and immune cell extravasation into muscle tissue — mechanistically interrupting the cascade that leads to PEM. This differs from the "cardiovascular subgroup" framing in the paper (which focuses on POTS) by targeting the endothelial barrier itself as a PEM prerequisite.

**Evidence:** GLP-1 RAs protect endothelial glycocalyx in T2D (meta-analytic). Exercise-induced glycocalyx shedding is a normal physiological response that resolves within hours; pathological persistence occurs in endothelial dysfunction. No study has measured glycocalyx dynamics post-exercise in ME/CFS. PEM in ME/CFS involves a delayed (24-48h) worsening that parallels the time course of glycocalyx repair failure.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (glycocalyx not studied in ME/CFS; mechanism logically connects GLP-1 RA biology to PEM; purely inferential)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show elevated serum glycocalyx degradation products (syndecan-1, hyaluronan) at 48h post-CPET compared to athletes and sedentary controls. A 4-week course of liraglutide will reduce post-exercise glycocalyx shedding and correlate with reduced PEM severity.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** PEM is the cardinal symptom. If glycocalyx dysfunction is a mechanistic step in PEM generation, GLP-1 RAs would be the first pharmacological intervention targeting this pathway.

### Idea 1.4: Bidirectional Gut L-Cell → Enteric Neuron → Vagus → Brain Axis as a Drug-Free Target for GLP-1 Modulation (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** Endogenous GLP-1 is released from intestinal L-cells in response to nutrients. L-cells express mechanosensors (PIEZO2, TRPA1) and chemosensors (FFAR2/3 for SCFAs). The gut → vagus → NTS → hypothalamus circuit is the major physiological route for GLP-1 signalling, independent of blood-brain barrier penetration. In ME/CFS, dysbiosis (reduced SCFAs, altered bile acids), slow gut motility, and vagal dysfunction may collectively suppress endogenous GLP-1 release, creating a vicious cycle: low GLP-1 → reduced vagal tone → gut dysmotility → further GLP-1 suppression. This cycle could be interrupted by prokinetics, prebiotics (SCFA boosters), or vagal nerve stimulation — without drugs entering systemic circulation.

**Evidence:** Ruhrländer 2026 describes orexin-GLP-1 regulatory cycles disrupted in post-viral syndromes. Qi et al. 2024 demonstrate that gut Desulfovibrio H2S suppresses GLP-1 production. ME/CFS has documented dysbiosis (reduced SCFA producers). Vagal tone is reduced in ME/CFS (HRV studies). No study has measured postprandial GLP-1 secretion in ME/CFS.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (each component individually plausible; integrated cycle entirely hypothetical; ME/CFS GLP-1 secretion dynamics unmeasured)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show blunted postprandial GLP-1 response to a mixed meal compared to controls. Baseline GLP-1 will correlate with α-diversity and SCFA (butyrate) levels. Vagal tone (HRV high-frequency power) will predict GLP-1 response amplitude.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If reduced endogenous GLP-1 is a component of ME/CFS pathophysiology, then non-pharmacological restoration of GLP-1 secretion (diet, prebiotics, vagal stimulation) becomes a low-risk intervention — bypassing the tolerability, cost, and accessibility barriers of injectable GLP-1 RAs.

### Idea 1.5: Combined GLP-1 + GIP Dual Agonism for Superior Neuroprotection in ME/CFS vs GLP-1 Alone (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** GIP receptors are also expressed in the brain, and GIP-GLP-1 co-agonism (tirzepatide) shows synergistic effects on weight, insulin sensitivity, and possibly neuroprotection compared to GLP-1 alone. However, Forny Germano 2024 found tirzepatide (like semaglutide) ineffective in AD mice. This suggests that dual agonism alone is insufficient if the compound lacks CNS penetrance. Next-generation triple agonists (retatrutide: GLP-1/GIP/glucagon) and CNS-optimised peptides may differ. For ME/CFS, the question is whether combined incretin agonism affects a broader range of the 250+ PrecisionLife-identified genes than GLP-1 alone — testable by comparing pathway enrichment overlap between GLP-1R, GIPR, and GCGR target genes.

**Evidence:** Vear 2025 Nature Metabolism reviews incretin dual/triple agonists for neurodegeneration. Forny Germano 2024 is negative for tirzepatide in AD mice, but this may reflect BBB penetrance rather than pharmacology. No dual/triple agonist has been tested in ME/CFS. PrecisionLife genetics for ME/CFS could be intersected with GIPR and GCGR target gene pathways to compute predicted efficacy scores.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (no ME/CFS data; Forny Germano negative; speculative that triple agonism covers more ME/CFS pathways)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Computational intersection of PrecisionLife ME/CFS risk genes with GLP-1R, GIPR, and GCGR target pathways will show that GIPR and GCGR targets do NOT add significant additional coverage beyond GLP-1R targets alone. If so, dual/triple agonism offers no genetic advantage for ME/CFS.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Prevents premature investment in expensive next-generation GLP-1 RAs if the simpler (and better understood) GLP-1 RAs already cover the relevant pathways. Also guides compound selection for any future trial.

## Category 2: Research Directions — Experiments Resolving Key Uncertainties

### Idea 2.1: Postprandial GLP-1 Secretion Time-Course in ME/CFS vs Controls — First Quantitation of Endogenous GLP-1 Axis (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.50)

**Rationale:** No study has measured whether ME/CFS patients have normal endogenous GLP-1 secretion. This is the most basic unresolved question. If GLP-1 secretion is normal, then the genetic pathway enrichment likely reflects GLP-1 RA target pathways that are *downstream* of the receptor, and endogenous GLP-1 biology is intact. If GLP-1 secretion is blunted (due to dysbiosis, vagal dysfunction, or L-cell mitochondrial impairment), then GLP-1 RAs are replacing a deficient hormone — a fundamentally different therapeutic rationale. A simple meal tolerance test with serial GLP-1, GIP, glucose, insulin, and C-peptide measurements would answer this for ~$200/patient.

**Evidence:** Standard endocrine challenge methodology. Qi et al. 2024 shows gut microbiota H2S suppresses GLP-1, relevant to ME/CFS dysbiosis. Ruhrländer 2026 posits orexin-GLP-1 disruption in post-viral syndromes. Zero GLP-1 secretion data exist in ME/CFS.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50 (standard assay; well-established protocol; no data yet in ME/CFS)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Blunted GLP-1 AUC (area under curve) in ME/CFS vs BMI-matched controls during mixed meal tolerance test. Secondary: GLP-1 AUC correlates negatively with fatigue severity (Chalder) and positively with HRV (vagal tone). Null result: no difference in GLP-1 secretion, falsifying the deficiency hypothesis.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Resolves whether GLP-1 RA repurposing is hormone replacement vs supraphysiological pharmacology — affects trial design, dosing, and expected effect size.

### Idea 2.2: Combinatorial Genetic Classifier for GLP-1 RA Response — Retrospective Validation in Non-ME/CFS Cohorts (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.45)

**Rationale:** PrecisionLife is developing AI classifiers to predict GLP-1 RA response using combinatorial genetics, but validation in ME/CFS is years away. An intermediate step: retrodict GLP-1 RA response in existing T2D/obesity trials using the same classifier trained on ME/CFS risk SNPs. If the ME/CFS-derived classifier predicts GLP-1 RA efficacy in non-ME/CFS populations (where outcome data exist), it cross-validates the genetic pathway enrichment framework without requiring an ME/CFS trial. This would raise the certainty of the PrecisionLife findings from 0.45 (conference presentation) to ~0.60 (independent validation).

**Evidence:** Gardner 2026 BRMEC15 presented the enrichment but did not report cross-disease classifier validation. Published T2D GLP-1 RA trial data with baseline genetics (e.g., EXSCEL, REWIND, LEADER) could serve as validation cohorts if genotype data are accessible.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45 (requires data access; classifier methodology not peer-reviewed; strong face validity)

**Falsifiable prediction:** The ME/CFS-derived combinatorial classifier will NOT predict GLP-1 RA HbA1c response in existing T2D trial cohorts, failing to cross-validate. This would reduce confidence in the ME/CFS enrichment findings.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Cheap, fast validation step that could substantially derisk the GLP-1 RA repurposing hypothesis before any patient exposure.

### Idea 2.3: GLP-1 RA Effects on NK Cell Cytotoxicity in ME/CFS PBMCs — First Ex Vivo Human Data (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.40)

**Rationale:** NK cell dysfunction is one of the most replicated immune findings in ME/CFS (reduced cytotoxicity, altered CD56 subset distribution). GLP-1 receptors are expressed on NK cells in T2D, where they enhance cytotoxicity. No study has examined GLP-1 RA effects on ME/CFS NK cells. Ex vivo exposure of ME/CFS PBMCs to GLP-1 RA (liraglutide, semaglutide) provides the first human ME/CFS-specific mechanistic data — zero patient exposure, but directly tests whether GLP-1 RAs modulate the most replicated immune defect.

**Evidence:** Deng 2025 reviews GLP-1 RA immune cell modulation but does not specifically address NK cells. Gardner 2026 enrichment includes immune pathways. Existing ME/CFS NK cell literature (reduced CD57, perforin, granzyme B). Ex vivo design is feasible with small blood volumes.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40 (well-established assay; GLP-1R on NK cells confirmed; zero ME/CFS-specific data)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS PBMC NK cells exposed to GLP-1 RA (1-100 nM) will show dose-dependent increase in K562 target cell killing vs vehicle, reaching significance at 10 nM. Effect size correlates with baseline NK dysfunction severity. Null: no effect, suggesting GLP-1 RA mechanism in ME/CFS is independent of NK modulation.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If NK cytotoxicity is enhanced, provides the first ME/CFS-specific mechanistic data supporting GLP-1 RA immune modulation. If null, narrows the immune pathways likely responsible.

### Idea 2.4: Low-Dose Liraglutide Crossover Trial in Genetically-Selected ME/CFS — Proof-of-Concept Design (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.35)

**Rationale:** The paper speculates about GLP-1 RA trials but does not provide a concrete trial design. A practical PoC: n=20 ME/CFS patients selected for high PrecisionLife GLP-1 pathway enrichment score, randomised to liraglutide (starting 0.3 mg/day, titrating to 1.2 mg over 8 weeks) vs placebo in crossover design (8-week washout). Liraglutide chosen over semaglutide because: (a) shorter half-life (13h vs 7d) — safer in case of adverse events, (b) positive CNS data in AD/PD (semaglutide negative in Forny Germano 2024), (c) subcutaneous injection similar to standard. Primary endpoint: DSQ-PEM at 8 weeks. Secondary: CRP, NK cytotoxicity, CPET (if tolerated).

**Evidence:** Liraglutide has the best CNS evidence base among GLP-1 RAs. Dosing 10-25% of standard T2D dose is recommended by Gardner. N=20 is feasible for an investigator-initiated trial.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35 (design is feasible; zero data in ME/CFS; tolerability unknown; power uncertain)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ≥30% reduction in DSQ-PEM in active vs placebo in the genetically-enriched subgroup at week 8. CRP reduction correlates with symptom improvement.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** The first GLP-1 RA ME/CFS trial design. Provides the blueprint for moving from speculation to data.

### Idea 2.5: GLP-1 RA × PEM Provocation Substudy — Does GLP-1 RA Blunt the Delayed Worsening? (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** No pharmacological intervention has been shown to prevent or reduce PEM. Since PEM involves a delayed (24-72h) worsening after exercise, and GLP-1 RAs have half-lives spanning this window, a substudy embedded in a trial would use standardised CPET provocation at baseline and at end-of-treatment, with serial symptom and biomarker measurement (0, 24, 48, 72h). The hypothesis: GLP-1 RA pre-treatment reduces the AUC of PEM symptoms and inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP) post-CPET compared to placebo.

**Evidence:** ME/CFS CPET provocation studies document 24-48h delayed worsening. GLP-1 RAs reduce post-exercise inflammation in T2D (measured at 24h). No PEM provocation study has been done with any GLP-1 RA.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (PEM provocation is ME/CFS-specific methodology; no prior GLP-1 PEM data; unclear whether single-dose or chronic dosing is needed)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Liraglutide 1.2 mg/day x 8 weeks reduces CPCT PEM AUC ≥30% vs placebo, with parallel reduction in post-CPET IL-6 spike at 24h. Null: no effect on PEM timeline despite improved baseline symptoms.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Directly tests whether GLP-1 RAs modify the defining ME/CFS symptom (PEM). A positive result would be transformative. A negative result with positive baseline symptom improvement would suggest GLP-1 RAs work through mechanisms independent of PEM generation.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas — Repurposed Drugs, New Targets, Dosing

### Idea 3.1: Low-Dose DPP-4 Inhibitor (Sitagliptin 25 mg) as a Tolerable, Accessible Alternative to Injectable GLP-1 RAs (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** DPP-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin, vildagliptin) raise endogenous GLP-1 levels 2-3x, are oral (no injections), have excellent safety/tolerability profiles, are off-patent/generic ($5-30/month vs $300-1000+), and have negligible risk of nausea/vomiting compared to GLP-1 RAs. They do not cause weight loss (potentially beneficial in ME/CFS where weight maintenance may be difficult). The trade-off: less potent than GLP-1 RAs, but potentially better suited for ME/CFS patients who are medication-sensitive and orthostatically intolerant (weight loss from GLP-1 RAs could worsen OI). If the goal is GLP-1 pathway modulation with minimal adverse effects, DPP-4 inhibitors may be optimal for first exploration.

**Evidence:** DPP-4 inhibitors are standard T2D therapy with decades of safety data. DPP-4/CD26 is on T cells — inhibition could modulate T cell function independently of GLP-1 elevation, potentially relevant to immune exhaustion in ME/CFS. Zero DPP-4 inhibitor data in ME/CFS. No DPP-4 inhibitor weight loss = no orthostatic worsening from volume depletion.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (excellent safety; cheap; oral; mechanism plausible; zero ME/CFS data)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Sitagliptin 25 mg/day x 12 weeks will produce detectable (2-3x) increase in postprandial GLP-1 AUC in ME/CFS. Improvement in fatigue (FSS) ≥0.5 points at 12 weeks. Tolerability: zero treatment discontinuations due to GI adverse events.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If effective, DPP-4 inhibitors would be dramatically more accessible (cost, oral route, tolerability) than GLP-1 RAs for the ME/CFS population. A negative result would not necessarily rule out GLP-1 RAs (which achieve supraphysiological levels).

### Idea 3.2: Liraglutide (Low-Dose, Slow-Titration Protocol) as First GLP-1 RA Candidate Over Semaglutide/Tirzepatide (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.40)

**Rationale:** Compound selection matters critically (Forny Germano 2024: semaglutide/tirzepatide negative in AD mice; liraglutide has positive CNS data). Liraglutide has:
- Shorter half-life (13h) — safer for first-in-ME/CFS use, allows daily stop if adverse effects
- Most extensive CNS evidence (PD, AD clinical trials)
- Predictable dose-response at low doses (0.3-1.2 mg)
- Weight loss is dose-dependent but modest at low doses
- Subcutaneous injection — no need for weekly dosing compliance
The paper mentions low-dose strategy but does not specify compound. This idea nominates liraglutide as the specific first candidate.

**Evidence:** Liraglutide has positive PD clinical trial data (exenatide also). Semaglutide negative in AD mouse model (Forny Germano 2024). Liraglutide's shorter half-life provides safety advantage in first-in-population use.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40 (established drug; CNS data exist; safety profile known; no ME/CFS data; Forny Germano negative for other compounds strengthens liraglutide rationale)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Liraglutide 0.6 mg/day (25% of standard dose) will be tolerated by ≥80% of ME/CFS patients (vs ~90% in T2D). The difference between liraglutide and semaglutide tolerability in ME/CFS is itself a falsifiable prediction.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Directly addresses the paper's call for low-dose GLP-1 RA trials by specifying the compound, dose range, and rationale. Prevents the common error of assuming GLP-1 RA class-wide equivalence.

### Idea 3.3: Intranasal GLP-1 (7-36) Amide as a CNS-Targeted, Minimally Systemic Option (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.15)

**Rationale:** The major barrier to GLP-1 RA neuroprotection is BBB penetration. Most GLP-1 RAs reach CNS primarily through circumventricular organs and vagal afferents — limited and variable. Intranasal delivery bypasses BBB and delivers GLP-1 directly to the brain via olfactory and trigeminal pathways, achieving high CNS concentrations with minimal systemic exposure. This would target neuroinflammation, cerebral glucose metabolism, and orexin-system modulation without the GI adverse effects, weight loss, and cost of systemic GLP-1 RAs. The endogenous peptide (GLP-1 7-36 amide) is short-acting (~2 min half-life), providing a brief CNS pulse — potentially sufficient for neuroprotection without continuous receptor activation.

**Evidence:** Intranasal insulin is being studied in AD with some positive signals. Intranasal GLP-1 has been studied in healthy volunteers and showed CNS effects (reduced appetite, altered brain activity on fMRI). No intranasal GLP-1 study in any disease has progressed past phase 1. The approach is technically feasible but requires formulation for stability.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.15 (no clinical GLP-1 intranasal data in ME/CFS or any disease; technically feasible; safety unknown; formulation challenges with peptide stability)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Intranasal GLP-1 (0.5 mg) in ME/CFS will increase prefrontal cortex FDG uptake at 30 min post-dose vs placebo spray (measured by PET). No change in serum GLP-1 or glucose (confirming CNS-selectivity).

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If feasible, provides a route to CNS-specific GLP-1 modulation without systemic effects — ideal for medication-sensitive patients. Extremely early stage.

### Idea 3.4: Bismuth Subsalicylate (BSS) as a GLP-1 Secretagogue via H2S Chelation in the Gut (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** Qi et al. 2024 demonstrated that Desulfovibrio-produced H2S suppresses GLP-1 secretion from L cells by inhibiting mitochondrial function. BSS chelates luminal H2S, rescuing GLP-1 production and improving metabolic outcomes in mice. ME/CFS patients have altered gut microbiota including potential increases in H2S producers. BSS is an OTC, cheap, well-tolerated, oral medication. This provides a mechanism to increase endogenous GLP-1 without any injected drug, bypassing tolerability concerns.

**Evidence:** Qi et al. 2024 Nature Metabolism (mouse + human correlational data). BSS safety established >50 years of OTC use. ME/CFS gut dysbiosis literature (variable findings, some showing Desulfovibrio increases). Zero BSS GLP-1 data in ME/CFS.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (compelling mouse data; BSS has established safety; OTC; mechanism plausible; zero human GLP-1 data with BSS in any condition; effect size unknown)

**Falsifiable prediction:** BSS 524 mg QID x 2 weeks will increase postprandial GLP-1 AUC by ≥50% in ME/CFS vs placebo, and GLP-1 increase will correlate with reduction in breath H2S (hydrogen breath test). Null: no GLP-1 change, suggesting H2S-mediated GLP-1 suppression is not a relevant mechanism in ME/CFS.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If effective, offers an extremely accessible (OTC, oral, cheap) method for endogenous GLP-1 enhancement in ME/CFS. Worth exploring as a pilot even without formal trials.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas — Mechanistic Rationale

### Idea 4.1: Butyrate/SCFA Supplementation to Enhance Endogenous GLP-1 Secretion via FFAR2/3 (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** Short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate) produced by gut microbiota are the primary endogenous stimulus for GLP-1 secretion from L cells, acting through FFAR2/FFAR3 (GPR43/GPR41) receptors. SCFAs also directly enhance vagal afferent sensitivity to GLP-1. ME/CFS patients have reduced SCFA producers (butyrate-producing Firmicutes). Supplementing butyrate (as tributyrin or sodium butyrate) could restore endogenous GLP-1 secretion, offering a nutritional route to GLP-1 modulation.

**Evidence:** SCFA → GLP-1 axis is well-established in endocrinology. ME/CFS microbiome studies show reduced Faecalibacterium, Roseburia, and butyrate levels (replicated, not consistent across all cohorts). SCFA supplementation is safe, OTC, and used in IBS. No ME/CFS study has measured GLP-1 after SCFA supplementation.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (established SCFA→GLP-1 mechanism; replicated ME/CFS dysbiosis; supplement safety; zero ME/CFS-specific GLP-1 data)

**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of tributyrin 2g/day will increase postprandial GLP-1 AUC by ≥30% in ME/CFS vs placebo, and increase in GLP-1 will correlate with reduction in ME/CFS fatigue score (VAS). Null: no GLP-1 change, suggesting L-cell dysfunction is beyond reversible.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Extremely accessible (OTC, oral, cheap) nutritional route to GLP-1 pathway modulation. Used alongside rather than instead of drug development.

### Idea 4.2: Taurine as a GLP-1 Secretagogue and Metabolic Modulator in ME/CFS (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** Taurine is a semi-essential amino acid that stimulates GLP-1 secretion from L cells via TAS1R2/TAS1R3 sweet taste receptors and via calcium-dependent exocytosis. It also improves mitochondrial function, reduces ER stress, and enhances insulin sensitivity — all mechanisms relevant to ME/CFS energy metabolism dysfunction. Taurine levels are reduced in ME/CFS in some studies. Taurine supplementation (500mg-3g/day) is safe, cheap, and OTC. It could provide a gentle "nutritional GLP-1 boost" as opposed to pharmacological GLP-1 RA activation.

**Evidence:** Taurine stimulates GLP-1 secretion in vitro and in vivo (rodent/human data). Taurine is depleted in ME/CFS (some metabolomics studies). Taurine improves mitochondrial function and reduces exercise-induced muscle damage. No ME/CFS trial of taurine with GLP-1 measurement.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (established GLP-1 secretagogue activity; safety excellent; ME/CFS data on taurine levels limited and mixed; effect size likely small)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Taurine 3g/day x 4 weeks will increase fasting and postprandial GLP-1 by ≥20% and improve DSQ-PEM score by ≥15% vs placebo in ME/CFS.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Simple, low-risk nutritional intervention that could provide a modest GLP-1 boost. Even if effect size is small, it is safe enough to try without trial infrastructure.

### Idea 4.3: Berberine as a GLP-1 Secretagogue and AMPK-Metabolic Modulator — with Dosing Specificity (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** Berberine activates AMPK, improves mitochondrial function, stimulates GLP-1 secretion (via sweet taste receptors and TGR5 bile acid receptors), and improves glucose metabolism — an oral, cheap, OTC alkaloid with a mechanism that partially overlaps GLP-1 RA pharmacology. Berberine also has direct anti-inflammatory (NF-kB inhibition) and gut microbiota-modulating effects. However, berberine has poor oral bioavailability (~5%), variable GLP-1 effects across studies, and can cause GI side effects. The key question: is berberine's GLP-1 effect substantial enough to be clinically meaningful in ME/CFS, or does it act mainly through AMPK-independent mechanisms?

**Evidence:** Berberine increases GLP-1 in T2D (meta-analytic). Berberine has been studied in ME/CFS in small trials (mixed results, underpowered). Berberine activates AMPK directly, providing an alternative route to GLP-1 RA-like metabolic effects. Berberine is widely used by ME/CFS patients informally. No study has measured berberine's GLP-1 effect specifically in ME/CFS.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (some ME/CFS trial data; established GLP-1 secretagogue; bioavailability limits efficacy; dose-response unclear in ME/CFS)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Berberine 500mg TID x 8 weeks will increase postprandial GLP-1 AUC by ≥15% and reduce CRP by ≥0.5 mg/L in ME/CFS. GLP-1 change will NOT correlate with symptom improvement (suggesting AMPK-mediated effects are more important than GLP-1 in berberine's action).

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Berberine is already used by many ME/CFS patients. Understanding whether its benefits are GLP-1-mediated or AMPK-mediated affects combination strategies with other GLP-1-targeted approaches.

### Idea 4.4: Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) as a PPAR-α Agonist Enhancing GLP-1 Sensitivity in the CNS (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** PEA is an endocannabinoid-like molecule that activates PPAR-α, which upregulates GLP-1 receptor expression in the brain. By increasing GLP-1R density, PEA could enhance the response to endogenous GLP-1 (and exogenous GLP-1 RAs) without increasing GLP-1 levels. This "GLP-1 sensitivity enhancer" approach is distinct from GLP-1 secretagogues or receptor agonists. PEA is OTC, safe, and used in fibromyalgia (some RCT data).

**Evidence:** PPAR-α regulates GLP-1R expression (preclinical evidence). PEA has positive RCT data in fibromyalgia and chronic pain. PEA reduces mast cell activation (relevant to ME/CFS-MCAS). No study has examined PEA + GLP-1 axis in ME/CFS or any disease.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (PEA has human safety; fibromyalgia RCT data; GLP-1R upregulation mechanism is preclinical; combination effect unstudied)

**Falsifiable prediction:** PEA 600mg BID x 8 weeks will increase CSF GLP-1R expression (surrogate marker) and enhance the GLP-1 response to a mixed meal (higher peak, longer duration) in ME/CFS vs placebo.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If PEA enhances GLP-1 sensitivity, it could be a low-risk co-nutrient with GLP-1 RAs to increase efficacy at lower doses (reducing adverse effects and cost). Also directly targets MCAS, which may be the relevant subgroup for GLP-1 RA response.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions — Breathing, Pacing, Vagal Stimulation

### Idea 5.1: Chronobiotic Meal Timing to Entrain Endogenous GLP-1 Peaks (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** GLP-1 secretion follows a circadian rhythm with a peak in the morning (when L-cell sensitivity is highest). ME/CFS patients often have disrupted meal timing (late eating, skipping breakfast) due to morning fatigue, nocturnal sleep disruption, or orthostatic intolerance. This may cause a phase shift that desynchronises GLP-1 secretion from the circadian orexin/arousal cycle (Ruhrländer 2026). A chronobiotic intervention — consuming the largest meal within 2 hours of waking — could entrain GLP-1 peaks to the early morning, supporting orexin signalling and arousal.

**Evidence:** Circadian GLP-1 rhythm well-documented in humans (peak ~8 AM, nadir ~8 PM). ME/CFS patients have altered meal timing and circadian disruption. Ruhrländer 2026 describes orexin-GLP-1 coupling. No chronobiotic GLP-1 intervention has been tested in ME/CFS.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (circadian GLP-1 biology is solid; behavioural intervention is zero-risk; effect size in ME/CFS unknown)

**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of time-restricted feeding (≥50% of daily calories within 2h of waking) will increase morning GLP-1 peak AUC by ≥25% and improve morning fatigue scores (VAS) compared to habitual eating patterns in ME/CFS.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Free, zero-risk, self-manageable intervention that targets the GLP-1 system without any drug or supplement. If effective, provides immediate practical guidance.

### Idea 5.2: Auricular Vagal Nerve Stimulation (tVNS) to Enhance Vagal GLP-1 Signalling (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** GLP-1 exerts many of its CNS effects through vagal afferents (NTS → hypothalamus pathway), not direct BBB penetration. Vagal tone is reduced in ME/CFS (HRV evidence), which would blunt the sensitivity of the vagal GLP-1 signalling pathway even if GLP-1 levels are normal. Auricular tVNS activates vagal afferents directly, potentially restoring the sensitivity of the GLP-1→vagal→CNS axis. This would enhance the effect of endogenous GLP-1 without changing GLP-1 levels.

**Evidence:** Vagal GLP-1 signalling pathway is well-characterised (GLP-1R on nodose ganglion neurons → NTS → hypothalamus). HRV studies show reduced vagal tone in ME/CFS. tVNS RCTs show benefit in pain, inflammation, and possibly fatigue (small effect). No tVNS study has measured GLP-1 sensitivity or GLP-1 responses. A study combining tVNS with meal tolerance test (measuring GLP-1, vagal HRV, and satiety) would test this.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (tVNS is safe; vagal GLP-1 pathway established; ME/CFS vagal dysfunction replicated; zero combination data)

**Falsifiable prediction:** 20 min daily auricular tVNS x 8 weeks will increase postprandial vagal HRV response (high-frequency power) and subjective energy (VAS) after a standardised meal vs sham stimulation in ME/CFS. Null: tVNS improves HRV but does not alter GLP-1-related outcomes, confirming the vagal dysfunction hypothesis but suggesting vagal and GLP-1 pathologies are dissociable.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If effective, provides a drug-free route to enhancing GLP-1 pathway function. Non-invasive, low-risk, self-administered at home.

### Idea 5.3: Protein-First Meal Sequencing to Optimise Endogenous GLP-1 Release (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** GLP-1 secretion is maximally stimulated by protein and fat (not carbohydrate), with whey protein being the most potent known dietary GLP-1 secretagogue (via L-cell amino acid sensors). Simple meal sequencing — consuming protein first, before carbohydrates — enhances GLP-1 and delays gastric emptying, improving glycaemic control in T2D. The "protein-first" strategy costs nothing, requires no supplements, and could be implemented immediately by ME/CFS patients to boost GLP-1 at each meal.

**Evidence:** Meal sequencing (protein first) improves GLP-1 and reduces postprandial glucose excursions in T2D (replicated RCTs). Whey protein preloads show consistent GLP-1 increases. No meal sequencing study has been done in ME/CFS. Even a small GLP-1 increase could have clinical relevance if the GLP-1 system is dysregulated.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (replicated in T2D; zero risk; zero cost; ME/CFS-specific effect unknown)

**Falsifiable prediction:** A protein-first meal sequence (whey shake before carbohydrate-rich meal) will increase GLP-1 AUC by ≥40% compared to standard mixed meal in ME/CFS. Postprandial fatigue and cognitive fog will be reduced by ≥20% with the protein-first sequence vs standard.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Immediate actionable dietary strategy. No permission required, no cost, no risk. Patients can self-experiment with protein-first eating today.

## Category 6: Combinations + Access — Multi-Component Protocols, Severity-Accessible

### Idea 6.1: Escalation Cascade — Dietary GLP-1 Boost → OTC Modulator → DPP-4 Inhibitor → Low-Dose GLP-1 RA (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** Rather than a single drug-or-nothing approach, design a stepwise GLP-1 pathway modulation protocol that escalates by mechanism, cost, and invasiveness. Each step can be tried for 4-8 weeks before escalation:
- Step 1: Protein-first meal timing, chronobiotic eating (zero cost, zero risk)
- Step 2: Berberine 500mg TID or tributyrin 2g/day (~$30-50/month, OTC)
- Step 3: Sitagliptin 25mg/day (~$15/month, generic, requires prescription)
- Step 4: Liraglutide 0.6mg/day (injectable, ~$300/month, requires physician)
This cascade maximises access (severe patients can try Steps 1-2 independently), provides logical stop points, and ensures each patient operates at the lowest sufficient intervention level.

**Evidence:** Each individual step has mechanistic plausibility (see individual ideas). The cascade structure is a clinical algorithm — no data exist comparing stepped vs direct GLP-1 RA initiation. Analogous to stepped-care in chronic pain.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (algorithm design is sound; individual steps are safe; combination effect entirely untested; optimal step progression unknown)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ≥50% of ME/CFS patients attempting the cascade will achieve clinically significant improvement (≥30% DSQ-PEM reduction) by Step 2 or 3 and not require Step 4. This would validate a "lowest sufficient dose" approach.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Addresses the access problem — injectable GLP-1 RAs are expensive, require refrigeration, and are inaccessible to severe/very-severe patients. A cascade provides multiple entry points.

### Idea 6.2: GLP-1 Pathway Activation + Glymphatic Enhancement + Orexin Stabilisation — Triple Circuit Protocol (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** The paper's treatment map (ch18) targets individual pathways but not their cross-circuit interactions. A triple-intervention protocol that simultaneously (a) activates GLP-1 signalling (dietary or pharmacological), (b) enhances glymphatic clearance (sleep posture, low-dose melatonin, overnight glymphatic support), and (c) stabilises orexin signalling (morning light exposure, low-dose modafinil or orexin agonist if available) could break the self-reinforcing orexin↔GLP-1 disruption cycle described by Ruhrländer 2026. The hypothesis: each intervention alone is insufficient, but their combination produces synergy.

**Evidence:** Ruhrländer 2026: orexin-GLP-1 reciprocal dysregulation in post-viral syndromes. Glymphatic clearance requires slow-wave sleep (which is fragmented in ME/CFS) and is enhanced by lateral sleep positioning. GLP-1 RAs improve sleep quality in some studies. No study has attempted triple circuit modulation.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (each component plausible alone; triple combination speculation; risk of intervention burden; adherence concerns)

**Falsifiable prediction:** A 4-week triple protocol (morning protein-first meal + lateral sleep positioning + 30 min morning bright light) will improve DSQ-PEM by ≥40% vs a single intervention (protein-first alone). Triple synergy indicated by disproportionate improvement vs additive individual effects.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Recognises the interlocking circuit-level dysregulation — GLP-1, orexin, glymphatic — and proposes a practical, non-pharmacological protocol that patients can implement.

### Idea 6.3: Severe/Very-Severe ME/CFS-Adapted GLP-1 Strategy — Sublingual/Transdermal Berberine + Timed Nutrition (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** Severe patients cannot access injectable GLP-1 RAs (cost, tolerance, refrig, travel for injection). Even oral drugs may be problematic (swallowing, GI sensitivity). Adapt the GLP-1 pathway approach for this population: (a) sublingual berberine (bypasses GI, avoids nausea), (b) liquid whey protein for GLP-1 stimulation (easier than solids), (c) chronobiotic pureeing of first meal, (d) transdermal magnesium (which supports GLP-1 secretion via calcium-dependent pathways). Focus entirely on endogenous GLP-1 enhancement rather than exogenous GLP-1 RAs, since severe patients cannot tolerate the adverse effect profile.

**Evidence:** Severe ME/CFS patients have unique needs (documented in severe ME/CFS care guidelines). Berberine safety, whey protein safety, transdermal magnesium safety. No severe-ME/CFS-adapted GLP-1 protocol exists.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (severe ME/CFS adaptations are needed regardless; GLP-1 relevance in severe subgroup unknown; safety profile acceptable)

**Falsifiable prediction:** A severe-ME/CFS-adapted GLP-1 protocol (sublingual berberine 200mg + liquid whey protein 20g, both TID) will be tolerable (≥80% completion) and show ≥20% improvement in daily living scores (Bell Disability Scale) at 12 weeks.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Severe patients are most in need and least served. A GLP-1 approach that works for this subgroup would be transformative.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions — New ODE/DAG Variables/Equations

### Idea 7.1: Endogenous GLP-1 Dynamics Node in the Causal DAG — Feedback with Orexin, Vagal Tone, and Glycaemic Variability (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** The existing DAG lacks a GLP-1 node. Add: [Endogenous GLP-1] → [Vagal afferent activation] → [NTS activation] → [Orexin neuron stability]. Input edges: gut SCFA levels, meal protein content, circadian phase, DPP-4 activity. Output edges: orexin stability, cerebral glucose uptake, systemic inflammation. The GLP-1 node should be modelled with a circadian forcing function (peak AM, trough PM) and a nutrient-input response function. This adds the endogenous GLP-1 axis as a modifiable node distinct from exogenous GLP-1 RA effects.

**Evidence:** GLP-1 circadian rhythm is well-characterised. Meal composition → GLP-1 dose-response is quantified (whey > mixed > carb alone). Orexin-GLP-1 coupling suggested by Ruhrländer 2026. DPP-4 activity dynamics are known.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (GLP-1 dynamics well-quantified in endocrinology; ME/CFS-specific parameters unknown; node structure is transferable)

**Falsifiable prediction:** The model with the GLP-1 node will predict that reduced SCFA-producing bacteria (common in ME/CFS) lowers GLP-1 by ≥30%, which reduces orexin stability by 20%, producing measurable worsening in morning fatigue — this prediction can be tested by measuring postprandial GLP-1 and morning orexin-A in ME/CFS.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Formalises the endogenous GLP-1 axis in the DAG, enabling quantitative predictions about dietary, microbiome, and circadian interventions that could modulate GLP-1.

---

### Idea 7.2: DPP-4/CD26 Activity as a Rate-Limiting Variable Modelled as a Michaelis-Menten Degradation Function (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** The GLP-1 node requires a DPP-4 degradation term. Endogenous GLP-1 half-life is ~1-2 min, determined primarily by DPP-4. Model d[GLP-1]/dt = secretion_rate(t, nutrients) - (Vmax_DPP4 × [GLP-1])/(Km + [GLP-1]). If DPP-4 activity is elevated in ME/CFS (hypothesis 1.1), the Vmax term increases, producing the same steady-state GLP-1 level only if secretion_rate doubles — which may be impossible due to L-cell mitochondrial limitation (Qi et al. 2024). This predicts that GLP-1 deficiency is non-linear with DPP-4 activity: a 2x DPP-4 increase requires a 3-4x secretion increase to maintain GLP-1 levels.

**Evidence:** DPP-4 kinetics well-characterised (Km ~5 µM for GLP-1). DPP-4 activity varies 2-3x between individuals due to genetics and inflammation. No DPP-4 activity data in ME/CFS. Qi et al. 2024 shows L-cell mitochondrial impairment suppresses GLP-1 secretion.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (enzyme kinetics established; individual variation established; ME/CFS-specific DPP-4 measurement needed for parameterisation)

**Falsifiable prediction:** If ME/CFS DPP-4 activity is elevated 1.5x, the model predicts GLP-1 AUC will be reduced by ~33% at normal secretion rates, but by ~50% if L-cell secretion is impaired. This is testable with a single meal tolerance test per patient.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Makes quantitative predictions about GLP-1 dynamics that can be tested with a simple meal challenge, generating priors for trial design.

---

### Idea 7.3: GLP-1 RA Pharmacokinetic-Pharmacodynamic (PKPD) Model for ME/CFS with Subgroup-Specific Parameters (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** No PKPD model exists for GLP-1 RAs in ME/CFS. Extend existing T2D PKPD models by adding: (a) reduced baseline vagal tone shifts the EC50 for GLP-1 RA CNS effects (vagal afferent sensitivity is a physiological amplifier), (b) reduced cerebral glucose metabolism alters the dose-response curve for cognitive endpoints, (c) baseline inflammation (CRP) modifies the anti-inflammatory dose-response. These subgroup-specific PKPD parameters would predict that inflammatory-subgroup patients require lower doses for anti-inflammatory effects but higher doses for CNS effects — informing trial dosing design.

**Evidence:** GLP-1 RA PKPD models exist for T2D (semaglutide, liraglutide). Vagal GLP-1 RA sensitivity is documented preclinically. Cerebral glucose metabolism data exists in ME/CFS (limited). This is a parameterisation exercise, not new biology.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (existing models; parameterisation requires ME/CFS-specific data not yet collected; could guide trial design once data exist)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Once fitted to trial data, the model will predict that the autoimmune/inflammatory subgroup achieves CRP reduction at 0.6 mg liraglutide/day while the energy metabolism subgroup requires 1.2 mg/day for cognitive improvements — proportions falsifiable in a dose-ranging trial.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Moving from qualitative subgroup speculation to quantitative dose-response predictions. Essential for efficient trial design.

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges — Parallels Suggesting Testable ME/CFS Predictions

### Idea 8.1: T2D Protective Allele Overlap Suggests GLP-1 RA Efficacy in ME/CFS May Be Independent of Metabolic Improvement (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.40)

**Rationale:** Gardner 2026 reports protective ME/CFS alleles overlap with T2D protection. This creates a testable prediction: GLP-1 RA effects in ME/CFS may occur through the same pathways that protect against T2D, which are distinct from glucose lowering — specifically, anti-inflammatory (NF-kB, CRP), endothelial (eNOS, glycocalyx), and mitochondrial (PGC-1α, biogenesis) effects. This predicts that GLP-1 RA efficacy in ME/CFS will NOT correlate with glycaemic improvement. If true, GLP-1 RAs could benefit normal-weight, normoglycaemic ME/CFS patients as much as pre-diabetic ones.

**Evidence:** GLP-1 RA anti-inflammatory effects are glucose-independent (Ren 2025 meta-analysis, Mehdi 2023 review). Athauda 2026 describes neuroprotection independent of glucose. Protective allele overlap (Gardner 2026). No normoglycaemic ME/CFS GLP-1 RA data exist.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40 (glucose-independence established in T2D; protective allele overlap is new; extension to ME/CFS is inference)

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a GLP-1 RA ME/CFS trial, baseline HbA1c will NOT predict symptom response (PEM reduction). CRP reduction and symptom improvement will correlate, but neither will correlate with HbA1c change. Falsified if only pre-diabetic ME/CFS patients respond.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Expands the eligible patient population beyond those with metabolic abnormalities. Most ME/CFS patients have normal HbA1c — the glucose-independent claim is critical for their inclusion.

---

### Idea 8.2: Post-Viral ME/CFS as a GLP-1 Deficiency Syndrome — Parallels with Post-Gastric-Bypass Reactive Hypoglycaemia (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** After gastric bypass, some patients develop severe reactive hypoglycaemia driven by postprandial GLP-1 hypersecretion from a remodelled gut — the opposite of what we suspect in ME/CFS. This naturally occurring human model of GLP-1 excess produces autonomic symptoms (palpitations, sweating, tremor, fatigue) that partially overlap ME/CFS. If ME/CFS involves GLP-1 deficiency, the symptom profile should be the inverse: blunted postprandial response, stable but low blood glucose, and absence of reactive hypoglycaemia. Comparing meal-challenge data across these conditions would test whether GLP-1 excess (post-bypass) vs deficiency (ME/CFS) produces distinguishable symptom profiles.

**Evidence:** Post-bypass reactive hypoglycaemia is driven by GLP-1 hypersecretion (proven by GLP-1 receptor antagonist blockade studies). Postprandial symptoms in ME/CFS include fatigue, brain fog, and sometimes palpitations — but the GLP-1 dynamics are unmeasured.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (post-bypass GLP-1 biology is solid; symptom comparison is indirect; ME/CFS GLP-1 deficiency is speculative)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will NOT show postprandial hyperinsulinaemic hypoglycaemia (as in post-bypass) but will show blunted GLP-1 and stable low-normal glucose. Post-bypass patients will not report ME/CFS-like PEM, confirming that GLP-1 hypersecretion and deficiency produce distinct syndromes.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Uses a surgical human model to test whether GLP-1 pathophysiology is relevant to ME/CFS symptom generation.

---

### Idea 8.3: Anorexia Nervosa as an Inverse Model — Caloric Restriction Suppresses GLP-1 and Produces Fatigue/Bradycardia (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** Anorexia nervosa involves chronic caloric restriction, reduced GLP-1 secretion (due to low nutrient stimulation), low body weight, fatigue, bradycardia, and cold intolerance. Many ME/CFS symptoms overlap with starvation physiology. The key insight: AN patients recover with refeeding, including restored GLP-1 secretion and resolution of fatigue. If ME/CFS involves a GLP-1 deficiency that is *calorie-independent* (normal intake but low GLP-1 due to L-cell dysfunction, vagal dysregulation, or DPP-4 elevation), then refeeding alone won't help. Comparing GLP-1 dynamics in ME/CFS vs AN before and after nutritional intervention would distinguish "starvation-like" deficiency from "L-cell failure" deficiency.

**Evidence:** AN patients have reduced GLP-1 secretion (replicated). AN recovery normalises GLP-1. ME/CFS patients do not have low BMI but have some features of starvation physiology (reduced metabolic rate, bradycardia, cold intolerance). No AN-ME/CFS direct GLP-1 comparison exists.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (AN GLP-1 data are solid; ME/CFS starvation physiology is debated; the comparison is novel and testable)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will have lower GLP-1 AUC than BMI-matched controls and comparable GLP-1 to AN patients — but unlike AN, GLP-1 will NOT normalise after 4 weeks of caloric normalisation (ensuring adequate intake). This would confirm a GLP-1 deficiency that is primary, not secondary to low intake.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Distinguishes whether GLP-1 deficiency (if present) is a starvation epiphenomenon vs a primary gut-endocrine pathology — with treatment implications (nutrition alone vs pharmacological GLP-1).

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas — New Ways to Measure, Stratify, Predict Response

### Idea 9.1: Postprandial GLP-1 AUC as a Biomarker for GLP-1 Pathway Dysfunction Subgroup Stratification (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.40)

**Rationale:** The simplest actionable biomarker: standardised mixed meal tolerance test with serial GLP-1, GIP, PYY, and glucose measurements at 0, 15, 30, 60, 90, 120 min. Calculated GLP-1 AUC provides a continuous measure of endogenous GLP-1 secretory capacity. Patients can be stratified into tertiles (low/normal/high GLP-1 responders). Those with low GLP-1 AUC are predicted to benefit most from GLP-1 RAs (replacement rationale). Those with normal/high GLP-1 AUC may benefit more from DPP-4 inhibitors (which raise already-adequate GLP-1) or may be GLP-1 RA non-responders. This biomarker would enrich a trial for predicted responders, reducing sample size requirements.

**Evidence:** Mixed meal tolerance test is a standard endocrine procedure. GLP-1 measurement by ELISA is established. AUC variability in healthy populations is 2-3x between individuals. No ME/CFS GLP-1 AUC data exist.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40 (standard assay; logical stratification; zero ME/CFS GLP-1 AUC data)

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a GLP-1 RA trial, baseline GLP-1 AUC tertile will significantly predict treatment response (PEM improvement). The lowest tertile will show ≥2x the effect size of the highest tertile. Falsified if response is uniform across tertiles.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Provides a simple, cheap, accessible biomarker to enrich clinical trials for likely GLP-1 RA responders — reducing sample size, cost, and risk of false-negative trials.

---

### Idea 9.2: DPP-4 Activity as a Blood-Based Biomarker for GLP-1 RA vs DPP-4 Inhibitor Selection (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)

**Rationale:** Serum DPP-4 activity can be measured by a simple fluorogenic substrate assay ($20/sample). Elevated DPP-4 activity predicts rapid GLP-1 degradation. Patients with high DPP-4 activity may benefit more from DPP-4 inhibitors (blocking degradation) than from GLP-1 RAs (which are resistant to DPP-4 but still rely on endogenous GLP-1 production for vagal afferent effects). Patients with low DPP-4 activity may be GLP-1 analogue-responsive regardless. This biomarker stratifies the choice between two drug classes rather than simple presence/absence of GLP-1 pathology.

**Evidence:** DPP-4 activity varies 3x between individuals, is genetically regulated, and is modifiable by inflammation. DPP-4 fluorogenic assay is a standard lab technique. No DPP-4 activity data exist in ME/CFS. DPP-4 levels correlate with T cell PD-1 expression in some cancer studies.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30 (assay established; individual variation known; zero ME/CFS data; DPP-4→treatment selection untested)

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a crossover trial of sitagliptin vs liraglutide, baseline DPP-4 activity will predict the better-tolerated and more-effective drug at an individual level. High DPP-4 predicts sitagliptin response; low DPP-4 predicts liraglutide response.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Moves from "should we use a GLP-1 RA?" to "which GLP-1 pathway agent for this patient?" — personalising the approach beyond simple yes/no.

---

### Idea 9.3: Multivariate "GLP-1 Pathway Integrity Score" — Integrating GLP-1 AUC, DPP-4 Activity, Vagal HRV, and Gut Microbiome SCFA-Producer Abundance (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)

**Rationale:** A single biomarker will never capture the GLP-1 pathway's complexity. Build an integrative score from 4 cheap, accessible measures:
1. Postprandial GLP-1 AUC (meal tolerance test)
2. Fasting DPP-4 activity (serum)
3. HRV high-frequency power (vagal tone, 5-min recording)
4. Faecal butyrate producers (qPCR for Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia)
Each component independently predicts GLP-1 pathway integrity. Combined into a weighted score (e.g., PCA-derived), it would capture the multi-level GLP-1 pathway (secretion, degradation, neural sensitivity, nutritional input) and identify patients with "GLP-1 pathway failure" vs isolated defects.

**Evidence:** Each component has established biology linking it to GLP-1 pathway function. No integrative GLP-1 score exists in any disease. The score would need validation against GLP-1 RA treatment response.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25 (components individually validated; integrative approach novel; validation requires treatment outcome data)

**Falsifiable prediction:** The GLP-1 pathway integrity score will predict GLP-1 RA treatment response with AUC > 0.75, outperforming any single component. Patients with low scores (<25th percentile) will show ≥3x the symptom improvement of those with high scores (>75th percentile).

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Recognises that GLP-1 pathway dysfunction in ME/CFS is likely multi-factorial. A single biomarker (GLP-1 AUC alone) may miss patients with normal secretion but high degradation or low vagal sensitivity. Integrative score captures the full pathway.

---

### Idea 9.4: FDG-PET Cerebral Metabolic Response to Acute GLP-1 (7-36) Infusion as a CNS GLP-1 Sensitivity Biomarker (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** If central GLP-1 resistance is present in ME/CFS (Idea 1.2), then measuring the acute cerebral metabolic response to IV GLP-1 (7-36) infusion by FDG-PET provides a direct CNS GLP-1 sensitivity readout. Healthy brains show increased FDG uptake in prefrontal cortex and hypothalamus after GLP-1 infusion. An ME/CFS patient with blunted FDG response has central GLP-1 resistance and may need higher GLP-1 RA doses or CNS-penetrant compounds.

**Evidence:** FDG-PET cerebral metabolic response to GLP-1 infusion is documented in healthy controls and T2D (small studies, n=10-20). GLP-1 safety for acute infusion is well-established. No ME/CFS infusion study exists. Cost and radiation exposure limit scalability.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (procedure established in healthy humans; CNS resistance not established in ME/CFS; high cost limits use to proof-of-concept)

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show blunted prefrontal FDG activation after GLP-1 infusion vs healthy controls. Blunting correlates with brain fog severity and treatment response to chronic GLP-1 RA.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** If central GLP-1 resistance exists, it would explain why peripherally measurable GLP-1 biology looks normal in some patients who still have CNS symptoms. Identifies "CNS-GLP-1-deficient" subgroup.

---

### Idea 9.5: Urinary GLP-1 (or GLP-1 Metabolite) as a Non-Invasive, Repeatable Biomarker (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.20)

**Rationale:** Measuring GLP-1 requires venipuncture, precise timing (GLP-1 half-life ~2 min with DPP-4 inhibition), and expensive ELISA kits. Urinary GLP-1 fragments (GLP-1 9-36 amide, the primary DPP-4 cleavage product) may provide a non-invasive integrated measure of GLP-1 secretion over hours. If validated against serum GLP-1 AUC, this would enable repeated home-based measurement (e.g., before/after meals, PEM episodes, drug administration) without clinical visits.

**Evidence:** GLP-1 fragments are excreted in urine in rodents. No validated human urinary GLP-1 assay exists. Urinary peptide measurement is technically challenging (degradation, concentration variability). The approach is speculative.

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.20 (no validated assay; technical challenges significant; if feasible, transformative for at-home monitoring)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Urinary GLP-1 9-36/creatinine ratio will correlate with serum GLP-1 AUC (r ≥ 0.6) after mixed meal in healthy controls. If correlation is poor (<0.3), the approach is likely infeasible.

**Relevance to ME/CFS:** Non-invasive, home-based GLP-1 pathway monitoring would enable n-of-1 trials, dose-finding, and PEM-correlation studies without requiring clinical infrastructure.
