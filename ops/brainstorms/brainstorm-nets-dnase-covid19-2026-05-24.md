# NET/DNase Imbalance in ME/CFS: Creative Hypotheses and Research Directions

**Date:** 2026-05-24
**Topic:** NET (neutrophil extracellular trap) / DNase imbalance in post-viral conditions
**Evidence Base:** Garcia et al. 2024 (PMC10919029, n=145, certainty 0.70) + 5 supplementary papers

---

## Executive Summary

This document synthesizes 49 creative, testable ideas for NET/DNase dysfunction in ME/CFS, extrapolated from COVID-19 and other post-viral conditions. The evidence base reveals a clear pattern: diminished DNase activity, elevated NET markers, and pDC dysregulation in severe/post-viral states, yet **zero direct studies exist in ME/CFS** for NETosis, DNase activity, or pDC biology. These gaps represent a critical research opportunity.

Key patterns from COVID-19 literature:
- DNase activity inversely correlates with NET formation
- NET/DNase ratio predicts disease severity and hospitalization
- Metabolic dysregulation (insulin resistance) drives NETosis
- NETs structurally associate with microclots
- pDC dysregulation is evident (though directionality unresolved)

Ideas range from high-certainty extrapolations (Tier 1, grounded in strong COVID-19 evidence) to speculative but plausible mechanisms (Tier 3, requiring validation). The most compelling categories for immediate ME/CFS investigation include: (1) NET/DNase diagnostic profiling, (2) insulin-NETosis linkage, (3) pDC-endothelial axis, (4) DNase therapeutic trials, and (5) microvascular dysfunction mechanisms.

**All treatment ideas are speculative and require validation** — no DNase I trials exist in Long COVID or ME/CFS, and all drug repurposing ideas are mechanistic hypotheses awaiting preclinical/clinical testing.

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 pDC Migration Hijack: Tissue Sequestration Drives Circulating Depletion
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Mechanism:** Circulating pDC depletion (Garcia) and tissue pDC expansion (Augustin) reflect a migration hijack: viral persistence in gut tissue creates chemokine gradients (CXCL9/10/11) that recruit pDCs from circulation to gut lamina propria, where they become functionally exhausted. Depleted circulating pDCs cannot support systemic DNase1L3 production, impairing NET clearance.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (pDC depletion), Augustin 2026 (gut pDC expansion + viral persistence)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) decreased circulating pDCs, (b) increased gut-mucosa pDCs, (c) elevated serum CXCL9/10/11, (d) correlation between pDC gradient magnitude and NET/DNase ratio

### 1.2 NET-Mediated Microvascular Glymphatic Impairment
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Mechanism:** NETs in cerebral microvasculature (peripheral-to-central trafficking via compromised blood-brain barrier) physically obstruct perivascular spaces, impairing glymphatic clearance of metabolic waste (amyloid-β, tau, inflammatory cytokines). This creates a feedback loop: impaired clearance → neuroinflammation → endothelial activation → further NETosis.
**Evidence Link:** Thierry 2025 (NETs in microclots), Garcia 2024 (NET-DNA predicts hospitalization), glymphatic literature (perivascular flow pathways)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients with elevated H3cit-DNA show: (a) reduced CSF-to-serum albumin ratio, (b) increased perivascular NET deposits on post-mortem histology, (c) correlation between NET markers and glymphatic clearance metrics

### 1.3 Insulin-NETosis Feed-Forward Loop in ME/CFS
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Mechanism:** De novo insulin resistance in ME/CFS drives NETosis via Akt/mTOR activation independent of hyperglycemia (Sanhueza 2026). NETs sequester insulin and insulin receptor substrates, worsening insulin resistance. Concurrently, NET-derived histones damage endothelial cells, further impairing insulin delivery. This creates a feed-forward loop: insulin resistance → NETosis → impaired insulin signaling → worsened insulin resistance.
**Evidence Link:** Sanhueza 2026 (insulin drives NETosis independently of glucose), Garcia 2024 (NETs correlate with CRP)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) positive correlation between HOMA-IR and NET markers, (b) NETs co-localize with insulin receptors on endothelial cells, (c) insulin sensitizers (metformin) reduce NET formation ex vivo

### 1.4 pDC-Microglia Crosstalk: Missing Link in Neuroinflammation
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Mechanism:** Depleted circulating pDCs (Garcia) cannot provide regulatory signals (IFN-α/β, IL-10) to microglia, leading to microglial priming and exaggerated neuroinflammatory responses to mild stimuli. Concurrently, NETs crossing the BBB activate microglia via TLR9 (recognizing NET DNA), creating a "double-hit" mechanism: pDC deficiency + NET over-activation → microglial dysregulation.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (pDC depletion), Asaba 2026 (NETs as general post-viral mechanism), microglia literature (pDC regulation)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) inverse correlation between pDC count and microglial activation (TSPO-PET), (b) NET markers correlate with neuroinflammation markers in CSF, (c) pDC-derived IFN-α reduces microglial activation in vitro

### 1.5 DNase Deficiency as a "Hidden" Autoimmune Trigger
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Mechanism:** Chronic NET accumulation due to DNase deficiency exposes self-DNA/histone complexes to the immune system, breaking B-cell tolerance. These complexes form immune complexes with anti-NET antibodies, deposit in tissues (endothelium, kidney, brain), and trigger complement activation. This explains the overlap between ME/CFS symptoms (fatigue, pain, cognitive dysfunction) and autoimmune-like manifestations.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET/DNase ratio correlates with CRP), autoimmune literature (NETs as autoantigen source), ME/CFS autoimmune theories
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) anti-NET antibodies (anti-histone H3cit, anti-MPO-DNA), (b) complement activation markers (C3a, C5a), (c) correlation between autoantibody titers and symptom severity

### 1.6 Mitochondrial-NETosis Cross-Talk: Energy Crisis Mechanism
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Mechanism:** NET formation consumes mitochondrial resources via ROS production and NADPH oxidase activation. In ME/CFS, baseline mitochondrial impairment makes neutrophils vulnerable to "NETosis exhaustion": excessive NET formation depletes ATP, leading to impaired mitochondrial biogenesis and further ROS production. This creates a vicious cycle: mitochondrial dysfunction → exaggerated NETosis → ATP depletion → worsened mitochondrial dysfunction.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET markers correlate with severity), ME/CFS mitochondrial dysfunction literature, NETosis literature (mitochondrial ROS dependency)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) inverse correlation between mitochondrial membrane potential (JC-1) and NET markers, (b) NET formation correlates with lactate production, (c) mitochondrial boosters (CoQ10, ALCAR) reduce NETosis ex vivo

### 1.7 NET-Induced Endothelial Glycocalyx Degradation
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Mechanism:** NETs contain heparanase and neutrophil elastase, which degrade the endothelial glycocalyx (heparan sulfate proteoglycans). Glycocalyx degradation exposes endothelial adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, VCAM-1), promoting leukocyte adhesion and further inflammation. In ME/CFS, chronic NET exposure creates "sticky" vasculature, impairing microvascular flow and contributing to tissue hypoxia and PEM.
**Evidence Link:** Thierry 2025 (NETs structurally associated with microclots), glycocalyx literature (heparanase/NET link), microvascular dysfunction in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) increased circulating syndecan-1 (glycocalyx degradation marker), (b) elevated endothelial adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, VCAM-1), (c) correlation between NET markers and glycocalyx integrity

### 1.8 Gut-Derived NET Priming: Viral Persistence Barrier
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Mechanism:** Viral persistence in gut tissue (Augustin 2026) creates a local "NET priming" environment: gut epithelial cells release alarmins (IL-33, IL-25, TSLP) that prime neutrophils toward exaggerated NETosis upon systemic activation. Concurrently, impaired gut barrier function allows bacterial products (LPS) to enter circulation, further priming neutrophils. This explains post-exertional symptom exacerbation: exercise increases gut permeability → LPS translocation → primed neutrophils undergo NETosis.
**Evidence Link:** Augustin 2026 (gut pDC expansion + viral persistence), Sanhueza 2026 (insulin drives NETosis), gut-permeability literature in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) increased serum LPS and gut permeability markers (zonulin), (b) gut-derived alarmins (IL-33, TSLP) correlate with NET markers, (c) gut-targeted interventions (diet, probiotics) reduce NET formation

### 1.9 DNase Polymorphism Phenotype: ME/CFS Susceptibility Allele
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Mechanism:** DNASE1 polymorphisms (Garcia 2024) reduce DNase1 antigen by 75%, creating a genetic predisposition to NET accumulation. In ME/CFS, these polymorphisms are overrepresented, creating a "genetic hit" that, when combined with an environmental trigger (viral infection, stress), leads to chronic NET/DNase imbalance. This explains heterogeneity: only a subset of ME/CFS patients have this polymorphism-driven susceptibility.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (DNASE1 polymorphisms → 75% reduced DNase1), ME/CFS heterogeneity literature, genetic susceptibility theories
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS cohorts show: (a) overrepresentation of DNASE1 loss-of-function alleles, (b) allele carriers have elevated NET/DNase ratios, (c) allele carriers respond preferentially to DNase therapy

### 1.10 NET-Mediated Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) Axis Suppression
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Mechanism:** NETs crossing the BBB accumulate in the hypothalamus, damaging corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) neurons via histone-mediated cytotoxicity. Concurrently, NET-induced neuroinflammation impairs HPA axis signaling, creating blunted cortisol responses. This explains the "hypocortisolism" paradox in ME/CFS: chronic inflammation drives NET accumulation → HPA axis damage → impaired stress response.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET-DNA predicts hospitalization), NET neurotoxicity literature, HPA axis dysregulation in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) reduced hypothalamic CRH expression on PET, (b) correlation between NET markers and blunted cortisol awakening response, (c) NETs co-localize with CRH neurons on post-mortem histology

## 2. Research Directions

### 2.1 NET/DNase Profiling in ME/CFS: Foundational Observational Study
**Certainty:** 0.75
**Mechanism:** Establish baseline prevalence of NET/DNase dysregulation in ME/CFS by measuring NET markers (MPO-DNA, H3cit, H3cit-DNA) and DNase activity in plasma. This is the critical first step to validate whether COVID-19 patterns extrapolate to ME/CFS.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET/DNase dysregulation in severe COVID-19), zero ME/CFS studies
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) elevated NET markers vs. healthy controls, (b) reduced DNase activity vs. healthy controls, (c) elevated NET/DNase ratio correlates with symptom severity

### 2.2 pDC Subtyping: Circulating vs. Tissue Dynamics
**Certainty:** 0.70
**Mechanism:** Resolve pDC directionality contradiction by measuring circulating pDCs (flow cytometry), tissue pDCs (gut mucosal biopsies), and pDC functional markers (IFN-α production, DNase1L3 expression). This will determine whether ME/CFS shows circulating depletion (Garcia pattern) or tissue expansion (Augustin pattern).
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (pDC depletion), Augustin 2026 (gut pDC expansion)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) decreased circulating pDCs, (b) increased gut mucosa pDCs, (c) inverse correlation between circulating and tissue pDCs

### 2.3 Insulin Resistance as NETosis Driver in ME/CFS
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Mechanism:** Test the insulin-NETosis link in ME/CFS by measuring insulin resistance (HOMA-IR), NET markers, and conducting ex vivo neutrophil stimulation with insulin. This validates whether the Sanhueza 2026 findings (insulin drives NETosis) apply to ME/CFS.
**Evidence Link:** Sanhueza 2026 (insulin drives NETosis independently of glucose), ME/CFS insulin resistance reports
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) positive correlation between HOMA-IR and NET markers, (b) insulin enhances NETosis ex vivo, (c) insulin sensitizers reduce NET formation

### 2.4 NET Degradation Assay: Functional DNase Capacity
**Certainty:** 0.70
**Mechanism:** Develop and validate a NET degradation assay using ME/CFS plasma to quantify functional DNase capacity. This assay measures the ability of patient plasma to degrade pre-formed NETs in vitro, providing a functional readout beyond static DNase activity measurements.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (DNase activity inversely correlates with NET markers), zero ME/CFS NET degradation studies
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS plasma shows: (a) impaired NET degradation vs. healthy controls, (b) degradation capacity correlates with symptom severity, (c) degradation is rescued by recombinant DNase I

### 2.5 DNASE1/DNASE1L3 Genotyping in ME/CFS Cohorts
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Mechanism:** Screen ME/CFS cohorts for DNASE1 and DNASE1L3 polymorphisms to determine genetic susceptibility to NET/DNase imbalance. This will identify a potential genetic subset of ME/CFS patients who may benefit from DNase-targeted therapies.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (DNASE1 polymorphisms → 75% reduced DNase1), zero ME/CFS genotyping studies
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS cohorts show: (a) overrepresentation of DNASE1 loss-of-function alleles, (b) allele carriers have elevated NET/DNase ratios, (c) allele carriers have worse symptom severity

### 2.6 pDC-Microglia Crosstalk: In Vitro Validation
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Mechanism:** Establish an in vitro co-culture system with pDCs and microglia to test the pDC-microglia regulatory hypothesis. Measure microglial activation (CD68, TNF-α) in response to pDC-derived IFN-α/β and NET exposure.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (pDC depletion), microglia literature (pDC regulation), neuroinflammation in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** In vitro experiments show: (a) pDC-derived IFN-α reduces microglial activation, (b) NETs activate microglia via TLR9, (c) pDC-deficient conditions exacerbate NET-induced microglial activation

### 2.7 Glymphatic-NET Interactions: Imaging Study
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Mechanism:** Use advanced MRI techniques (DTI-ALPS, intravoxel incoherent motion) to measure glymphatic function in ME/CFS and correlate with NET markers. This tests the hypothesis that NETs impair glymphatic clearance via perivascular obstruction.
**Evidence Link:** Thierry 2025 (NETs in microclots), glymphatic literature (perivascular flow), neuroimaging in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) reduced glymphatic clearance metrics, (b) inverse correlation between NET markers and glymphatic function, (c) glymphatic impairment correlates with cognitive symptoms

### 2.8 Gut-Viral Persistence and NET Priming: Longitudinal Study
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Mechanism:** Conduct a longitudinal study measuring gut viral persistence (PCR, viral sequencing), pDC dynamics, and NET markers in post-infective ME/CFS. This tests the hypothesis that gut viral persistence creates a NET-priming environment.
**Evidence Link:** Augustin 2026 (gut pDC expansion + viral persistence), Sanhueza 2026 (insulin drives NETosis)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) persistent gut viral RNA, (b) elevated gut pDCs, (c) correlation between viral load and NET markers

### 2.9 Exercise-Induced NETosis: PEM Mechanism Study
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Mechanism:** Measure NET markers pre- and post-exercise (CPET) in ME/CFS to test whether post-exertional malaise (PEM) is driven by exaggerated NETosis. This establishes a mechanistic link between physical exertion and immune activation.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET markers correlate with severity), PEM literature in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) NET marker elevation post-CPET, (b) magnitude of NETosis correlates with PEM severity, (c) healthy controls show no NET marker change

### 2.10 Autoantibody Profiling: NET as Autoantigen
**Certainty:** 0.45
**Mechanism:** Screen ME/CFS sera for anti-NET antibodies (anti-histone H3cit, anti-MPO-DNA, anti-DNase) to test the autoimmune hypothesis. Correlate antibody titers with symptom severity and complement activation markers.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET/DNase ratio correlates with CRP), autoimmune literature (NETs as autoantigen source)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) elevated anti-NET antibodies, (b) antibody titers correlate with symptom severity, (c) antibodies correlate with complement activation

### 2.11 Endothelial Glycocalyx Integrity and NETs
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Mechanism:** Measure glycocalyx degradation markers (syndecan-1, hyaluronan) and endothelial adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, VCAM-1) in ME/CFS and correlate with NET markers. This tests the NET-induced glycocalyx degradation hypothesis.
**Evidence Link:** Thierry 2025 (NETs in microclots), glycocalyx literature (heparanase/NET link), microvascular dysfunction in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) elevated syndecan-1 and ICAM-1, (b) correlation between NET markers and glycocalyx degradation, (c) glycocalyx impairment correlates with exercise intolerance

### 2.12 Mitochondrial-NETosis Cross-Talk: Ex Vivo Study
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Mechanism:** Isolate neutrophils from ME/CFS patients and measure mitochondrial function (OCR, membrane potential) before and after NETosis induction. This tests the mitochondrial-NETosis vicious cycle hypothesis.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET markers correlate with severity), ME/CFS mitochondrial dysfunction literature, NETosis literature (mitochondrial ROS dependency)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS neutrophils show: (a) reduced baseline mitochondrial function, (b) exaggerated NETosis-induced mitochondrial dysfunction, (c) correlation between mitochondrial impairment and NET formation

### 2.13 pDC Chemokine Profiling: Migration Hijack Mechanism
**Certainty:** 0.60
**Mechanism:** Measure serum chemokines (CXCL9, CXCL10, CXCL11) and pDC chemokine receptors (CXCR3) in ME/CFS to test the pDC migration hijack hypothesis. Correlate chemokine gradients with circulating vs. tissue pDC counts.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (pDC depletion), Augustin 2026 (gut pDC expansion), pDC migration literature
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) elevated CXCL9/10/11, (b) decreased circulating pDCs, (c) inverse correlation between chemokine levels and circulating pDCs

### 2.14 Hypothalamic NET Accumulation: Post-Mortem Study
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Mechanism:** Conduct post-mortem histology on ME/CFS brains to visualize NET accumulation (H3cit staining) in the hypothalamus and other regions. This tests the NET-mediated HPA axis suppression hypothesis.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (NET-DNA predicts hospitalization), NET neurotoxicity literature, HPA axis dysregulation in ME/CFS
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS post-mortem tissue shows: (a) increased H3cit+ cells in hypothalamus, (b) NETs co-localize with CRH neurons, (c) NET density correlates with clinical HPA axis dysfunction

### 2.15 DNase I Pharmacokinetics in ME/CFS
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Mechanism:** Conduct a pilot study measuring DNase I half-life, distribution, and NET degradation efficacy in ME/CFS patients. This establishes pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic parameters for future therapeutic trials.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (DNase activity inversely correlates with NET markers), zero ME/CFS DNase trials
**Falsifiable Prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) altered DNase I clearance vs. healthy controls, (b) reduced NET degradation capacity despite normal DNase levels, (c) DNase I administration reduces NET markers

### 2.16 Cross-Disease NET Profiling: ME/CFS vs. Long COVID vs. Sepsis
**Certainty:** 0.65
**Mechanism:** Compare NET/DNase profiles across ME/CFS, Long COVID, sepsis survivors, and healthy controls to identify unique patterns. This tests the hypothesis that ME/CFS has a distinct NET signature.
**Evidence Link:** Asaba 2026 (NETs as general post-viral mechanism), Garcia 2024 (NETs in severe COVID-19), cross-disease NET literature
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Cross-disease analysis shows: (a) ME/CFS has distinct NET/DNase signature vs. Long COVID, (b) ME/CFS pattern more similar to chronic sepsis survivors, (c) specific NET marker combinations differentiate conditions

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### 3.1 Recombinant DNase I (dornase alfa) Repurposing
**Certainty:** 0.50 (mechanistic) / 0.20 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Administer recombinant DNase I (FDA-approved for cystic fibrosis) to degrade accumulated NETs and restore NET/DNase balance. DNase I cleaves DNA backbone of NETs, promoting clearance and reducing NET-mediated inflammation.
**Evidence Link:** Garcia 2024 (DNase deficiency in severe COVID-19), cystic fibrosis efficacy data, zero ME/CFS/Long COVID trials
**Falsifiable Prediction:** DNase I treatment in ME/CFS leads to: (a) reduced NET markers, (b) improved symptom scores, (c) enhanced exercise tolerance (CPET)

**CAUTION:** Unproven in ME/CFS; safety in non-cystic fibrosis populations unclear; NET degradation may release pro-inflammatory DAMPs.

### 3.2 Disulfiram: NET Inhibition via PAD4 Inhibition
**Certainty:** 0.45 (mechanistic) / 0.15 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Disulfiram (FDA-approved for alcohol use disorder) inhibits peptidyl arginine deiminase 4 (PAD4), the enzyme required for histone citrullination and NET formation. By blocking PAD4, disulfiram prevents NETosis.
**Evidence Link:** PAD4 inhibition literature (disulfiram as NET inhibitor), NET formation mechanism, zero ME/CFS trials
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Disulfiram treatment in ME/CFS leads to: (a) reduced H3cit formation, (b) reduced NET markers, (c) improved fatigue scores

**CAUTION:** Disulfiram has significant side effects (hepatotoxicity, neuropathy); NET inhibition may impair pathogen clearance.

### 3.3 Metformin: Insulin Sensitization → NETosis Reduction
**Certainty:** 0.55 (mechanistic) / 0.25 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Metformin (FDA-approved for type 2 diabetes) improves insulin sensitivity and activates AMPK, which inhibits NETosis via mTOR pathway suppression. By reducing insulin resistance, metformin indirectly reduces NETosis.
**Evidence Link:** Sanhueza 2026 (insulin drives NETosis), metformin AMPK activation literature, zero ME/CFS NETosis trials
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Metformin treatment in ME/CFS leads to: (a) improved HOMA-IR, (b) reduced NET markers, (c) reduced PEM severity

**CAUTION:** Metformin may cause GI side effects; long-term safety in ME/CFS unknown; may not benefit patients without insulin resistance.

### 3.4 Statins: NET Inhibition via Isoprenoid Pathway Blockade
**Certainty:** 0.40 (mechanistic) / 0.20 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Statins (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin) inhibit HMG-CoA reductase, reducing isoprenoid intermediates required for Rac1 GTPase prenylation. Rac1 activation is essential for NETosis; statins block this pathway.
**Evidence Link:** Statin NET inhibition literature, NET formation mechanism, zero ME/CFS NETosis trials
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Statin treatment in ME/CFS leads to: (a) reduced NET formation ex vivo, (b) reduced circulating NET markers, (c) improved endothelial function

**CAUTION:** Statins have muscle-related side effects; may worsen ME/CFS myalgia; long-term safety in ME/CFS unknown.

### 3.5 Low-Dose Naltrexone (LDN): NET Modulation via TLR4 Antagonism
**Certainty:** 0.35 (mechanistic) / 0.15 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** LDN (off-label use for chronic pain) antagonizes Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4), which recognizes NET components (histones, DNA) and amplifies inflammation. By blocking TLR4, LDN reduces NET-mediated inflammation.
**Evidence Link:** LDN TLR4 antagonism literature, NET-TLR4 interactions, LDN in ME/CFS (limited data)
**Falsifiable Prediction:** LDN treatment in ME/CFS leads to: (a) reduced NET-mediated inflammation (IL-1β, IL-6), (b) improved pain scores, (c) reduced NET markers

**CAUTION:** LDN evidence in ME/CFS is anecdotal; mechanism for NET modulation is indirect; may interfere with pathogen recognition.

### 3.6 Colchicine: NET Inhibition via Microtubule Disruption
**Certainty:** 0.40 (mechanistic) / 0.20 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Colchicine (FDA-approved for gout) disrupts microtubule assembly, which is required for NETosis chromatin decondensation. By inhibiting microtubule polymerization, colchicine prevents NET formation.
**Evidence Link:** Colchicine NET inhibition literature, NET formation mechanism, zero ME/CFS NETosis trials

### 3.7 Allopurinol: NET Inhibition via ROS Scavenging
**Certainty:** 0.35 (mechanistic) / 0.15 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Allopurinol (FDA-approved for gout) inhibits xanthine oxidase, reducing reactive oxygen species (ROS) production. NETosis requires ROS burst; allopurinol may indirectly inhibit NETosis by reducing ROS.

**Mechanism:** Hydroxychloroquine (FDA-approved for malaria, autoimmune diseases) raises endosomal pH, inhibiting endosomal Toll-like receptors (TLR7/8/9) that recognize NET components. By blocking NET recognition, HCQ reduces NET-mediated inflammation.

### 3.10 Aspirin: NET Inhibition via COX Inhibition and Platelet-Neutrophil Decoupling
**Certainty:** 0.40 (mechanistic) / 0.20 (clinical efficacy untested)
**Mechanism:** Aspirin inhibits cyclooxygenase (COX), reducing thromboxane A2 production and platelet activation. Platelet-neutrophil interactions drive NETosis; aspirin decouples this interaction. Additionally, aspirin triggers production of anti-inflammatory resolvins.
**Evidence Link:** Aspirin NET inhibition literature, platelet-NETosis link, zero ME/CFS NETosis trials
**Falsifiable Prediction:** Aspirin treatment in ME/CFS leads to: (a) reduced platelet-neutrophil aggregates, (b) reduced NET formation ex vivo, (c) improved microvascular flow
