# Brainstorm: Infection-Driven CoQ10 Depletion in ME/CFS — Consumption vs Biosynthesis

**Topic:** Whether CoQ10 deficiency in ME/CFS is causally driven by ongoing pathogen-induced oxidative consumption (persistent/reactivated infection → sustained ROS → oxidative consumption of CoQ10 faster than regeneration) — as opposed to a biosynthesis defect, mitochondrial-biogenesis downregulation, statins, or malabsorption.

**Date:** 2026-07-17

**Evidence base (10 papers):** Maes2009CoQ10 (0.55), Morris2013CoQ10 (0.65), Morris2014Mito (0.60), Maes2014OSEs (0.50), Maes2021Reification (0.55), Schreiner2020HHV6Mito (0.60), Al-Hakeim2023LongCOVIDOx (0.65), Laredj2014CoQBiosynth (0.70), Maes2009IOandNS (0.50), Maes2011DepressionCFS (0.55).

**Key fact:** The core causal chain (infection → ROS → CoQ10 consumption → deficiency) is entirely untested in ME/CFS. The competing biosynthesis explanation (Laredj2014CoQBiosynth) is equal-weighted. CoQ10 data is plasma-only, no tissue data.

**Existing paper anchors (do NOT duplicate):**

## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### Idea 1.1 — Consumption-Rate > Regeneration-Rate as Core Defect (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The cycle: pathogen (EBV, HHV-6, enterovirus, tick-borne) → sustained ROS (from immune NADPH oxidase, mitochondrial leak, xanthine oxidase) → CoQ10 consumed quenching lipid peroxyl radicals faster than ETC + glutathione/vitamin E can regenerate it → membrane lipid peroxidation unchecked (Maes2014OSEs) → ETC complex I/III damage → more ROS leak → faster consumption. Morris2013CoQ10 provides the biochemical framework but the quantitative rate question (consumption vs regeneration) has never been measured in ME/CFS. The critical parameter — CoQ10 turnover half-life in vivo — is unknown for any ME/CFS population.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (consumption/regeneration framework), Maes2014OSEs (lipid peroxidation), Maes2021Reification (MDA adducts), Maes2009CoQ10 (deficiency)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** CoQ10 turnover rate measured by 13C-isotope labeling will be 2–4× higher in ME/CFS vs controls and correlate with plasma MDA adducts (r > 0.5). CoQ10 regeneration capacity (ubiquinol/ubiquinone ratio) will be lower at any given total CoQ10 level.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The body's natural CoQ10 recycling system may be overwhelmed by constant demand from infection-driven inflammation — like a fire extinguisher emptied faster than it can be refilled.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.2 — Pathogen-Specific ROS Output Determines CoQ10 Depletion Magnitude (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Not all infections produce equal ROS. HHV-6 causes mitochondrial fragmentation (Schreiner2020HHV6Mito) → direct structural damage that amplifies ROS leak intrinsically. EBV lytic reactivation induces NOX2 in B cells → extracellular superoxide. Tick-borne pathogens activate trained immunity → chronic low-grade ROS from macrophages. Enteroviruses cause direct proteolytic damage to mitochondrial proteins. The magnitude of CoQ10 depletion may track the pathogen-specific ROS profile: HHV-6 > EBV-lytic > tick-borne > enterovirus (hypothesized) — predicting a measurable biomarker signature.
- **Evidence link:** Schreiner2020HHV6Mito (HHV-6 → mito fragmentation → ROS), Maes2009IOandNS (infection framework), Maes2011DepressionCFS (infections as triggers)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Plasma CoQ10 will be lowest in ME/CFS subsets with active HHV-6 serology (elevated IgM or high IgG), intermediate in EBV-lytic (EA-D IgG+), and least depleted in enterovirus-PCR+ subsets, after controlling for illness duration and severity.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Different infections may drain CoQ10 at different rates — HHV-6 might be worst because it directly damages cell power plants, not just the immune system.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.3 — CoQ10 Depletion Links NO/ONOO- Cycle and ETC Damage Feed-Forward (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The paper's NO/ONOO- cycle (Pall) and Morris2014Mito feed-forward loop can be unified: CoQ10 depletion is the mechanistic link. NO/ONOO- → peroxynitrite → lipid peroxidation → CoQ10 consumed → ETC complex I/III impaired → electron leak → more superoxide → ONOO- → more lipid peroxidation → more CoQ10 consumption. CoQ10 is the "battery" whose depletion collapses the whole system. The model predicts CoQ10 supplementation should simultaneously improve ETC function AND reduce oxidative markers AND break the NO/ONOO- cycle — three endpoints rarely measured together.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (biochemical framework), Morris2014Mito (feed-forward inflammation→ROS→ETC), Maes2014OSEs, Maes2021Reification
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a CoQ10 supplementation trial measuring all three domains: (1) plasma MDA will fall, (2) ETC complex I activity (PBMC) will rise, (3) NO metabolites will fall — and the magnitude of change in each will be correlated pairwise (r > 0.4 each pair).
- **Non-specialist consequence:** CoQ10 may be the critical linchpin connecting several known ME/CFS dysfunctions — not just an antioxidant shortage but the collapse of a whole cycle of energy production and waste management.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.4 — Tissue-Specific CoQ10: Plasma ≠ Muscle ≠ Brain (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.28
- **Mechanistic rationale:** All ME/CFS CoQ10 data is from plasma (Maes2009CoQ10). CoQ10 turnover differs across tissues: muscle CoQ10 turns over in days (exercise-responsive), brain CoQ10 in weeks (BBB isolates it). If infection-driven consumption is tissue-specific (HHV-6 in glia → brain CoQ10 depletion; enterovirus in muscle → muscle CoQ10 depletion; EBV in B cells → plasma CoQ10 depletion), plasma could be normal while critical tissues are depleted — explaining why some patients with normal plasma CoQ10 respond to supplementation or have discordant symptom profiles.
- **Evidence link:** Maes2009CoQ10 (plasma only), Morris2013CoQ10 (biochemical framework), Schreiner2020HHV6Mito (HHV-6 in glia)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Muscle biopsy CoQ10 and CSF CoQ10 will show poor correlation with plasma CoQ10 (r < 0.3) in ME/CFS, and cognitive vs muscle fatigue severity will correlate with tissue-specific but not plasma CoQ10.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A blood test for CoQ10 may not tell us about the brain or muscles — normal blood levels could hide critically low CoQ10 in tissues that matter most.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.5 — Glutathione Depletion Secondarily Impairs CoQ10 Regeneration (rank: 5)
- **Certainty:** 0.32
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Morris2013CoQ10 states CoQ10 is regenerated by the ETC AND by glutathione/vitamin E. If glutathione is depleted (well-documented in ME/CFS), CoQ10 regeneration is impaired even if ETC function is normal. Glutathione deficiency → CoQ10 can't be recharged → functional CoQ10 deficiency despite adequate total CoQ10 → unchecked lipid peroxidation → ETC damage → more ROS → more glutathione consumption. A parsimonious explanation connecting two well-documented findings (low GSH, low CoQ10) via a specific mechanism (regeneration failure).
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (regeneration by glutathione), Maes2014OSEs, Maes2021Reification (oxidative stress)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with lowest GSH/GSSG ratio will show the lowest ubiquinol/ubiquinone ratio, independent of total CoQ10. NAC supplementation will increase the ubiquinol/ubiquinone ratio before changing total CoQ10.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The body's master antioxidant (glutathione) may be needed to "recharge" CoQ10. When glutathione runs low, CoQ10 can't be recycled — causing a functional deficiency even if total levels are normal.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 1.6 — Enteroviral Protease 2A Directly Cleaves COQ Biosynthesis Enzymes (rank: 6)
- **Certainty:** 0.12
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Enteroviral protease 2A (found in ME/CFS muscle, Chia) cleaves host proteins at specific sequences (dystrophin, — known). COQ3, COQ5, COQ7 (CoQ biosynthesis enzymes) contain potential 2A cleavage motifs (LxxGxG, computational prediction — speculative). If enteroviral 2A directly degrades CoQ biosynthesis enzymes, this bridges the consumption vs biosynthesis false dichotomy: a biosynthesis defect that is infection-driven and dynamic (worse during active infection, improving with clearance) rather than static genetic (Laredj2014CoQBiosynth).
- **Evidence link:** Laredj2014CoQBiosynth (static genetic defect — this would be a dynamic, infection-driven version), Maes2009IOandNS (enterovirus in ME/CFS). No experimental evidence exists for 2A cleavage of COQ proteins.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Enterovirus-positive ME/CFS will show lower COQ3/5/7 protein levels (not mRNA) in muscle biopsy vs enterovirus-negative ME/CFS. In vitro, enteroviral 2A protease expression will reduce COQ3/5/7 in HEK293 cells. Refuted if no protein-level difference or no in-vitro cleavage.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Enteroviruses found in some ME/CFS patients' muscles might directly destroy the cellular machinery that builds CoQ10 — not just by creating oxidative stress, but by attacking the production factory itself.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 2: Research Directions

### Idea 2.1 — Plasma CoQ10 Turnover Kinetics by Stable Isotope Labeling (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The central untested parameter is CoQ10 consumption rate. A single-dose 13C-labeled CoQ10 study with serial plasma sampling over 72h can measure: fractional clearance rate, half-life, volume of distribution, and endogenous synthesis rate. Compare ME/CFS (n=15) vs matched controls (n=15). If clearance is 2× faster → supports consumption > regeneration. If clearance normal but baseline low → supports biosynthesis defect. This single experiment distinguishes the two competing hypotheses. Cost: ~$50K.
- **Evidence link:** Maes2009CoQ10 (deficiency), Morris2013CoQ10 (consumption vs synthesis), Laredj2014CoQBiosynth (competing biosynthesis)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS CoQ10 half-life will be significantly shorter than controls (e.g., 18h vs 36h). Endogenous synthesis rate will be normal or increased (compensatory). Refuted if half-life is equal.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A single outpatient study using a labeled (non-radioactive) form of CoQ10 could settle whether ME/CFS patients burn through CoQ10 too fast or simply don't make enough.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.2 — Ubiquinol/Ubiquinone Ratio as Regeneration Capacity Measure (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.40
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Total CoQ10 cannot distinguish consumption from biosynthesis. The ubiquinol/ubiquinone ratio reflects regeneration capacity. In healthy cells >90% is reduced (ubiquinol). Lower ratio indicates consumption exceeds regeneration. Never reported in ME/CFS. HPLC-ECD measurement is standard, cost ~$5K for 100 samples. If ratio is normal despite low total CoQ10 → biosynthesis defect. If ratio low → regeneration failure (consumption or glutathione depletion).
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (ratio as functional index), Maes2009CoQ10 (total only — this adds the missing dimension)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS will show lower %ubiquinol (<70%) vs controls (>85%). %ubiquinol will correlate with GSH/GSSG (r > 0.5) more strongly than with total CoQ10. 30% of patients with normal total CoQ10 will have low %ubiquinol, revealing hidden functional deficiency.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A simple upgrade to the standard CoQ10 blood test — measuring charged vs discharged forms — could reveal whether the real problem is using CoQ10 too fast or just having too little.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.3 — Stratified CoQ10 by Infection Serology (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Measure plasma CoQ10, %ubiquinol, and MDA in ME/CFS stratified by infection serology: (1) HHV-6 IgG/IgM+, (2) EBV EA-D IgG+ (lytic), (3) enterovirus PCR+, (4) tick-borne IgG+, (5) multiple, (6) no active markers. Cross-sectional, N=120 (20/group). Adds only CoQ10 and MDA to existing serology panels — low marginal cost. Would generate the first pathogen-stratified CoQ10 data, directly testing Idea 1.2.
- **Evidence link:** Maes2009CoQ10, Schreiner2020HHV6Mito, Maes2009IOandNS, Al-Hakeim2023LongCOVIDOx
- **Falsifiable prediction:** HHV-6-positive group will have lowest mean CoQ10 and %ubiquinol, followed by tick-borne, EBV-lytic, and enterovirus. CoQ10 will correlate inversely with number of active reactivations. Refuted if no difference by serology group.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A blood test comparing CoQ10 levels across infection profiles could reveal which pathogens most drain this essential molecule — guiding personalized treatment.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.4 — CoQ10 Supplementation RCT Stratified by Infection Marker (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If CoQ10 depletion is infection-driven, supplementation response should be greater in patients with active infection markers (consumption > synthesis) than in those without (biosynthesis defect → poor response). Re-analyse Castro-Marrero2021 CoQ10+NADH RCT data stratified by baseline infection serology, or run a new 12-week RCT of cocrystal ubiquinol 200mg + NADH 20mg vs placebo in N=120 stratified by HHV-6/EBV-EA-D serology. Primary: fatigue score. Secondary: CoQ10, MDA, serology change.
- **Evidence link:** Maes2009CoQ10 (deficiency), Castro-Marrero2021 (CoQ10+NADH RCT), Schreiner2020HHV6Mito (HHV-6→ROS), Laredj2014CoQBiosynth (competing)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Patients with ≥1 active infection marker will show ≥50% greater fatigue improvement on CoQ10+NADH than those without markers. Refuted if response equal across serology groups.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Past supplement trials may have yielded modest results because they didn't account for which patients' CoQ10 is being actively drained by infections — those with active infections might benefit most.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.5 — CSF CoQ10 in Cognitive vs Muscle-Fatigue Phenotypes (rank: 5)
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Plasma CoQ10 may not reflect brain CoQ10. If glial HHV-6 (Schreiner2020HHV6Mito) causes local ROS → local CoQ10 consumption, the brain could be depleted despite normal plasma. CSF CoQ10 never measured in ME/CFS. Lumbar puncture in n=20 ME/CFS (10 predominantly cognitive fatigue, 10 predominantly muscle fatigue) + 10 controls. Primary: CSF CoQ10 and CSF-to-plasma ratio. Correlation with neurocognitive testing.
- **Evidence link:** Schreiner2020HHV6Mito (glial HHV-6 → ROS), Maes2009CoQ10 (cognitive fatigue correlates with plasma CoQ10), Morris2013CoQ10 (BBB-limited)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Cognitive-fatigue-predominant patients will show lower CSF CoQ10 than muscle-fatigue-predominant patients or controls, even if plasma CoQ10 is similar. CSF CoQ10 will correlate with processing speed (r > 0.5). Refuted if no CSF difference.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A spinal tap could reveal whether brain fog is caused by CoQ10 shortage inside the brain that a blood test would completely miss.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 2.6 — In Vitro CoQ10 Consumption Assay: Patient PBMCs Under Controlled ROS (rank: 6)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Isolate PBMCs from ME/CFS vs controls, measure baseline CoQ10 and %ubiquinol, expose to controlled oxidative stress (H2O2, xanthine/xanthine oxidase), and measure CoQ10 consumption rate and post-challenge recovery over 60min. If ME/CFS cells consume at same rate but recover slower → regeneration defect (glutathione-dependent). If consumption faster → higher intrinsic ROS production or lower antioxidant defenses. Inexpensive in-vitro experiment that distinguishes mechanisms without isotope studies. Cost: ~$10K.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (consumption and regeneration), Maes2014OSEs, Maes2021Reification
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS PBMCs will show 1.5–3× faster CoQ10 consumption rate under oxidative challenge vs controls (normalized to baseline content), and slower %ubiquinol recovery at 60min post-challenge. Refuted if rates are equal.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A simple lab test on patient blood cells could reveal whether the CoQ10 recycling machinery is broken — before expensive human studies.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### Idea 3.1 — NAC to Restore Glutathione-Dependent CoQ10 Regeneration (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.30
- **Mechanistic rationale:** NAC replenishes glutathione, required for CoQ10 regeneration (Morris2013CoQ10). If CoQ10 deficiency is driven by regeneration failure (not biosynthesis defect), NAC should increase the ubiquinol/ubiquinone ratio and improve functional CoQ10 status. NAC is generic, OTC, safe at 600–1200mg/day. This is a test of mechanism, not just treatment. If NAC improves %ubiquinol without changing total CoQ10, the regeneration hypothesis is supported. Research-stage only.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (regeneration by glutathione), Maes2014OSEs (oxidative stress), Maes2009CoQ10 (deficiency)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of NAC 1200mg/day in ME/CFS (n=20) will: (1) increase plasma %ubiquinol by ≥25%, (2) decrease MDA by ≥20%, (3) improve fatigue score by ≥3 points. Refuted if no change in %ubiquinol.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A common antioxidant supplement (NAC) could help recharge CoQ10 by boosting the body's master antioxidant glutathione — potentially improving energy without directly taking more CoQ10.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.2 — Low-Dose Naltrexone as CoQ10-Sparing Anti-Inflammatory (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.20
- **Mechanistic rationale:** LDN reduces microglial activation and inflammatory cytokines via TLR4 antagonism. Reduced inflammation → reduced ROS → reduced CoQ10 consumption. Indirect CoQ10-sparing: if regeneration can't be fixed, reduce consumption. LDN is widely used off-label in ME/CFS. This idea: LDN responders may be those with highest CoQ10 consumption rate (infection-driven oxidative burden). LDN responders should have lower baseline CoQ10 and higher MDA than non-responders.
- **Evidence link:** Maes2014OSEs (oxidative stress), Maes2021Reification, Morris2014Mito (inflammation→ROS→ETC), Maes2009IOandNS
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS starting LDN 4.5mg/day, baseline plasma CoQ10 below median will predict ≥30% fatigue improvement at 12 weeks (AUC > 0.70). Refuted if baseline CoQ10 does not predict response.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A low-dose drug that calms brain inflammation might work best in patients whose CoQ10 is being burned up by inflammation — the drug conserves CoQ10 indirectly.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.3 — MitoQ for Infection-Driven Intramitochondrial Depletion (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.22
- **Mechanistic rationale:** MitoQ is ubiquinol conjugated to a TPP cation targeting it to mitochondria, achieving 100–500× higher mitochondrial concentrations than oral CoQ10. If the critical depletion is intramitochondrial (consumed at complex I/III where ROS is produced), MitoQ bypasses absorption, plasma transport, and membrane penetration barriers. MitoQ has been studied in ME/CFS (small trials, mixed) but never stratified by infection status. In infection-driven depletion, MitoQ should be superior to standard CoQ10. Research-stage.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (consumed at mitochondrial membrane), Schreiner2020HHV6Mito (HHV-6 → mito fragmentation → ROS), Maes2014OSEs
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In HHV-6-seropositive ME/CFS, MitoQ 40mg/day for 12 weeks will produce 2× greater fatigue reduction vs equivalent-dose oral ubiquinol, and better normalize the %ubiquinol. Refuted if responses are equivalent.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A specially engineered CoQ10 that penetrates directly into mitochondria might work better for patients whose energy crisis is driven by infection-related mitochondrial damage.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 3.4 — Doxycycline as Antimicrobial + ROS Scavenger (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.12
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Doxycycline has dual action: (1) antimicrobial against tick-borne pathogens (Borrelia, Ehrlichia, Anaplasma), (2) inhibits MMPs and reduces NO production independently of antimicrobial effect. In tick-borne-triggered ME/CFS, doxycycline could target both the driver (pathogen) and the mechanism (ROS → CoQ10 consumption). However, doxycycline is widely used without clear evidence in this context, and prolonged use carries risks. Research-stage only.
- **Evidence link:** Maes2009IOandNS (infections as triggers), Maes2014OSEs (oxidative stress), Morris2014Mito
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In tick-borne-seropositive ME/CFS, doxycycline 100mg BID for 8 weeks will increase plasma CoQ10 by ≥20% and reduce MDA by ≥25% in a subset analysis. Refuted if CoQ10 does not change.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** An antibiotic used for tick infections might help not just by killing bacteria but by reducing the oxidative stress that consumes CoQ10 — though risks limit its use to research.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Idea 4.1 — Ubiquinol (Reduced Form) over Ubiquinone for Infection-Driven Depletion (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.35
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Ubiquinol is the antioxidant form consumed by ROS. Ubiquinone must be reduced by ETC to become active. In infection-driven depletion with ETC damage (Morris2014Mito), the ETC's ability to reduce ubiquinone → ubiquinol may be impaired, creating a bottleneck. Supplementing pre-reduced ubiquinol bypasses this. Additionally, ubiquinol has higher oral bioavailability. This idea provides a mechanistic rationale for preferring ubiquinol that is infection-specific, beyond general bioavailability arguments.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (ubiquinol as the active form), Morris2014Mito (ETC damage → impaired reduction), Maes2009CoQ10 (deficiency)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS, single-dose ubiquinone 200mg will produce lower peak %ubiquinol (<60% of total) vs ubiquinol 200mg (>85%). %ubiquinol after ubiquinone will correlate with ETC complex I activity (r > 0.5). Refuted if %ubiquinol is equivalent.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The pre-reduced form of CoQ10 may be substantially more effective for ME/CFS patients whose damaged energy factories struggle to activate the standard form.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 4.2 — Selenium + CoQ10 for GPX Support + CoQ10 Sparing (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.28
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Selenium is required for glutathione peroxidase (GPX), which reduces H2O2 and lipid peroxides. By supporting GPX, selenium: (1) spares CoQ10 from being consumed quenching lipid peroxyl radicals, (2) preserves glutathione for CoQ10 regeneration, (3) breaks the feed-forward loop (Morris2014Mito). Selenium is cheap ($5/month), safe at 100–200μg/day. Synergistic pair for infection-driven depletion: CoQ10 replaces what's consumed, selenium reduces how much gets consumed.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (consumption by lipid peroxyl radicals), Maes2014OSEs, Maes2021Reification (lipid peroxidation)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** CoQ10 200mg/day + selenium 200μg/day for 12 weeks will reduce plasma MDA by ≥35% vs CoQ10 alone (≤20%) or selenium alone (≤10%). MDA reduction will correlate with GPX activity increase.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A cheap mineral (selenium) paired with CoQ10 may work better than CoQ10 alone by helping the body's peroxide-cleaning system, reducing how much CoQ10 gets burned up.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 4.3 — Vitamin E (Mixed Tocopherols) for Antioxidant Spillover Relief (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.25
- **Mechanistic rationale:** CoQ10 and vitamin E share membrane antioxidant duty: vitamin E traps the initial lipid peroxyl radical, CoQ10 regenerates vitamin E (Morris2013CoQ10). When CoQ10 is depleted, vitamin E cannot be regenerated and is consumed, creating a secondary vitamin E deficiency that accelerates membrane damage. Supplementing moderate-dose vitamin E (100–200 IU mixed tocopherols) with CoQ10 could: (1) share the antioxidant load (sparing CoQ10 from antioxidant duty), (2) allow remaining CoQ10 for ETC function. Caution: high-dose vitamin E (≥400 IU) associated with increased mortality in meta-analyses.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (CoQ10 regenerates vitamin E), Maes2014OSEs, Maes2021Reification
- **Falsifiable prediction:** CoQ10 200mg + vitamin E 200 IU daily for 12 weeks will produce greater reduction in F2-isoprostanes than CoQ10 alone, and show higher %ubiquinol (reduced CoQ10 consumption via vitamin E sparing). Refuted if no difference.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Adding moderate vitamin E could take some antioxidant load off CoQ10, letting more be used for energy — but high doses are risky, so dosing matters.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 4.4 — Riboflavin as CoQ10 Biosynthesis Cofactor (rank: 4)
- **Certainty:** 0.22
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Riboflavin (B2) is precursor for FAD/FMN, cofactors for COQ biosynthesis enzymes (COQ6, COQ8, COQ9). Riboflavin deficiency impairs CoQ10 biosynthesis in animal models. If ME/CFS CoQ10 deficiency includes a biosynthesis component (Laredj2014CoQBiosynth), riboflavin could increase endogenous production. This complements rather than competes with the consumption hypothesis — both could be true. Riboflavin is cheap, safe, water-soluble. A simple pre-post trial can test.
- **Evidence link:** Laredj2014CoQBiosynth (biosynthesis pathway), Morris2013CoQ10 (synthesis vs consumption)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Riboflavin 100mg/day for 8 weeks will increase plasma CoQ10 by ≥15% only in patients with low riboflavin status (EGRAC > 1.3), not in those with adequate status. Refuted if no increase in either group.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A simple B vitamin might help the body produce more CoQ10 naturally — especially in patients whose problem is partly sluggish production, not just excess consumption.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Ideas

### Idea 5.1 — Pacing to Reduce ROS Production Rate (rank: 1)
- **Certainty:** 0.20
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Exercise increases mitochondrial ROS 10–100× above resting levels. In ME/CFS with infection-driven CoQ10 depletion, each exercise bout consumes a larger fraction of the already-depleted CoQ10 pool. Post-exertional malaise may represent the period when CoQ10 is being regenerated below a functional threshold. Strict pacing — staying below the anaerobic threshold — minimizes the ROS spike that consumes CoQ10, allowing regeneration to catch up. This provides a specific biochemical rationale for pacing, linking it to CoQ10 kinetics.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (consumed by exercise-induced ROS), Morris2014Mito (ROS→ETC→more ROS), Maes2014OSEs
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a pacing intervention, patients who stay below PEM threshold will show slow increase in plasma CoQ10 over 12 weeks (≥10%), while those with intermittent PEM will show stable or declining CoQ10, controlling for supplement intake.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Staying within one's energy envelope may physically allow CoQ10 reserves to slowly replenish by reducing how much gets burned during activity.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 5.2 — Red/NIR Light Therapy to Upregulate COQ Biosynthesis (rank: 2)
- **Certainty:** 0.10
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Red/NIR light (630–850nm) is absorbed by cytochrome c oxidase, increasing ETC activity. In some cell models, photobiomodulation upregulates COQ biosynthesis genes (PGC-1α → COQ2, COQ7). Two mechanisms: (1) increased endogenous CoQ10 production, (2) improved ETC efficiency reduces electron leak → less ROS → less CoQ10 consumption. Evidence is thin: studied in ME/CFS (small trials, mixed results), CoQ10 levels never measured.
- **Evidence link:** Laredj2014CoQBiosynth (biosynthesis pathway), Morris2014Mito (ETC efficiency → less ROS), Maes2009CoQ10 (deficiency)
- **Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of transcranial + systemic red light (660nm, 40J/cm², 3×/week) will increase plasma CoQ10 by ≥15% and COQ7 mRNA in PBMCs by ≥1.5-fold. Refuted if no change.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Red light therapy might stimulate the body's own CoQ10 production — a drug-free approach to correcting the deficiency.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### Idea 5.3 — Reduced Environmental Toxin Exposure to Conserve CoQ10 (rank: 3)
- **Certainty:** 0.08
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Environmental toxins (pesticides, air pollution) increase CYP450 xenobiotic metabolism, which consumes CoQ10 as an electron carrier. Some directly inhibit ETC complexes (rotenone → complex I), increasing ROS leak and CoQ10 consumption. Reducing total xenobiotic load could modestly decrease CoQ10 consumption rate over years. Practical: HEPA filtration, filtered water, organic produce for high-pesticide items. Effect is weak but additive and safe.
- **Evidence link:** Morris2013CoQ10 (CoQ10 in detoxification pathways), Morris2014Mito (ETC inhibition → ROS), Maes2014OSEs
- **Falsifiable prediction:** A 4-week environmental toxin reduction protocol (HEPA, filtered water, organic diet) will show a modest 5–10% increase in plasma CoQ10 and reduction in urinary 8-OHdG. Refuted if no change.
