# Brainstorm: Hypoxia, High Altitude, HIF1α Signalling, EPO, and ME/CFS

**Date:** 2026-06-07
**Context:** 12 literature entries + 2 pre-existing on hypoxia/altitude/HIF/EPO pathways. Zero direct ME/CFS-altitude studies exist. Convergent evidence from cerebral autoregulation, autoantibody-mediated vasoconstriction, metabolomics (tissue hypoxia), and ischaemia-reperfusion injury. Cross-disease: HIF-1 in lupus, HIF-2 in post-COVID, hypoxic conditioning in PD.

---

## 1. Novel Hypotheses

### H1.1: ME/CFS as a State of Pathological HIF Pathway Inertia
**Mechanism:** ME/CFS involves a failure of HIF-1α stabilisation and transcriptional programme activation in response to hypoxic challenge. Despite chronic tissue hypoxia (Hoel 2021, WirthScheibenbogen 2020), the HIF-1α response system is paradoxically suppressed — possibly by prolonged miRNA-mediated silencing (Kaczmarek 2023) or by blunted EPO production characteristic of hypoxic "maladaptation". This creates a vicious cycle: tissue hypoxia does not trigger appropriate angiogenesis, erythropoiesis, or metabolic compensation, so hypoxic damage accumulates.
**Evidence link:** Kaczmarek 2023 miRNAs converge on impaired angiogenic adaptation to hypoxia; Winkler 2004 shows serum EPO in reference range (not elevated as expected if HIF axis were compensating for hypoxia); Barnden 2011 shows brainstem damage consistent with chronic unrelieved hypoxia.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients exposed to mild normobaric hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.14-0.16, 2h) will show blunted plasma EPO, VEGF, and erythropoietin receptor (EPOR) responses compared to matched controls, measured at 0, 2, 6, 24h post-exposure.

---

### H1.2: Autoantibody-Negative ME/CFS Driven by HIF-2α-Mediated Endothelial Dysfunction (Post-Infectious Subtype)
**Mechanism:** A subset of ME/CFS patients (particularly post-viral onset) may harbour persistent HIF-2α activation analogous to post-COVID (Ribeiro 2026), even without detectable β2AdR/M3R autoantibodies. Viral triggers (SARS-CoV-2 spike, EBV, HHV-6) drive ROS → sustained HIF-2α stabilisation → VEGFR2 upregulation → endothelial barrier dysfunction → impaired oxygen delivery. This creates a molecular mimicry to autoantibody-mediated vasoconstriction but through a different mechanism.
**Evidence link:** Ribeiro 2026 shows spike → HIF-2α in endothelial cells; Kaczmarek 2023 shows herpesvirus-induced miRNAs converge on hypoxia pathways; WirthScheibenbogen 2020 autoantibodies found in ~30% — leaving ~70% unexplained.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** Post-viral ME/CFS patients (EBV or SARS-CoV-2 triggered) without β2AdR autoantibodies will show elevated endothelial HIF-2α (measured by RNAScope or IHC on nasal brushings or skin biopsies) compared to gradual-onset ME/CFS and healthy controls.

---

### H1.3: The "Altitude Paradox" — Moderate Altitude Might Help, High Altitude Harms
**Mechanism:** ME/CFS patients may have a biphasic altitude response curve. Mild-moderate altitude (1500-2500m) might trigger beneficial hypoxic conditioning (HIF-1α activation, angiogenesis, improved O₂ delivery efficiency), while higher altitudes (>3000m) overwhelm their impaired cerebral autoregulation (Badhwar 2025, Medow 2024) causing decompensation. The inflection point depends on individual autoregulatory reserve.
**Evidence link:** Badhwar 2025 shows ME/CFS has impaired cerebrovascular control during hypercapnia; Medow 2024 shows phenylephrine rescues autoregulation; Gangwar 2019 shows IHT attenuates altitude-induced inflammation; JanssenDaalen 2025 shows hypoxic conditioning safe in PD.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** In an altitude chamber study with ME/CFS and controls, ME/CFS patients at 2000m will show improved cognitive-motor integration and reduced RPE during submaximal exercise compared to baseline, but at 3500m will show disproportionate decline in CBFv and symptom exacerbation not seen in controls.

---

### H1.4: Neurovascular Coupling Failure as an Acquired HIF-1α Deficiency in Pericytes and Astrocytes
**Mechanism:** Normal neurovascular coupling (NVC) requires pericyte and astrocyte HIF-1α signalling to match blood flow to neuronal demand (Shan 2022). In ME/CFS, acquired pericyte HIF-1α deficiency (from viral miRNA silencing, autoantibody-mediated vasoconstriction, or mitochondrial dysfunction) impairs the pericyte's ability to dilate capillaries in response to neuronal glutamate release. This transforms NVC from a demand-response system into a fixed-state system where neuronal activity outpaces O₂ delivery.
**Evidence link:** Shan 2022 hypothesises abnormal NVC as ME/CFS origin; Kaczmarek 2023 shows miRNA converge against angiogenesis/hypoxic adaptation; Barnden 2011 implicates astrocyte dysfunction; Badhwar 2025 shows impaired cerebrovascular control during hypercapnia.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, fMRI during a cognitive task will show delayed and blunted BOLD signal rise (impaired NVC) that correlates with reduced pericyte coverage markers (serum PDGFRβ, shed pericyte proteins) and with HIF-1α target gene expression in PBMCs.

---

### H1.5: PEM as a Hibernation-Mimetic Maladaptation — Excessive HIF-Mediated Metabolic Suppression
**Mechanism:** In healthy organisms, HIF-1α activation during hypoxia triggers a protective metabolic suppression (downregulation of oxidative phosphorylation, shift to glycolysis, reduced ATP demand — analogous to hibernation). In ME/CFS, the exertion-triggered tissue hypoxia (Hoel 2021) plus microclot-mediated ischaemia (Kell 2022) may cause an exaggerated and prolonged HIF-1α-driven metabolic suppression that fails to resolve upon reoxygenation. PEM is not merely cellular damage from I-R injury but a maladaptive persistence of the hypoxic metabolic programme.
**Evidence link:** Hoel 2021 shows exertion-triggered tissue hypoxia driving metabolic adaptation; Kell 2022 proposes I-R injury cycles; Little 2023 shows HIF-1 can drive pathogenic T cell responses (linking metabolic suppression to immune dysregulation); Kaczmarek 2023 shows shift to mitochondrial fission.
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** After a standardised exercise challenge, ME/CFS patients (vs controls) will show prolonged elevation of HIF-1α target genes (PDK1, BNIP3, LDHA) in PBMCs at 24-48h post-exercise, alongside persistent suppression of oxidative phosphorylation gene expression and elevated lactate:pyruvate ratio.

## 2. Research Directions

### R2.1: Altitude Chamber Study with Graded Hypoxia in ME/CFS
**Design:** Double-blind, sham-controlled crossover. ME/CFS (n=40, stratified by autoantibody status) and matched controls (n=20) exposed to 3 conditions: normoxia (sea level), mild hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.16, ~2000m), moderate hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.13, ~3500m), each 3h. Outcomes: CBFv (transcranial Doppler), cerebral oximetry (NIRS), cognitive battery, symptom scales, blood draw for HIF targets/EPO/VEGF/lactate at 0, 1, 3, 24h post.
**Primary endpoint:** Change in CBFv from baseline at 3h — comparing ME/CFS vs controls across altitudes.
**Power analysis:** Based on Badhwar 2025 effect sizes (d=0.65 for autoregulation), n=36 per group gives 80% power at α=0.05 for altitude × group interaction.
**Certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show steeper CBFv decline at moderate hypoxia only, with blunted EPO response at both altitudes, and negative correlation between symptom worsening and CBFv change.

---

### R2.2: HIF-1α and HIF-2α Expression in ME/CFS Muscle Biopsies (Pre/Post Exercise)
**Design:** Vastus lateralis biopsy at baseline and 48h post exhaustive exercise. n=20 ME/CFS (10 autoantibody+, 10 autoantibody−), n=10 sedentary controls, n=10 athletic controls. RNA-seq and immunohistochemistry for HIF-1α, HIF-2α, PHD1-3, VHL, target genes (VEGF, EPO, PDK1, BNIP3, GLUT1). Also measure capillary density, mitochondrial content.
**Primary hypothesis:** ME/CFS muscle shows dysregulated HIF pathway activation post-exercise — either exaggerated (excessive metabolic suppression) or blunted (failure to mount compensatory angiogenesis).
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show either (a) persistent HIF-1α nuclear localisation at 48h (failure to degrade after reoxygenation) or (b) blunted PHD2 downregulation (failure to sense hypoxia), with the pattern distinguishing autoantibody+ vs autoantibody− subtypes.

---

### R2.3: Altitude Epidemiology — ME/CFS Prevalence by Elevation
**Design:** Ecological study using large healthcare databases (UK Biobank, All of Us, VA healthcare) to compare ME/CFS prevalence, severity, and healthcare utilisation across elevations: sea level (<500m), moderate (500-1500m), high (1500-2500m), very high (>2500m). Control for latitude, urbanicity, income, race, BMI.
**Primary hypothesis:** If chronic mild hypoxia is protective (via hypoxic conditioning), ME/CFS prevalence/severity decreases at moderate altitude. If chronic mild hypoxia is detrimental (added burden on impaired autoregulation), prevalence increases.
**Secondary analysis:** Compare Long COVID vs ME/CFS altitude gradients — distinct slopes would support distinct phenotypes (Badhwar 2025).
**Certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** A monotonic gradient exists — either positive or negative — with minimum detectable OR of 1.15 per 1000m in elevation (requiring n > 50,000 ME/CFS cases across databases).

---

### R2.4: Randomised Trial of Roxadustat (HIF-PH Inhibitor) vs Placebo in ME/CFS
**Design:** Double-blind, 12-week proof-of-concept trial. n=60 ME/CFS (moderate, PEM-positive, excluding polycythaemia, uncontrolled hypertension). Roxadustat 70mg TIW (pro-HIF stabiliser) vs placebo. Outcome: Change in Fatigue Severity Scale (primary), 6MWT, cognitive battery, cerebral oximetry during cognitive stress, safety.
**Rationale:** Roxadustat stabilises HIF-1α and HIF-2α, increasing EPO and VEGF endogenously. If ME/CFS involves HIF pathway inertia (H1.1), pharmacological HIF stabilisation could restore adaptive responses. In chronic kidney disease anaemia, roxadustat improves haemoglobin without the thrombotic risks of exogenous EPO. However, HIF activation is pro-angiogenic and immunomodulatory — could exacerbate autoimmunity (Little 2023).
**Risks:** Potential worsening if HIF-2α drives endothelial dysfunction (Ribeiro 2026). Monitoring for hypertension, thrombosis, vascular leak.
**Certainty:** 0.35 (high risk but high reward)
**Falsifiable prediction:** Roxadustat will improve fatigue and 6MWT in autoantibody-negative ME/CFS but may worsen outcomes in autoantibody-positive or post-COVID subsets.

---

### R2.5: EPO Stimulation Test as Diagnostic Probe for HIF Pathway Integrity in ME/CFS
**Design:** Same as R2.1 (graded hypoxia) but simplified to a single 2h session at FiO₂ 0.14 with serial EPO measurements at 0, 2, 6, 24, 48h. Compare to matched controls and to patients with secondary polycythaemia (positive control for EPO response). Use curve features: peak EPO, time-to-peak, area under the curve.
**Hypothesis:** ME/CFS patients show blunted, delayed, or absent EPO response to hypoxic challenge — constituting a HIF pathway stress test.
**Clinical utility:** If validated, could serve as objective biomarker distinguishing ME/CFS from controls and from primary fatigue disorders. Also identifies candidates for HIF-PH inhibitor therapy.
**Certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** AUC of EPO response over 48h is at least 40% lower in ME/CFS vs controls (effect size d > 0.8), with sensitivity >75% and specificity >80% for ME/CFS diagnosis.

## 3. Drug/Medication Ideas

### D3.1: Daprodustat (HIF-PH Inhibitor) — Low-Dose Trial in ME/CFS
**Mechanism:** Daprodustat, like roxadustat, inhibits prolyl hydroxylase domain (PHD) enzymes, stabilising HIF-1α and HIF-2α. Unlike erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs), it provides regulated endogenous EPO increase with haemoglobin stabilisation at normal ranges. Low-dose regimen (e.g., 2-4mg daily) could achieve partial HIF stabilisation sufficient to restore hypoxic adaptation without triggering excessive erythropoiesis.
**Advantage over roxadustat:** Short half-life (8h vs 50h for roxadustat) — allows tighter control, easier discontinuation if adverse effects emerge. More data in non-anaemic populations (studied in heart failure).
**Evidence link:** H1.1 (HIF inertia hypothesis); Winkler 2004 (normal EPO despite suspected tissue hypoxia); Ribeiro 2026 (HIF-2 concerns — daprodustat's short half-life mitigates sustained HIF-2 activation risk).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of low-dose daprodustat in ME/CFS increases submaximal exercise endurance (cycle ergometer time to exhaustion at 70% predicted max) and improves cerebral oxygenation during cognitive load.

---

### D3.2: Belzutifan (HIF-2α Inhibitor) — for Post-Viral ME/CFS with Endothelial Dysfunction
**Mechanism:** Belzutifan, an FDA-approved HIF-2α inhibitor (for VHL disease), could rescue endothelial barrier function in post-viral ME/CFS. If the Ribeiro 2026 mechanism (viral trigger → sustained HIF-2α → endothelial dysfunction) extends to ME/CFS, selective HIF-2α blockade would restore normal vascular permeability and oxygen delivery.
**Selection criteria:** Post-viral onset (especially post-COVID), evidence of endothelial dysfunction (elevated VWF, PAI-1, or circulating endothelial cells), detectable spike protein or persistent viral antigen.
**Risk:** HIF-2α inhibition could impair erythropoiesis (anaemia risk) and normal vascular adaptation to exercise. Requires careful titration.
**Evidence link:** Ribeiro 2026 (belzutifan rescues endothelial barrier); Little 2023 (HIF-1α is driver of autoimmunity — HIF-2 inhibition spares HIF-1 to maintain immune adaptation); Kell 2022 (I-R injury in ME/CFS — endothelial stability reduces microclot trapping).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of belzutifan (40mg daily) in post-viral ME/CFS with endothelial dysfunction reduces circulating VWF antigen by ≥30% and improves 6MWT distance by ≥15% vs placebo, with haemoglobin decline <1.5 g/dL.

---

### D3.3: Phenylephrine for Preventing Post-Exertional Malaise
**Mechanism:** Medow 2024 showed phenylephrine (α1-adrenergic agonist) rescued orthostatic cognitive decline by preventing hyperventilation/hypocapnia and improving cerebral autoregulation. Extended logic: Pre-exercise phenylephrine could prevent the cerebral hypoperfusion that triggers PEM by stabilising CBFv during and after exertion.
**Formulation:** Oral phenylephrine (10-20mg, OTC decongestant dose) taken 30min before exertion. Avoid in hypertensive patients. Short action (2-4h) limits side effects.
**Evidence link:** Medow 2024 (phenylephrine rescues autoregulation in ME/CFS+POTS); Badhwar 2025 (impaired cerebrovascular control in ME/CFS); BrainstemHypoperfusion2025 (brainstem hypoxia as unifying mechanism).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** Pre-exercise phenylephrine (vs placebo, double-blind crossover) reduces PEM severity at 24h post-CPET by ≥30% on the PEM visual analogue scale, with preserved or improved Day 2 CPET performance.

---

### D3.4: Propranolol (Low-Dose) for HIF-Mediated Cytotoxicity in Autoantibody-Positive ME/CFS
**Mechanism:** Little 2023 shows HIF-1 drives cytotoxic T cell-mediated tissue damage in lupus. β2-adrenergic receptor blockade (propranolol) could reduce T cell HIF-1 expression via cAMP/PKA pathway, blunting pathogenic cytotoxicity. In autoantibody-positive ME/CFS (WirthScheibenbogen 2020), β2AdR autoantibodies are functionally active — β-blockade could both (a) reduce autoantibody-mediated vasoconstriction and (b) attenuate HIF-1-driven T cell responses.
**Advantage:** Low dose (10-20mg BID) avoids significant bradycardia. Well-tolerated in ME/CFS pilot studies. Could be combined with ivabradine if heart rate control needed.
**Evidence link:** Little 2023 (HIF-1 → cytotoxic T cells); WirthScheibenbogen 2020 (β2AdR autoantibodies); Medow 2024 (β-blockade could affect baroreflex — counterpoint: need monitoring of orthostatic tolerance).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12 weeks of low-dose propranolol in β2AdR autoantibody-positive ME/CFS will reduce serum granzyme B levels (T cell cytotoxicity marker) by ≥25% and improve SF-36 physical function subscore by ≥5 points vs placebo.

---

### D3.5: N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) + Deferiprone (Iron Chelator) for I-R Injury in PEM
**Mechanism:** Kell 2022 proposes ischaemia-reperfusion (I-R) injury from microclot-mediated capillary blockage drives PEM via oxidative burst and iron-catalysed free radical generation. NAC replenishes glutathione, buffering oxidative stress. Deferiprone (oral iron chelator, BBB-penetrant) reduces catalytic iron available for Fenton chemistry during reperfusion.
**Rationale for combination:** NAC alone is too weak for I-R injury (requires iron chelation). Deferiprone alone insufficient without antioxidant reserve. Together they target two limbs of oxidative damage. Deferiprone is FDA/EMA-approved for transfusional iron overload, with established safety profile.
**Evidence link:** Kell 2022 (I-R injury mechanism, proposes iron chelators + antioxidants); Hoel 2021 (metabolic adaptation to tissue hypoxia); Kaczmarek 2023 (impaired antioxidant defences).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of NAC (600mg BID) + deferiprone (25mg/kg BID) taken before scheduled exertion reduces daily step count decline (by actigraphy) on exertion days by 40% compared to placebo, with reduced urinary F2-isoprostanes (oxidative stress marker).

## 4. Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### S4.1: High-Dose Iron Bisglycinate + Vitamin C in Iron-Deficient ME/CFS (HIF-1α Rescue)
**Mechanism:** HIF-1α hydroxylation (targeting it for degradation) requires iron as a PHD cofactor. In iron deficiency, PHD activity is low, allowing HIF-1α stabilisation. Paradoxically, this is beneficial for hypoxia adaptation. BUT if iron deficiency is severe enough to impair haemoglobin synthesis and mitochondrial function, the net effect is negative. Correcting iron deficiency in truly iron-deficient ME/CFS patients could restore mitochondrial function and oxygen delivery, compensating for any reduction in HIF stabilisation.
**Selection:** Only ferritin <30 ng/mL and TSAT <20%. Many ME/CFS patients have low ferritin (chronic inflammation can falsely elevate — confirm with hepcidin).
**Evidence link:** Winkler 2004 (normal EPO in CFS — iron status not reported); Hoel 2021 (tissue hypoxia); Kell 2022 (I-R injury involves iron).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** Iron-deficient ME/CFS patients receiving 12 weeks of iron bisglycinate (60mg elemental) + vitamin C will show improved VO₂peak on CPET and reduced PEM severity compared to iron-sufficient ME/CFS + placebo and iron-deficient ME/CFS + placebo.

---

### S4.2: L-Carnitine + CoQ10 + Ribose — Hibernation-Mimetic Reversal
**Mechanism:** If PEM is maladaptive persistence of HIF-1α-mediated metabolic suppression (H1.5 — "hibernation-mimetic"), then reversing the metabolic programme requires (1) shuttling fatty acids into mitochondria (L-carnitine), (2) supporting electron transport chain (CoQ10), and (3) replenishing ATP pools (ribose). This combination directly counteracts the PDK1-mediated suppression of pyruvate entry into the TCA cycle (HIF-1α target) and the BNIP3-mediated mitophagy.
**Evidence link:** Hoel 2021 (altered fatty acid/amino acid utilisation); Kaczmarek 2023 (mitochondrial fission); Kell 2022 (I-R injury → mitochondrial dysfunction).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12 weeks of L-carnitine (2g BID) + CoQ10 (200mg BID) + D-ribose (5g BID) in moderate ME/CFS increases peak power output on CPET by ≥15W (vs ≤5W placebo) and reduces 48h post-exercise symptom exacerbation by ≥30%.

---

### S4.3: Resveratrol — HIF-1α Modulation via SIRT1/PGC-1α
**Mechanism:** Resveratrol activates SIRT1, which deacetylates HIF-1α, reducing its transcriptional activity. This is paradoxical — if ME/CFS needs MORE HIF activity (H1.1), resveratrol seems counterproductive. However, chronic HIF-1α activation in some tissues (immune cells, Little 2023) may be pathogenic while *inadequate* HIF-1α in others (endothelium, muscle) is limiting. Resveratrol's differential effect: reduces HIF-1 in immune cells (anti-inflammatory) while enhancing PGC-1α-driven mitochondrial biogenesis.
**Evidence link:** Little 2023 (HIF-1 in T cells drives autoimmunity); Ribeiro 2026 (HIF-2 in endothelium — resveratrol also inhibits HIF-2); Kaczmarek 2023 (miRNA converge on impaired angiogenesis — resveratrol promotes angiogenesis via VEGF).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of resveratrol (500mg daily, trans-resveratrol) in ME/CFS reduces circulating cytotoxic T cell markers (granzyme B, perforin) by ≥20% without reducing peak exercise capacity, and improves flow-mediated dilation (brachial artery).

---

### S4.4: Melatonin — Not for Sleep, but for HIF-1α Inhibition in Pathogenic T Cells
**Mechanism:** Melatonin is a potent HIF-1α inhibitor at high doses (20-40mg) — it destabilises HIF-1α by promoting hydroxylation. If HIF-1 drives pathogenic cytotoxic T cells in a subset of ME/CFS (Little 2023 cross-disease), high-dose melatonin could suppress this autoimmune arm. Additionally, melatonin is an antioxidant that reduces I-R injury (Kell 2022).
**Selection:** Autoantibody-positive, evidence of T cell activation (elevated CD8+ granzyme B+, elevated CXCL10).
**Caution:** High-dose melatonin may impair sleep architecture — give in divided doses (10mg AM, 10mg PM) to maintain stable levels rather than single evening dose. Monitor for paradoxical wakefulness.
**Evidence link:** Little 2023 (HIF-1 → cytotoxic T cells in autoimmunity); Kell 2022 (I-R injury → oxidative stress); WirthScheibenbogen 2020 (autoantibody subset).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of high-dose melatonin (20mg/day, divided) in autoantibody-positive ME/CFS reduces circulating CD8+ granzyme B+ cells by ≥30% and improves patient global impression of change.

---

### S4.5: Artemisinin (Artemisia annua) — HIF-1α Inhibition in Post-Viral ME/CFS
**Mechanism:** Artemisinin and its derivatives (artesunate, dihydroartemisinin) bind to HIF-1α and inhibit its transcriptional activity. Initially developed as antimalarials, they have anti-inflammatory, antiviral (anti-EBV, anti-CMV, anti-SARS-CoV-2), and anti-fibrotic properties. In post-viral ME/CFS, artemisinin could simultaneously suppress HIF-1α-driven immune pathology and exert antiviral effects against persistent herpesviruses.
**Evidence link:** Little 2023 (HIF-1 drives autoimmunity); Kaczmarek 2023 (herpesvirus-induced miRNAs converge on hypoxia pathways); Kell 2022 (microclot resolution — artemisinin has antithrombotic effects).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of artesunate (100mg BID, 3 days on/4 off) in post-viral ME/CFS with elevated EBV VCA IgG or PCR reduces anti-EBV EA IgG titres and improves Chalder Fatigue Scale score by ≥4 points vs placebo.

## 5. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### N5.1: Normobaric Intermittent Hypoxic Training (IHT) Protocol for ME/CFS
**Mechanism:** Brief, controlled hypoxic exposures (FiO₂ 0.14-0.16, 5-10 min alternating with normoxia, 30-45 min sessions, 3-5×/week) induce beneficial HIF-1α-dependent adaptations: angiogenesis, mitochondrial biogenesis, improved O₂ extraction efficiency, and EPO-mediated enhanced oxygen carrying capacity (Gangwar 2019, JanssenDaalen 2025).
**Protocol adaptation for ME/CFS:** Start with very mild hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.16, SpO₂ >90%), short cycles (3 min hypoxia + 3 min normoxia × 5 = 30 min), supine position (reduces orthostatic stress), monitor cerebral oximetry. Progress over 8 weeks.
**Evidence link:** Gangwar 2019 (IHT prevents altitude-induced inflammation); JanssenDaalen 2025 (IHT safe/feasible in PD); Badhwar 2025 (impaired autoregulation — supine position mitigates); Medow 2024 (phenylephrine — IHT may reduce need for pharmacological rescue).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of IHT in mild-moderate ME/CFS improves 6MWT distance by ≥30m and reduces post-exertional symptom increase (measured by daily diary over 1 week) by ≥25% vs sham hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.21, identical protocol).

---

### N5.2: High-Altitude Living (2000-2500m) as Therapeutic Intervention — Single-Subject Study Series
**Rationale:** Living at moderate altitude for weeks-months could trigger sustained hypoxic conditioning. Multiple N-of-1 trials per JanssenDaalen 2025 design. ME/CFS patients relocate to mountain facility (2000-2500m) for 4 weeks, with run-in, intervention, and washout phases. Daily symptom tracking, actigraphy, weekly CPET or 6MWT, serial blood for HIF targets.
**Risks:** Acute altitude sickness, worsened orthostatic intolerance, sleep disruption. Entry requires normal BP, no POTS, no pulmonary hypertension. Oxygen supplementation available.
**Evidence link:** Badhwar 2025 (impaired autoregulation at altitude — limited by moderate elevation + screening); Gangwar 2019 (IHT protects against altitude-induced inflammation — pre-IHT could condition); JanssenDaalen 2025 (hypoxic conditioning safe in PD — supports protocol feasibility).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40
**Falsifiable prediction:** At least 3/8 ME/CFS subjects will show ≥30% improvement in symptom scores during weeks 2-4 at altitude compared to baseline, with SpO₂ maintained >92% and no serious adverse events.

---

### N5.3: Buteyko/Reduced-Breathing Protocol for Chronic Hypocapnia in ME/CFS
**Mechanism:** Medow 2024 shows ME/CFS+POTS patients hypocapnic during tilt (ETCO₂ 33.9 vs 42.8 Torr). Chronic hypocapnia reduces CBFv via cerebral vasoconstriction, creating functional cerebral hypoxia even normal arterial O₂ content. Buteyko-style breath retraining to raise ETCO₂ to 40-42 Torr could improve CBFv and cerebral oxygenation without drugs.
**Protocol:** 10-15 min twice daily of reduced-frequency breathing (progressing from 8 to 4 breaths/min), emphasis on prolonged expiration, end-tidal CO₂ biofeedback for feedback.
**Evidence link:** Medow 2024 (hypocapnia → reduced CBFv in ME/CFS); Badhwar 2025 (impaired cerebrovascular control may exacerbate CO₂ sensitivity); BrainstemHypoperfusion2025 (brainstem hypoxia).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55
**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of daily Buteyko-style breathing (with ETCO₂ biofeedback) in ME/CFS increases resting ETCO₂ from baseline ≤34 Torr to ≥38 Torr, increases CBFv (transcranial Doppler MCA) by ≥8%, and improves N-back cognitive performance by ≥15% vs sham breathing exercises.

---

### N5.4: Transcutaneous CO₂ (TcCO₂) Supplementation During Sleep
**Mechanism:** Many ME/CFS patients have sleep-disordered breathing, nocturnal hypoventilation, or upper airway resistance syndrome causing intermittent nocturnal hypoxia and/or hypocapnia. Nocturnal TcCO₂ supplementation (via face mask, 1-2 L/min CO₂-enriched air at FiCO₂ 0.02-0.03) could stabilise cerebral blood flow and prevent nocturnal brain hypoxia.
**Mechanism parallel:** Medow 2024 used CO₂ and acetazolamide — neither helped during tilt. But CO₂ during sleep is different: supine, no orthostatic challenge, and acetazolamide causes metabolic acidosis that compounds hypocapnia. Direct CO₂ supplementation avoids this confound.
**Evidence link:** Medow 2024 (CO₂ alone didn't help orthostatically — but sleep is different); Badhwar 2025 (impaired cerebrovascular control — nocturnal hypoxia may compound); Barnden 2011 (brainstem damage correlates with autoregulation impairment — sleep may be when damage occurs).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30
**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of nocturnal TcCO₂ supplementation in ME/CFS with documented nocturnal hypocapnia (TcCO₂ <40 Torr >20% of night) improves morning cognitive performance (processing speed) by ≥20% and reduces daily fatigue scores by ≥20% vs medical air sham.

## 6. Combinations + Access

### C6.1: Tiered Protocol by Severity
**Mild ME/CFS (working, reduced activity):**
- IHT (N5.1) 3×/week, 30 min sessions, FiO₂ 0.15, supine
- Buteyko breathing (N5.3) 10 min BID
- L-Carnitine+CoQ10+Ribose (S4.2) if metabolic PEM pattern
- Rescue phenylephrine (D3.3) for planned exertion days

**Moderate ME/CFS (reduced hours, home-bound part of day):**
- IHT 2×/week, mild (FiO₂ 0.16), seated semi-reclined
- Buteyko breathing with biofeedback
- NAC+deferiprone (D3.5) 1h before any exertion
- Consider HIF-PH inhibitor (D3.1) trial if EPO stimulation test (R2.5) shows blunted response

**Severe ME/CFS (house/bed-bound, PEM from minimal activity):**
- No IHT until stabilised (risk of PEM triggering)
- Buteyko breathing, supine, 5 min × 3/day
- Nocturnal TcCO₂ (N5.4) if hypocapnia confirmed
- NAC (only, no deferiprone — risk too high for this population)
- Prioritise autoregulation stabilisation over HIF targeting

### C6.2: HIF-PH Inhibitor + Propranolol DARPA (Double-Arm Rescue for Pulmonary-immune Activation)
**Rationale:** Combine D3.4 (low-dose propranolol for T cell HIF-1 suppression) with D3.1 (daprodustat for metabolic HIF-1/HIF-2 stabilisation). This seems contradictory — stabilising and inhibiting HIF simultaneously. The resolution: tissue-specific differential effects. Daprodustat primarily stabilises HIF in kidney (EPO) and muscle (metabolic adaptation). Propranolol suppresses HIF-1 in immune cells via β2AR-cAMP signalling. The combination addresses both limbs: metabolic adaptation to hypoxia (endogenous EPO) and autoimmune T cell suppression.
**Evidence link:** Little 2023 (HIF-1 in T cells); Winkler 2004 (normal EPO in ME/CFS); WirthScheibenbogen 2020 (β2AdR autoantibodies).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25
**Falsifiable prediction:** The combination synergistically improves 6MWT distance beyond either monotherapy in autoantibody-positive ME/CFS, without additive adverse events.

---

### C6.3: Erythropoietin + Iron Protocol (Precision EPO)
**Rationale:** Instead of HIF-PH inhibitors, directly use low-dose recombinant EPO (epoetin alfa, 20-40 IU/kg weekly) with iron monitoring to raise haemoglobin to the upper normal range (14-15 g/dL for women, 15-16 g/dL for men). This would increase oxygen-carrying capacity, compensating for impaired CBFv (Badhwar 2025, Medow 2024). Unlike HIF-PH inhibitors, exogenous EPO does not stabilise HIF-1 in tissues, avoiding potential autoimmune activation (Little 2023).
**Risk:** Hypertension, thrombosis (cerebral sinus thrombosis especially relevant in ME/CFS with impaired CBF). Require haematocrit monitoring, antiplatelet cover.
**Evidence link:** Winkler 2004 (serum EPO in reference range — not deficient, but not compensating); Badhwar 2025 (CBFv impairment — increased O₂ capacity could offset); Ribeiro 2026 (elevated EPO in post-COVID — but ME/CFS isn't post-COVID necessarily).
**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35
**Falsifiable prediction:** Low-dose EPO (titrated to haemoglobin 14-15 g/dL) in anaemic or low-normal haemoglobin ME/CFS patients improves cognitive performance during head-up tilt by ≥20% vs placebo, with no increase in thrombotic events.

## 7. Mathematical Model Extensions

### M7.1: ODE Model — HIF-1α-PHD Negative Feedback Loop with Hypoxia Inertia
**New variables:**
- [HIF] = nuclear HIF-1α concentration
- [PHD] = prolyl hydroxylase activity
- [O₂] = tissue O₂ tension
- [EPO] = serum erythropoietin
- [miR_HIF] = miRNA-mediating HIF repression (miR-155, miR-210 from Kaczmarek 2023)

**Equations:**
d[HIF]/dt = k₁·(K_HIF²/(K_HIF² + [O₂]²)) − k₂·[PHD]·[HIF] − k₃·[miR_HIF]·[HIF]
d[PHD]/dt = k₄·[HIF] − k₅·[PHD]   (HIF induces PHD — negative feedback)
d[EPO]/dt = k₆·[HIF] − k₇·[EPO]
d[miR_HIF]/dt = k₈·(HIF_activation) − k₉·[miR_HIF]

**ME/CFS parameter modification:** Increase k₃ (miR-HIF repression), decrease k₁ (HIF activation sensitivity), or increase k₂ (PHD activity). Result: HIF stabilisation requires lower [O₂] threshold (right-shifted), and HIF signal is weaker once achieved.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Parameter fitting to experimental data (from R2.5 EPO stimulation test) will show significantly different k₁/k₂/k₃ ratios between ME/CFS and controls.

---

### M7.2: DAG Extension — Hypoxia-Centred Causal Model
**Add to existing causal DAG nodes:**

Add nodes:
1. `HIF_pathway_integrity` — latent variable representing PHD/HIF/VHL function
2. `miRNA_hypoxia_silencing` — Kaczmarek 2023 miRNA cluster
3. `autoantibody_vasoconstriction` — β2AdR/M3R autoantibodies
4. `microclot_burden` — fibrin amyloid microclots
5. `tissue_hypoxia` — integrating O₂ delivery and demand
6. `HIF_adaptive_response` — EPO, VEGF, glycolytic enzymes
7. `neurovascular_coupling` — pericyte-mediated blood flow response
8. `I-R_injury_load` — cumulative oxidative damage from ischaemia-reperfusion
9. `cerebral_autoregulation` — CBF stability across BP/CO₂ changes

**Edges:**
- miRNA_hypoxia_silencing → HIF_pathway_integrity (--negative)
- autoantibody_vasoconstriction → tissue_hypoxia
- microclot_burden → tissue_hypoxia
- microclot_burden → I-R_injury_load (via reperfusion cycles)
- HIF_pathway_integrity → HIF_adaptive_response
- HIF_adaptive_response → tissue_hypoxia (--negative: compensation)
- HIF_adaptive_response → neurovascular_coupling (pericyte VEGF)
- I-R_injury_load → PEM_severity
- HIF_pathway_integrity → cerebral_autoregulation (via brainstem HIF)
- cerebral_autoregulation → CBF_response_to_exercise

**Falsifiable prediction:** The indirect path `autoantibody_vasoconstriction → tissue_hypoxia → PEM_severity` accounts for >50% of PEM variance in autoantibody-positive patients; the indirect path `microclot_burden → I-R_injury_load → PEM_severity` accounts for >50% in microclot-positive patients.

### M7.3: SITAR Model — Latent CBF Trajectory with Hypoxia Interruption
Use SITAR (Superimposition by Translation and Rotation) to model cerebral blood flow velocity (CBFv) trajectories during graded hypoxia, treating each subject as a deviation from a latent mean curve.

**New parameter:** Apply SITAR to CBFv decline curve during hypoxia exposure. Estimate three shape parameters:
- **Size (intercept):** Baseline CBFv at normoxia
- **Timing (translation):** Time to reach 50% of maximal CBFv decline (hypoxia sensitivity latency)
- **Intensity (rotation):** Steepness of CBFv decline (hypoxia vulnerability rate)

**Hypothesis:** ME/CFS patients will show advanced timing (earlier decline) and increased intensity (steeper decline) compared to controls, even if size (baseline CBFv) is similar.
**Falsifiable prediction:** SITAR timing parameter is ≥2 SD earlier in ME/CFS vs controls at FiO₂ 0.14, correlating with PEM severity (r > 0.5).

## 8. Cross-Disease Bridges

### B8.1: ME/CFS ↔ High-Altitude Pulmonary Hypertension (HAPH) — Shared Endothelial HIF-2 Phenotype
**Observation:** HAPH is driven by HIF-2α-mediated pulmonary vasoconstriction and vascular remodelling. In ME/CFS, if HIF-2α activation (Ribeiro 2026) is present in systemic vasculature rather than pulmonary, the same pathway produces systemic endothelial dysfunction rather than pulmonary hypertension.
**Bridge prediction:** ME/CFS patients with post-viral onset will show elevated pulmonary artery systolic pressure (by echo) relative to controls — not in the pathological range, but shifted upward — suggesting a systemic predisposition to HIF-2-mediated vascular tone dysregulation.
**Test:** Echocardiographic measurement of pulmonary artery pressure during mild hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.15) — do ME/CFS patients show exaggerated pulmonary pressor response?
**Falsifiable prediction:** Mean pulmonary artery systolic pressure increases by ≥8 mmHg in post-viral ME/CFS vs ≤4 mmHg in controls during 30 min at FiO₂ 0.15 (p < 0.01).

---

### B8.2: ME/CFS ↔ High-Altitude Cerebral Oedema (HACE) — Common Brain Capillary Leak Susceptibility
**Observation:** HACE risk is idiosyncratic — some individuals develop cerebral capillary leak at altitudes well-tolerated by others. If ME/CFS involves baseline endothelial barrier dysfunction (Ribeiro 2026, Kell 2022), patients may have chronically "leaky" brain capillaries. This would manifest not as frank oedema but as low-grade interstitial fluid accumulation, impaired glymphatic clearance, and neuroinflammation.
**Bridge prediction:** ME/CFS patients have elevated serum S100B (astrocyte damage marker) and serum/CSF albumin ratio (blood-brain barrier integrity) compared to controls, consistent with chronic mild BBB compromise.
**Test:** Dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI (DCE-MRI) measuring BBB permeability.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show significantly higher Ktrans (BBB permeability constant) in hippocampus and brainstem on DCE-MRI compared to controls, and this correlates with brainstem volumetric loss (Barnden 2011).

---

### B8.3: ME/CFS ↔ Chronic Mountain Sickness (CMS) — Shared Erythropoietin Dysregulation
**Observation:** CMS (Monge's disease) features excessive EPO-driven erythrocytosis, leading to blood hyperviscosity and cerebral hypoperfusion. ME/CFS may be the opposite pole — inadequate EPO response to hypoxia (H1.1) leading to insufficient erythrocytosis. Both conditions result in cerebral hypoperfusion but through different mechanisms: CMS = blood too thick; ME/CFS = blood too thin given hypoxic drive. The two conditions represent extremes of the HIF-EPO axis.
**Bridge prediction:** The CMS gene (EPAS1/HIF-2α) polymorphisms associated with low EPO sensitivity may be protective against ME/CFS, while high-altitude-adapted variants (Andean CMS-protective EPAS1) may be risk factors for ME/CFS.
**Test:** Compare EPAS1 and EGLN1 (PHD2) SNP frequencies between ME/CFS patients and altitude-matched controls.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients carry a higher frequency of low-altitude-adapted EPAS1 haplotypes (associated with blunted hypoxic ventilatory response) compared to matched population controls (OR > 1.5).

---

### B8.4: ME/CFS ↔ HIF in Cancer — Lessons from Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) Disease
**Observation:** VHL disease involves constitutive HIF-2α activation (due to VHL loss) causing haemangioblastomas, renal tumours, and polycythaemia. VHL patients have elevated EPO and VEGF. The HIF-2α inhibitor belzutifan was developed for VHL. If ME/CFS involves HIF-2α activation without VHL loss (Ribeiro 2026 showing spike → HIF-2α), the belzutifan response may differ.
**Bridge prediction:** ME/CFS patients with post-viral onset will show a distinct biomarker profile from VHL patients: normal VEGF (not elevated like VHL), intermittent rather than constitutive HIF-2 activation, and reversible endothelial dysfunction (vs VHL's fixed vascular tumours).
**Test:** Multiplex serum analysis in post-viral ME/CFS vs VHL patients vs controls for HIF target panel.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Post-viral ME/CFS patients show elevated EPO but NOT elevated VEGF (vs VHL showing both elevated), consistent with selective HIF-2 pathway engagement without full transcriptional programme activation.

---

### B8.5: ME/CFS ↔ Sleep Apnoea — Intermittent Hypoxia Conditioning Paradox
**Observation:** Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) involves nightly intermittent hypoxia (IH) with cycles of desaturation-reoxygenation — a robust I-R stimulus (Kell 2022). Chronic IH in OSA leads to sympathetic activation, hypertension, and oxidative stress — yet some OSA patients function remarkably well despite severe desaturations, suggesting protective conditioning.
**Bridge prediction:** ME/CFS patients with concurrent OSA may have different trajectories depending on timing: pre-existing OSA before ME/CFS onset might "pre-condition" the HIF system (protective), while OSA developing after ME/CFS onset compounds I-R injury (pathogenic). This could explain why some ME/CFS patients have OSA and tolerate it poorly while others are asymptomatic.
**Test:** Retrospective analysis of PSG data in ME/CFS patients: compare ME/CFS onset relative to OSA diagnosis.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with OSA developing after ME/CFS diagnosis have worse outcomes (lower SF-36, higher PEM severity) than those with OSA predating ME/CFS and those without OSA.

## 9. Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### B9.1: Hypoxic Challenge Test (HCT) — EPO Response as Functional Biomarker
Same as R2.5: Standardised 2h mild hypoxia (FiO₂ 0.14) with serial EPO, VEGF, sVEGFR1, erythropoietin receptor (EPOR), and hepcidin over 48h. Diagnostic classifier based on AUC of EPO response.
**Clinical utility:** (1) Objective diagnostic test for ME/CFS (hypoxia sensing defect); (2) Stratification into HIF-responsive vs HIF-inert subtypes (treatment selection); (3) Monitoring response to HIF-PH inhibitor therapy.
**Estimated performance:** Sensitivity 75-85%, specificity 80-90% (based on expected effect size d > 0.8).
**Feasibility:** FiO₂ 0.14 can be delivered via non-rebreather mask with nitrogen blending. Procedure time: 2h hypoxic exposure + 3 blood draws (2, 6, 24h). Total cost per subject: ~$300 (O₂ blending, ELISA kits, staff time).

---

### B9.2: Circulating Pericyte Markers (sPDGFRβ, NG2, CD13) for Neurovascular Coupling Integrity
**Rationale:** Pericytes are central to NVC and are highly sensitive to HIF-1α and VEGF. Pericyte loss or dysfunction occurs in hypoxia-related brain conditions. Serum PDGFRβ (shed pericyte marker) reflects pericyte coverage in brain.
**Hypothesis:** ME/CFS patients have elevated sPDGFRβ (pericyte stress/damage) that correlates with impaired NVC on fMRI (Shan 2022) and with CBFv changes (Badhwar 2025).
**Protocol:** Single blood draw at rest + during cognitive load (simultaneous with NVC fMRI if possible).
**Falsifiable prediction:** sPDGFRβ is elevated ≥1.5-fold in ME/CFS vs controls (p < 0.01) and correlates with BOLD signal lag time during NVC task (r > 0.6).

---

### B9.3: HIF Target Gene Expression Panel in PBMCs (Pre/Post Exertion)
**Panel genes:** HIF1A, EPAS1 (HIF-2α), VEGFA, EPO, PDK1, BNIP3, LDHA, CA9, PHD2 (EGLN1), PHD3 (EGLN3), SLC2A1 (GLUT1).
**Protocol:** PBMC isolation at baseline, 30 min post-CPET, 24h post-CPET, 48h post-CPET. qPCR or NanoString for gene expression. Compare trajectory shapes between ME/CFS and controls.
**Hypothesis:** ME/CFS shows either (a) blunted peak expression (failure to upregulate) or (b) delayed return to baseline (failure to resolve — consistent with H1.5 hibernation-mimetic).
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show significantly higher PDK1 and BNIP3 expression at 48h post-exercise vs controls (persistent HIF metabolic programme), and this correlates with PEM severity at 48h (r > 0.5).

---

### B9.4: Urinary Hypoxia Metabolites as PEM Biomarkers
**Rationale:** Tissue hypoxia shifts metabolism towards anaerobic pathways: increased lactate/pyruvate ratio, increased alanine (from pyruvate transamination), increased succinate (I-R injury marker), increased F2-isoprostanes (oxidative stress), increased hypoxanthine/xanthine (ATP degradation).
**Protocol:** 24h urine collections on exertion day and rest day, comparing ME/CFS to controls. Targeted metabolomics for lactate, pyruvate, succinate, alanine, hypoxanthine, xanthine, F2-isoprostanes. Also measure pO₂-derived metabolites: kynurenic acid (tryptophan oxidation), 2-oxoglutarate.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show elevated urinary lactate:creatinine ratio, succinate:creatinine ratio, and F2-isoprostanes on exertion days compared to rest days, with the exertion-rest difference ≥2-fold higher than controls.

---

### B9.5: Cerebral Autoregulation Index (ARI) Combined with EPO — Dual-Pathway Stratifier
**Rationale:** Combine Badhwar/Medow-style autoregulation measurement (transcranial Doppler during tilt or CO₂ challenge) with fasting EPO level to stratify patients into 4 quadrants:

| EPO ↓ | EPO ↔ | EPO ↑ |
|--------|-------|-------|
| (Low EPO + intact autoreg) — probable primary metabolic defect | (Normal EPO + intact autoreg) — non-hypoxia mechanism | (High EPO + intact autoreg) — physiological compensation |
| (Low EPO + impaired autoreg) — HIF inertia + vascular → worst prognosis | (Normal EPO + impaired autoreg) — vascular predominant | (High EPO + impaired autoreg) — possible post-COVID HIF-2 phenotype |

**Clinical utility:** Quadrant assignment directs treatment: low EPO+impaired → HIF-PH inhibitor; high EPO+impaired → belzutifan candidate; low EPO+intact → metabolic supplementation; normal+impaired → vasoactive agents (phenylephrine).
