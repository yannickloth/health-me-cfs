## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 Hepatic Acute-Phase EV Signaling Suppresses Bone Marrow Lymphopoiesis/Erythropoiesis

**Rationale:** Rydland 2026's most evocative pattern is the directional split — liver-derived proteins up (ITIH3, AMBP, FGB), B-cell and erythrocyte-derived proteins down (immunoglobulin light chains, hemoglobin, F13A1). FGB (fibrinogen beta) is an acute-phase protein synthesized by hepatocytes under IL-6 stimulation. If FGB-enriched hepatocyte EVs are taken up by bone marrow stromal cells, they could shift the hematopoietic niche away from lymphopoiesis/erythropoiesis toward myelopoiesis — matching the myeloid bias documented in ME/CFS (monocytosis, MDSC expansion, relative lymphopenia). ITIH3 and AMBP are liver-derived protease inhibitors; their elevation in EVs may reflect a broader hepatic acute-phase program. The single-direction pattern (all liver up, all B-cell/erythrocyte down) is unlikely by chance across 6 of 11 nominally-significant proteins — it suggests a coordinated shift in EV source and target organs.

**Evidence link:** Rydland 2026 pattern (3 liver up, 3 B-cell/erythrocyte down). IL-6-driven hepatic acute-phase response in ME/CFS (documented by multiple studies). MDSC expansion and relative lymphopenia in ME/CFS. No study has tested whether hepatocyte-derived EVs enter bone marrow or whether FGB-carrying EVs alter hematopoietic differentiation.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.15

**Falsifiable prediction:** Plasma EVs from ME/CFS patients, fractionated by hepatocyte surface markers (ASGR1+, CD26+), will suppress erythroid (BFU-E) and B-lymphoid (CFU-pre-B) colony formation from healthy CD34+ hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells in methylcellulose culture, while myeloid colony formation (CFU-GM) is unaffected or increased. Control EVs from healthy donors will show no suppression.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If true, your liver — which produces proteins that help you respond to infection — may be sending out "distress packages" that tell your bone marrow to stop making red blood cells and immune B-cells, explaining the anemia and reduced antibody production seen in some ME/CFS patients.

### 1.2 Reduced EV Immunoglobulin as B-Cell Exhaustion Marker — EBV Linkage

**Rationale:** Three of 4 downregulated EV proteins are immunoglobulin light chain variable domains (IGKV1-12, IGKV1-8, IGLV3-15, IGHV3-7). This is unlikely to reflect simple B-cell lymphopenia (which is inconsistently found in ME/CFS). A more specific explanation: B-cell exhaustion from chronic antigenic stimulation — likely driven by EBV reactivation (documented in ME/CFS). Exhausted B-cells downregulate antibody production and instead adopt regulatory or anergic phenotypes. The EV immunoglobulin depletion would then be a functional marker of B-cell exhaustion, not of B-cell abundance. This reframes the light chain findings from "what B-cells are present" to "what B-cells are doing."

**Evidence link:** ME/CFS has documented EBV reactivation (viral DNA, early antigen IgG). Exhausted B-cells in chronic infection downregulate immunoglobulin secretion 50-80%. Rydland did not measure B-cell count — only EV cargo — so functional exhaustion cannot be distinguished from numerical depletion.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.20

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with low EV immunoglobulins will show elevated EBV early antigen IgG and elevated exhausted B-cell surface markers (CD21loCD27−, CD11c+T-bet+) in PBMCs. If EV immunoglobulins correlate with EBV serology and not with B-cell count, functional exhaustion is supported over numerical depletion.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Your antibody-producing cells may be "burned out" from constantly fighting a virus that won't go away — showing up not as low cell counts in your blood but as low antibody levels in the packages (EVs) those cells release.

### 1.3 EV Count Elevation as Failed Cell Danger Response (CDR) Resolution

**Rationale:** The Cell Danger Response (CDR) is a coordinated cellular program switching from growth/repair mode to defense/isolation mode. EV release is a core CDR component — cells shed EVs containing damage signals (mtDNA, HSP70, oxidized lipids) to alert neighboring cells. In a normal CDR, EV release peaks during acute threat and subsides when the threat is cleared. In ME/CFS, EV counts remain persistently elevated, suggesting the CDR "off switch" is broken. This reframes EV elevation not as excessive production but as failed resolution — cells are chronically stuck in "danger signaling" mode, releasing EVs that would normally be transient. The ceramide pathway (nSMase2) is directly CDR-associated: TNF-α-induced ceramide production is a CDR hallmark. The existing dual-pathway model describes HOW EVs are produced; this hypothesis addresses WHY they fail to return to baseline.

**Evidence link:** CDR model in ME/CFS (Naviaux, cert 0.60–0.80 across chapters). Rydland 2026 and 5+ prior studies showing persistent EV elevation. No study has measured EV count longitudinally post-trigger or after resolution attempts.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.20

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show elevated CDR markers (ATP, adenosine, sphingosine-1-phosphate) paired with elevated EV counts, and CDR marker levels will correlate with EV count (r>0.4). After isometric exercise challenge, healthy controls show transient EV elevation returning to baseline within 2 hours; ME/CFS patients show EV elevation at >50% of peak at 24 hours (failed resolution). If EV elevation resolves normally post-exercise, CDR resolution failure is not the mechanism.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Your cells may be stuck in "panic mode" — constantly sending out emergency signals (EVs) to neighboring cells even when the original threat is long gone, creating a chronic state of alert that drains energy from normal cell functions.

### 1.4 EV Cargo Ratio (Ceramide:Phosphatidylserine) as a Severity and Pathway Readout

**Rationale:** The dual-pathway model predicts ceramide-driven exosomes (small) vs complement-shed microvesicles (large), but currently lacks a direct chemical readout distinguishing them. Ceramide is enriched in exosomes from the nSMase2 pathway. Phosphatidylserine (PS) exposure is a hallmark of microvesicles shed from complement-attacked cells (C5b-9 MAC flips PS to outer leaflet). The ceramide:PS ratio in bulk EV preparations could serve as a proxy for which pathway dominates — low ceramide:PS = complement-driven microvesicle shedding dominant; high ceramide:PS = ceramide-driven exosome production dominant. This ratio is measurable by lipidomics on plasma EV isolates and could be tested using existing Rydland samples or prior cohorts.

**Evidence link:** nSMase2 produces ceramide-enriched exosomes (Trajkovic 2008, Science). Complement-mediated shedding produces PS-exposed microvesicles (Tran 2021, J Extracell Vesicles). Rydland 2026 samples (pre-pandemic, n=99) could be re-analyzed for lipid composition. The paper already asserts that pathway dominance varies by cohort.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.15

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show a wider range of plasma EV ceramide:PS ratios than healthy controls (F-test for variance, p<0.05). Patients with high ceramide:PS will show increased nSMase2 activity and small EV dominance; patients with low ceramide:PS will show elevated C5b-9 levels and large EV dominance. The ratio should stratify patients into subgroups that differ in complement activation markers and inflammatory cytokine profiles.

**Non-specialist consequence:** A simple test measuring the fat composition of the packages your cells release could tell doctors which of two different disease processes is dominant in you — helping target treatment to the right mechanism rather than using a one-size-fits-all approach.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### 2.1 The EV Proteomics Power Study — Minimum Viable Sample Size to Reach FDR

**Rationale:** The single clearest problem in ME/CFS EV research is that protein findings have NEVER survived FDR correction. Rydland 2026 (n=49/50) is the largest study and found 0 FDR-significant proteins out of 424 detected. The effect sizes of the 11 nominally-significant proteins are unknown (not reported in the paper). A power analysis is urgently needed: what sample size would be required to detect true differences at α=0.05 FDR with 80% power given the observed effect sizes? Without this, we cannot distinguish "no real protein cargo differences" from "real differences but underpowered study." This is a design study, not a new experiment — the data exist in Rydland's discovery cohort effect sizes (which should be obtainable from the authors).

**Evidence link:** Rydland 2026 reports 11 proteins at p<0.05 uncorrected; none at FDR q<0.05. For 424 proteins, Bonferroni threshold is p<1.2×10⁻⁴ (or Benjamini-Hochberg ~p<0.003). If ME/CFS effect sizes are small (Cohen's d<0.5), sample sizes of n>200 per group may be required. ME/CFS funding bodies need concrete numbers to design adequately powered studies.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.90 (that this study needs to be done)

**Falsifiable prediction:** Power analysis from Rydland 2026 effect size distribution: either (a) effect sizes justify n<100 per group — meaning 2-3 existing studies are underpowered but correctable with a modest multicenter effort; or (b) effect sizes require n>200 — meaning resource allocation should prioritize a consortium. If (a), a single-center study is feasible; if (b), three years of multicenter work at substantial cost.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Before spending money on EV protein studies, researchers need to know how many patients they actually need — the answer determines whether a single hospital can do the study or whether a multi-hospital collaboration is required.

### 2.2 The CERAMIC Study — Direct Ceramide and nSMase2 Measurement in ME/CFS EVs

**Rationale:** The ceramide-driven exosome pathway is the most specific mechanistic prediction of the dual-pathway model but has NEVER been tested in ME/CFS. The experiment is straightforward: isolate plasma EVs from ME/CFS patients and healthy controls (n=30/group is adequate for a first test), measure EV ceramide content by LC-MS/MS and plasma nSMase2 activity by fluorometric assay. The falsifiable prediction is unambiguous (elevated EV ceramide in ME/CFS). If confirmed, it strengthens the dual-pathway model from "plausible inference" to "measured mechanism." If not confirmed, the ceramide pathway drops to the bottom of the candidate list, and complement-driven shedding takes priority.

**Evidence link:** Rydland 2026 and 5+ prior studies provide the EV count elevation foundation. Ceramide:S1P rheostat shift documented in ME/CFS (hypothesis cert 0.40). This study fills the direct measurement gap identified in spec:ev-dual-pathway-count.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS plasma EVs will show ≥1.5-fold higher ceramide content (C16:0, C24:0 ceramide species) vs healthy controls. Plasma nSMase2 activity will be elevated ≥1.3-fold. EV ceramide content will correlate with plasma TNF-α (r>0.4). If ceramide is NOT elevated, the nSMase2 pathway is not driving EV elevation.

**Non-specialist consequence:** If the fat molecule ceramide is confirmed as the trigger for the extra EV packages in ME/CFS blood, it points to a specific cellular pathway that doctors could target — rather than treating EVs as a vague "inflammation signal."

### 2.3 The CLEAR-ANCE Study — EV Clearance Rate Measurement in ME/CFS

**Rationale:** The third arm of the dual-pathway model — impaired EV clearance — is the most difficult to test but also the most mechanistically informative. Current evidence for clearance impairment is indirect (extrapolation from sepsis/SLE where EV elevation reflects saturation). A direct test is feasible using labeled EV infusion: isolate EVs from healthy donors, fluorescently label them, reinfuse into ME/CFS patients and controls, measure plasma fluorescence decay over time. However, this is invasive. A lower-cost alternative: measure endogenous EV half-life by serial blood draws after a standardized perturbation (exercise challenge transiently increases EV release — measure the decay slope post-exercise). If ME/CFS patients show slower EV clearance (longer t½), impaired clearance is confirmed.

**Evidence link:** EV clearance via Kupffer cells, splenic macrophages, PS receptors. Impaired clearance documented in sepsis and SLE. ME/CFS has documented reticuloendothelial system dysfunction markers (reticulocyte abnormalities, cellular fog).

**Provisional certainty:** 0.15

**Falsifiable prediction:** After maximal exercise challenge, ME/CFS patient EV counts will remain elevated longer than healthy controls (EV t½ ≥2× control t½). If EV decay slope is identical between groups, impaired clearance is not a significant contributor.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The excess packages in your blood might not be from making too many — they might be from your body's "garbage disposal" (the spleen and liver) failing to clear them fast enough, like a recycling center on strike.

### 2.4 Cross-Disease EV Comparison: ME/CFS vs Long COVID vs Autoimmune Fatigue

**Rationale:** EV count elevation is non-specific — seen in sepsis, autoimmunity, cancer, cardiovascular disease. A critical unresolved question is whether EV elevation in ME/CFS is quantitatively or qualitatively different from other fatiguing illnesses. A head-to-head comparison of EV count, size distribution, and protein cargo (targeted proteomics for the 11 Rydland nominals + ceramide + PS) across ME/CFS, Long COVID, rheumatoid arthritis fatigue, and healthy controls would test disease-specificity. If EV profiles are identical across fatiguing diseases, EV elevation is a generic sickness-behavior signal with no diagnostic value. If ME/CFS shows a distinct pattern (e.g., higher liver-derived EV fraction, lower EV immunoglobulin), it gains diagnostic specificity.

**Evidence link:** Rydland 2026, Seifert 2026 (post-COVID EV). No direct ME/CFS vs autoimmune fatigue comparison exists. González-Cebrián 2022 EV miRNA discrimination (AUC 0.87-0.93) was ME/CFS vs HC, not vs other fatiguing diseases.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS and Long COVID will show similar EV counts (both elevated vs HC) but different protein cargo ratios (ME/CFS: higher liver-derived fraction; Long COVID: higher endothelial-derived fraction reflecting vascular injury). Rheumatoid arthritis fatigue patients will show elevated EV counts that correlate with CRP (r>0.5), unlike ME/CFS where EV count is CRP-independent. If all three show identical EV profiles, EV measurement has no disease-specific value.

**Non-specialist consequence:** Comparing the blood packages from different chronic illnesses could reveal whether ME/CFS has a unique molecular fingerprint — or whether the elevated package count is just a generic sign of feeling unwell, common to many conditions.

## Category 10: Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS

### 10.1 EV Count Elevation as Generic Sickness Behavior Signal — The "Fever of Inflammation" Problem

**Rationale:** EV count elevation is found in sepsis, autoimmune disease, cancer, cardiovascular disease, pregnancy, and post-exercise states. It is among the most non-specific biomarkers in human pathophysiology. If EV elevation in ME/CFS simply reflects that patients are systemically unwell — analogous to a fever that tells you "you're sick" but not what you're sick with — then the entire EV literature in ME/CFS may be chasing a generic stress signal. The Rydland cohort was pre-pandemic but still included patients meeting Canadian criteria who had been ill for years — decades of chronic illness would be expected to elevate stress-sensitive biomarkers. Without a comparator group with equal illness burden but different diagnosis (multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, post-cancer fatigue), EV specificity cannot be evaluated.

**Evidence link:** EV elevation documented in sepsis (p<0.001), SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, cancer, myocardial infarction, pregnancy. The Rydland 2026 and prior ME/CFS studies all compare ME/CFS vs healthy controls — no "sick control" comparator.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** If EV elevation is a generic sickness signal, then patients with multiple sclerosis fatigue, rheumatoid arthritis fatigue, and ME/CFS will all show equally elevated EV counts (ANOVA p>0.05 for group effect). Only discovery of EV cargo or size patterns unique to ME/CFS would rescue disease-specificity.

**Non-specialist consequence:** A blood package count that goes up in almost every illness tells you little more than "this person is ill" — like a check-engine light that comes on for everything from a loose gas cap to a blown engine.

### 10.2 Publication Bias — Null EV Studies Never Published

**Rationale:** Zero null/negative EV studies have been published in ME/CFS. Every single study reports elevated EV counts. In a field where small sample sizes (n=15-99 total) and noisy assays are the norm, a 100% positive rate is suspicious. The most likely explanation is publication bias: studies finding no difference between ME/CFS and controls are either not submitted or not accepted. Small studies with null results (n=8-10 per group) would never reach publication. This does not prove EV elevation is false — the replication across labs is genuine — but the true effect size may be smaller than reported (Winner's Curse: the first positive report overestimates effect size, and subsequent "replications" are biased toward positive results in underpowered studies).

**Evidence link:** No null EV studies in ME/CFS. Similar pattern in early NK cytotoxicity research (28 studies, all positive, before meta-analysis revealed Hedges' g=0.96 with smaller-than-reported effects in larger studies). The field publishes positive findings.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** A pre-registered multi-center EV count study with n>100 per group, analysis-blind, and no flexibility in exclusion criteria will find EV count elevation in ME/CFS but with effect size d<0.4 (smaller than any single published study estimates). If effect size is negligible (d<0.2), published studies are inflated.

**Non-specialist consequence:** It's possible the package-count difference between ME/CFS patients and healthy people is smaller than any single study reports — because scientists, like everyone else, tend to publish exciting results and file away boring ones.

### 10.3 Pre-Analytical Variables Confound All Published EV Studies in ME/CFS

**Rationale:** EV measurement (NTA, tunable resistive pulse sensing, protein quantification) is exquisitely sensitive to pre-analytical variables: blood collection tube type (citrate vs EDTA vs serum), needle gauge (shear stress generates EVs), processing delay (EVs released ex vivo from platelets and leukocytes), centrifugation protocol, freeze-thaw cycles, and storage conditions. None of the ME/CFS EV studies standardized these variables across patients and controls in a way that would eliminate confounding by differences in platelet activation (documented in ME/CFS), leukocyte activation, or coagulation status. If ME/CFS patients have higher baseline platelet activation or processing delay generated more ex vivo EVs, the entire EV elevation could be a pre-analytical artifact.

**Evidence link:** ISEV (International Society for Extracellular Vesicles) guidelines emphasize pre-analytical standardization. ME/CFS has documented platelet abnormalities. Rydland 2026 used citrate tubes and 2 centrifugation steps but did not report processing delay differences between groups.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** If pre-analytical variables are rigorously controlled (identical tourniquet time, same needle gauge, same delay to processing, single freeze-thaw), the mean EV count difference between ME/CFS and HC will shrink by ≥30% relative to studies without such controls. If the difference disappears entirely under rigorous conditions, published EV elevation is largely an artifact.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The "extra packages" in ME/CFS blood might partly come from how the blood sample was handled — if ME/CFS patients' blood clots differently or has more activated platelets, the act of drawing blood could create the difference rather than the disease.

## Category 11: Null Hypothesis Assessment

### 11.1 What If EV Elevation Has No Causal Role in ME/CFS Pathophysiology?

**Rationale:** The strongest case against EV elevation as a mechanism: it is a correlate of cell stress, not a cause of symptoms. If EV elevation is purely epiphenomenal — the metabolic exhaust of dysfunctional cells — then reducing EV counts therapeutically would do nothing for symptoms. The entire EV therapeutic paradigm (exosome engineering, EV clearance enhancement, exosome biogenesis inhibition) would be misdirected. Under this null, EV count remains potentially useful as a biomarker (like CRP or ESR) but has no more pathophysiological significance than a fever. The existing paper's speculations (spec:ev-dual-pathway-count, spec:hsat2-exosome-mdsc, spec:hsat2-ets-spi1-sponge, spec:exosome-biogenesis-impairment, spec:ev-cdr-propagation) would need revision to state explicitly that EV changes are consequences, not drivers.

**Evidence links:** All ME/CFS EV studies are cross-sectional — none demonstrate that EV manipulation changes symptoms. In sepsis (where EV elevation is massive), therapeutic EV reduction has not improved outcomes. The strongest "EV as cause" evidence comes from in vitro cancer studies (Evdokimova 2019), not from ME/CFS.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** If EV elevation is causal, then reducing EV count in ME/CFS (via nSMase2 inhibitor, complement inhibitor, or plasmapheresis) should improve symptoms. If EV reduction does not improve symptoms — even in a limited open-label trial — then EV elevation is a correlate, not a cause.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The packages floating in your blood may be more like smoke from a fire (a sign something is burning) than the fire itself — putting out the smoke would leave the fire untouched, so treating EV counts might not help symptoms.

### 11.2 What If the Nominal Protein Findings Are Statistical Noise?

**Rationale:** Of 424 detected EV proteins, 11 were nominally significant at p<0.05 (uncorrected). At α=0.05, we expect 21 false positives by chance alone (424 × 0.05 = 21.2). The observed 11 hits are thus fewer than what random chance would predict. This does not prove the hits are noise — but it means the data are compatible with the null hypothesis that NO EV protein cargo difference exists between ME/CFS and controls. The directional pattern (liver up, B-cell/erythrocyte down) is suggestive but could emerge from a small number of correlated proteins (if ITIH3 and AMBP are co-regulated, or immunoglobulins co-vary). Under this null, the paper's strongest remaining finding is EV count elevation — and that too may be a generic stress signal (10.1). The existing EV protein cargo narrative in the paper would need to be downgraded from "hypothesis-generating" to "random noise requiring independent replication before further speculation."

**Evidence links:** Rydland 2026 reported 11 hits vs 21.2 expected by chance. Distribution of p-values not reported but would likely show no enrichment of p<0.05 beyond the expected uniform distribution. No protein survived FDR. No replication in any independent cohort.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** In a replication cohort (n≥100 per group, same targeted proteomics panel), zero of the 11 nominal hits will replicate at p<0.05 (same direction). One hit would replicate by chance (11 × 0.05 = 0.55). If zero replicate, the 11 hits are sampling noise.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The specific protein differences from the largest ME/CFS EV study may be nothing more than statistical noise — like finding 11 "significant" coin flips in a row when you flip 424 times, which happens by chance every time you try.

## Category 12: Evidence Quality Concerns

### 12.1 All Studies Underpowered for Their Stated Aims

**Rationale:** The entire ME/CFS EV literature suffers from inadequate sample size. Rydland 2026, at n=99 total, is the largest but still underpowered for FDR correction across 424 proteins (needs n>200 per group for moderate effect sizes). Prior studies: Castro-Marrero n=15, Giloteaux n=17, Almenar-Pérez n=39, Eguchi n=47, Bonilla n=21, Seifert n≤12. The total ME/CFS EV literature comprises fewer than 200 unique patients across all studies. No study has (a) an a priori power analysis for its primary endpoint, (b) a pre-registered analysis plan, or (c) sample size adequate for correction for multiple comparisons. The RepliME/JUSME/DecodeME large cohorts (>1000 patients) have not included EV measurements.

**Evidence link:** Specific sample sizes from each study (synthesis document). Rydland 2026 explicitly notes power limitation.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.85

**Consequence:** If EV proteomics funding continues without adequate sample size, the field will accumulate more nominally-significant unreplicated hits, creating a literature that looks confirmatory but is composed entirely of noise.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The total number of ME/CFS patients ever studied for blood packages is smaller than a single school bus can hold — too few for researchers to be confident in any specific finding.

### 12.2 Single-Lab Dominance in EV miRNA Research

**Rationale:** The strongest EV cargo discrimination finding (González-Cebrián 2022, AUC 0.87-0.93 for EV miRNA) comes exclusively from the Oltra group at the University of Valencia. No independent laboratory has replicated the EV miRNA panel. Single-lab findings in biomarker research have notoriously poor replication rates (Ioannidis 2005: >80% of single-lab biomarker claims fail to replicate). The Oltra group has published multiple ME/CFS EV papers (Almenar-Pérez 2020, González-Cebrián 2022) from overlapping cohorts (n=22 and n=51 — the smaller may be a subset of the larger). The EV miRNA discrimination may reflect lab-specific technical factors (isolation protocol, miRNA array platform, normalization strategy) rather than disease biology. Independent replication by a non-Oltra lab is essential before any clinical claims can be made.

**Evidence link:** González-Cebrián 2022 (n=51/78, PLS-DA AUC 0.87-0.93). Almenar-Pérez 2020 (n=22/17, same group). No independent replication.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.60

**Consequence:** If the EV miRNA panel fails independent replication, the strongest cargo-based biomarker claim in ME/CFS EV research collapses to EV count elevation alone (which is non-specific).

**Non-specialist consequence:** The most promising test for ME/CFS using blood packages (miRNA panel) was developed by a single research group and hasn't been independently verified — like a promising drug tested by the company that invented it, without any outside confirmation.

### 12.3 No Longitudinal EV Data — Cannot Distinguish Trait From State

**Rationale:** Every ME/CFS EV study is cross-sectional — a single blood draw at a single timepoint. Cross-sectional data cannot distinguish between trait markers (stable features of the disease) and state markers (fluctuating with symptoms, exertion, flares). If EV counts fluctuate with symptom severity (state), a single timepoint may capture a patient on a good day or bad day, adding noise that underestimates the true relationship. If EV counts are stable (trait), they could be valuable for diagnosis but would not track with PEM or treatment response. Without longitudinal data (multiple draws over weeks to months, paired with symptom diaries, activity tracking, and PEM logs), the temporal relationship between EV changes and symptom changes is unknown. This is a fundamental gap: it prevents EV from being used as a pharmacodynamic marker in trials.

**Evidence link:** Rydland 2026, all prior studies. No longitudinal EV data in ME/CFS exist. Exercise challenge studies (Giloteaux 2024, pre/post exercise) have one post-exercise timepoint, not full decay curves.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.90

**Consequence:** Until EV data are collected longitudinally in ME/CFS, EV measurement cannot serve as a trial endpoint — and the field lacks basic knowledge of whether EV changes precede, accompany, or follow symptom changes.

**Non-specialist consequence:** We know ME/CFS patients have more packages in their blood than healthy people, but we don't know whether the count goes up when symptoms get worse — like knowing your car's engine light is on but not whether it flickers when you press the gas.

### 12.4 Severity Representation Gap — Severe ME/CFS Nearly Absent

**Rationale:** The EV literature disproportionately represents mild-to-moderate ME/CFS. Bonilla 2022 is the only study to examine severe patients (n=8 severe, all from the same clinic). Rydland 2026 used Canadian criteria but did not stratify by severity — the cohort likely included mostly ambulatory patients (clinic-based recruitment). Almenar-Pérez 2020 used UK ME Biobank (includes severe but composition not reported per EV measurement). Severe ME/CFS patients (bedbound, housebound) may have more extreme EV profiles — or may have different EV profiles entirely (e.g., complement pathway may dominate when immobility drives complement activation). The absence of severe patients means EV findings may not generalize to the most disabled patients — the group with the greatest clinical need.

**Evidence link:** Bonilla 2022 (n=8 severe). All other studies: clinic-recruited, likely mild-moderate. No home-based EV collection study exists.

**Provisional certainty:** 0.70

**Consequence:** If severe ME/CFS patients show different EV profiles (quantitatively or qualitatively), current EV literature describes only the mild-to-moderate majority. EV-based diagnostics or therapies would be designed for the wrong population.

**Non-specialist consequence:** The blood package studies mostly included patients well enough to visit a clinic — the sickest patients, who can't leave bed or home, are almost invisible in this research, so we don't know if the findings apply to them.
