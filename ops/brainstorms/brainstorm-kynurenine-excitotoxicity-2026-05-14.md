# Brainstorming Session: Kynurenine-Sleep-Mitochondria-Excitotoxicity Loop in ME/CFS

**Date:** 2026-05-14
**Context:** Phase 1 integration of kynurenine pathway, sleep/glymphatic, mitochondrial dysfunction, and EAAT2/excitotoxicity findings into multi-lock trap hypothesis
**Goal:** Generate novel, testable ideas for research, treatment, and mechanistic understanding

---

## Summary of Core Mechanism

The integrated hypothesis proposes a self-reinforcing feedback loop:

```
Immune activation (e.g., post-viral)
    → IDO activation
    → Tryptophan shunt to kynurenine pathway
    → Elevated quinolinic acid (QA) / decreased kynurenic acid (KYNA)
    → QA activates NMDA receptors (excitotoxicity)
    → NAD+ depletion from kynurenine shunt
    → Mitochondrial dysfunction → ATP depletion
    → Na+/K+ pump failure → EAAT2 impairment
    → Glutamate accumulation → NMDA overactivation
    → Neuroinflammation → IDO activation (loop closure)

Parallel pathways:
    - Sleep disruption (SWS deficit) → Glymphatic impairment → QA/glutamate accumulation
    - Exercise intolerance → Impaired kynurenine clearance in muscle → kynurenine accumulation
```

**Key evidence:** Cervenka2017, Schwarcz2012, Che2025, Chojnacki2026, Xie2013glymphatic, Zhang2026MitochondriaFatigue, FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle, Jin2026PEMMechanism

## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### Tier 1 (High-Impact, Strong Evidence)

#### 1.1 Glymphatic Clearance Rate as the Critical Variable in Disease Trajectory

**Mechanistic rationale:** The kynurenine-excitotoxicity loop predicts toxic metabolite accumulation (QA, glutamate). Glymphatic clearance during slow-wave sleep is the primary mechanism for removing these metabolites. Individual variation in glymphatic efficiency may determine whether the loop stabilizes at a tolerable level (recovery) or escalates to chronic pathology (ME/CFS).

**Evidence links:** Xie2013glymphatic (glymphatic clearance during sleep), Che2025 (metabolites worsen post-exercise), Chojnacki2026 (baseline QA elevation in ME/CFS)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show lower DTI-ALPS index (glymphatic function MRI measure) than healthy controls, and within ME/CFS cohort, lower ALPS index predicts: (a) higher CSF QA concentration, (b) reduced SWS percentage on polysomnography, (c) slower recovery from symptom exacerbation after standardized cognitive challenge.

**Research implication:** Non-invasive glymphatic assessment (DTI-ALPS) could stratify patients by metabolite clearance capacity and predict treatment response to sleep-enhancing interventions.

---

#### 1.2 Mitochondrial-EAAT2 Coupling Failure as the "Bottleneck" Point

**Mechanistic rationale:** The feedback loop has multiple amplification points, but the ATP-dependent EAAT2 transporter may be the most fragile: it requires both adequate ATP (mitochondrial function) and intact Na+ gradient (Na+/K+ pump). This dual dependence creates a "bottleneck" where small mitochondrial deficits cascade into large excitotoxicity increases.

**Evidence links:** Rothstein1996 (EAAT2 requires Na+ gradient), Che2025 (mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS), Zhang2026MitochondriaFatigue (genetic links to fatigue), Elbaga2026 (EAAT2 downregulation in excitotoxicity)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, EAAT2 function (measured in astrocyte-derived exosomes or CSF) shows stronger correlation with mitochondrial ATP production (measured in PBMCs) than with neuroinflammatory markers, suggesting EAAT2 is downstream of mitochondrial output. Pharmacological Na+/K+ pump stabilization (e.g., digitalis-like compounds at sub-toxic doses) improves glutamate clearance independent of mitochondrial function.

**Research implication:** Testing agents that stabilize EAAT2 function (bypassing ATP requirement) could have outsized effects compared to mitochondrial support alone.

---

#### 1.3 Kynurenine Pathway Biphasic Dysregulation: Acute Activation → Chronic Depletion

**Mechanistic rationale:** Contradictory findings in literature (Che2025 shows activation; Yan2026 shows inhibition in chronic fatigue) may reflect time-dependent biphasic response: acute inflammation triggers IDO activation and kynurenine surge; chronic activation depletes tryptophan pool and impairs downstream enzymes (KAT, KMO), causing metabolite imbalance. ME/CFS patients are in depleted phase but with elevated QA:KYNA ratio due to differential enzyme sensitivity.

**Evidence links:** Che2025 (activation in ME/CFS), Yan2026 (inhibition in chronic fatigue model), Cervenka2017 (inflammation drives kynurenine), Schwarcz2012 (metabolite balance matters)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** In longitudinal study of acute viral infection followed to chronic phase: (a) Kynurenine:tryptophan ratio peaks at 1-3 months post-infection in those who develop ME/CFS, (b) Ratio declines by 6-12 months but QA:KYNA ratio remains elevated, (c) Enzyme expression (IDO, KAT, KMO) shows temporal pattern: IDO up early, KAT down late.

**Research implication:** Treatment timing matters: IDO inhibitors early, KAT activators late.

---

### Tier 2 (Plausible, Moderate Evidence)

#### 1.4 Muscle-Liver-Kynurenine Axis Failure Explains Exercise Intolerance

**Mechanistic rationale:** Skeletal muscle is major site of kynurenine clearance (Cervenka2017). In ME/CFS, mitochondrial dysfunction in muscle (FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle) impairs clearance capacity, causing kynurenine accumulation during exercise. This creates vicious cycle: exercise → kynurenine release → brain accumulation → fatigue → reduced exercise tolerance → muscle deconditioning → worse clearance.

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (muscle clears kynurenine), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (ME/CFS muscle mitochondrial dysfunction), Che2025 (exercise worsens metabolic abnormalities)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show reduced expression of kynurenine-metabolizing enzymes in skeletal muscle biopsy (KAT, KMO) compared to controls. Exercise challenge causes greater plasma kynurenine increase in ME/CFS than controls, proportional to muscle mitochondrial dysfunction markers.

**Treatment implication:** Muscle-targeted interventions (gradual reconditioning with mitochondrial support) may improve kynurenine clearance and reduce neurotoxicity.

---

#### 1.5 Gut Microbiota-Kynurenine-Predicts-Treatment-Response Hypothesis

**Mechanistic rationale:** Chojnacki2026 showed probiotic treatment improved kynurenine metabolism and fatigue in CFS+IBS patients. Gut bacteria produce tryptophan metabolites (indoles) that compete with host kynurenine pathway. Baseline microbiota composition may predict which patients respond to kynurenine-targeted treatments vs. immune-targeted treatments.

**Evidence links:** Chojnacki2026 (probiotic improves QA:KYNA ratio), Che2025 (microbial antigen response), Cervenka2017 (inter-organ kynurenine cross-talk)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** Baseline gut microbiota composition (16S rRNA sequencing) predicts response to probiotic treatment: patients with low Bifidobacterium and high Proteobacteria show greater improvement in QA:KYNA ratio and fatigue scores after CDS22 probiotic treatment.

**Treatment implication:** Microbiome stratification could guide probiotic vs. alternative treatment selection.

---

#### 1.6 NAD+ Sirtuin Threshold Hypothesis

**Mechanistic rationale:** NAD+ depletion from kynurenine shunt is gradual; cells may tolerate moderate depletion until crossing threshold where sirtuin activity collapses, triggering epigenetic lock. This threshold model explains gradual onset and treatment resistance: partial NAD+ restoration insufficient to reactivate sirtuins and reverse epigenetic programming.

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (kynurenine depletes NAD+), Che2025 (impaired energy production), Multi-lock model (epigenetic lock)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS PBMCs, NAD+ levels show bimodal distribution: cluster near normal levels, cluster at critically low levels. Low-NAD+ cluster shows reduced sirtuin activity, differentially methylated genes, and correlates with disease duration. NAD+ precursor (NR/NMN) treatment shows threshold effect: partial restoration improves fatigue markers only in low-NAD+ cluster.

**Research implication:** Sirtuin activity as biomarker for epigenetic lock status; threshold-guided dosing of NAD+ precursors.

---

### Tier 3 (Speculative, Indirect Evidence)

#### 1.7 Vagal Nucleus as Kynurenine Accumulation Site Driving Dysautonomia

**Mechanistic rationale:** Nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) in brainstem integrates vagal afferents and regulates autonomic function. Kynurenine accumulation in NTS could cause excitotoxicity, altering autonomic regulation and explaining dysautonomia symptoms (POTS, orthostatic intolerance) in ME/CFS.

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (QA causes excitotoxicity), Jin2026PEMMechanism (vagal pathways transmit inflammation), ME/CFS dysautonomia clinical observations

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** Post-mortem brainstem tissue (from brain banks) shows elevated QA and reduced KYNA in NTS region of ME/CFS patients compared to controls. In vivo MR spectroscopy shows elevated glutamate in brainstem region of ME/CFS patients correlating with orthostatic symptom severity.

**Research implication:** Brainstem-targeted NMDA antagonists (dextromethorphan) could treat dysautonomia in subset with elevated brainstem glutamate.

---

#### 1.8 Redox-NAD+ Coupling via Kynurenine 3-Monooxygenase

**Mechanistic rationale:** Kynurenine 3-monooxygenase (KMO) is redox-sensitive enzyme converting kynurenine to 3-hydroxykynurenine. Oxidative stress in ME/CFS may impair KMO, causing kynurenine accumulation and shunting toward QA pathway, creating redox-kynurenine vicious cycle.

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (KMO in pathway), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (oxidative distress in ME/CFS), Cervenka2017 (metabolite balance)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients, KMO expression is reduced in peripheral tissues and correlates with oxidative stress markers (MDA, 8-OHdG). Antioxidant treatment (N-acetylcysteine) restores KMO expression and shifts kynurenine metabolism away from QA.

**Treatment implication:** Antioxidant therapy may work via KMO restoration, reducing QA production.

## Category 2: Research Directions

### Tier 1 (High-Impact, Actionable)

#### 2.1 Glymphatic-Metabolite Correlation Study

**Design:** Cross-sectional n=100 ME/CFS + 50 controls with multimodal assessment:
- DTI-ALPS index (glymphatic function)
- Overnight polysomnography (SWS quantification)
- CSF sampling (QA, KYNA, glutamate, kynurenine)
- Symptom severity scales

**Hypothesis:** Lower DTI-ALPS correlates with higher CSF QA and glutamate; reduced SWS mediates this relationship.

**Evidence links:** Xie2013glymphatic (glymphatic clearance), Che2025 (metabolite elevation), Chojnacki2026 (QA:KYNA in ME/CFS)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** If hypothesis correct, glymphatic enhancement (via sleep pharmacotherapy) should reduce CSF metabolites and improve symptoms in low-ALPS subgroup but not high-ALPS subgroup.

**Impact:** First direct test of glymphatic-metabolite axis in ME/CFS; establishes non-invasive biomarker for stratification.

---

#### 2.2 Mitochondrial-EAAT2 Coupling Assay Development

**Design:** Develop simultaneous assay measuring:
- Mitochondrial ATP production (Seahorse assay on PBMCs)
- EAAT2 function (glutamate uptake in astrocyte-derived exosomes)
- Na+/K+ pump activity (Rb+ uptake assay)

**Hypothesis:** EAAT2 function correlates with ATP production and Na+/K+ activity in ME/CFS but not controls, suggesting coupling failure.

**Evidence links:** Rothstein1996 (EAAT2 Na+ dependence), Che2025 (mitochondrial dysfunction), Elbaga2026 (EAAT2 in excitotoxicity)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** In vitro manipulation of ATP (using oligomycin) or Na+ gradient (using ouabain) produces disproportionate EAAT2 impairment in ME/CFS astrocytes vs. controls.

**Impact:** Establishes EAAT2 as bottleneck; identifies patients likely to benefit from EAAT2-enhancing agents.

---

#### 2.3 Probiotic Stratification Trial

**Design:** Randomized, placebo-controlled trial (n=120 CFS+IBS):
- Pre-treatment microbiome profiling (16S rRNA)
- Treatment: CDS22 probiotic 450B CFU/day or placebo, 12 weeks
- Outcomes: QA:KYNA ratio (blood), fatigue score, symptom responder status

**Hypothesis:** Baseline microbiome predicts response; low Bifidobacterium/high Proteobacteria = high responders.

**Evidence links:** Chojnacki2026 (probiotic effect), Che2025 (microbial antigen response), Cervenka2017 (gut-kynurenine axis)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** Microbiome-based classification predicts response with AUC >0.75. Non-responders show no change in QA:KYNA ratio despite probiotic administration.

**Impact:** Demonstrates precision probiotic approach; validates microbiome stratification.

---

### Tier 2 (Plausible, Feasible)

#### 2.4 Temporal Kynurenine Pathway Study in Acute Viral Infection

**Design:** Longitudinal cohort (n=200 acute viral infection) followed for 12 months:
- Timepoints: baseline, 1mo, 3mo, 6mo, 12mo
- Measures: Kynurenine pathway metabolites (tryptophan, kynurenine, QA, KYNA), enzyme expression (IDO, KAT, KMO), symptom scores
- Outcome: Identify who develops ME/CFS vs. recovers

**Hypothesis:** Biphasic pattern in those who develop ME/CFS: early activation (kynurenine:tryptophan peak), late depletion (QA:KYNA elevated despite lower kynurenine).

**Evidence links:** Che2025 (activation), Yan2026 (inhibition in chronic), Cervenka2017 (inflammation drives pathway)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Kynurenine:tryptophan ratio at 3 months predicts ME/CFS development with AUC >0.80. QA:KYNA ratio at 6 months predicts symptom severity in ME/CFS cases.

**Impact:** Identifies early biomarker for ME/CFS development; validates biphasic hypothesis.

---

#### 2.5 Muscle Kynurenine Clearance Capacity Study

**Design:** Biopsy study (n=40 ME/CFS, 20 controls):
- Skeletal muscle biopsy pre- and post-standardized exercise
- Measures: KAT/KMO expression, mitochondrial function (respiratory chain), kynurenine metabolites in muscle and blood

**Hypothesis:** ME/CFS muscle shows reduced kynurenine-metabolizing enzyme expression and impaired clearance; exercise exacerbates kynurenine accumulation proportionally to mitochondrial dysfunction.

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (muscle clears kynurenine), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (muscle mitochondrial dysfunction), Che2025 (exercise worsens metabolites)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** Muscle KAT expression correlates with post-exercise kynurenine increase; ME/CFS muscle shows 30-50% lower KAT expression than controls.

**Impact:** Establishes muscle-liver-kynurenine axis as therapeutic target; guides exercise prescription.

---

#### 2.6 Sirtuin Threshold and Epigenetic Markers Study

**Design:** Cross-sectional n=80 ME/CFS + 40 controls:
- NAD+ levels (blood)
- Sirtuin activity (SIRT1, SIRT3 enzymatic assays)
- Epigenetic markers (DNA methylation at sickness behavior genes)
- Symptom severity and duration

**Hypothesis:** Bimodal NAD+ distribution in ME/CFS; low-NAD+ cluster shows reduced sirtuin activity and distinct epigenetic signature.

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (kynurenine depletes NAD+), Multi-lock model (epigenetic lock), Che2025 (energy depletion)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** NAD+ levels show bimodal distribution in ME/CFS (Hartigan's dip test p<0.05). Low-NAD+ cluster correlates with: (a) reduced sirtuin activity, (b) hypermethylated promoters of energy metabolism genes, (c) longer disease duration.

**Impact:** Identifies sirtuin activation threshold; guides NAD+ precursor dosing strategies.

---

### Tier 3 (Exploratory, High-Risk)

#### 2.7 Brainstem Kynurenine-Glutamate Imaging Study

**Design:** Advanced neuroimaging (n=30 ME/CFS, 20 controls):
- MR spectroscopy of brainstem (glutamate, GABA)
- PET imaging with NMDA receptor ligands
- Autonomic testing (tilt table, heart rate variability)

**Hypothesis:** Elevated brainstem glutamate and NMDA receptor binding in ME/CFS correlates with dysautonomia severity.

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (QA NMDA agonist), Jin2026PEMMechanism (vagal pathways), ME/CFS dysautonomia

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show 20-30% higher brainstem glutamate concentration on MRS. Glutamate elevation correlates with orthostatic heart rate increase (r >0.6).

**Impact:** First in vivo evidence for brainstem excitotoxicity; informs treatment selection.

---

#### 2.8 KMO Redox Sensitivity Assay

**Design:** In vitro study using cell lines:
- Manipulate redox state (oxidative stress via H2O2)
- Measure KMO expression and activity
- Test rescue by antioxidants (NAC, alpha-lipoic acid)

**Hypothesis:** Oxidative stress reduces KMO activity, causing kynurenine accumulation; antioxidant treatment restores KMO.

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (KMO in pathway), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (oxidative distress), Yan2026 (kynurenine pathway modulation)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** Dose-dependent reduction in KMO activity with H2O2 exposure. NAC pretreatment prevents KMO impairment.

**Impact:** Validates redox-kynurenine coupling; supports antioxidant mechanism of action.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### Tier 1 (Strong Mechanistic Rationale, Lower Risk)

#### 3.1 Low-Dose NMDA Antagonist for Symptom Flares

**Mechanistic rationale:** QA is NMDA receptor agonist causing excitotoxicity (Schwarcz2012). During symptom flares, transient NMDA overactivation may contribute to symptom intensity. Low-dose NMDA antagonists could reduce excitotoxicity without blocking physiological NMDA signaling.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Dextromethorphan (10-30 mg, sub-anesthetic doses)
- Memantine (5-10 mg, lower than Alzheimer's doses)
- Amantadine (50-100 mg)

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (QA NMDA agonist), Che2025 (exacerbation triggers), Chojnacki2026 (QA elevation in ME/CFS)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** In crossover trial (n=40 ME/CFS), low-dose dextromethorphan reduces post-exertional symptom exacerbation by 30% compared to placebo, measured by symptom flare severity score over 48h post-exercise challenge. No significant cognitive side effects at study dose.

**Risk profile:** Low at sub-therapeutic doses; well-characterized safety profiles.

**Implementation considerations:** Use as "rescue" medication during flares rather than continuous dosing to avoid tolerance.

---

#### 3.2 KAT Activator Strategy

**Mechanistic rationale:** Kynurenine aminotransferase (KAT) converts kynurenine to neuroprotective KYNA. ME/CFS shows elevated QA:KYNA ratio (Chojnacki2026). KAT activation would shift balance toward KYNA, reducing NMDA agonist load.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Cefotaxime (antibiotic with KAT-enhancing properties)
- Sodium phenylbutyrate (histone deacetylase inhibitor with KAT effects)

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (KYNA neuroprotective), Chojnacki2026 (QA:KYNA elevation), Cervenka2017 (metabolite balance)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** 4-week treatment with sodium phenylbutyrate increases blood KYNA levels by 50% and reduces QA:KYNA ratio by 30% in ME/CFS patients (n=30). Improvement correlates with fatigue score reduction.

**Risk profile:** Moderate; off-label use requires monitoring for liver toxicity (phenylbutyrate) or antibiotic resistance (cefotaxime).

**Implementation considerations:** Prefer phenylbutyrate for non-antibiotic KAT activation; monitor liver enzymes.

---

#### 3.3 Low-Dose Na+/K+ Pump Stabilizer

**Mechanistic rationale:** ATP depletion causes Na+/K+ pump failure, collapsing gradient for EAAT2 function (Rothstein1996). Sub-toxic doses of digitalis-like compounds could enhance Na+/K+ pump efficiency, improving glutamate clearance despite ATP deficit.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Digoxin (extremely low dose, 0.0625-0.125 mg)
- Ouabain derivatives (experimental)

**Evidence links:** Rothstein1996 (EAAT2 Na+ dependence), Zhang2026MitochondriaFatigue (mitochondrial-ATP link), Elbaga2026 (EAAT2 in excitotoxicity)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** Ultra-low dose digoxin (0.0625 mg daily) improves CSF glutamate clearance (measured by CSF:plasma glutamate ratio) by 20% in ME/CFS patients without affecting cardiac output or ECG.

**Risk profile:** Moderate-high; digoxin has narrow therapeutic window. Requires strict cardiac monitoring.

**Implementation considerations:** Only in clinical trial setting; exclude patients with cardiac conditions; frequent ECG monitoring.

---

### Tier 2 (Plausible, Moderate Risk)

#### 3.4 mTOR Modulator for EAAT2 Upregulation

**Mechanistic rationale:** PirB-mTOR-EAAT pathway identified in Alzheimer's models (Zhang2026PirBEAAT2). mTOR activation upregulates EAAT expression. ME/CFS may show impaired mTOR signaling; mTOR modulators could enhance EAAT2 expression.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Rapamycin (low-dose, intermittent)
- Metformin (indirect mTOR effects via AMPK)

**Evidence links:** Zhang2026PirBEAAT2 (mTOR-EAAT pathway), Che2025 (metabolic dysregulation), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (mitochondrial dysfunction)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.35

**Falsifiable prediction:** Low-dose rapamycin (1 mg weekly) increases EAAT2 expression in astrocyte-derived exosomes by 30% and improves fatigue scores in ME/CFS patients after 12 weeks.

**Risk profile:** Moderate; rapamycin is immunosuppressive. Requires infection monitoring.

**Implementation considerations:** Intermittent dosing to reduce immunosuppression; monitor for infections and metabolic effects.

---

#### 3.5 IDO Transient Inhibition for Early Intervention

**Mechanistic rationale:** IDO activation initiates kynurenine shunt (Cervenka2017). Transient IDO inhibition early in disease course could prevent kynurenine accumulation and downstream NAD+ depletion. Timing critical—inhibition may help early but worsen later if kynurenine already depleted.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Epacadostat (IDO1 inhibitor, developed for cancer)
- BMS-986205 (IDO1 inhibitor)

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (IDO initiates pathway), Che2025 (pathway activation), Biphasic hypothesis (early activation, late depletion)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** In early ME/CFS (<12 months duration), 8-week epacadostat treatment reduces kynurenine:tryptophan ratio by 50% and improves fatigue scores. In late ME/CFS (>3 years), same treatment shows no benefit or worsens symptoms due to further kynurenine depletion.

**Risk profile:** Moderate; IDO inhibitors have immune effects. Developed for cancer with established safety profiles.

**Implementation considerations:** Disease duration-stratified trial; monitor immune parameters; avoid in late-stage disease.

---

#### 3.6 NAD+ Precursor Dose-Response Optimization

**Mechanistic rationale:** Kynurenine shunt depletes NAD+ (Cervenka2017). NAD+ precursors (NR, NMN) show variable responses in ME/CFS. Threshold hypothesis suggests need for sufficient dose to reactivate sirtuins.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Nicotinamide riboside (NR) - 500-2000 mg/day
- Nicotinamide mononucleotide (NMN) - 300-1000 mg/day

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (kynurenine depletes NAD+), Che2025 (energy depletion), Threshold hypothesis (sirtuin activation threshold)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Dose-response curve shows threshold effect: NR doses <1000 mg show minimal effect on NAD+ and fatigue; doses >=1500 mg increase NAD+ >50% and improve fatigue scores in low-NAD+ subgroup only.

**Risk profile:** Low; NAD+ precursors have excellent safety profiles.

**Implementation considerations:** Baseline NAD+ measurement to identify low-NAD+ subgroup; start low and titrate to threshold.

---

### Tier 3 (Speculative, High-Risk)

#### 3.7 Brainstem-Targeted NMDA Antagonist

**Mechanistic rationale:** Hypothesis 1.7 suggests kynurenine accumulation in NTS causes dysautonomia. NMDA antagonists with brainstem penetration could specifically target autonomic symptoms.

**Candidate drugs:**
- Ketamine (sub-anesthetic infusion, brainstem penetrating)
- Memantine (standard formulation, cross BBB)

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (QA NMDA agonist), Jin2026PEMMechanism (vagal pathways), Hypothesis 1.7

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.25

**Falsifiable prediction:** Sub-anesthetic ketamine infusion (0.2 mg/kg over 1h) improves orthostatic tolerance (tilt table duration) by 50% in ME/CFS patients with elevated brainstem glutamate on MRS, but not in patients with normal brainstem glutamate.

**Risk profile:** High; ketamine has abuse potential, dissociative effects. Requires controlled setting.

**Implementation considerations:** Only in research setting; stratify by brainstem glutamate on MRS.

---

#### 3.8 Redox-Modulating KMO Enhancer

**Mechanistic rationale:** Hypothesis 1.8 suggests KMO is redox-sensitive. Redox-modulating agents could restore KMO activity, shifting kynurenine away from QA pathway.

**Candidate drugs:**
- N-acetylcysteine (NAC) - 600-2400 mg/day
- Alpha-lipoic acid - 300-600 mg/day

**Evidence links:** Yan2026 (kynurenine pathway modulation), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (oxidative distress), Hypothesis 1.8

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.30

**Falsifiable prediction:** NAC treatment (1800 mg/day, 12 weeks) increases KMO expression by 40% in peripheral blood cells and reduces QA:KYNA ratio by 25% in ME/CFS patients with elevated oxidative stress markers.

**Risk profile:** Low; NAC and alpha-lipoic acid have excellent safety profiles.

**Implementation considerations:** Stratify by oxidative stress markers; measure KMO expression as biomarker.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### Tier 1 (Strong Evidence, Low Risk)

#### 4.1 Targeted Probiotic (CDS22) for QA:KYNA Modulation

**Mechanistic rationale:** Chojnacki2026 showed CDS22 probiotic reduced QA, increased KYNA, improved fatigue by 40.3% in CFS+IBS. Gut microbiota produces tryptophan metabolites competing with kynurenine pathway.

**Formulation:** CDS22 multi-strain probiotic (450B CFU/day)
- Specific strains: Bifidobacterium longum, Lactobacillus plantarum, etc. (from study)

**Evidence links:** Chojnacki2026 (probiotic improves QA:KYNA), Che2025 (microbial response), Cervenka2017 (gut-kynurenine axis)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.60

**Falsifiable prediction:** In replication trial (n=60 CFS+IBS), CDS22 reduces QA:KYNA ratio by 40% and fatigue score by 35% vs. placebo. Responders show increased fecal indole metabolites.

**Risk profile:** Very low; probiotics have excellent safety.

**Implementation considerations:** Most effective in IBS comorbidity; 12-week minimum duration; measure QA:KYNA ratio as biomarker.

---

#### 4.2 Vitamin B6 (P5P) for KAT Activation

**Mechanistic rationale:** Kynurenine aminotransferase (KAT) requires pyridoxal-5-phosphate (P5P, active B6) as cofactor. B6 deficiency could impair KAT activity, reducing KYNA production. ME/CFS patients may have suboptimal B6 status.

**Formulation:** P5P (pyridoxal-5-phosphate) 50-100 mg/day

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (KAT requires B6), Chojnacki2026 (QA:KYNA elevation), Cervenka2017 (metabolite balance)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** P5P supplementation increases blood KYNA levels by 30% and reduces QA:KYNA ratio by 20% in ME/CFS patients with low baseline B6 status. Improvement correlates with fatigue score reduction.

**Risk profile:** Very low; B6 toxicity only at very high doses (>500 mg/day long-term).

**Implementation considerations:** Measure baseline B6 (P5P) levels; target low-normal or deficient patients.

---

#### 4.3 Magnesium for NMDA Receptor Modulation

**Mechanistic rationale:** Magnesium is endogenous NMDA receptor antagonist, blocking receptor at resting membrane potential. Magnesium deficiency could increase NMDA sensitivity to QA. ME/CFS patients often report magnesium deficiency.

**Formulation:** Magnesium glycinate or threonate (200-400 mg elemental Mg/day)
- Threonate for better brain penetration

**Evidence links:** Schwarcz2012 (QA NMDA agonist), Chojnacki2026 (QA elevation), Magnesium-NMDA mechanism (established literature)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** Magnesium threonate supplementation increases CSF magnesium by 20%, reduces NMDA receptor activity (measured by ERK phosphorylation in PBMCs), and improves post-exertional symptom recovery time by 30%.

**Risk profile:** Low; magnesium has excellent safety. Diarrhea at high doses.

**Implementation considerations:** Check baseline magnesium status; prefer glycinate or threonate for absorption.

---

#### 4.4 Zinc for EAAT2 Function Support

**Mechanistic rationale:** EAAT2 transporter function requires zinc as cofactor. Zinc deficiency could impair glutamate clearance. ME/CFS patients may have low zinc status due to chronic inflammation.

**Formulation:** Zinc picolinate or bisglycinate 15-30 mg/day with copper 1-2 mg/day

**Evidence links:** Rothstein1996 (EAAT2 function), Elbaga2026 (EAAT2 in excitotoxicity), Zinc-EAAT2 literature

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** Zinc supplementation increases EAAT2 expression in astrocyte-derived exosomes by 25% and reduces CSF glutamate by 15% in ME/CFS patients with low baseline zinc.

**Risk profile:** Low; zinc toxicity at >40 mg/day long-term. Copper depletion requires monitoring.

**Implementation considerations:** Check baseline zinc/copper ratio; co-supplement copper if zinc >30 mg/day.

---

### Tier 2 (Plausible, Low Risk)

#### 4.5 N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) for Redox-KMO Coupling

**Mechanistic rationale:** Hypothesis 1.8 suggests KMO is redox-sensitive. NAC increases glutathione, improves redox status, may restore KMO activity. Also has glutamate-modulating effects via cystine-glutamate antiporter.

**Formulation:** NAC 600-1800 mg/day in divided doses

**Evidence links:** Yan2026 (kynurenine pathway modulation), FanoIllic2026SkeletalMuscle (oxidative distress), Hypothesis 1.8, NAC-glutamate mechanism

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**ME/CFS implication:** Long COVID serves as "natural experiment" confirming kynurenine mechanism; treatments developed for Long COVID may benefit ME/CFS.

**Research direction:** Joint ME/CFS+Long COVID kynurenine pathway study.

#### 8.2 Alzheimer's EAAT2 Mechanism Extrapolation

**Mechanistic rationale:** EAAT2 impairment is well-established in Alzheimer's (Elbaga2026, Manisha2026). If ME/CFS involves similar excitotoxicity mechanism, should share EAAT2 biomarkers and respond to EAAT2-targeted treatments.

**Cross-disease evidence:**
- Alzheimer's: Elbaga2026, Manisha2026, Zhang2026PirBEAAT2 (EAAT2 downregulation)
- ME/CFS: Hypothetical (no direct EAAT2 studies)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show reduced EAAT2 expression in astrocyte-derived exosomes similar to early Alzheimer's patients. EAAT2 enhancers (crocin, mTOR modulators) improve fatigue scores in ME/CFS at doses effective in Alzheimer's models.

**ME/CFS implication:** EAAT2-targeted treatments from Alzheimer's research can be repurposed for ME/CFS with accelerated development timeline.

**Research direction:** Collaborative EAAT2 study across ME/CFS and Alzheimer's cohorts.

#### 8.3 Depression KYNA Deficit Paradox

**Mechanistic rationale:** Depression shows low KYNA (neuroprotective), not elevated QA like ME/CFS. Both have fatigue and neurocognitive symptoms but different kynurenine profiles. This suggests distinct mechanistic subtypes of fatigue.

**Cross-disease evidence:**
- Depression literature: KYNA deficiency contributes to NMDA overactivity
- ME/CFS: Chojnacki2026 (elevated QA, reduced KYNA, but QA:KYNA ratio key)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** Depression patients show low KYNA with normal QA; ME/CFS patients show elevated QA with low KYNA. Combined cohort shows two distinct clusters on KYNA-QA plane. KYNA supplementation (or KAT activation) helps depression; QA reduction (or IDO inhibition) helps ME/CFS.

**ME/CFS implication:** ME/CFS fatigue mechanistically distinct from depression fatigue; requires different treatment approach.

**Research direction:** Comparative kynurenine study in ME/CFS vs. depression.

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### Tier 1 (Actionable, Grounded)

#### 9.1 QA:KYNA Ratio as ME/CFS Biomarker

**Mechanistic rationale:** Chojnacki2026 showed elevated QA:KYNA ratio in CFS+IBS patients; ratio improved with probiotic treatment. QA:KYNA reflects neurotoxic:neuroprotective balance.

**Measurement:** Blood or CSF measurement of QA and KYNA via LC-MS/MS

**Evidence links:** Chojnacki2026 (QA:KYNA elevation), Schwarcz2012 (QA toxic, KYNA protective), Che2025 (pathway dysregulation)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.65

**Falsifiable prediction:** QA:KYNA ratio distinguishes ME/CFS from healthy controls with AUC >0.80. Ratio correlates with fatigue severity (r >0.5). Ratio predicts response to kynurenine-modulating treatments (probiotics, P5P).

**Implementation:**
- Blood draw, fasting sample preferred
- LC-MS/MS assay (specialized lab)
- Cost: $100-300 per test

**Clinical utility:** Diagnosis, treatment selection, response monitoring

---

#### 9.2 DTI-ALPS Index for Glymphatic Function

**Mechanistic rationale:** Diffusion tensor imaging along the perivascular space (DTI-ALPS) index non-invasively measures glymphatic function. Xie2013glymphatic established glymphatic clearance mechanism; ME/CFS patients likely have impaired glymphatic function.

**Measurement:** MRI scan with diffusion tensor imaging; calculate ALPS index

**Evidence links:** Xie2013glymphatic (glymphatic clearance), DTI-ALPS literature (validation in other diseases)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.55

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show reduced DTI-ALPS index compared to controls. ALPS index correlates with: (a) SWS percentage on polysomnography, (b) CSF QA and glutamate levels, (c) fatigue severity. ALPS index predicts response to sleep enhancement therapies.

**Implementation:**
- MRI scan with DTI sequence (30-45 minutes)
- Image analysis for ALPS calculation
- Cost: $500-1500 per scan

**Clinical utility:** Glymphatic impairment assessment, sleep therapy stratification, prognosis

---

#### 9.3 ATP-NAD+ Coupling Assay

**Mechanistic rationale:** NAD+ depletion from kynurenine shunt impairs mitochondrial ATP production. Simultaneous measurement of NAD+ and ATP identifies "energy-depleted" phenotype.

**Measurement:** PBMC assay for NAD+ (fluorometric) and ATP (luciferase)

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (kynurenine depletes NAD+), Che2025 (mitochondrial dysfunction), Zhang2026MitochondriaFatigue (genetic link)

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.50

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show reduced ATP and NAD+ levels compared to controls. ATP:NAD+ ratio shows bimodal distribution in ME/CFS (threshold hypothesis). Low ATP:NAD+ patients respond better to NAD+ precursors.

**Implementation:**
- Blood draw, PBMC isolation
- ATP and NAD+ assays (commercial kits)
- Cost: $50-150 per test

**Clinical utility:** Mitochondrial phenotype identification, treatment selection, response monitoring

#### 9.5 Kynurenine Pathway Enzyme Activity Panel

**Mechanistic rationale:** Enzyme activities (IDO, KAT, KMO) determine kynurenine metabolite balance. Measuring enzyme activities identifies which nodes are dysfunctional.

**Measurement:** PBMC enzyme assays: IDO activity (tryptophan to kynurenine conversion), KAT activity (kynurenine to KYNA), KMO activity (kynurenine to 3-HK)

**Evidence links:** Cervenka2017 (pathway enzymes), Schwarcz2012 (enzyme balance), Biphasic hypothesis

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.45

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show: (a) elevated IDO activity (early disease) or reduced IDO activity (late disease), (b) reduced KAT activity across disease course, (c) variable KMO activity. Enzyme activity pattern predicts response to pathway-specific interventions.

**Implementation:**
- Blood draw, PBMC isolation
- Enzyme activity assays (substrate conversion with HPLC detection)
- Cost: $150-400 per panel

**Clinical utility:** Patient stratification, personalized treatment, mechanistic insight

#### 9.6 Circadian Phase Assessment via Melatonin Rhythm

**Preliminary certainty:** 0.40

**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients show delayed or irregular melatonin rhythm compared to controls. DLMO correlates with: (a) SWS timing, (b) DTI-ALPS index, (c) morning fatigue severity. Light therapy advances DLMO and improves symptoms.

**Implementation:**
- Saliva collection kit (home use)

- Melatonin assay
- Cost: $100-250 per test

**Clinical utility:** Circadian assessment, light therapy guidance, sleep optimization
