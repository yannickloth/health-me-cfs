## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 — Orexin Tone as a Metabolic Thermostat for R_headroom (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin neurons are among the most metabolically demanding cells in the CNS — they express high levels of mitochondrial enzymes, fire rapidly during wake, and are exquisitely sensitive to peripheral energy status (glucose, leptin, ghrelin). If Architecture C posits that ME/CFS reflects a systemic metabolic reserve deficit, orexin neuron dysfunction may be the *CNS-specific expression* of the same deficit: the orexin system fails not because it is attacked, but because its energy ceiling is chronically below the threshold needed for normal firing. Orexin tone becomes a readout of central R_headroom.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (orexin suppressed by IL-1β/TNF-α), Horiuchi 2015 (carnitine deficiency → orexin dysfunction), Zametkin ADHD 8.1% glucose hypometabolism (same systemic energy deficit theme). No direct study links R_headroom to orexin firing.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with the lowest PBMC mitochondrial respiration (lowest R_headroom by Seahorse assay) will also have the lowest CSF orexin-A levels, and this correlation will be stronger than the correlation between inflammatory cytokines and orexin-A.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Directly ties the paper's Architecture C (metabolic reserve deficit) to the orexin suppression story — they may be the same disease expressed in different compartments (systemic vs hypothalamic).

### 1.2 — The "Orexin-Leaky" NREM State: Subclinical SOREMs as PEM Triggers (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Ito 2023 demonstrated that orexin deficiency causes REM fragmentation — inappropriate REM intrusions into NREM and wake. In NT1, this produces SOREMs (sleep-onset REM periods). In ME/CFS, partial orexin deficiency may produce *subclinical* REM intrusions — brief (<1 minute) alpha-delta intrusions during NREM that do not meet SOREM criteria but fragment sleep architecture. These micro-fragments could prevent the sustained NREM epochs needed for glymphatic clearance, metabolite washout, and autonomic resetting, thereby directly causing unrefreshing sleep and lowering the threshold for PEM the next day.
**Evidence link:** Ito 2023 PNAS (orexin → REM gating), Maksoud 2021 (91-100% unrefreshing sleep in ME/CFS with near-normal PSG — the micro-fragmentation would not appear on conventional scoring), Grossberg 2011.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Overnight EEG with automated SOREM/micro-sleep-onset-REM detection will reveal 2-5x more REM intrusions in ME/CFS than matched controls; count of nocturnal REM intrusions will predict next-day PEM threshold (by actigraphy, r > 0.4).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Explains the most common paradox in ME/CFS sleep research — markedly abnormal subjective sleep quality with near-normal standard PSG — by proposing the relevant pathology is sub-scoring-threshold.

### 1.3 — Dual-Hit Orexin Pathology: Functional Suppression + Incomplete Autoimmune Destruction (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.30)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The literature frames two distinct mechanisms for orexin dysfunction: (1) cytokine-mediated functional suppression (Grossberg 2011, Gaykema 2009, Weymann 2014) and (2) autoimmune T-cell-mediated destruction (Lopez 2023, NT1). These are treated as separate diseases. The novel hypothesis: they coexist on a spectrum. Chronic low-grade neuroinflammation (as in ME/CFS) both *functionally suppresses* orexin neuron firing AND gradually *primes* the immune system against orexin neurons — a slow autoimmune process too subtle to cause cataplexy but capable of reducing orexin neuron number by 10-30% over years. The dual hit explains both (a) potential reversibility (functional component) and (b) incomplete recovery even when inflammation resolves (structural component).
**Evidence link:** Germain 2025 found no anti-orexin *antibodies* in ME/CFS, but the autoimmune mechanism in NT1 is T-cell-mediated (Lopez 2023), not humoral — Germain's null finding does not rule out T-cell attack. Constantinescu 2011 (MS fatigue null) is a counterexample where inflammation exists without orexin suppression — suggesting MS lacks the autoimmune priming component. No study has measured CSF orexin neuron-specific T cells or orexin neuron density in ME/CFS.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients will show (a) elevated CD8+ T-cell reactivity to orexin peptide epitopes (vs healthy controls, by activation assay), (b) a negative correlation between T-cell reactivity and CSF orexin-A levels (r < -0.4), and (c) reduced HCRT neuron density on postmortem hypothalamic tissue vs controls (20-40% reduction, not the 95% of NT1).
**ME/CFS relevance:** If true, this changes treatment strategy: anti-inflammatory+OX2R agonist is insufficient — immunosuppression (IVIG, rituximab) may be needed to halt gradual orexin neuron loss. This is actionable.

### 1.4 — Orexin Neuron Vulnerability as a Sex-Specific Effect (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin neurons express estrogen receptors and their firing is modulated by estrogen. ME/CFS is 3-4x more common in women. Narcolepsy shows a more complex sex ratio (NT1 ~1:1, NT2 possibly female-skewed). The hypothesis: estrogen *increases* orexin neuron metabolic demand (estrogen is pro-excitatory to hypothalamic circuits), making women's orexin neurons more vulnerable to cytokine-mediated suppression. This would explain both (a) the female predominance of ME/CFS and (b) the perimenopausal/menopausal onset pattern (estrogen fluctuation destabilizes orexin homeostasis).
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 and Gaykema 2009 used male animals predominantly (standard practice); no sex-disaggregated orexin suppression data exist. Orexin neuron estrogen receptor expression is well-established basic neuroscience (pre-2000 literature). Horiuchi 2015 (carnitine/CPT1B link) is relevant as fatty acid oxidation is also estrogen-modulated.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In an LPS challenge in ovariectomized female rodents with vs without estradiol replacement, estradiol-treated animals will show significantly greater orexin suppression and lethargy than untreated animals. In human ME/CFS, CSF orexin-A will be lowest in women with perimenopausal onset.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Provides a testable mechanism for the female predominance of ME/CFS and suggests a role for hormonal modulation of orexin function (HRT, SERMs) as potential treatment.

### 1.5 — OX2R Downregulation as a Consequence of Chronic Low-Orexin Tone (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Chronic low-level orexin signaling may produce OX2R downregulation in target tissues (LC, TMN, sympathetic preganglionic neurons) as a homeostatic adaptation to the reduced ligand availability. If true, even if orexin levels were restored, the receptors would be unresponsive — a form of "pharmacological tolerance" to endogenous orexin. This would explain why some ME/CFS patients might not respond to OX2R agonists and would require a gradual dose escalation protocol.
**Evidence link:** GPCR desensitization/internalization following chronic low agonist exposure is a general pharmacological principle (well-established for catecholamines, opioids). No direct OX2R studies in the context of chronic endogenous orexin suppression exist. Rauf 2025 notes danavorexton had variable response in NT1 — receptor downregulation may contribute.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In an animal model of chronic neuroinflammation (e.g., 4-week LPS infusion), OX2R mRNA and protein expression in the LC and TMN will be significantly reduced vs controls, and the behavioral response to a single dose of danavorexton will be blunted vs naive animals.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Directly informs OX2R agonist trial design in ME/CFS — start with a dose-escalation phase, include non-responder analysis of OX2R expression, and do not prematurely conclude drug failure if dose-escalation is not attempted.

### 1.6 — [CHALLENGING HYPOTHESIS] Orexin Suppression is an Epiphenomenon, Not a Mechanism (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The null findings (Constantinescu 2011: no CSF orexin reduction in MS fatigue; Germain 2025: no anti-orexin antibodies in ME/CFS) combined with the animal model limitation (acute LPS vs chronic ME/CFS) raise the possibility that orexin suppression is merely a *consequence* of a deeper metabolic failure — a canary in the coal mine rather than the toxic gas. The true driver may be upstream mitochondrial failure in hypothalamic circuits that happens to suppress orexin neurons along with many other neuronal populations. Orexin-oriented treatments would then fail because they target a downstream readout, not the root cause. This hypothesis directly challenges the paper's existing orexin integration.
**Evidence link:** Constantinescu 2011 (MS fatigue null), Germain 2025 (autoantibody null), and the failure of multiple orexin-targeted interventions beyond narcolepsy to show robust fatigue benefits (no trials in ME/CFS exist, but the principle is that targeting downstream mechanisms in complex fatigue has historically failed). The paper's own Architecture C predicts that mitochondrial deficit is primary.
**Falsifiable prediction:** OX2R agonist (danavorexton or oveporexton) in a well-powered ME/CFS RCT (n=200, 8 weeks) will produce no significant improvement in fatigue or PEM severity vs placebo, despite measurable improvements in sleep consolidation and daytime sleepiness. The improvement in sleep architecture will not translate to improved energy.
**ME/CFS relevance:** This is the hardest challenge to the orexin hypothesis and must be addressed explicitly. If true, orexin research in ME/CFS is a distraction from the real mechanism (mitochondrial/IMM failure).

### 1.7 — [CHALLENGING HYPOTHESIS] NT2 and ME/CFS are the Same Disease at Different Stages (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.10)
**Mechanistic rationale:** NT2 is diagnosed by EDS + MSLT criteria without cataplexy, with intermediate CSF orexin (110-200 pg/mL). ME/CFS is diagnosed by PEM + fatigue + unrefreshing sleep. But ME/CFS patients also meet EDS criteria on MSLT (many show reduced sleep latency), and NT2 patients report fatigue (not just sleepiness). The diagnostic boundary may be an artifact of which specialty the patient sees (sleep medicine vs ME/CFS clinic). Cerebrospinal fluid orexin may be the same across both diagnostic labels. This challenges the classification entirely.
**Evidence link:** Rauf 2025 notes the NT2 "gray zone" with intermediate orexin (110-200 pg/mL). LopezAmador 2025 reviews 27 studies reporting reduced orexin-A in ME/CFS. Ruhrländer 2025 shows orexin reduction in PASC fatigue. No study has simultaneously measured CSF orexin in NT2 and ME/CFS with the same assay.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a cross-sectional study of n=50 NT2, n=50 ME/CFS, n=50 comorbid NT2+ME/CFS, and n=50 healthy controls, the distribution of CSF orexin-A levels will overlap substantially (no statistically significant difference between NT2 and ME/CFS groups), and symptom profiles will show a continuous distribution rather than a bimodal separation.
**ME/CFS relevance:** If true, it collapses two disease categories into one — with major implications for diagnosis, treatment, and research funding (ME/CFS could borrow from the narcolepsy drug development pipeline).

## Category 2: Research Directions

### 2.1 — CSF Orexin-A Levels in ME/CFS: A Definitive Case-Control Lumbar Puncture Study (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** No study has directly measured CSF orexin-A in well-characterized ME/CFS patients. This is the single most important experiment to resolve the fundamental question. The LopezAmador 2025 review relies on indirect evidence and plasma measures (which correlate poorly with CSF). Simultaneous plasma and CSF measurement in the same patients would establish the peripheral-CNS relationship.
**Evidence link:** This is a direct response to the critical gap identified in the literature survey. NT2 threshold: <110 pg/mL (NT1), 110-200 pg/mL (NT2 gray zone), >200 pg/mL (normal). Ruhrländer 2025 found reduced *plasma* orexin-A in PASC, which may not reflect CSF levels.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 30-50% of ME/CFS patients will show CSF orexin-A in the NT2 gray zone (110-200 pg/mL), and orexin-A levels will correlate with unrefreshing sleep severity (Likert scale, r > 0.4) and PEM severity (r > 0.3).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Directly determines whether the orexin hypothesis is viable. If 70%+ of ME/CFS patients have normal CSF orexin, the hypothesis is significantly weakened.

### 2.2 — OX2R PET Ligand Development for Orexin Neuron Integrity (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** CSF orexin measures total ligand output but does not distinguish between (a) intact orexin neurons that are functionally suppressed vs (b) partially destroyed orexin neurons. A PET ligand targeting OX2R (highly expressed on orexin neurons themselves, as autoreceptors) would provide an in vivo measure of orexin neuron density and integrity, resolving the functional-vs-structural debate.
**Evidence link:** NT1 postmortem studies show 95% orexin neuron loss (Rauf 2025). No PET ligand for orexin neurons currently exists. OX2R is a GPCR, and GPCR PET ligands are challenging but feasible (e.g., [11C]raclopride for D2, [11C]PIB for amyloid). The Sakurai 1998 discovery mapped OX1R/OX2R distribution.
**Falsifiable prediction:** If developed, OX2R PET signal will be reduced by >30% in NT2 patients vs controls, and reduced by >60% in NT1. ME/CFS patients with the lowest CSF orexin-A will show intermediate OX2R PET signal reduction (10-30%), indicating partial structural loss.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Would definitively resolve whether ME/CFS involves structural orexin neuron loss (requiring neuroprotective + replacement strategy) vs functional suppression (requiring anti-inflammatory strategy).

### 2.3 — Orexin-A Challenge Test for ME/CFS Subtyping (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** A single IV dose of orexin-A (or an OX2R agonist such as danavorexton) with serial measurement of autonomic parameters (HRV, BP, pupil diameter), subjective fatigue (VAS), and cognitive performance (PVT) could identify which ME/CFS patients have an orexin-responsive fatigue component. Non-responders may have downstream OX2R downregulation (hypothesis 1.5) or non-orexin mechanisms. This is analogous to the dopamine challenge test in Parkinson's or the histamine challenge in allergy.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (orexin-A IV reversed LPS-induced lethargy in rodents — rescue was near-complete), Weymann 2014 (same in chemotherapy fatigue model). Danavorexton (OX2R agonist) shows +11.1 MWT points in NT1 (Rauf 2025). No human challenge data in ME/CFS.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 40-60% of ME/CFS patients will show a >20% improvement in fatigue VAS and >15% improvement in PVT reaction time within 60 minutes of OX2R agonist administration vs placebo, defining an "orexin-responsive" subtype. Non-responders will have the lowest CSF orexin-A and/or the longest disease duration.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Provides a practical, fast subtyping tool. If an orexin-responsive subtype exists, it justifies a targeted OX2R agonist trial in that subgroup.

### 2.4 — HLA-DQB1*06:02 Frequency in ME/CFS (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** HLA-DQB1*06:02 is the strongest genetic association in sleep medicine (>98% of NT1 carry it, vs 25% of general population). If some ME/CFS patients share the NT1 autoimmune mechanism (anti-orexin T-cell attack, hypothesis 1.3), they should carry this allele at elevated frequency. Conversely, a null result would strengthen the case that ME/CFS involves functional orexin suppression rather than autoimmune destruction.
**Evidence link:** Lopez 2023 (HLA-DQB1*06:02 in NT1), Rauf 2025 (same). No study has examined this allele in ME/CFS. The ME/CFS literature has sparse HLA data with inconsistent results across studies.
**Falsifiable prediction:** HLA-DQB1*06:02 frequency will be 30-40% in ME/CFS patients with the lowest CSF orexin-A (<150 pg/mL), vs 25% in ME/CFS with normal orexin, vs 2-3% in population controls. The frequency in high-orexin ME/CFS will not differ from controls.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Low-cost genetic test that could identify an autoimmune-orexin ME/CFS subtype eligible for immunosuppressive therapy (IVIG, rituximab) tested in NT1 prevention trials.

### 2.5 — Orexin Neuron Activity During PEM: A Preclinical Model (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** If orexin suppression drives PEM, then the post-exertional phase should show the greatest orexin suppression. A rodent model of post-exertional fatigue (repeated swim/forced running + immune activation) with fiber photometry or in vivo calcium imaging of orexin neurons would directly test whether exercise inflammation suppresses orexin firing and whether this suppression correlates with recovery time.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 and Gaykema 2009 used acute LPS. No one has combined exercise + immune challenge in a chronic paradigm to model PEM. Weymann 2014 used chemotherapy + exercise but not ME/CFS-like timing.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In the combined exercise + immune challenge model, orexin neuron firing will drop by 40-60% within 30 minutes of exercise in the immune-primed group (vs 10-20% in exercise-only), and recovery of orexin firing to baseline will correlate with recovery of locomotor activity (R² > 0.6). The time course (12-72h delayed onset) will parallel human PEM.
**ME/CFS relevance:** A validated PEM-orexin animal model would enable rapid screening of OX2R agonists, anti-inflammatories, and other interventions without the logistical challenges of human ME/CFS trials.

## Category 3: Drug/Medication Ideas

### 3.1 — Danavorexton (TAK-925/OX2R Agonist) for ME/CFS PEM (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Danavorexton is a selective OX2R agonist in clinical development for NT1. It increased MWT by +11.1 points in NT1 (Rauf 2025). If ME/CFS involves partial orexin deficiency, OX2R agonism should improve fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, and potentially PEM. The drug's mechanism (direct receptor activation bypassing endogenous orexin) means it works even if orexin neurons are functionally suppressed or partially destroyed — making it ideal for the dual-hit hypothesis (1.3).
**Evidence link:** Rauf 2025 (danavorexton NT1 data), Grossberg 2011 (orexin-A rescue in animal lethargy), Weymann 2014 (orexin rescue in chemotherapy fatigue). No ME/CFS human trial. TAK-861/oveporexton is an oral OX2R agonist with earlier clinical data.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Danavorexton IV infusion (or TAK-861 oral) during a 2-day CPET protocol will reduce post-exertional symptom flare (FAS score) by >40% at 24h post-exercise vs placebo in an orexin-low ME/CFS subgroup (CSF orexin-A < 200 pg/mL).
**ME/CFS relevance:** First mechanistically rational drug for PEM that directly targets a known deficit. Could be disease-modifying if PEM drives neuroinflammatory orexin suppression in a feedback loop.

### 3.2 — Almorexant as a Sleep Architecture → Glymphatic Enhancer (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Almorexant is a DORA (dual orexin receptor antagonist) that was in clinical development for insomnia. Unlike other DORAs (suvorexant, lemborexant, daridorexant), almorexant has a very short half-life (~3h), which may produce a narrower window of orexin antagonism — suppressing the orexin signal just long enough to initiate consolidated NREM sleep without impairing the later-night orexin-mediated processes that may be protective (glymphatic function, norepinephrine oscillations). Its short action may actually *strengthen* the orexin on-off signal, serving as a "sleep primer."
**Evidence link:** Ito 2023 (orexin withdrawal during NREM permits normal sleep architecture), glymphatic dependence on NREM (Hauglund 2025 NE oscillations). Almorexant PK data from Phase 2 insomnia trials (withdrawn from development due to potential CNS side effects in 2011 — safety concerns, not efficacy). The short half-life hypothesis is novel and untested.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Almorexant 200 mg vs placebo in an overnight crossover study in ME/CFS will increase SWS duration by >20% in the first sleep cycle, improve DTI-ALPS glymphatic index the next morning, and reduce subjective unrefreshing sleep score — effects that exceed those of longer-acting DORAs (suvorexant).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Repurposing a drug that was abandoned for non-efficacy reasons (safety in elderly). If the short half-life is actually beneficial for sleep architecture rather than harmful, this represents a novel use case.

### 3.3 — Sulforaphane as an Orexin-Protective Agent (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Sulforaphane (from broccoli sprout extract) is an Nrf2 activator that upregulates antioxidant and mitochondrial protective genes. Orexin neurons are metabolically demanding and vulnerable to oxidative stress. Nrf2 activation in the hypothalamus could protect orexin neurons from cytokine-mediated damage (both functional suppression and gradual destruction). Additionally, sulforaphane is anti-inflammatory and crosses the BBB — potentially reducing the upstream inflammatory signal that suppresses orexin.
**Evidence link:** No direct orexin-Nrf2 studies. Nrf2 activation protects dopaminergic neurons in Parkinson's models (well-established). Hypothalamic Nrf2 expression is documented. The paper already covers BH4/neopterin as Nrf2-pathway markers (Williams 2025). Horiuchi 2015 (carnitine/CPT1B) supports the metabolic vulnerability theme.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of oral sulforaphane (50 mg/day glucoraphanin, standardized) in ME/CFS will increase plasma orexin-A by >15% from baseline and improve fatigue severity score by >20% compared to placebo, with the largest effects in the orexin-low subgroup.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Low-risk, well-tolerated, OTC supplement candidate with a plausible mechanism for orexin protection. Could be used as a preventative in post-infectious onset to prevent orexin neuron loss.

### 3.4 — PGE2/EP3 Antagonists to Disinhibit Orexin Neurons (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Grossberg 2011 showed that cytokine-mediated orexin suppression is mediated by prostaglandin signaling and/or neurotensin interneurons (not direct cytokine action on orexin neurons — they lack cytokine receptors). Specifically, PGE2 acting on EP3 receptors at the BBB endothelium may transmit the inflammatory signal to orexin neurons. EP3 antagonists could block this signal at the BBB gateway, disinhibiting orexin neurons without systemic immunosuppression. This is a more targeted approach than anti-TNF or IL-1 blockers.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (orexin suppression mechanism established, prostaglandin pathway implicated). EP3 antagonists exist as research compounds (not yet in clinical use). The paper's PGE2/glymphatic loop (spec-pge-sub-2.typ) already discusses this pathway. No EP3 antagonist has been tested for fatigue.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In the LPS-induced lethargy rodent model (Grossberg 2011 paradigm), pretreatment with an EP3-selective antagonist (e.g., L-798,106) will prevent orexin neuron suppression and attenuate lethargy by >50%, comparable to chemogenetic orexin activation.
**ME/CFS relevance:** If the PGE2/EP3 gateway is the proximal cause of orexin suppression, an EP3 antagonist would rescue orexin function without the cardiovascular/abuse concerns of stimulants or the global immunosuppression of biologics.

### 3.5 — Low-Dose Naltrexone → Orexin Disinhibition via Microglia (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** LDN is already used in ME/CFS (covered in the paper) but the mechanism is incompletely understood — typically attributed to glial modulation or endorphin upregulation. A novel mechanism: LDN's microglial suppression (Toll-like receptor 4 antagonism) may reduce the neuroinflammatory environment that suppresses orexin neurons. Specifically, LDN may reduce hypothalamic microglial activation, lowering local PGE2/TNF-α tone, thereby disinhibiting orexin neurons. This would make LDN an orexin-restoring agent — a mechanism that has not been proposed.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (cytokine-microglia-orexin cascade). LDN's TLR4 antagonism on microglia is established (preclinical). No study has measured orexin in LDN-treated ME/CFS patients. The paper already has extensive LDN coverage but not this specific mechanism.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a randomized trial of LDN (4.5 mg nightly) vs placebo in ME/CFS, responders to LDN (defined as >30% fatigue reduction) will show a significant increase in plasma orexin-A (or CSF orexin-A if available) compared to non-responders and placebo, with the increase correlating with fatigue improvement (r > 0.5).
**ME/CFS relevance:** If confirmed, this reclassifies LDN from a "try-it-and-see" empirical treatment to a mechanistically rational orexin-restoring intervention — strengthening the existing LDN recommendation.

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical Ideas

### 4.1 — L-Carnitine + CoQ10 → Orexin Neuron Bioenergetic Support (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin neurons are among the most metabolically active neurons, with high mitochondrial density and a particular dependence on fatty acid oxidation (not just glucose). Horiuchi 2015 showed carnitine deficiency → orexin neuron dysfunction → fatigue in mice, and a CPT1B polymorphism associated with narcolepsy. CoQ10 supports complex I-III electron transport. The combination may provide the specific metabolic support orexin neurons need to maintain firing in a low-R_headroom environment. Carnitine shuttles fatty acids into mitochondria for beta-oxidation — orexin neurons' preferred fuel source.
**Evidence link:** Horiuchi 2015 (carnitine-orexin-fatigue link, cert 0.3). CoQ10 has modest evidence in ME/CFS (multiple small trials, generally positive but underpowered). No study has tested the combination or measured orexin. The paper covers carnitine/CoQ10 in the mitochondrial supplement section but not the orexin-specific rationale.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 12 weeks of L-carnitine (2 g/day) + CoQ10 (200 mg/day) in ME/CFS will increase plasma orexin-A by >20% and improve the 6-minute walk test distance by >30 meters vs placebo, with a correlation between orexin increase and functional improvement (r > 0.4).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Low-risk, low-cost intervention. If orexin-specific metabolic support works, it would be one of the few treatments with a validated CNS mechanism.

### 4.2 — Glycine as an Orexin-Sparing Sleep Enhancer (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Glycine is a neurotransmitter that promotes sleep by activating NMDA receptors in the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) and inhibiting orexin neurons via glycinergic interneuron input to the lateral hypothalamus. Unlike GABA-A agonists (zolpidem, zopiclone) that suppress NE oscillations and impair glymphatic clearance, glycine's mechanism may be orexin-sparing — it downregulates orexin firing only during the sleep initiation window, then permits normal oscillatory recovery. The paper already lists glycine as a glymphatic-friendly sleep aid but without the orexin-mechanistic rationale.
**Evidence link:** Glycine's sleep-promoting effects are documented (3 g before bed improves sleep quality in human studies). Basic neuroscience: glycinergic input to orexin neurons modulates their firing. The paper covers glycine as a glymphatic-friendly sleep agent (ch28 integrative approaches). No study on glycine-orexin interaction in ME/CFS.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 3 g glycine before bed in ME/CFS will increase SWS duration by >15% vs placebo without reducing morning plasma orexin-A levels (contrasting with DORAs, which should reduce morning orexin).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Low-risk, OTC, with a plausible dual benefit: sleep enhancement without orexin suppression, contrasting with both Z-drugs (orexin-negative) and DORAs (orexin-negative by design).

### 4.3 — Apigenin (Chamomile) as an Orexin-Protective Flavonoid (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.10)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Apigenin is a flavonoid that acts as a GABAA receptor positive allosteric modulator (like benzodiazepines but milder) AND as a potent anti-inflammatory agent (NF-κB inhibitor). Its dual action — mild sedation plus hypothalamic anti-inflammatory activity — could protect orexin neurons from cytokine-induced suppression while promoting sleep initiation. The NF-κB inhibition is particularly relevant because the cytokine-orexin cascade (Grossberg 2011) requires pro-inflammatory gene transcription.
**Evidence link:** Apigenin's GABAA modulation is established (anxiolytic, mild sedative). NF-κB inhibition at CNS-relevant concentrations has been demonstrated in animal models. No orexin-specific studies. The paper does not currently discuss apigenin.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 7 days of apigenin supplementation (50 mg/day, standardized) in the LPS-induced lethargy mouse model will attenuate orexin neuron suppression by >30% (indexed by Fos activation) vs LPS-only controls, and will reduce lethargy by >25%.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Extremely safe, food-derived, could be integrated into the sleep protocol alongside glycine and low-dose trazodone as a non-pharmacological sleep cocktail.

### 4.4 — Pyrroloquinoline Quinone (PQQ) as an Orexin Mitochondrial Enhancer (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.10)
**Mechanistic rationale:** PQQ is a redox cofactor that stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis and protects against oxidative damage. Orexin neurons' high mitochondrial demand makes them dependent on robust mitochondrial turnover — PQQ stimulates PGC-1α, the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. This may increase the number of functional mitochondria in orexin neurons, raising their energy ceiling and resistance to cytokine-induced suppression.
**Evidence link:** PQQ's mitochondrial biogenesis effect is documented in hepatocytes and neuronal cell lines. No orexin-specific studies. The paper covers mitochondrial supplements but not PQQ specifically. Horiuchi 2015 (CPT1B/carnitine) supports the metabolic theme.
**Falsifiable prediction:** PQQ (20 mg/day for 8 weeks) will increase PBMC mitochondrial density (by MitoTracker staining) in ME/CFS patients, and those with the largest increase will also show the largest improvement in fatigue (r > 0.4). CSF orexin-A will not change, but orexin *responsiveness* (by challenge test, idea 2.3) will improve.
**ME/CFS relevance:** If mitochondrial biogenesis in orexin neurons restores their functional capacity, this would be a disease-modifying approach (slow, cumulative improvement) rather than symptom palliation.

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological Interventions

### 5.1 — Morning Bright Light Therapy for Orexin Entrainment (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin neuron firing is entrained by the SCN via circadian input — orexin release peaks during the wake phase and reaches nadir during sleep. Morning bright light (10,000 lux, 30 min) is the strongest Zeitgeber for the SCN. In ME/CFS, reduced orexin tone may create a "flattened" circadian orexin rhythm — insufficient morning peak and insufficient nighttime trough. Morning light exposure may strengthen the SCN-orexin entrainment signal, normalizing the orexin rhythm even if total orexin production is modestly reduced.
**Evidence link:** Bright light therapy has modest evidence in ME/CFS (some trials, generally small). Circadian entrainment via melanopsin-retinal-SCN pathway is well-established. Orexin's circadian regulation is documented (Sakurai 1998 foundational work). No study has measured orexin levels in ME/CFS before and after light therapy. The paper covers light therapy but not the orexin-specific mechanism.
**Falsifiable prediction:** 4 weeks of morning bright light therapy (10,000 lux, 30 min within 30 min of waking) in ME/CFS will increase the amplitude of the circadian orexin-A rhythm by >30% (assessed by 6-point plasma orexin-A over 24h) and reduce fatigue severity by >20% vs dim-red-light placebo.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Low-cost, minimal-risk, widely available. If the mechanism is orexin entrainment, timing is critical (not just any light, but morning light specifically) — a refinement over current general light therapy recommendations.

### 5.2 — Timed Cold Exposure (Mammalian Dive Reflex) for Orexin Activation (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The mammalian dive reflex — triggered by cold water applied to the face (cold face immersion) — activates the trigeminal-parasympathetic reflex arc, which has monosynaptic connections to the lateral hypothalamus and orexin neurons. Orexin neurons are activated by sympathetic activation and by cold stress. Brief cold exposure (not cold water swimming, which is too severe for ME/CFS) could transiently activate orexin neurons, providing a short window of improved alertness, autonomic regulation, and pain modulation.
**Evidence link:** Orexin neurons are activated by cold stress in animal models (documented). The dive reflex is well-established physiology. The paper's cold face immersion research stream (separate brainstorm) has already explored this. The novel connection is the orexin-activation mechanism. Dauvilliers 2011 (pain in NT1) suggests orexin loss impairs nociception — cold exposure could transiently restore this.
**Falsifiable prediction:** A 30-second cold face immersion (10°C water) in ME/CFS will increase plasma orexin-A by >15% at 30 minutes post-immersion, improve PVT reaction time by >10%, and reduce pain VAS by >20% — all effects correlating with baseline orexin-A levels (lowest baseline → largest increase).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Zero-cost, no-equipment intervention with a plausible orexin-activation mechanism. Opposite approach to the DORA/sleep strategies — uses orexin system activation during wake rather than suppression during sleep.

### 5.3 — Slow Breathing + Auditory Beat Entrainment for Orexin-Vagal Cross-Talk (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Gaykema 2009 showed that vagal afferent signaling mediates the inflammation → orexin suppression pathway. The vagus transmits both inflammatory signals (from the periphery) and anti-inflammatory signals (via the cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex). Slow breathing (6 breaths/min) at resonance frequency enhances vagal tone. Hypotheses: enhanced vagal tone both (a) reduces peripheral inflammatory signaling to the hypothalamus (reducing orexin suppression) and (b) directly modulates orexin neuron firing through vagal-NTS-hypothalamic connectivity. Adding binaural beats (theta, 4-7 Hz) may entrain hypothalamic oscillatory activity, amplifying the vagal-orexin coupling.
**Evidence link:** Gaykema 2009 (vagal-orexin pathway). The paper's slow breathing research stream (separate brainstorm) covers vagal enhancement. No study has combined slow breathing with orexin measurement. Binaural beats' effect on hypothalamic activity is documented but debated (small effect sizes).
**Falsifiable prediction:** 8 weeks of daily 20-min slow breathing (6 breaths/min) + theta binaural beats will increase serum orexin-A by >10% and reduce fatigue severity by >30% in ME/CFS, with the increase in heart rate variability (RMSSD) predicting the orexin increase (R² > 0.5).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Completely safe, free, home-based. If vagal → orexin enhancement is a real mechanism, this could be a first-line intervention for all severity levels.

### 5.4 — Eliminating Orexin-Suppressing Medications (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Multiple medication classes suppress orexin neurons either directly (DORAs, clonidine via alpha-2 agonism) or indirectly through enhancing GABAergic tone (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs, gabapentinoids) or suppressing the LC-NE system (beta-blockers, clonidine). In ME/CFS where orexin tone is already low, these medications may be unwittingly exacerbating orexin deficiency. This is especially relevant for beta-blockers used for POTS (covered in the paper) and Z-drugs for sleep (covered in the paper's glymphatic warnings).
**Evidence link:** DORAs (suvorexant, lemborexant, daridorexant) suppress orexin by design. Beta-blockers reduce central NE tone which is downstream of orexin. Z-drugs suppress REM and SWS. The paper already warns about Z-drugs and glymphatic clearance (warn-glymphatic-impairing-medications.typ) and discusses the DORA risk-benefit trade-off. The novel idea: a systematic audit of all medications for orexin-suppressing effects.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a prospective cohort study, ME/CFS patients who discontinue orexin-suppressing medications (Z-drugs → L-tryptophan/glycine switch, beta-blockers → ivabradine switch where appropriate) will show a mean >15% increase in plasma orexin-A and >25% improvement in fatigue severity at 8 weeks.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Immediate clinical actionability. No drug development needed. Could be implemented as a clinical practice guideline change.

## Category 6: Combinations + Access

### 6.1 — Multi-Level Orexin Restoration Protocol: Morning Light + Evening DORA + Night Glycine (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The orexin system is bidirectional — it can be entrained (strengthened during wake) and suppressed (allowed to withdraw during sleep). A multi-level protocol could operate on both sides: (1) morning bright light → strengthen circadian orexin peak (idea 5.1), (2) timed low-dose DORA (daridorexant 12.5 mg) specifically at bedtime → facilitate natural orexin withdrawal to initiate consolidated NREM without complete suppression, (3) glycine 3 g at bedtime → support sleep depth without orexin-suppression. The combination may produce a "cleaner" orexin on-off signal than any single intervention.
**Evidence link:** Bright light (5.1), DORAs (Rauf 2025, paper's DORA coverage), glycine (paper as glymphatic-friendly sleep aid). The combination rationale is novel — no study has layered orexin-enhancing wake interventions with orexin-sparing sleep interventions.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The combination (light + low-dose DORA + glycine) will produce additive or synergistic effects on unrefreshing sleep score (composite > sum of individual effects) and will outperform any single intervention in a 4-arm crossover design (n=40 ME/CFS).
**ME/CFS relevance:** The multi-lock framework implies that single-node interventions may fail while multi-node combinations succeed. This protocol targets the orexin node from both sides (enhance during wake, normalize during sleep).

### 6.2 — OX2R Agonist + LDN for Orexin-Positive Feedback (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** OX2R agonists (danavorexton) directly activate post-synaptic orexin receptors, bypassing endogenous orexin. LDN (idea 3.5) may disinhibit orexin neurons by reducing microglial inflammation. If LDN partially restores endogenous orexin release and the OX2R agonist activates remaining receptors, the combination could create a positive feedback loop: LDN → more endogenous orexin → more OX2R activation → strengthened orexin signaling → improved sleep/wake consolidation → reduced inflammation → more orexin. The initial OX2R agonist "kick" may help the LDN-restored orexin signal reach a clinically meaningful threshold.
**Evidence link:** LDN for microglial modulation (established, covered in paper). OX2R agonist (Rauf 2025). No study has combined them. The positive feedback rationale is novel.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The combination (danavorexton IV 3x/week + LDN 4.5 mg nightly) for 12 weeks will produce >50% improvement in FAS fatigue score, significantly exceeding either monotherapy (<30%), in an orexin-low ME/CFS subgroup.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Parallels the multi-lock framework's prediction that dual-node interventions may succeed where single-node fail. If LDN works partially via orexin restoration, combining with a direct agonist targets both endogenous production and receptor activity.

### 6.3 — Triage-Based Orexin Protocol for Severe/Bedbound Patients (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Severe/bedbound ME/CFS patients cannot undergo CPET, lumbar puncture, or sleep lab studies needed for orexin assessment. A triage-based protocol: (1) non-invasive screening: plasma orexin-A (correlates imperfectly with CSF but is feasible) + actigraphy-based sleep fragmentation + subjective sleepiness/fatigue ratio, (2) if plasma orexin-A < 200 pg/mL AND high sleep fragmentation: initiate morning bright light + glycine (low-burden, minimal-risk orexin-supportive care), (3) if no response in 4 weeks: escalate to LDN trial (idea 3.5, monitoring tolerability), (4) if LDN-eligible and willing: CNS orexin assessment via LP if feasible. This respects the ethical constraint: no invasive testing without potential benefit, but also no withholding of orexin-directed care.
**Evidence link:** Ruhrländer 2025 (plasma orexin-A in PASC, feasibility). Heinicke 2025 (plasma orexin in COVID ICU, demonstrating the assay is feasible in severely ill populations). The paper's severe ME/CFS care guide (separate brainstorm) covers triage principles. No orexin-specific protocol exists for severe ME/CFS.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Implementation of this triage protocol in a severe ME/CFS cohort (n=30) will result in >50% of patients receiving at least one orexin-directed intervention, with >20% showing clinically meaningful improvement (>30% fatigue reduction) within 8 weeks.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Addresses the critical access gap — severe patients are excluded from most trials but may have the most orexin dysfunction (most inflammatory, most sleep-disrupted).

### 6.4 — Orexin-Responsive ME/CFS Subtype: A Diagnostic Classification Algorithm (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Not all ME/CFS patients will have orexin dysfunction. An algorithm combining (a) plasma orexin-A, (b) overnight actigraphy sleep fragmentation index, (c) subjective sleepiness/fatigue ratio (ESS vs FAS), (d) sleep-onset latency on MSLT, and (e) CPT1B genotype could define an "orexin-responsive ME/CFS subtype." This subtype would be eligible for targeted treatments (OX2R agonists, LDN, light therapy) while other subtypes pursue different mechanistic pathways. This parallels the paper's broader subtyping framework (Part 4, proposed studies).
**Evidence link:** Rauf 2025 (NT2 gray zone, intermediate orexin), Dauvilliers 2011 (pain subtype in NT1), Horiuchi 2015 (CPT1B genetics). The paper's existing subtyping proposals do not include an orexin-defined subtype. Ruhrländer 2025 (plasma orexin in PASC) supports screening feasibility.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The orexin-responsive subtype (defined by the algorithm) will show >50% response rate to an OX2R agonist challenge (idea 2.3) vs <10% in non-orexin ME/CFS, supporting subtype validity. Prevalence of this subtype will be 20-40% of the overall ME/CFS population.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Stratification is the most efficient path to clinical trials. A 40% response rate in an unselected population masks a 80% response rate in a 50%-prevalent subtype.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 — R_headroom Model: Adding Orexin Tone as a CNS-Specific Reserve Variable (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The paper's Architecture C ODE model (metabolic reserve deficit) currently has variables for systemic metabolic reserve, energy demand, and production rates. Adding an orexin-specific variable (O_t = orexin neuron firing rate, normalized 0-1) would allow the model to represent CNS-specific energy reserve separately from systemic reserve. Key insight: orexin neurons may drain from the systemic reserve faster than other cells — representing a "priority consumer." When systemic R_headroom falls below a threshold, orexin firing drops, which further reduces whole-brain R_headroom through the glymphatic-sleep feedback loop.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (orexin suppressed by inflammation), Horiuchi 2015 (metabolic vulnerability of orexin neurons), paper's Architecture C model. The mathematical extension is novel — no ODE model has incorporated orexin.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The extended model will predict that (a) PEM occurs at a systemic R_headroom value that is higher when CNS orexin tone is impaired (the brain "crashes" before the body), (b) OX2R agonist treatment shifts the PEM threshold to the left (more exertion tolerated at the same peripheral R_headroom), and (c) an 8.1% reduction in global cerebral glucose metabolism (ADHD analogy) directly reduces orexin tone by 15-25% in the model.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Makes the Architecture C model more complete by representing the CNS-specific energy deficit. Predicts that peripheral metabolic interventions (mitochondrial supplements) alone may be insufficient if orexin is the bottleneck.

### 7.2 — DAG Extension: Orexin → Glymphatic → Microglia Positive Feedback Loop with Bifurcation Analysis (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The existing DAG (causal hierarchy ch44) includes orexin suppression and glymphatic failure as separate nodes but does not model their bidirectional interaction. Adding explicit edges: (1) orexin suppression → impaired LC-NE oscillations → glymphatic failure, (2) glymphatic failure → metabolite accumulation → microglial activation → cytokine release → orexin suppression (closed positive feedback loop). Bifurcation analysis would determine whether the system has two stable states (health ↔ disease) that would explain sudden onset vs gradual onset ME/CFS subtypes.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (cytokine → orexin), Ito 2023 (orexin → sleep architecture), Hauglund 2025 (LC-NE → glymphatic), paper's PGE2 loop (spec-pge-sub-2.typ). The bifurcation analysis is novel — no ME/CFS model has identified a tipping-point between gradual and sudden onset.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The DAG will show a bifurcation parameter (e.g., neuroinflammatory cytokine concentration at the BBB) where the system transitions from a single stable state (health) to a bistable regime (health or disease). Patients with sudden onset ME/CFS crossed this threshold rapidly (e.g., post-infection cytokine surge); patients with gradual onset sit near the threshold and cross it slowly. This predicts that early anti-inflammatory treatment (within 2 weeks of infection) could prevent threshold crossing — a testable prediction.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Explains two forms of onset (sudden vs gradual) within a single model, generates a testable prevention hypothesis for post-infectious ME/CFS.

### 7.3 — ODE Model: Orexin-A Dynamics Under Pacing/Boom-Bust Cycle (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** The boom-bust cycle in ME/CFS (over-exertion → crash → recovery → over-exertion) may be modeled as a relaxation oscillator in the orexin system. During the "boom" phase, orexin firing is pushed toward maximum by high behavioral demand. During the "bust" (PEM), orexin firing crashes below a sustainable baseline — the system may oscillate between these states. Pacing stabilizes orexin firing in a mid-range where the neuron can maintain tonic activity without exhausting its mitochondrial capacity. The model would predict optimal pacing parameters (activity duration, rest ratio) that maintain orexin firing above the PEM threshold.
**Evidence link:** Grossberg 2011 (orexin firing dynamics under stress), Horiuchi 2015 (metabolic limitation). No orexin-based mathematical model of PEM exists. The paper's Architecture C PEM model does not include CNS-specific dynamics.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The orexin oscillation model will predict that the optimal rest-to-activity ratio for preventing PEM is 2:1 (20 min activity → 40 min rest) in orexin-deficient ME/CFS, vs 1:1 in healthy controls. This ratio will be testable in a within-subject pacing intervention using wearable-based activity logs and symptom tracking.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Generates a quantitative pacing prescription from a CNS mechanism, not just empirical trial-and-error.

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### 8.1 — [OREXIN × ADHD] Shared Orexin-Dopamine Dysregulation (Tier 1, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin neurons project densely to the ventral tegmental area (VTA) and regulate dopamine neuron firing — orexin enhances phasic dopamine release, which is critical for motivation, reward salience, and sustained attention. The ADHD literature demonstrates reduced tonic dopamine and altered phasic dopamine release in prefrontal circuits. The hypothesis: orexin deficiency simultaneously reduces (a) prefrontal dopamine tone (producing ADHD-like cognitive symptoms in ME/CFS — brain fog, working memory deficits, attention instability) and (b) mesolimbic reward signaling (producing anhedonia and reduced motivation). This means the ADHD- and ME/CFS-associated cognitive deficits may share an orexin-dopamine mechanism: the 8.1% lower global cerebral glucose metabolism in ADHD (Zametkin) may be the *systemic* expression of the same hypothalamic orexin deficit that produces ME/CFS brain fog.
**Evidence link:** Orexin → VTA dopamine regulation (well-established basic neuroscience, multiple labs). Zametkin ADHD 8.1% glucose hypometabolism. The paper covers ADHD as brain energy deficit (ch14d) and connects ADHD to ME/CFS via metabolic reserve. The specific orexin-dopamine bridge is novel. No ME/CFS study has measured dopamine in relation to orexin or attention.
**Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with the lowest CSF orexin-A will show the lowest CSF homovanillic acid (HVA, a dopamine metabolite) and the worst performance on the Conners Continuous Performance Test (CPT-3). Orexin-low ME/CFS patients will also show higher ADHD self-report scores (ASRS-v1.1) than orexin-normal ME/CFS patients, independent of fatigue severity.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Directly bridges the existing ADHD-ME/CFS connection (ch14d) with the orexin mechanism — both diseases may involve orexin→dopamine→prefrontal dysfunction, but ME/CFS adds the inflammatory component.

### 8.2 — [OREXIN × MCAS] Bidirectional Orexin-Mast Cell Amplification Loop (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Mast cells express orexin receptors (both OX1R and OX2R). Orexin-A can directly activate mast cells to release histamine, tryptase, and other mediators. Conversely, mast cell-derived histamine and prostaglandins suppress orexin neuron firing (via H1 receptors on orexin-adjacent interneurons and PGE2/EP3 at the BBB). This creates a bidirectional amplification loop: orexin deficiency → reduced mast cell activation (initially) → but also reduced orexin-mediated *stabilization* of the BBB (orexin normally tightens the BBB via OX1R on endothelial cells) → increased BBB permeability → more mast cell mediator entry into the hypothalamus → more orexin suppression. The net effect may depend on disease stage.
**Evidence link:** Mast cells express orexin receptors (documented, ~2010-2015 literature). The paper covers MCAS extensively (ch14e, ch28). The BBB integrity role of orexin is established. The bidirectional loop is novel and untested.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In an MCAS-ME/CFS subgroup, plasma histamine and tryptase will correlate negatively with plasma orexin-A (r < -0.3). Administering an OX2R agonist will reduce histamine release (by stabilizing mast cell degranulation threshold) in ex vivo mast cell assays from these patients.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Connects two major paper themes (orexin + MCAS) in a testable loop. If orexin stabilizes mast cells, OX2R agonists could have anti-allergic effects beyond fatigue reduction.

### 8.3 — [OREXIN × POTS] Orexin-Baroreflex Failure as the POTS Mechanism (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.25)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin neurons project to brainstem cardiovascular centers (NTS, RVLM) and directly modulate baroreflex sensitivity. Orexin-A potentiates the baroreflex (increasing vagal response to BP changes), while orexin-B may have differential effects. In POTS, baroreflex sensitivity is impaired — the heart rate overcompensates for small BP drops. The hypothesis: partial orexin deficiency (specifically orexin-B, which acts at OX2R in the brainstem) causes baroreflex blunting, producing the characteristic POTS phenotype. This predicts that (a) POTS severity in ME/CFS will correlate inversely with CSF orexin-B (not just orexin-A) and (b) OX2R agonists will improve POTS symptoms by restoring baroreflex gain — an effect independent of sleep or fatigue.
**Evidence link:** Ruhrländer 2025 (orexin-autonomic-POTS link in PASC). Dauvilliers 2011 (pain, but autonomic not measured). The paper covers both POTS (ch14d, ch28) and orexin (ch15). The specific orexin-B/POTS-baroreflex connection is novel. No study has measured CSF orexin-B in POTS patients.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a head-up tilt table test in ME/CFS with POTS, plasma orexin-B levels during tilt will correlate with baroreflex sensitivity (r > 0.5) and inversely with heart rate increment (r < -0.4). OX2R agonist (danavorexton) administered 30 min before tilt will reduce the heart rate increment by >15 bpm in orexin-low patients.
**ME/CFS relevance:** If orexin-B is specific to POTS, it suggests a targeted biomarker and treatment for the ME/CFS + POTS subgroup, which is one of the largest comorbidity clusters.

### 8.4 — [OREXIN × hEDS] Orexin-Collagen Crosstalk via TGF-β (Tier 3, Certainty: 0.10)
**Mechanistic rationale:** hEDS involves altered TGF-β signaling and abnormal collagen structure. Orexin-A modulates TGF-β signaling: OX1R activation inhibits TGF-β1-induced collagen production in some cell types. The hypothesis: orexin deficiency in ME/CFS → reduced OX1R-mediated TGF-β inhibition → increased TGF-β signaling → altered extracellular matrix remodeling that could interact with hEDS collagen abnormalities. This is highly speculative but connects two comorbidity streams in the paper (hEDS + orexin) that currently have no mechanistic link.
**Evidence link:** Orexin-TGF-β crosstalk: limited literature, primarily in non-neuronal cells (e.g., hepatic stellate cells). hEDS/TGF-β link: established. No direct orexin-hEDS studies. The paper discusses hEDS extensively (ch14e).
**Falsifiable prediction:** Plasma TGF-β1 will be elevated in ME/CFS patients with comorbid hEDS vs ME/CFS without hEDS, and will correlate negatively with plasma orexin-A (r < -0.3).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Highly speculative but if true, would connect two of the paper's major themes and suggest that orexin dysfunction in hEDS patients may predispose to ME/CFS through TGF-β-mediated mechanisms.

### 8.5 — Post-COVID Narcolepsy: A Sentinel Condition for Orexin-Targeted ME/CFS Prevention (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** COVID-19 has been reported as a potential new trigger for narcolepsy (Lopez 2023, emerging case reports). If SARS-CoV-2 can trigger autoimmune orexin neuron destruction in some patients, then the same mechanism in a milder form (partial orexin suppression without full narcolepsy) may underlie post-COVID ME/CFS. This creates a sentinel opportunity: patients with new-onset EDS after COVID could be screened with CSF orexin and followed prospectively. Those developing intermediate orexin levels without full narcolepsy could be the ME/CFS-prevention target: early anti-inflammatory or OX2R agonist treatment to prevent chronic orexin suppression from becoming permanent.
**Evidence link:** Lopez 2023 (COVID as narcolepsy trigger, emerging). Ruhrländer 2025 (orexin in PASC). Heinicke 2025 (orexin in COVID ICU). Rauf 2025 (post-infectious narcolepsy). The sentinel study design is novel — no prospective post-COVID narcolepsy/orexin study exists.
**Falsifiable prediction:** In a prospective cohort of n=1000 COVID-19 patients, 2-5% will develop new-onset EDS within 6 months; of those, 30-50% will have CSF orexin-A in the NT2 gray zone (110-200 pg/mL). Those with gray zone orexin will have >50% risk of meeting ME/CFS diagnostic criteria at 12 months — representing a high-risk sentinel group.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Prevention is the holy grail. If a sentinel population (post-COVID EDS + low orexin) can be identified, early intervention could prevent the orexin dysfunction from becoming chronic.

## Category 9: Diagnostic/Biomarker Ideas

### 9.1 — CSF Orexin-B/Orexin-A Ratio as ME/CFS-Specific Subtype Biomarker (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Orexin-A and orexin-B are differentially processed from the same prepro-orexin precursor. They have different receptor affinities (OX1R: orexin-A > orexin-B; OX2R: equal affinity). In NT1, both are severely reduced. In NT2, orexin-A is intermediate but orexin-B may be differentially affected. The ratio (orexin-B/A) may distinguish between (a) global orexin neuron loss (both reduced, ratio preserved) vs (b) processing disruption (ratio altered). Orexin-B is rarely measured — most studies only measure orexin-A. LopezAmador 2025 noted "variable orexin-B responses" as a candidate subtyping biomarker.
**Evidence link:** LopezAmador 2025 (orexin-B variability in ME/CFS, cert 0.35). Rauf 2025 (only orexin-A reported in most NT1/NT2 studies). No study has systematically compared orexin-B/A ratio across ME/CFS, NT1, NT2, and controls. The differential receptor pharmacology (Sakurai 1998) supports functional relevance.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The CSF orexin-B/orexin-A ratio will differ between ME/CFS subtypes: a high-orexin-B/low-orexin-A ratio will identify a "processing-deficit subtype" (possible prohormone convertase dysfunction), while a balanced low ratio will identify a "neuron-loss subtype." These subtypes will have different OX2R agonist response rates.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Novel biomarker that costs nothing beyond standard CSF orexin measurement (both peptides can be quantified from the same sample by RIA/ELISA). Could guide treatment selection.

### 9.2 — Nighttime Salivary Orexin-A as a Home-Assessable Biomarker (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.15)
**Mechanistic rationale:** CSF orexin requires lumbar puncture — a barrier for many ME/CFS patients. Salivary orexin-A has been measured in some studies (non-ME/CFS) and shows a circadian pattern. If salivary orexin-A correlates with CSF orexin-A (uncertain, but testable), a timed overnight saliva collection (hourly or at key timepoints) could provide a home-assessable proxy for CNS orexin dynamics. The critical timepoint is the evening orexin withdrawal (pre-sleep) and the early-morning rise (pre-wake) — flattened slopes would indicate orexin dysfunction.
**Evidence link:** Ruhrländer 2025 (plasma orexin-A in PASC — peripheral measurement is feasible). No validated salivary orexin-A assay for clinical use. Heinicke 2025 (plasma orexin in COVID ICU — demonstrates assay feasibility in sick patients). The paper's sleep/saliva biomarker section (ch20) already covers salivary metabolomics.
**Falsifiable prediction:** Salivary orexin-A will show a circadian rhythm in healthy controls (peak at wake, nadir at sleep onset) that is blunted in ME/CFS. The amplitude of the salivary orexin-A rhythm will correlate with the amplitude of the actigraphy-based rest-activity rhythm (r > 0.4) and with subjective unrefreshing sleep (r < -0.3).
**ME/CFS relevance:** Enables orexin assessment without LP — critical for severe patients and for large-scale screening.

### 9.3 — Pupillographic Sleepiness Test (PST) as a Surrogate for Orexin Tone (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** Pupil diameter is controlled by the balance of sympathetic (dilator) and parasympathetic (constrictor) input — both modulated by orexin. Orexin deficiency reduces sympathetic tone to the pupil, producing smaller baseline pupil diameter and more pupillary instability in darkness (pupillary fatigue waves). The PST (pupillographic sleepiness test) measures these oscillations. In NT1, PST shows characteristic instability. If this reflects orexin deficiency rather than just sleepiness, the PST could serve as a non-invasive orexin proxy.
**Evidence link:** Nardone 2011 (cortical hypoexcitability in orexin-deficient states — the pupil may show similar hypoexcitability). PST-EDS correlations are documented in sleep medicine. No orexin-PST correlation has been directly studied. The paper does not currently discuss pupillometry.
**Falsifiable prediction:** PST pupillary instability index (PUI) will correlate negatively with CSF orexin-A in a population of NT1, NT2, ME/CFS, and healthy controls (combined analysis, r > 0.5 for the inverse correlation: lower orexin → higher instability). PST > 6th percentile of healthy controls will predict CSF orexin-A < 200 pg/mL with >80% sensitivity.
**ME/CFS relevance:** Cheap ($50/device), quick (11 min), non-invasive, office-based. Could become a screening tool for orexin-deficient ME/CFS subtype identification.

### 9.4 — CPT1B Genotyping as a Genetic Risk Marker for Orexin Dysfunction in ME/CFS (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.20)
**Mechanistic rationale:** CPT1B encodes carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1B, the rate-limiting enzyme for mitochondrial fatty acid oxidation. Horiuchi 2015 found a CPT1B polymorphism associated with narcolepsy (risk allele at rs2267384). Orexin neurons preferentially use fatty acid oxidation (their high firing rate demands sustained ATP from multiple fuel sources). A CPT1B variant that reduces FAO efficiency would specifically impair orexin neuron energy supply, making them vulnerable to failure under inflammatory stress. This would create a genetic predisposition to orexin dysfunction in ME/CFS.
**Evidence link:** Horiuchi 2015 (CPT1B-narcolepsy association, cert 0.3). CPT1B basic biology: rate-limiting for FAO. No ME/CFS CPT1B study. The paper covers genetic predisposition (Part 4, proposed studies). Horiuchi is a low-certainty paper but the CPT1B-orexin link is biologically plausible.
**Falsifiable prediction:** The CPT1B rs2267384 risk allele will be enriched in ME/CFS patients with low CSF orexin-A (<200 pg/mL) compared to ME/CFS patients with normal orexin and healthy controls. The allele frequency in orexin-low ME/CFS will resemble the NT1 frequency.
**ME/CFS relevance:** A 5-minute PCR test (inexpensive) could identify a genetic predisposition to orexin dysfunction, enabling early screening in at-risk populations (e.g., post-infectious, family history).

### 9.5 — [CHALLENGING] The Orexin-Measurement Trap: CSF Orexin-A Variability Within and Between ME/CFS Patients (Tier 2, Certainty: 0.30)
**Mechanistic rationale:** CSF orexin-A levels vary significantly by time of day (circadian, 2x difference between peak and nadir), sampling conditions (fasting, sleep deprivation), and assay methodology (RIA vs ELISA, different commercial kits). The NT1 diagnostic threshold (<110 pg/mL) was established under standardized conditions (morning LP, after overnight PSG). No one has standardized or documented these variables in the few ME/CFS studies with CSF measures. The "orexin measurement trap" hypothesis: apparent orexin deficiency in ME/CFS may be an artifact of uncontrolled sampling conditions — ME/CFS patients with poor sleep may have been sampled at a different circadian phase than controls. This would mean the orexin "deficit" is actually a phase-shift, not a magnitude deficit.
**Evidence link:** Rauf 2025 (NT1 diagnostic threshold), Ruhrländer 2025 (plasma, not CSF, with known diurnal variation). The paper's sleep architecture coverage documents phase-shifts in ME/CFS (circadian disruption). No study has controlled for circadian phase in ME/CFS CSF orexin measurement. This idea challenges the interpretation of the LopezAmador 2025 review's central claim.
**Falsifiable prediction:** When ME/CFS patients are strictly sampled under controlled circadian conditions (after 3 days of standardized sleep-wake schedule, LP at 09:00 ± 1h after PSG-confirmed sleep), CSF orexin-A levels will not differ from matched healthy controls (p > 0.3), and the observed "low orexin" in uncontrolled studies will be explained by circadian phase misalignment rather than absolute deficiency.
**ME/CFS relevance:** If the orexin "deficit" is a phase-shift artifact, it redirects treatment from orexin replacement to circadian entrainment (light therapy, chronotherapy, melatonin) — a fundamentally different approach.
