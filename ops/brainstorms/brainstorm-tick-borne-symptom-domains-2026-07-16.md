## Category 1: Novel Hypotheses

### 1.1 — Bartonella Endothelial HIF-1α Stabilization → Glycolytic Shift → PEM Susceptibility
- **Mechanistic rationale:** *Bartonella* spp. live intracellularly in endothelial cells, inducing angiogenesis via HIF-1α-dependent VEGF secretion. Chronic Bartonella infection creates persistent HIF-1α stabilization, shifting endothelial and perivascular metabolism toward glycolysis even at rest. This positions the tissue closer to its anaerobic threshold — exertion triggers earlier and more severe metabolic crisis (PEM). The mechanism is endothelial-centered, not myocyte-centered, meaning standard muscle biopsy would miss it.
- **Evidence link:** Bartonella-induced HIF-1α stabilization (Kempf 2005, *Cell Microbiol*). HIF pathway in ME/CFS (#spec:hif-pathway, ch14k). PEM pathophysiology involves abnormal lactate/metabolic debt.
- **Certainty:** 0.18 — extrapolation of in-vitro Bartonella biology to clinical ME/CFS. No direct human data.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with Bartonella seropositivity show elevated HIF-1α target genes (VEGF, GLUT1, PDK1, LDHA) in resting endothelial biopsy or circulating endothelial cells vs seronegative ME/CFS.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Would mean that a persistent intracellular bacterial infection can "reprogram" the energy systems of blood vessel walls, making the whole body more prone to energy crashes after exertion.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 1.2 — Tick-Borne Coinfection Combinatorial Immune Signatures as Hidden Variable in ME/CFS Immunophenotyping
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The immune signature of tick-triggered ME/CFS may depend on which pathogen combination drives it. Borrelia mono-infection → different T-cell shifts than Borrelia+Babesia or Borrelia+Bartonella triple infection. Each pathogen engages different PRRs: Borrelia lipoproteins → TLR2; Babesia merozoites → TLR9/endosomal; Bartonella LPS → TLR4. Combinatorial PRR engagement produces non-linear cytokine/chemokine profiles. This would explain immunophenotyping heterogeneity in ME/CFS cohorts — hidden tick-exposure status is a confound.
- **Evidence link:** Girgis2025PTLDTcell (T-cell subsets in PTLDS, single-pathogen focus). Coinfection common (Krause1996babesia). No study has stratified ME/CFS immunophenotyping by tick-borne serology.
- **Certainty:** 0.25 — plausible but untested. Requires serological stratification of existing datasets.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Cluster analysis of ME/CFS immunophenotyping data (flow cytometry, cytokines) reveals subgroups whose immune signatures correlate with tick-borne seropositivity patterns — each combination yields a distinct immune profile.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** People who got ME/CFS from different tick-borne infections may have different-looking lab results under the microscope, meaning "one-size-fits-all" immune tests in ME/CFS may miss real patterns unless tick exposure is accounted for.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 1.3 — Borrelia Persisters in Muscle Tissue → Localized Lactate Microenvironment → Worsened Muscle PEM
- **Mechanistic rationale:** *Borrelia burgdorferi* can persist in muscle tissue (documented in animal models and human muscle biopsy, Sapi 2016). Persister spirochetes produce localized metabolic waste including lactate via anaerobic metabolism. In ME/CFS, this creates a chronic low-grade muscle acidosis that lowers the threshold for exertional lactic acidosis — the muscle starts closer to anaerobic threshold before any work is done. This is distinct from systemic metabolic failure (mitochondrial dysfunction already described) in being anatomically localized and infection-driven.
- **Evidence link:** Borrelia persistence documented (ch07, microbial persistence bullet). PEM involves abnormal lactate response in ME/CFS. Sapi 2016 *J Transl Med* (Borrelia biofilm in human muscle).
- **Certainty:** 0.15 — animal model + in vitro data, no controlled human ME/CFS muscle biopsy study for persisters.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Muscle biopsy of ME/CFS patients with positive Borrelia serology (or PCR of muscle) shows Borrelia DNA/antigen and elevated resting lactate compared to seronegative ME/CFS muscle biopsies, and the lactate level correlates with PEM severity by validated scale.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** If right, then the muscle fatigue in some ME/CFS patients isn't from the energy factories (mitochondria) being broken — it's from a bacterial infection in the muscle producing waste acid at all times, like having a tiny acid factory running inside the muscle 24/7.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 1.4 — Tick-Borne Kynurenine Pathway Activation → Tryptophan Depletion → Peripheral Serotonin Loss → GI/Cognitive Symptoms
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Borrelia lipoproteins and Babesia hemozoin both activate IFN-γ and TNF-α, which induce indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO). IDO shunts tryptophan from serotonin synthesis to kynurenine pathway. Three consequences: (a) peripheral serotonin depletion (already documented in ME/CFS, ch13), (b) kynurenine → quinolinic acid (NMDA agonist, excitotoxic → cognitive dysfunction), (c) kynurenic acid (NMDA antagonist — competing effects). The quinolinic acid/kynurenic acid ratio may be the key variable — neuroinflammation tilts balance toward quinolinic acid. This provides a specific tryptophan-handling mechanism for tick-triggered cognitive symptoms beyond general neuroinflammation.
- **Evidence link:** Peripheral serotonin depletion (ch13). IDO activation in post-Lyme (Grygorczuk 2011 *Ticks Tick Borne Dis*, in vitro/human data). Kynurenine pathway in post-infectious fatigue (references in ME/CFS literature).
- **Certainty:** 0.20 — plausible pathway with partial evidence in each step, but no integrated study in tick-triggered ME/CFS.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** PTLDS patients have elevated kynurenine/tryptophan ratio (K/T ratio) compared to recovered Lyme patients; K/T ratio correlates with cognitive test scores (Trail Making, DSST) and GI symptom severity.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A single metabolic pathway could explain why tick-borne infections produce both "brain fog" and digestive problems — both come from the same amino acid being diverted away from normal functions.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 1.5 — Tick-Borne Pathogen Evasion of Neutrophil Extracellular Traps (NETs) → Sustained Antigen Exposure → NETosis-Driven Endothelial Damage
- **Mechanistic rationale:** *Borrelia burgdorferi* evades NET-mediated killing by expressing a surface nuclease (BBD27, aka Nuc) that degrades NET DNA backbones. *Bartonella* also resists NETs via LPS modifications. Failed NETosis leaves neutrophil debris (histones, MPO, LL-37, cell-free DNA) in circulation — the identical substrate that drives the microclot pathology described in ME/CFS. This connects tick infection directly to microclot pathology without invoking autoantibodies: immune evasion activates the clot-forming cascade, providing a "sterile inflammation" driver that persists even if pathogen load is low.
- **Evidence link:** Borrelia nuclease degrading NETs (Ramos 2022 *Front Immunol*). Microclots / endothelial dysfunction (ch10). NET-driven microclots described in Long COVID; application to ME/CFS is newer. Self-reinforcing loop: NET debris → microclot → endothelial damage → more immune activation → more NETs.
- **Certainty:** 0.15 — novel synthesis of two literatures (tick immunology + ME/CFS microclots). No direct ME/CFS data.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** ME/CFS patients with tick-borne seropositivity have higher circulating NET markers (citrullinated histone H3, MPO-DNA complexes, cell-free DNA) than seronegative ME/CFS, and NET marker levels correlate with microclot burden by fluorescence microscopy.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The failed attempt by immune cells to kill tick bacteria with "net traps" might leave debris that triggers abnormal blood clotting — a mechanism that doesn't require the bacteria to be alive, only for the immune system to have tried and failed to catch them.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 1.6 — Borrelia CSpA (Complement Evasion) × ME/CFS Complement Dysregulation → Synergistic Amplification
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Borrelia expresses complement-acquiring surface proteins (CspA, OspE) that bind factor H, evading complement-mediated killing. In ME/CFS, baseline complement dysregulation (elevated C4a, low C3, documented in ME/CFS) creates a permissive environment: the host complement system already cannot clear targets efficiently. Borrelia's factor H hijacking operates on top of this pre-existing vulnerability. This produces a "two-hit" amplification where neither hit alone explains persistence but the combination does. Pre-existing complement weakness may predict which Lyme patients develop PTLDS.
- **Evidence link:** Borrelia CspA/factor H mechanism (Kraiczy 2001, well-established in Borrelia literature). C4a elevation in ME/CFS (Sorensen 2009, Nunez 2013). Complement dysregulation in ME/CFS generally.
- **Certainty:** 0.12 — plausible cross-literature synthesis; no direct study testing the interaction.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a prospective cohort of acute Lyme patients, those with pre-infection complement abnormalities (low C3, elevated C4a) are more likely to develop PTLDS at 6 months than those with normal pre-infection complement.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Some people's immune "net" (complement system) may have holes that Borrelia bacteria are evolutionarily designed to slip through — meaning the same tick bite has very different consequences depending on the person's genetic immune background.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 2: Research Directions

### 2.1 — Retrospective Tick-Borne Serology Stratification of Existing ME/CFS Cohort Data
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Many ME/CFS cohorts (e.g., Cornell, SolveCFS, NIH, IiME) have stored plasma/serum with full immunophenotyping, transcriptomics, metabolomics. A targeted serological panel (Borrelia C6 ELISA + VlsE, Bartonella IFA + enrichment PCR if blood available, Babesia IFA + PCR, plus standard tick-borne panel including Anaplasma/Ehrlichia) would allow retrospective re-analysis of these data stratified by tick exposure status. The cost is modest (~$200/sample × N = $50k for 250 samples); the value is resolving whether tick-borne exposure is a hidden variable in prior null biomarker studies.
- **Evidence link:** Existing ME/CFS cohort repositories. Zero studies have stratified by tick-borne serology. If 10-26% of ME/CFS patients are tick-exposed (from Bartonella data, Breitschwerdt), this is a significant confound for all biomarker studies done in endemic areas.
- **Certainty:** 0.70 — high feasibility, moderate cost, actionable result regardless of outcome. Would produce both positive findings (detectable seroprevalence excess) and negative findings (no excess, excluding the hypothesis).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** At least one tick-borne pathogen shows elevated seroprevalence in a well-matched geographic ME/CFS vs control comparison.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A relatively cheap blood test on stored samples could reveal whether tick infections explain why some prior ME/CFS studies found conflicting results — like realizing a medical study accidentally mixed together people with and without a hidden infection.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 2.2 — PTLDS vs ME/CFS Head-to-Head Multi-Platform Immunophenotyping
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The paper documents symptom overlap (26/29 symptoms, ch07) and shared mechanisms (ch35). What is missing is a direct head-to-head immune comparison using the same platform, same lab, same time window. Needed: (1) flow cytometry (T-cell subsets per Girgis2025 markers plus B-cell/NK panels), (2) cytokine panel (Th1/Th2/Th17 + Type I IFN), (3) EV profiling (ch07 bridge), (4) autoantibody panel (GPCR, anti-neuronal), (5) complement panel. Groups: PTLDS (n=100), tick-seropositive ME/CFS (n=100), tick-seronegative ME/CFS (n=100), recovered Lyme (n=50), healthy controls (n=100). Outcome: which immune parameters are shared between PTLDS and tick-ME/CFS vs specific to one.
- **Evidence link:** ch07 (symptom overlap), ch35 (shared mechanisms). Girgis2025PTLDTcell (template). Availability of ME/CFS cohorts in endemic areas.
- **Certainty:** 0.65 — feasible, platform exists, but expensive ($500k-1M). Risk: null result is possible (immune parameters don't distinguish groups).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Tick-seropositive ME/CFS and PTLDS share the CXCR3+CCR4-CCR6- CD8 expansion and CXCR5+ CD4+ naïve reduction that distinguish both from tick-seronegative ME/CFS.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** This study would answer a question patients and doctors have asked for 20 years: is "chronic Lyme" the same disease as ME/CFS or a different one with similar symptoms?
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 2.3 — Babesia Serosurvey in ME/CFS with Symptom Domain Mapping
- **Mechanistic rationale:** This directly addresses the gap identified in the paper (lim:babesia-evidence-gap). A cross-sectional serosurvey in Babesia-endemic areas (Northeast US, Upper Midwest, parts of Europe) comparing ME/CFS patients to age/sex/geography-matched controls using Babesia IFA (IgG, IgM) + PCR + blood smear + hemolytic markers (LDH, haptoglobin, reticulocyte count, bilirubin). Symptom phenotyping via validated instruments (DSQ-2 for ME/CFS symptoms; PROMIS fatigue; cognitive battery). Primary: Babesia seroprevalence difference. Secondary: correlation of seropositivity with specific symptom domains (fatigue, cognitive, autonomic, pain) and hemolytic markers.
- **Evidence link:** lim:babesia-evidence-gap (explicit gap in paper). Krause1996babesia (coinfection → worse symptoms). Babesia-ME/CFS link rests on inference only.
- **Certainty:** 0.60 — straightforward study design; primary risk is that seroprevalence is too low to detect difference with feasible N.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Babesia seropositivity (IgG ≥1:64 or PCR+) is >5% in ME/CFS vs <2% in matched controls, with seropositive patients showing elevated LDH and lower haptoglobin.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** This would settle a specific question: is the "Babesia form" of tick-borne illness actually involved in ME/CFS, or is it just a theoretical possibility without real-world evidence?
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 2.4 — TCR Repertoire Sequencing in Lyme-Triggered ME/CFS vs PTLDS vs Resolved Lyme
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If persistent antigen drives chronic T-cell activation in PTLDS/ME/CFS (Girgis2025), the TCR repertoire should show clonal expansions specific to Borrelia epitopes that persist in chronic cases and contract in recovered. Longitudinal TCR-seq (acute Lyme → 6mo → 12mo) could identify which clones expand in those who develop chronic illness and whether these clones are Borrelia-specific. This is the most direct evidence for or against ongoing antigen-driven T-cell activation.
- **Evidence link:** Girgis2025PTLDTcell. TCR clonality tracking used in cancer and autoimmune disease; not yet applied to PTLDS.
- **Certainty:** 0.45 — methodologically sound but expensive. Risk: signal may be too weak if Borrelia antigen drives only a small fraction of the repertoire.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** PTLDS patients show sustained TCR clonal expansions against Borrelia epitope peptides (by MHC multimer or capture assay) at 12 months post-Lyme, while recovered patients show contraction.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** If specific T-cell clones remain activated years after Lyme treatment, it would prove that the immune system is still "seeing" Borrelia — the strongest evidence yet for persistent infection driving chronic symptoms.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 3: Drug/Medication

### 3.1 — Low-Dose Rapamycin in Tick-Seropositive ME/CFS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** mTOR pathway is activated by Borrelia lipoproteins via TLR2/PI3K/Akt. mTOR activation drives T-cell exhaustion (PD-1 upregulation, reduced proliferative capacity). Low-dose rapamycin (1-5mg/week) inhibits mTOR, restores T-cell function by reversing exhaustion in chronic infection models. Rapamycin also reduces Borrelia burden in animal models (via autophagy induction). For ME/CFS, the trial would test whether tick-seropositive patients respond preferentially to rapamycin vs tick-seronegative — a stratified Phase 2 design.
- **Evidence link:** Borrelia → mTOR activation (Gondard 2022 *Front Immunol*). mTOR → T-cell exhaustion (cancer literature). Rapamycin in ME/CFS: no published trial, but proposed by some researchers. T-cell exhaustion in ME/CFS (ch07).
- **Certainty:** 0.15 — no human ME/CFS data for rapamycin; mechanism plausible but unproven; stratified trial required.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In a 12-week randomized trial of low-dose rapamycin (1mg/day or 5mg/week) vs placebo in ME/CFS, tick-seropositive patients show ≥2× the improvement rate in fatigue (PROMIS-F) vs tick-seronegative.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** An existing transplant-rejection drug might work better in ME/CFS patients with tick exposure histories, meaning future trials should sort patients by tick infection status rather than testing everyone together.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 3.2 — JAK Inhibition (Baricitinib) for Bartonella-Neuroinflammation
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bartonella induces a robust Type I IFN signature via cGAS-STING (intracellular DNA sensing). Downstream IFN signaling proceeds through JAK1/JAK2. Baricitinib (JAK1/JAK2 inhibitor, FDA-approved for RA, tested in Long COVID) can block this pathway irrespective of whether Bartonella is alive or dead — i.e., even if only antigen persists. The neuroinflammatory symptoms (cognitive dysfunction, brain fog) may be driven by this IFN signaling in microglia and CNS endothelial cells, not by direct infection of neurons.
- **Evidence link:** Bartonella → STING → Type I IFN documented (Gürtler 2024 *Cell Microbiol*). Baricitinib in Long COVID (COV-BARRIER trial). Neuroinflammation persistence in PTLDS (ch35). JAK inhibition response may distinguish antigen-driven from non-inflammatory fatigue.
- **Certainty:** 0.12 — plausible pathway, but no direct Bartonella-ME/CFS trial; STING-IFN pathway not yet established in ME/CFS.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In an N-of-1 or small crossover trial (n=10 per group), Bartonella-seropositive ME/CFS patients on baricitinib 2mg/day show reduction in cognitive symptom scores (processing speed, working memory) compared to seronegative ME/CFS, with corresponding reduction in serum IFN-β and CXCL10.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A drug already approved for arthritis could potentially reduce "brain fog" in ME/CFS patients with past Bartonella exposure, though only a trial would tell.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 3.3 — Azlocillin for Borrelia Persisters — Phase 2 in PTLDS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Azlocillin (ureidopenicillin, IV) shows superior activity against Borrelia persisters in vitro compared to doxycycline or amoxicillin, likely due to better penetration into stationary-phase spirochetes (Feng 2015 *Front Med*). Unlike disulfiram (Kuvaldina2025DisulfiramLyme, which caused neuropathy), azlocillin is a conventional β-lactam with known safety profile. No human PTLDS trial exists. The trial would randomize PTLDS patients to 4 weeks IV azlocillin vs placebo, with primary endpoint at 6 months.
- **Evidence link:** In vitro persister data (Feng 2015, 2019). Disulfiram harm signal shows anti-persister approach is promising but needs a safer agent. Azlocillin safety in pseudomonal infections well-characterized.
- **Certainty:** 0.20 — strong in vitro rationale but first human use in this population; IV administration is a barrier.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Azlocillin reduces PTLDS symptom burden (composite symptom score) by ≥30% at 6 months vs placebo.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A four-week IV antibiotic course could potentially treat the chronic infection some researchers believe underlies persistent Lyme symptoms, using a drug already approved for other infections.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 3.4 — Dapsone Combination Therapy for Drug-Resistant Bartonella in ME/CFS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Bartonella is an intracellular pathogen that resists standard antibiotics. Dapsone (diaminodiphenyl sulfone, DHPS inhibitor) plus rifampin plus doxycycline has shown efficacy in refractory bartonellosis case series (HIV, cat scratch disease). Dapsone also has anti-inflammatory properties via inhibition of neutrophil myeloperoxidase — potentially dampening NET-mediated damage (see 1.5). The combination is low-cost, oral, and available globally, but requires G6PD screening.
- **Evidence link:** Bartonella treatment protocols in HIV literature. Dapsone + rifampin for Bartonella (case series). NET-inhibition property of dapsone (MPO pathway).
- **Certainty:** 0.10 — case series only; high side-effect burden (hemolysis, methemoglobinemia, G6PD screening required).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Bartonella-seropositive ME/CFS patients on dapsone combo for 8 weeks show reduction in Bartonella antibody titers and fatigue scores vs placebo.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** An old leprosy drug combined with two antibiotics already used for Lyme could hit Bartonella harder than standard treatment, but it requires a blood enzyme test first due to risk of severe anemia.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 4: Supplement/Nutraceutical

### 4.1 — Lactoferrin for Bartonella Burdens
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Lactoferrin sequesters iron (bacteriostatic for Bartonella, an iron-requiring intracellular pathogen), directly binds bacterial membranes (bactericidal activity shown in vitro), and modulates hepcidin to reduce iron availability. In ME/CFS, it additionally provides anti-oxidant and anti-inflammatory effects through TLR4 modulation. Bovine lactoferrin is OTC, low-risk, and can be taken orally.
- **Evidence link:** Lactoferrin anti-Bartonella activity (in vitro, limited data). Hepcidin/iron dysregulation in ME/CFS (some studies, not in current paper). TLR4 modulation established for lactoferrin.
- **Certainty:** 0.08 — speculative mechanism; no clinical data in ME/CFS or Bartonella patients; in vitro only.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Bartonella-seropositive ME/CFS patients on oral lactoferrin 500mg/day for 8 weeks show a trend toward reduced Bartonella antibody titers and reduced fatigue vs placebo.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A cheap, over-the-counter milk protein that binds iron could reduce bacterial burden in Bartonella-exposed patients, though the evidence is very preliminary.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 4.2 — Cryptolepis with Matrine as Anti-Borrelia Botanical Combination
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Cryptolepis sanguinolenta (alkaloid cryptolepine) and Sophora flavescens (alkaloid matrine) both show in vitro activity against Borrelia persister forms (Feng 2019 *Front Med*, tested 14 botanicals — cryptolepis was the most active). Combined, they may target different persister subpopulations. In ME/CFS patients with Borrelia exposure, this could reduce persister burden without antibiotics. Risk: hepatotoxicity at high doses; drug interactions via CYP3A4.
- **Evidence link:** Feng 2019 in vitro data. Botanical approach familiar to LLMD community. No human ME/CFS trial.
- **Certainty:** 0.10 — strong in vitro basis, minimal human safety data; quality control issues (standardization, adulteration) are barriers.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Borrelia-seropositive ME/CFS patients on 12 weeks of standardized cryptolepis+matrine (pharmaceutical-grade) show reduced symptom burden vs placebo and reduced Borrelia serological reactivity.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Two African plant extracts widely used in "herbal Lyme" protocols could have real anti-Borrelia activity, but patients need standardized preparations (not random internet products) for it to be meaningful.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 4.3 — Heme-Arginine for Babesia-Associated Hemolytic Fatigue
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Babesia causes chronic hemolysis → heme loss from lysed RBCs → impaired mitochondrial complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase requires heme). Heme-arginine is an oral bioavailable heme supplement (studied in porphyria). In Babesia-seropositive ME/CFS patients with hemolytic markers, heme supplementation could restore mitochondrial respiration and reduce fatigue. Even if hemolysis is subclinical (no anemia, only elevated LDH), heme loss may be significant enough to impair energy production.
- **Evidence link:** Babesia → hemolysis (established). Heme-arginine bioavailability (porphyria literature). Mitochondrial dysfunction in ME/CFS. No direct heme study in ME/CFS.
- **Certainty:** 0.08 — plausible but untested; heme-arginine is an orphan drug with limited availability.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Babesia-seropositive ME/CFS patients with elevated LDH/low haptoglobin show improved ATP production capacity (by respirometry) and reduced fatigue on heme-arginine vs placebo.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A supplement that helps the body make new hemoglobin could counteract the energy drain in ME/CFS patients who have a parasite quietly breaking down their red blood cells.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 5: Non-Pharmacological

### 5.1 — Tick-Exposure Prevention as Primary ME/CFS Prevention (Public Health)
- **Mechanistic rationale:** If tick-borne infections trigger ME/CFS in a subset, then reducing tick exposure in endemic areas could reduce ME/CFS incidence. This is primary prevention, not treatment. The intervention is existing public health infrastructure (DEET/acaricide education, landscape management, deer fences, Lyme vaccine). The novel angle is adding ME/CFS incidence as an endpoint in tick-prevention effectiveness studies.
- **Evidence link:** Known geographic overlap of Lyme endemicity and ME/CFS prevalence (ch07, ch35). No study has measured whether tick prevention reduces ME/CFS incidence.
- **Certainty:** 0.35 — plausible but hard to study (requires population-level longitudinal tracking of ME/CFS diagnosis before/after intervention).
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In communities implementing aggressive tick-prevention programs (e.g., Lyme vaccine rollout, acaricide treatment of deer), incident ME/CFS diagnoses decline within 5 years compared to matched non-intervention communities.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** If proven, tick-bite prevention (repellent, clothing, yard management) becomes a way to prevent some cases of ME/CFS — not just Lyme disease — especially for children in tick-endemic areas.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 5.2 — Therapeutic Plasma Exchange with Pre-Screening for Tick-Borne Antibodies
- **Mechanistic rationale:** TPE is being studied in ME/CFS for autoantibody removal (ch35 novel translational findings). Tick-borne antibodies in donor plasma could passively transfer to recipients, confound autoantibody measurements post-TPE, and — in the case of anti-neuronal cross-reactive antibodies — theoretically cause symptoms. Screening donor plasma for tick-borne antibodies before TPE would be a protocol refinement for trials.
- **Evidence link:** TPE trials in ME/CFS (immunoadsorption, Scheibenbogen group). Donor pools in endemic areas have tick-borne antibodies.
- **Certainty:** 0.10 — very small effect size; likely negligible clinically but relevant as a trial confound.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In an open-label TPE study, patients who receive plasma from donors with high tick-borne antibody titers show less improvement (or unexpected symptom profiles) compared to those receiving screened plasma.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A small technical detail — whether the donor blood used in a treatment has been exposed to tick infections — might influence how well ME/CFS patients respond to plasma exchange.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 5.3 — Sustained Natural Killer Cell Activation via Lifestyle Interventions Targeting Tick-Modulated Pathways
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Tick salivary proteins directly suppress NK cell function (Salp15, Iric-1). In tick-triggered ME/CFS, NK dysfunction (documented in ME/CFS generally) may have a specific salivary-protein contribution beyond the baseline defect. Interventions known to boost NK function (moderate exercise — NOT appropriate in ME/CFS due to PEM; cold exposure; beta-glucan supplementation; sleep optimization) could be targeted. Note: exercise-based NK boosting is contraindicated in ME/CFS, limiting options.
- **Evidence link:** Tick saliva → NK suppression (Kotal 2015 *Front Cell Infect Microbiol*). NK dysfunction in ME/CFS (ch07). PEM precludes exercise-based interventions.
- **Certainty:** 0.05 — too non-specific; exercise PEM risk outweighs benefit; NK boosting has unclear relevance to tick-triggered ME/CFS specifically. Included for completeness but low priority.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Tick-seropositive ME/CFS patients show lower NK cell activity than tick-seronegative ME/CFS, and NK activity correlates with salivary protein antibody titers.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Probably none actionable — the interventions that boost NK cells in healthy people (exercise) are harmful in ME/CFS, so this idea mainly identifies a research measure rather than a practical treatment.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 6: Combinations + Access

### 6.1 — Adaptive Platform Trial for Multi-Pathogen Antimicrobial Therapy in Tick-Triggered ME/CFS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The standard RCT design fails when the intervention is multi-pathogen combination therapy. An adaptive platform trial (I-SPY-like) could: (1) enroll ME/CFS patients with tick-borne serological evidence, (2) assign to arms based on individual serological profile (Borrelia-only → arm A: doxycycline × 12wk; Babesia → arm B: atovaquone+azithromycin × 10d; Bartonella → arm C: doxycycline+rifampin × 8wk; multiple → arm D: sequential combo), (3) use Bayesian response-adaptive randomization to drop futile arms and escalate promising ones, (4) measure symptom burden reduction at 6 and 12 months. This design respects the heterogeneity that makes fixed-protocol RCTs fail.
- **Evidence link:** Inadequate treatment duration problem (ch07; brainstorm inadequate-treatment-duration-research). Disulfiram trial (Kuvaldina2025) shows harm without prior adaptive screening. I-SPY platform model from oncology.
- **Certainty:** 0.30 — feasible with existing adaptive platform trial methods; high cost (~$5M); requires multi-center coordination.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** At least one antimicrobial arm (likely doxycycline+rifampin for Bartonella) will cross the Bayesian futility/efficacy boundary, showing benefit in the serologically-defined subgroup.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Instead of testing one drug in everyone and calling it a failure, a smart trial design could efficiently test multiple different antibiotic combinations at once — finding what works for which tick infection type.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 6.2 — Tick-Borne Testing Access as Health Equity Issue in ME/CFS
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Standard Lyme testing (two-tier serology) has sensitivity ~30-40% in early disease. Advanced testing (VlsE epitope panel, Marques2026; BAPGM enrichment culture; Babesia FISH PCR; Anaplasma PCR) is available only through specialized commercial labs (Galaxy, IGeneX, MDL) or research centers, usually out-of-pocket. This creates diagnostic access inequity: patients with resources can get tested and potentially treated; those without cannot. This confounds ME/CFS cohort studies by enriching research populations for high-resource patients who may have different exposure profiles.
- **Evidence link:** Diagnostic limitations documented in ch07 (IDSA/ILADS controversy). Nair2025VlsELyme, Marques2026PTLDSepitopes (advanced diagnostics exist but not widely available). Breitschwerdt BAPGM culture (single lab).
- **Certainty:** 0.40 — well-documented access problem; impact on ME/CFS cohort composition is less certain.

## Category 7: Mathematical Model Extensions

### 7.1 — Antibiotic PK/PD ODE: Why 2-4 Weeks Doxycycline Fails in Some
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Extend the paper's existing immune ODE models with antibiotic PK/PD compartments. The model would include: (a) plasma doxycycline concentration (first-order kinetics), (b) tissue penetration rate into CNS and synovium (slow, concentration-dependent), (c) Borrelia growth/survival with MIC-dependent killing, (d) persister subpopulation with reduced metabolic activity (antibiotic-tolerant). Simulate why 14-28 days of doxycycline fails to eradicate Borrelia in CNS (penetration ~20-40% of plasma) and synovium (even lower), predicting the necessary duration and dose for tissue sterilization.
- **Evidence link:** ODE models in paper (existing framework). Doxycycline PK/PD data (well-established). Borrelia persister in vitro data (Feng 2015, 2019). Can be fit to PTLDS clinical trial data for validation.
- **Certainty:** 0.40 — mathematical framework is standard; parameter uncertainty is the limitation.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Model predicts that 4 weeks of high-dose doxycycline (200mg BID, not 100mg BID) achieves CNS sterilization in 60% of simulated patients vs 20% with standard dosing, predicting a testable clinical hypothesis.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A computer model could show mathematically why a two-week antibiotic course doesn't work for brain infection — the drug levels in the brain never reach high enough concentrations for long enough.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 7.2 — DAG Extension: Tick-Borne Pathogen Nodes in the ME/CFS Causal Diagram
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The paper's causal DAG currently models immune dysregulation, autonomic dysfunction, and metabolic failure. Adding explicit tick-borne pathogen nodes with directed edges to known downstream targets would allow the DAG to predict which intervention points should differ between tick-triggered and non-tick ME/CFS. New edges: Borrelia → autoantibodies (molecular mimicry, anchored in ch07), Bartonella → endothelial dysfunction → microclots (ch10 bridge), Babesia → hemolysis → peripheral serotonin (ch13 bridge), tick exposure → mast cell degranulation (ch14b bridge). The extended DAG creates testable predictions about which subsets benefit from anti-infective vs anti-inflammatory therapy.
- **Evidence link:** Existing DAG in paper (structure needs reading). Each edge anchored in an existing paper chapter.
- **Certainty:** 0.35 — DAG extension is straightforward; the value depends on the empirical support for each new edge.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The extended DAG predicts that anti-infective treatment affects only symptoms downstream of the tick-borne nodes, while anti-inflammatory treatment affects symptoms downstream of both tick-borne and non-tick immune nodes — providing a graphical rationale for stratified trials.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Adding tick infections to a computer model of ME/CFS could help predict which ME/CFS patients should respond to antibiotics versus anti-inflammatory treatments, guiding personalized treatment decisions.

### 7.3 — Compartment Model: Antigen Persistence → T-Cell Exhaustion Threshold → Clinical Relapse
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Multi-compartment model: (1) tissue antigen reservoir (Borrelia persisters, Bartonella intracellular), (2) circulating antigen/shed products, (3) T-cell activation pool, (4) exhausted T-cell pool (PD-1hi, TIM-3+). The model would estimate the antigen load threshold that tips the system from controlled activation into exhaustion-driven persistence, and whether reducing antigen below that threshold allows T-cell recovery.

- **Certainty:** 0.25 — available data sufficient for parameter estimation; model predictions falsifiable.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Model predicts that patients with CXCR5+ CD4+ naïve T cells below 4% of total CD4 (vs 8.3% in controls) have crossed the exhaustion threshold and will not respond to antimicrobial therapy alone — requiring immune restoration (e.g., checkpoint inhibitor or IL-7).
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A mathematical model could reveal a "point of no return" where the immune system becomes so exhausted that killing the infection is no longer enough — the immune cells need to be revived separately.

## Category 8: Cross-Disease Bridges

### 8.1 — Post-Lyme / Post-COVID / Post-EBV Shared Immunopathology Table
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The paper argues for shared post-infectious mechanisms. A systematic comparison table at the mechanism level (not just symptom level) would strengthen this. Columns: pathogen type (Borrelia/ECM bacteria, SARS-CoV-2/RNA virus, EBV/DNA virus), innate immune activation (TLR2, TLR4, TLR7/8, STING), Type I IFN signature, autoantibody profile, T-cell phenotype shifts, endothelial/microclot involvement, persistence mechanism, and response to immunomodulation. Each cell filled with citation-graded support (strong/moderate/weak/none). This would reveal both overlaps (≥2 pathogens share mechanism) and gaps (mechanism unique to one).
- **Evidence link:** All three in paper (ch07 for tick, Long COVID sections elsewhere, EBV sections). Self-evident cross-referencing gap.
- **Certainty:** 0.50 — compilable from existing literature; added value is the visualization of gaps.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** The table will reveal that IFN-γ-driven IDO activation (kynurenine) is shared across all three, while mast cell degranulation is shared only between tick and COVID (not EBV).
- **Non-specialist consequence:** A side-by-side comparison of how three different infections produce similar chronic symptoms would reveal which biological pathways are final common denominators — and therefore the best drug targets — versus pathways unique to each trigger.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 8.2 — Rickettsia and Ehrlichia as Overlooked ME/CFS Triggers
- **Mechanistic rationale:** The paper focuses on Borrelia/Bartonella/Babesia. But tick-borne rickettsiae (Rickettsia rickettsii, R. conorii) and Ehrlichia/Anaplasma also cause persistent symptoms. Obligate intracellular niche (in endothelial cells for Rickettsia, monocytes for Ehrlichia) means they evade humoral immunity and persist. Rickettsia cause long-term endothelial damage via direct infection (vasculitis) that could underpin the microclot/endothelial dysfunction described in ch10. No ME/CFS seroprevalence study exists for these.
- **Evidence link:** ch10 (microclots, endothelial dysfunction). Rickettsia endothelial tropism well-established. Tick panels often omit Rickettsia serology (testing gap).
- **Certainty:** 0.15 — plausible but entirely unstudied in ME/CFS.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** In ME/CFS patients from areas endemic for spotted fever rickettsiosis (southeastern US), seroprevalence of anti-Rickettsia IgG is higher than matched controls.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** Other tick-borne bacteria besides the "big three" (Lyme, Bartonella, Babesia) could trigger ME/CFS through blood vessel damage — expanding the list of tick tests that may be relevant.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 8.3 — Borrelia Biofilm as a Model System for ME/CFS Pathogen Persistence Research
- **Mechanistic rationale:** Borrelia forms well-characterized biofilm-like aggregates in vitro, in mouse models, and in human tissue (Sapi 2016, *J Transl Med*). Biofilm is the most mechanistically studied persistence form across all tick-borne pathogens. This can serve as a model system for studying persistence mechanisms that may generalize to ME/CFS: antibiotic tolerance within biofilms, immune evasion via matrix shielding, antigen shedding from biofilm, biofilm-triggered NETosis. Drugs that disrupt Borrelia biofilms (bismuth, DNase, dispersin B) are already identified in vitro and could be tested in ME/CFS.
- **Evidence link:** Biofilm mentioned in ch07 (one bullet). Sapi 2016 data. NETosis-biofilm interactions (link to 1.5 above).
- **Certainty:** 0.50 — Borrelia biofilm is well-studied; its use as a model system for ME/CFS is novel; translational barriers remain.
- **Falsifiable prediction:** Addition of anti-biofilm agents (DNase I, bismuth subcitrate) enhances antibiotic killing of Borrelia persisters in vitro by ≥2 log compared to antibiotic alone.
- **Non-specialist consequence:** The slime layers that Borrelia bacteria build to hide from antibiotics could serve as a laboratory model to screen drugs that might also work against hidden chronic infections in ME/CFS generally.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 10: Reasons This Mechanism May NOT Be Relevant to ME/CFS

### 10.1 — CDC Claims Data (Nawrocki2025) Shows Modest Population-Level Excess
- **Argument:** The CDC analysis of 24,503 Lyme patients found that excess symptom coding declined to control levels by 6-12 months for most domains, with fatigue being the only persistent domain. The absolute excess risk was ~5%, meaning 95% of Lyme patients have the same symptom coding rate as controls within a year. At population level, Lyme disease does NOT cause disproportionate chronic illness — the ~5% excess is dwarfed by other etiologies.
- **What would disprove:** A well-controlled prospective cohort study showing >10% of confirmed, treated Lyme patients develop ME/CFS by validated diagnostic criteria (not just ICD fatigue codes).
- **Alternative explanation:** The 26/29 symptom overlap between PTLDS and ME/CFS reflects shared symptomatic description of post-infectious malaise (fatigue, sleep disturbance, pain are generic responses to any infection), not shared pathophysiology. PTLDS patients may meet symptom count criteria for ME/CFS without sharing the same mechanism — similar to how depression and hypothyroidism share fatigue as a symptom but through different pathways.
- **Certainty:** 0.25 — the CDC data is real and population-scale, but claims data undercounts milder cases and undiagnosed Lyme; the 10-20% PTLDS figure from clinical cohorts may better capture symptomatic burden.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 10.2 — Bartonella Detection in Healthy Controls Remains the Critical Unknown
- **Argument:** The Bartonella-ME/CFS link (26% positivity, Breitschwerdt) cannot be interpreted without knowing healthy control seroprevalence from the same geographic region. If Bartonella DNA is detectable in 15-20% of the general population in tick-endemic areas (possible, given Bartonella seroprevalence of 5-15% in asymptomatic US blood donors), then the 26% rate in ME/CFS may represent background exposure, not disease association.
- **What would disprove:** Controlled study using the same BAPGM platform showing Bartonella PCR rate significantly higher in ME/CFS than matched controls with same geographic exposure, season, and outdoor activity level.
- **Alternative explanation:** Bartonella is an incidentally detected commensal in an endemic area — like EBV seropositivity found in 90% of adults, clinically irrelevant for the majority. Even if Bartonella DNA is detectable, it may not be driving symptoms unless accompanied by serological or functional evidence of active infection.
- **Certainty:** 0.50 — this is the single biggest threat to the Bartonella-ME/CFS hypothesis. The paper already flags this (obs:bartonella-cfs).
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 10.3 — Zero 2024-2026 Babesia-ME/CFS Studies Suggests Absence of Signal
- **Argument:** Babesia causes acute hemolytic illness with fever, anemia, hemoglobinuria. If chronic Babesia were a common ME/CFS contributor, it would be expected to produce detectable hematological abnormalities (anemia, reticulocytosis, elevated LDH, splenomegaly) that would have been documented in ME/CFS cohorts. Standard ME/CFS clinical labs routinely measure CBC, LDH, haptoglobin — yet Babesia-associated abnormalities are not reported as common in the ME/CFS literature.
- **What would disprove:** Controlled Babesia serosurvey showing elevated prevalence in ME/CFS AND correlation with hemolytic lab abnormalities.
- **Alternative explanation:** Babesia-associated chronic fatigue is genuinely uncommon. When patients have persistent symptoms after Babesia infection, these symptoms are driven by co-infecting Borrelia (50% co-infection rate), not by Babesia itself. The Krause1996babesia finding of "more severe illness" may reflect additive burden of two infections rather than Babesia-specific chronicity.
- **Certainty:** 0.35 — absence of evidence is not evidence of absence, but Babesia is well-studied in transfusion medicine and would be expected to show signal if present.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 10.4 — HLA Weak-Binding Hypothesis (Georgopoulos2025) Is In-Silico Only
- **Argument:** The Georgopoulos2025 finding that ME/CFS-susceptibility HLA alleles weakly bind Borrelia antigens is based purely on in-silico prediction algorithms. The paper itself notes this limitation (certainty 0.30, #spec:hla-shared-susceptibility). If wet-lab validation shows the binding predictions don't hold, the entire "shared genetic susceptibility to post-infectious illness" framework for tick-borne infections loses its genetic anchor — and the cross-trigger coherence (Lyme → same HLA as COVID → same as EBV) would be an artifact of the prediction algorithm.
- **What would disprove:** Wet-lab HLA-peptide binding assays (competition binding, ELISA, or cellular presentation assay) confirming the in-silico predictions for C*07:04/B*08:01/DPB1*02:01 with Borrelia outer surface proteins.
- **Alternative explanation:** The clinical overlap between PTLDS and ME/CFS is driven by convergent symptom expression from divergent mechanisms. Both conditions cause fatigue — but PTLDS fatigue may be driven by persistent antigen, while viral-onset ME/CFS fatigue may be driven by autoantibodies. The symptom similarity is a diagnostic trap, not a mechanistic insight.
- **Certainty:** 0.30 — in-silico predictions are hypothesis-generating, not hypothesis-confirming, for this topic.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 11: Null Hypothesis Assessment (What If Tick-Borne Infections Have No Role?)

### 11.1 — If tick-borne infections have NO causal or contributory role in ME/CFS:
- **ch07 tick-borne section:** Would need major reduction — entire sec-06-tick-borne-infections would shift from "potential trigger/contributor" to "historically important differential diagnosis with symptom overlap but no demonstrated etiological connection." The 26/29 symptom overlap would be reinterpreted as false concordance from generic post-infection symptom lists.
- **ch35 PTLDS shared mechanisms section:** Would remain valid as a comparative model (PTLDS is a real entity), but its translational utility for ME/CFS would substantially diminish. The shared-mechanisms checklist (autoimmunity, neuroinflammation, autonomic dysfunction, SFN, PEM) would be reframed as parallel but independent pathophysiology.
- **Georgopoulos2025 HLA hypothesis:** Would require reinterpretation as a false positive prediction. Weak HLA binding to Borrelia antigens would not translate to clinical susceptibility — the in-silico algorithm would have failed to predict functional immune outcomes.
- **Bartonella DNA findings:** Would need to be explained as detection of background environmental contamination or specimen handling artifact — a significant methodological revision that would require commentary.
- **Treatment implications:** The approximately 10-20% of ME/CFS patients currently pursuing tick-focused treatment (antimicrobial therapy, LLMD consultation) would be doing so based on a mistaken premise, requiring clinical guideline corrections.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 11.2 — If Babesia specifically has no role (but Borrelia/Bartonella do):
- **Current paper impact:** Minimal. The gap is already identified (lim:babesia-evidence-gap). The paper explicitly states Babesia-ME/CFS rests on "the most inferential evidence." No claim needs retraction.
- **Benefit to field:** Clarifies treatment focus — patients and clinicians would stop pursuing anti-Babesia therapy for ME/CFS unless independently documented. Saves resources and avoids atovaquone/azithromycin side effects.
- **Remaining question:** Would need to explain why co-infected (Lyme+Babesia) patients seem to have worse outcomes — likely because of the additive burden of dual infection producing greater initial immune activation, not because Babesia independently produces chronicity.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 11.3 — If the tick→ME/CFS link operates through antibiotic-associated microbiome disruption rather than direct pathogen effects:
- **Mechanism swap:** The alternative hypothesis is that antibiotics used to treat acute Lyme (doxycycline, amoxicillin, cefuroxime) cause persistent gut dysbiosis → disrupted microbial metabolite production (SCFAs, bile acids, tryptophan metabolites) → immune dysregulation and metabolic dysfunction that mimics ME/CFS. In this model, the tick infection is a confound — it's the treatment, not the pathogen, that matters.
- **Evidence for this alternative:** Doxycycline is a broad-spectrum antibiotic; its microbiome effects persist for months to years. Gut dysbiosis is documented in ME/CFS. Microbial metabolite disruption can cause fatigue and cognitive dysfunction through well-established pathways. This alternative does NOT require Borrelia persistence, antigenic stimulation, or specialized tick immunology — it's simpler.
- **What would differentiate:** A study comparing untreated tick-exposed individuals (seropositive but never treated) vs treated Lyme patients vs ME/CFS patients would disentangle infection effects from treatment effects. If untreated seropositive individuals have normal gut microbiome and normal fatigue scores, the microbiome-alternative gains support.
- **Current paper sections affected:** The microbiome disruption bullet in ch07 (one bullet) would need to become a major alternative hypothesis, possibly a dedicated subsection. The Borrelia persistence and molecular mimicry sections would be de-emphasized.
- **origin:** brainstorm

## Category 12: Evidence Quality Concerns

### 12.1 — Single-Lab Dependency for Bartonella Data (Breitschwerdt)
- **Nature:** All positive Bartonella detection in ME/CFS comes from one research group (Breitschwerdt lab at NCSU) using one proprietary diagnostic platform (BAPGM enrichment culture, commercialized as Galaxy Diagnostics). No independent replication exists from an unaffiliated lab. The BAPGM method involves 2-3 weeks of liquid culture enrichment followed by PCR — this is not a standard diagnostic and has not been validated against any gold standard (none exists).
- **Risk:** Unknown false-positive rate. Environmental contamination during extended culture is a recognized risk. The single-lab dependency means the 26% figure could reflect a lab-specific artifact (cross-contamination, reagent contamination, PCR carryover).
- **Mitigation:** Independent replication is Category 9.3 above. Until done, the Bartonella-ME/CFS link must carry a "single source" caveat.
- **Certainty impact:** The paper's Bartonella section should explicitly note this dependency (partially already done in obs:bartonella-cfs).
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 12.2 — CDC Claims Data (Nawrocki2025): Coding vs Clinical Reality
- **Nature:** The CDC study uses ICD-10-CM codes from a large US claims database. Fatigue (R53.83) and malaise/fatigue (R53.81) codes are non-specific. A person who feels "more tired than usual" for 2 weeks after Lyme gets the same code as someone with full-criteria ME/CFS. The study cannot distinguish post-Lyme symptoms meeting ME/CFS diagnostic criteria from generic post-illness tiredness.
- **Additional concern:** The analysis captures only patients with coded Lyme disease. Undiagnosed Lyme — the population most relevant to ME/CFS (where tick exposure is unrecognized) — is invisible in claims data. The study may underestimate the tick-ME/CFS link by only studying diagnosed, treated Lyme patients and missing post-infection ME/CFS where the tick connection was never identified.
- **Mitigation:** The paper's obs:lyme-fatigue-persistence already addresses the denominator issue (claims vs clinical PTLDS). The undiagnosed-Lyme gap remains.
- **Certainty impact:** Nawrocki2025 is useful for population-level reassurance but cannot rule out a tick-ME/CFS link in undiagnosed populations.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 12.3 — Girgis2025 T-Cell Immunophenotyping: Design Vulnerabilities
- **Nature:** Cross-sectional design (single time point) cannot distinguish cause from consequence or confound. The T-cell subset differences in PTLDS may be: (a) cause of chronic symptoms, (b) consequence of chronic fatigue (immune changes from inactivity), (c) result of a common cause (persistent antigen driving both T-cell changes AND symptoms), or (d) a pre-existing difference that predicts PTLDS development.
- **Control group size:** 28 controls vs 272 patients. The small control group reduces statistical power for between-group comparisons and increases the risk of inflated effect sizes (winner's curse). Elastic-net classifier AUC 0.83 is from a training sample with disproportionate group sizes — not externally validated.
- **Subgroup analysis:** Female-specific central-memory CD8 fatigue correlation from an unadjusted subgroup analysis without correction for multiple comparisons. This is hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory.
- **Mitigation:** Require external validation cohort, age/sex balance, and prospective sampling (acute Lyme → chronic vs recovered) to establish temporal direction.
- **Certainty impact:** Girgis2025 provides important descriptive data but no causal evidence. Paper's certainty of 0.55 is appropriate.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 12.4 — Berkson Bias in PTLDS Literature
- **Nature:** Most PTLDS studies enroll from tertiary referral Lyme specialty clinics. Patients attending multiple specialists (infectious disease → neurology → rheumatology → fatigue clinic) are systematically more severe, more complex, and have higher psychiatric comorbidity than community patients with PTLDS who manage without specialist referral. This bias inflates the apparent severity and complexity of PTLDS.
- **Consequence for cross-comparison:** If PTLDS cohorts are systematically sicker than community ME/CFS cohorts (which include mild cases), the symptom overlap comparison may find more symptoms in PTLDS simply because the cohort is selected for severity.
- **Relevance:** The 26/29 symptom overlap figure (PTLDSMECFSReview2023) may partially reflect this — both groups are from referral centers.
- **Mitigation:** Population-based PTLDS cohort studies with minimal referral filter (e.g., Lyme registry-based sampling).
- **Certainty impact:** The 26/29 overlap statistic is likely an overestimate of true population-level overlap.
- **origin:** brainstorm

### 12.5 — VlsE and Epitope Diagnostics (Nair2025, Marques2026): Preliminary Status
- **Nature:** Both are small, single-center, proof-of-concept studies. Nair2025 VlsE assay (n=number? need to check paper) developed and tested within one research lab. Marques2026 CSF epitope profiling involved highly specialized techniques (CSF antibody capture using peptide microarrays) that are not clinically scalable. Neither has been independently validated in a multi-center cohort.
- **Circularity risk:** Marques2026's discrimination between PTLDS and resolved Lyme depends on the clinical definition of PTLDS. If the clinical definition includes patients whose symptoms have non-Borrelia causes (Berkson bias, see 12.4), the epitope profile may be learning to detect a clinical label with heterogeneous biology, not a biological entity.
- **Mitigation:** Multi-center validation with standardized PTLDS case definitions, blinded to clinical data. Addition of symptom-based subgroups within PTLDS.
- **Certainty impact:** Paper's optimism about VlsE/epitope diagnostics (ch07 symptom-domain findings) should be tempered with these replication caveats.
- **origin:** brainstorm
