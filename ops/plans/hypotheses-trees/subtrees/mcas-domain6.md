### 1. Your Allergy Tests Are Negative — But Your Mast Cells Aren't Listening

**Source:** Ch 14d, §14d.5.1 (non-IgE degranulation pathways)

**Core argument:** Standard allergy testing (skin prick, specific IgE) detects one pathway out of at least five. Four parallel non-IgE pathways can trigger mast cell degranulation from food without producing a single positive allergy test. A negative allergy workup does not rule out food-triggered mast cell activation — it rules out one mechanism while ignoring four others.

**Key content:**
- The four invisible pathways: MRGPRX2 (gut neuropeptides), complement C3a/C5a (food lectins), lectin–IgE glycan cross-linking (legumes/nightshades), oxidative (sulfites → NLRP3)
- Why each pathway is invisible to standard allergy testing
- The clinical implication: "not allergic" ≠ "not reacting"
- MRGPRX2 gain-of-function variants explaining individual susceptibility differences

**Audience hook:** Every ME/CFS patient who has been told "your tests are all normal, it's not food" when they can clearly feel food making them worse.

### 2. The Silent Symptom Driver: How Food Triggers Fatigue Without an Allergic Reaction

**Source:** Ch 14d, §14d.5.3 (mediator-symptom mapping); Kohno 2021, Rohrhofer 2025

**Core argument:** Mast cell mediators map directly onto core ME/CFS symptoms — but not the symptoms you'd associate with "allergy." No hives, no swelling, no anaphylaxis. Instead: fatigue (IL-1β/IL-6 sickness behavior), brain fog (histamine suppressing acetylcholine via H3), tachycardia (prostaglandin-driven vasodilation), and gut distress. These look like ME/CFS, not like allergy — which is why they're missed.

**Key content:**
- Mediator → symptom mapping table (histamine → brain fog; prostaglandins → POTS; cytokines → fatigue; tryptase → neuroinflammation)
- The Kohno finding: in POTS patients with mast cell activation, tryptase elevated in only 9% — but histamine in 52% and PGD₂ in 36%
- Rohrhofer 2025: ME/CFS patients with MCAS have 89% orthostatic intolerance vs. 72% without
- Weinstock 2023: brain fog resolved with mast cell treatment in all 8 patients (case series)
- Why clinicians trained to look for "allergic" symptoms miss mast-cell-driven "ME/CFS" symptoms

**Audience hook:** "What if some of your worst ME/CFS symptoms are being driven by what you ate — and nobody's looking because it doesn't look like allergy?"

### 3. Testing for Food Reactions Your Doctor Can't See

**Source:** Ch 20, §20.5.4 (urinary mediator panel); Voelker 2024/2025; Ch 19, §19.2.2 (protocol)

**Core argument:** If standard allergy tests miss non-IgE mast cell activation, what tests *can* detect it? Urinary mast cell mediators — collected at home, no venipuncture, 5-hour collection window — can identify mast cell activation that serum tryptase misses. A paired baseline-vs-challenge protocol turns this into a food-specific diagnostic tool.

**Key content:**
- The three-mediator urinary panel: N-methylhistamine (≥1.29× baseline), leukotriene E4 (≥1.36×), PGD₂ metabolite (≥1.31×)
- Why paired measurement (baseline vs. post-challenge) beats absolute values
- Why tryptase alone is insufficient (misses >90% of food-related activation)
- Hereditary alpha-tryptasemia as a diagnostic confound (5–6% of population)
- The practical protocol: collect baseline urine on a neutral day, eat the suspect food, collect urine 4–6 hours later
- Commercially available through reference labs — no specialist referral required for the test itself

**Audience hook:** Actionable diagnostic information that patients can discuss with their physicians. Empowering without being reckless.

### 4. The Leaky Gut–Mast Cell Vicious Cycle

**Source:** Ch 14d, §14d.5.2 (amplifying factors); Rohrhofer 2024, Folkerts 2018

**Core argument:** ME/CFS creates the perfect conditions for food-triggered mast cell activation — and mast cell activation worsens the conditions that enable it. Intestinal barrier dysfunction lets food antigens reach submucosal mast cells. Gut dysbiosis reduces SCFA production, removing a natural brake on mast cell degranulation. Mast cell mediators damage the gut barrier further. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle that doesn't exist in healthy people — explaining why ME/CFS patients react to foods they previously tolerated.

**Key content:**
- Elevated LBP in ME/CFS = bacterial endotoxin translocation across leaky gut (Rohrhofer 2024, n=39)
- LPS primes mast cells for lower-threshold degranulation on the next food challenge
- SCFAs (butyrate, propionate) actively inhibit mast cell degranulation via JNK suppression and HDAC inhibition (Folkerts 2018)
- The fiber paradox: patients who restrict fiber due to GI sensitivity reduce SCFA production, paradoxically increasing mast cell reactivity
- The cycle: leaky gut → more antigen exposure → more mast cell activation → more gut damage → leakier gut
- The open question: could targeted prebiotic/SCFA supplementation break the cycle?

**Audience hook:** "Why do I react to foods I used to eat without problems?" — directly addresses the acquired food sensitivity experience common in ME/CFS.

### 5. An Elimination Diet You Can Actually Do From Bed

**Source:** Ch 19, §19.2.2.3 (energy-adapted elimination-rechallenge protocol)

**Core argument:** Standard elimination diets assume you have the energy to cook, shop, plan meals, and track symptoms in detail. ME/CFS patients don't. A modified protocol — designed around energy limitations — makes food trigger identification feasible even for moderate-to-severe patients. The key shift: instead of eliminating everything and adding back, establish safe "anchor foods" first (crowd-in strategy), then test suspects one at a time with minimal cognitive and physical overhead.

**Key content:**
- The 6-step energy-adapted protocol:
  1. Establish 5–8 anchor foods first (crowd-in, not eliminate-everything)
  2. Batch-prepare meals during better-energy periods
  3. Tick-box symptom forms instead of written diaries
  4. Challenges timed to best energy window, seated/reclined, 2-hour rest post-challenge
  5. Optional urinary mediator confirmation (home collection)
  6. One new food every 5–7 days (not the standard 3–5)
- Why 6 weeks is the stopping point — if no improvement by then, stop rather than restrict further
- The energy cost of the protocol itself must be weighed against potential benefit
- The ~51% response rate in MCAS patients (no RCT; patient survey data)
- The limitation: no RCT has tested this in ME/CFS specifically

**Audience hook:** The most directly practical and shareable article. Patient communities will circulate this if it's well-written and honest about limitations.
