## Integration Context

**Priority:** 🟡
**Tier:** (if applicable)
**ME/CFS links:**
- Energy-before-mood paradox: restored energy precedes mood improvement during stimulant/antidepressant treatment, creating a high-risk window (weeks 1–3) with elevated suicide risk — especially in adolescents
- Herxheimer reactions: temporary worsening during antimicrobial treatment interpreted as treatment success; distinct from adverse effects; requires specific management protocol and pediatric cautions
- Dose-dependent benefit curves: U-shaped dose-response in ME/CFS medications; "start low, go slow" protocol has mechanistic basis in altered pharmacokinetics/sensitivity
- Therapeutic lag: benefits appear weeks to months after initiation; medication class × expected lag time varies systematically
- Withdrawal effects: discontinuation syndromes require tapering protocols; risk varies by medication class
- Pediatric pharmacokinetics: developmental differences alter dosing, timing, and safety profiles

**Target chapters:** `ch14a-urgent-action-severe.typ`, `ch14b-action-mild-moderate.typ`, `ch14c-pediatric-severe.typ`, `ch14d-pediatric-ambulatory.typ`, `ch15-medications-systems.typ`, `ch16-supplements-nutraceuticals.typ`
**Pre-identified hypotheses:**
- Energy-before-mood paradox mechanism (critical safety content)
- Herxheimer/die-off reaction as evidence of microbial burden in ME/CFS
- Dose sensitivity in ME/CFS as consequence of mitochondrial/autonomic dysfunction altering drug metabolism
**Research questions:**
1. What is the systematic framework for distinguishing transient vs. persistent adverse effects across medication classes?
2. What are the decision criteria for continuing vs. stopping treatment during the therapeutic lag period?
3. How do pediatric pharmacokinetic differences alter optimal dosing, timing, and safety thresholds for ME/CFS medications?
4. What are the mechanisms of Herxheimer reactions in ME/CFS, and how do they differ from adverse effects?
5. Can dose-response curves in ME/CFS be predicted from baseline autonomic or mitochondrial markers?
