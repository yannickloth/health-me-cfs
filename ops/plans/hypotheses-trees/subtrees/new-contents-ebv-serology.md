## Integration Context

**Priority:** 🟠  
**Tier:** 2  
**ME/CFS links:**
- EBV reactivation is documented in ME/CFS but serological patterns distinguishing patients from healthy seropositive individuals are not well-characterized
- Absent or low EBNA-1 IgG (normally present in all seropositive individuals) suggests atypical immune control or reactivation pattern in ME/CFS
- VCA IgM persistence beyond normal timeframe (weeks) indicates ongoing or recurrent lytic cycle activation
- EA-D IgG elevation marks active lytic EBV replication; Lerner's work identified a clinical subgroup with lytic reactivation evidence and cardiac involvement
- EBV-specific T cell exhaustion (reduced CD8+ T cells, functional impairment via PD-1/TIM-3) impairs latency control
- EBER tissue detection (EBV-encoded RNA in situ hybridisation) provides evidence of ongoing viral activity in tissue

**Pre-identified hypotheses:**
- "Ongoing EBV Lytic Reactivation in a ME/CFS Subgroup Identified by Serological Pattern" (cert 0.45)
  - Core: ME/CFS subgroup with elevated EA-D IgG, absent/low EBNA-1 IgG, persistently elevated VCA IgM has evidence of ongoing lytic cycle reactivation that maintains immune dysregulation
  - Testable: EA-D IgG elevation correlates with higher plasma cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF-α); EBV-specific CD8+ T cells lower in EA-D-positive vs negative patients; valacyclovir reduces EA-D titres in EA-D-positive patients

**Research questions:**
1. Do ME/CFS patients with elevated EA-D IgG show higher plasma cytokine levels than EA-D-negative patients, consistent with ongoing lytic antigen-driven immune activation?
2. Is EBV-specific CD8+ T cell frequency (by EBV-peptide tetramers) lower in EA-D-positive ME/CFS patients than in EA-D-negative patients and healthy controls?
3. Can antiviral therapy targeting EBV lytic replication reduce EA-D IgG titres over 6 months in EA-D-positive ME/CFS patients?
4. Does EBER in situ hybridisation on biopsies from EA-D-positive ME/CFS patients show positive signal in tonsil/lymph node tissue more frequently than in healthy controls?
5. What fraction of ME/CFS patients fall into the "lytic reactivation subgroup" and do they differ clinically in cardiac or neurological involvement?
