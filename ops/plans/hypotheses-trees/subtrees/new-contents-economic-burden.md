## Integration Context

**Priority:** 🟠  
**Tier:** 2  
**ME/CFS links:**
- Pre-COVID US economic burden: $18–51 billion annually (Jason et al.), with productivity losses dominant
- Post-COVID projections: $149–362 billion annually due to Long COVID-derived ME/CFS cases, a ~3–7× increase
- 75% of ME/CFS patients unable to work full-time; productivity losses represent the largest cost component
- Healthcare utilisation costs inflated by diagnostic odyssey (average 5–10 years to diagnosis, multiple specialist visits)
- Disability benefit system costs substantial; ME/CFS undercounted in disability statistics due to diagnostic challenges
- ME/CFS economic burden per patient substantially higher than other chronic diseases (MS, lupus) and dramatically underfunded relative to disease burden (NIH funding vs comparable conditions)
- Economic data critical for research funding advocacy and policy discussion; illustrates gap between patient burden and research resources

**Pre-identified hypotheses:**
- Post-COVID ME/CFS economic projections (cert 0.60 for point estimate, 0.70 for baseline pre-COVID)
  - Core: Long COVID incidence and ME/CFS transition rates drive economic burden projections; wide range reflects uncertainty in these transition rates
  - Evidence: published economic modelling studies; Long COVID prevalence estimates; ME/CFS incidence assumptions

**Research questions:**
1. What is the most accurate estimate of Long COVID incidence and the fraction progressing to ME/CFS-like illness?
2. Do post-COVID ME/CFS cases differ in economic burden (healthcare costs, disability duration) compared to virus-triggered ME/CFS cases?
3. How does the diagnostic delay (average 5–10 years) contribute to total economic cost through unnecessary tests, procedures, and productivity loss accumulation?
4. What is the per-patient annual cost to disability benefit systems (SSA/DWP) compared to other chronic diseases of similar prevalence?
5. What is the optimal research funding level (per patient-year) to match ME/CFS economic burden and research needs, compared to comparable disease areas?
