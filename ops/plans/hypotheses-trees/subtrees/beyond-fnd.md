## Integration Context

**Priority:** 🔴 Critical
**Category:** 2 — Neurological and psychiatric conditions

**ME/CFS links:**
- Most important differential/comorbidity after autoimmune diseases
- Central sensitization; brain-body disconnect mechanisms shared but FND is NOT the same as ME/CFS
- Both can occur together; comorbidity prevalence data needed
- FND has treatable component (physiotherapy, psychotherapy) while ME/CFS does not respond to same — differential treatment response is key
- Politically sensitive: conflating FND with ME/CFS has caused patient harm historically

**Target chapters:** ch04-08-differential-diagnosis, ch24-controversies

**Pre-identified hypotheses:** (critical differential, not a ME/CFS mechanism per se)

**Research questions:**
1. What biomarkers distinguish FND from ME/CFS? (objective markers, not symptom report)
2. What is the comorbidity prevalence of FND + ME/CFS in large cohorts?
3. Does FND with fatigue meet ME/CFS criteria — and if so, is this a classification problem?
4. Does physiotherapy or psychotherapy improve FND-comorbid ME/CFS or worsen PEM?
5. What neuroimaging findings distinguish FND (motor cortex hyperactivation) from ME/CFS (hypoperfusion, neuroinflammation)?
6. How should the paper frame FND to be scientifically accurate without political misuse?

**Note:** Handle with precision — any framing that implies ME/CFS is FND is scientifically incorrect and clinically harmful. The differential diagnosis section must be unambiguous.
