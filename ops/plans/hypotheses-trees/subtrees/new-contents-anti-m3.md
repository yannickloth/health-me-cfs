## Integration Context

**Priority:** 🟠  
**Tier:** 2  
**ME/CFS links:**
- Reduced accommodation is prevalent (72–88% of ME/CFS patients) and causes premature presbyopia-like symptoms and day-to-day vision variability
- ME/CFS patients have elevated anti-M3 muscarinic autoantibodies (53–91% prevalence across studies)
- M3 receptors are essential mediators of ciliary muscle contraction; anti-M3 autoantibodies can block this mechanism
- Accommodation dysfunction is energy-dependent; ciliary muscle ATP depletion from mitochondrial dysfunction contributes
- Reduced ocular blood flow (part of broader cerebral hypoperfusion pattern) further impairs ciliary function

**Pre-identified hypotheses:**
- "Anti-M3 Autoantibody-Mediated Accommodation Dysfunction" (cert 0.55–0.60)
  - Core: elevated anti-M3 autoantibodies block muscarinic receptors on ciliary muscle, producing functional accommodation insufficiency
  - Testable: anti-M3 titers correlate inversely with accommodation amplitude; pilocarpine (M3 agonist) partially restores accommodation

**Research questions:**
1. Do anti-M3 antibody titres correlate inversely with accommodation amplitude in ME/CFS patients?
2. Can pilocarpine eye drops partially restore accommodation in anti-M3-positive ME/CFS patients?
3. Are the four converging pathways (M3 blockade, autonomic dysfunction, ciliary energy deficit, reduced ocular blood flow) all present in individual patients or does one predominate?
4. Can accommodation deficits track with energy levels and exertion as predicted by the mitochondrial dysfunction model?
5. Is there an endotype of ME/CFS with predominantly ocular M3-mediated symptoms versus other autonomic targets?
