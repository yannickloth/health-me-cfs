## Integration Context

**Priority:** 🟠  
**Tier:** 2  
**ME/CFS links:**
- 3–4:1 female predominance is a biological signal, not a demographic quirk; reflects sex-dependent metabolomics and hormonal immune modulation
- Sex-dependent metabolomic signatures: glutathione pathway abnormalities more prominent in women, plasmalogen abnormalities in men
- Estrogen activates B cells and promotes Th2 skewing, driving autoimmunity risk in reproductive-age women
- X-linked immune gene dosage effects (TLR7, immune genes escaping X inactivation) create sex-specific B cell hyperactivation risk
- Menopause represents a critical disease transition point; pregnancy effects on ME/CFS course poorly studied but clinically reported

**Pre-identified hypotheses:**
- "Estrogen-Driven B Cell Hyperactivation as a Sex-Specific ME/CFS Mechanism" (cert 0.55)
  - Core: estrogen-mediated TLR7 upregulation and B cell activation creates sex-specific autoimmunity risk, explaining 3–4:1 female predominance
  - Testable: anti-GPCR autoantibodies higher in females; TLR7 expression on B cells elevated in female ME/CFS; menopausal transition shows immune profile shift

**Research questions:**
1. Are anti-GPCR and other ME/CFS autoantibody titres significantly higher in females vs males after disease-duration adjustment?
2. Does TLR7 expression on B cells correlate with B cell activation markers, and is this sex-dependent?
3. How do female ME/CFS patients in peri/post-menopausal transition differ immunologically from pre-menopausal women at equivalent disease duration?
4. Is the glutathione pathway deficit more pronounced in females and plasmalogen abnormalities in males as emerging data suggest?
5. Do male and female ME/CFS patients represent distinct endotypes with different treatment targets?
