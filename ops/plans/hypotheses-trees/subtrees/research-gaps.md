**ME/CFS links:**
- Symptom-producing mechanisms (10% documented): adenosine-mediated sleep pressure, inflammatory cytokine somnolence (IL-6/TNF-α/IL-1β), serotonin dysregulation, microglia activation and neuroinflammatory fatigue, and the integrated cascade maintaining "locked sleepy" state
- Cascade and feedback dynamics (30% documented): sleep-immune-adenosine cascade, energy-autonomic-vascular cascade, infection-HPA-immune cascade, metabolic stress-barrier-immune cascade, circadian disruption-immune cascade
- Heterogeneity explanation (0% documented): immune-dominant vs. autonomic-dominant phenotypes, severity spectrum mechanisms, sex differences, age-of-onset effects, treatment response heterogeneity, comorbidity variation
- Mitochondrial communication systems (15% documented): purinergic signaling (P2X7, P2Y1), DAMPs (mtDNA via TLR9, cardiolipin via TLR4), mitochondrial-derived peptides (humanin, MOTS-c), exosomal signaling, ROS signaling and redox biology
- Paradox resolution framework (0% documented): stimulant paradox, activity harm paradox, recovery paradox, pacing paradox, viral trigger paradox, cognitive-autonomic dissociation paradox
- Cellular stress response paradoxes (5% documented): absent robust heat shock response, insufficient compensatory metabolism, innate immune failure to clear triggers, absent mitochondrial quality control activation

**Pre-identified hypotheses:**
- Adenosine accumulation as primary fatigue-signaling mechanism (Category A)
- Integrated symptom cascade: Adenosine + Cytokines + Melatonin + Serotonin (Category A)
- mtDNA DAMP release as driver of chronic innate immune activation (Category I)
- Reduced mitochondrial-derived peptide production impairing systemic stress responses (Category I)
- Altered exosomal content driving systemic immune dysregulation (Category I)
- Epigenetic "memory" preventing return to pre-disease state (Category L)
**Research questions:**
1. Which symptom-producing mechanisms (adenosine, cytokines, serotonin, microglia) dominate in which patient phenotypes?
2. What governs the switch between hyperarousal and torpor-like fatigue states?
3. What genetic and environmental factors determine immune-dominant vs. autonomic-dominant phenotypes?
4. How does mitochondrial dysfunction communicate systemically via purinergic signaling and DAMPs?
5. Why do cellular stress responses (heat shock, compensatory glycolysis, mitophagy) appear insufficient in ME/CFS?
6. What epigenetic changes sustain disease mechanisms after the acute trigger is resolved?
7. Which paradoxes (stimulant efficacy, pacing benefit, viral trigger selectivity) can be mechanistically resolved with existing evidence?
