If you chronically don't sleep enough, your body accumulates inflammation over time, and that inflammation progressively damages your health. A 2016 meta-analysis in Biological Psychiatry (Irwin et al.), covering dozens of cohort studies and sleep deprivation experiments, makes this unambiguous: people who consistently sleep too little have elevated inflammatory markers, and those markers stay elevated as long as the sleep deficit persists.

The reason is that sleep is when your body cleans up and repairs itself. During deep sleep, the brain flushes out the waste it produced during the day, cells repair their energy-producing machinery, and the immune system resets. When you don't sleep enough, this maintenance doesn't finish, so the waste stays, inflammation builds up, and your body slowly deteriorates. On top of that, the inflammation itself damages the systems that produce energy, which makes it harder to sleep well the next night, which means even less repair happens, and so on.

This applies to everyone, adults and children alike, whether they notice it yet or not. Teenagers are probably the most vulnerable because their bodies need enormous amounts of energy for growth, brain development, and hormonal changes, and that energy budget depends on adequate deep sleep. Over years, the consequences of this accumulation show up as cardiovascular disease, diabetes, cognitive decline, or weakened immunity, depending on the person.

A study that followed 14,000 children from birth (Collin 2018, Sleep Medicine) found that those who later developed the chronic energy failure disease ME/CFS had measurably worse sleep — shorter nights, later bedtimes, more awakenings — starting 4 to 7 years before anyone noticed they were sick. The data was collected in real time by researchers who didn't know which children would later become ill. That's an extreme outcome where the body's energy reserve runs out completely, but the damage mechanism is the same for everyone who chronically undersleeps; how far it goes depends on how much reserve you had to begin with and what hits you along the way.

Sleeping enough is how your body maintains itself, and chronically skipping that maintenance has real, cumulative, measurable consequences.
