This chapter turns "failed" medication trials into diagnostic tests. Here's why.

Every ME/CFS patient has a drawer of medications they tried that didn't work. The antidepressant that made things worse. The stimulant that cleared the fog but caused a crash. The beta-blocker that slowed the heart but turned fatigue into collapse. We treat these as failures. Chapter 30 argues every one of those trials was a diagnostic test you already paid for in symptoms and time, and the results were never read.

For every major hypothesis about what causes ME/CFS, the chapter traces the full cascade from trigger to symptom. At each step: which medication could intercept, what a positive response means, and what a null response means. Null responses are data. If LDN doesn't help, your bottleneck is probably not TLR4-mediated neuroinflammation or TRPM3 channel dysfunction. If pyridostigmine doesn't improve your HRV, your vagal efferent pathway is non-functional. Every null pushes the bottleneck downstream and eliminates a class of upstream mechanisms.

Then there are side effects. Six diagnostic patterns: exaggerated effect (midodrine hypertension at 2.5 mg → denervation hypersensitivity confirming neuropathic POTS), paradoxical reaction (fludrocortisone worsening orthostasis → the volume went to your legs, not your heart — venous pooling, not hypovolemic POTS), unexpected off-target effect (aripiprazole akathisia at a microdose → D2/D3 supersensitivity confirming severe dopamine deficiency), tolerability ceiling (guanfacine helps cognition but crashes BP → sympathetically dependent; add midodrine and it becomes accessible), absence of an expected effect (no goosebumps from midodrine → cutaneous sympathetic denervation — small-fiber neuropathy; antihistamines don't make you drowsy → CNS histamine downregulation from chronic mast cell activation), and dissociated response (guanfacine improves brain fog but worsens fatigue → brain fog and fatigue are different mechanisms).

Here's what reframes everything for patients: a null is *more* informative than a positive. A positive LDN says "neuroinflammation, TRPM3, endorphin, or orexin." A null LDN says "none of these four." Your drawer of failed medications is a set of assays you already underwent. The beta-blocker that wrecked your energy told you your metabolism depends on β2-AR fatty acid oxidation — your glucose-to-ATP pathway is compromised. Every trial gave you information. The chapter is the decoder.

All medications discussed are mechanistic inference — not clinical recommendations. Physician oversight required. Default certainty 0.25–0.40.
