Every ME/CFS patient has a drawer of medications they tried that didn't work — the antidepressant that made things worse, the stimulant that cleared the fog then caused a crash, the beta-blocker that slowed the heart but turned the fatigue into collapse. Each one was stopped and replaced, and every time it was a diagnostic test whose results were never read.

The new chapter of my paper provides a way to read them. If LDN doesn't help, your bottleneck is probably not TLR4-mediated neuroinflammation or TRPM3 channel dysfunction. If pyridostigmine doesn't improve your HRV, your vagal efferent pathway is non-functional — ganglionic, central, or structural. Each null eliminates a class of mechanisms. A positive LDN trial tells you "neuroinflammation, or TRPM3, or endorphin, or orexin." A null tells you "none of these four." More diagnostic information from a failure than from a success.

Midodrine causing severe hypertension at 2.5 mg isn't a failed trial — that's denervation hypersensitivity, neuropathic POTS diagnosed by side effect. Fludrocortisone making your orthostatic symptoms worse isn't paradoxical — the volume went to your legs, not your heart: venous pooling, not hypovolemia. The drug that didn't work just told you which one will.

Aripiprazole giving you akathisia at a microdose — your D2/D3 receptors are supersensitive, your dopamine deficiency severe enough that even a partial agonist at minimal occupancy is too much. Start at 0.1 mg. Guanfacine improves brain fog but crashes your blood pressure: you're sympathetically dependent, and removing even a fraction of central sympathetic drive collapses your hemodynamics. Add midodrine and guanfacine becomes accessible.

Midodrine raises your BP but produces no goosebumps — cutaneous sympathetic denervation while vascular α1 receptors remain intact, small-fiber neuropathy without a skin biopsy. Antihistamines don't make you drowsy: CNS histamine receptors downregulated from chronic mast cell activation. Guanfacine improves brain fog but worsens fatigue — same drug, opposite effects on two symptoms because they're different mechanisms.

The beta-blocker that wrecked your energy told you your metabolism depends on β2-AR-mediated fatty acid oxidation — your glucose-to-ATP pathway compromised, your body on a metabolic fallback.

All medications discussed are mechanistic inference — not clinical recommendations. Any treatment decision requires physician oversight. The chapter's framework carries a default certainty of 0.25–0.40. Nothing here has been validated prospectively in ME/CFS cohorts.
