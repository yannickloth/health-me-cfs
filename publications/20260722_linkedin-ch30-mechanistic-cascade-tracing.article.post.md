Every ME/CFS patient has a drawer of medications they tried that didn't work — the antidepressant that made things worse, the stimulant that cleared the fog then caused a crash, the beta-blocker that slowed the heart but turned the fatigue into collapse. Each one was stopped and replaced, and every time it was a diagnostic test whose results were never read.

The new chapter of my paper provides a way to read them. If LDN doesn't help, your bottleneck is probably not TLR4-mediated neuroinflammation, and LDN-responsive TRPM3 dysfunction is less likely. If pyridostigmine doesn't improve your HRV, your vagal efferent pathway is likely non-functional — ganglionic, central, or structural (at least at that dose; false negatives occur if the dose didn't engage the target). Each null downgrades a class of mechanisms. A positive LDN trial tells you "neuroinflammation, or TRPM3, or endorphin, or orexin." A null tells you "deprioritize these four — look elsewhere."

Midodrine causing severe hypertension at 2.5 mg isn't a failed trial — it suggests denervation hypersensitivity, consistent with neuropathic POTS (the framework treats supine norepinephrine as the confirmatory test). Fludrocortisone making your orthostatic symptoms worse isn't paradoxical — one interpretation: the volume went to your legs, not your heart: venous pooling, not hypovolemia. The drug that seemed to fail just pointed to the mechanism to target next.

Aripiprazole giving you akathisia at a microdose — one reading: your D2/D3 receptors are supersensitive, your dopamine deficiency severe enough that even a partial agonist at minimal occupancy tips into net agonism. The framework suggests starting at 0.1 mg. Guanfacine improves brain fog but crashes your blood pressure: consistent with sympathetic dependence — central sympathetic drive is load-bearing, and removing even a fraction collapses hemodynamics. The framework suggests adding midodrine before guanfacine.

Midodrine raises your BP but produces no goosebumps — suggests cutaneous sympathetic denervation while vascular α1 receptors remain intact, small-fiber neuropathy pattern without a skin biopsy. First-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) don't make you drowsy: suggests CNS histamine receptors downregulated from chronic mast cell activation. Second-generation antihistamines are confounded by P-gp efflux and are not informative here. Guanfacine improves brain fog but worsens fatigue — same drug, opposite effects on two symptoms because they're different mechanisms.

The beta-blocker that wrecked your energy — if the fatigue exceeds what matched-rate ivabradine would produce — suggests your metabolism depends on β2-AR-mediated fatty acid oxidation: your glucose-to-ATP pathway compromised, your body on a metabolic fallback.

All medications discussed are mechanistic inference — not clinical recommendations. Any treatment decision requires physician oversight. The chapter's framework carries a default certainty of 0.25–0.40. Nothing here has been validated prospectively in ME/CFS cohorts.

### Acronyms

β2-AR (beta-2 adrenergic receptor), BP (blood pressure), CNS (central nervous system), D2/D3 (dopamine receptor subtypes), HRV (heart rate variability), I_f (funny current), LDN (low-dose naltrexone), P-gp (P-glycoprotein efflux transporter), POTS (postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome), TLR4 (Toll-like receptor 4), TRPM3 (transient receptor potential melastatin 3, non-selective cation channel)

### Medication Classes

| Drug | Class |
|------|-------|
| LDN (low-dose naltrexone) | Opioid receptor antagonist at low dose; TLR4 antagonist; TRPM3 restoration shown in vitro |
| Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) | Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (peripheral cholinergic) |
| Midodrine | α1-adrenergic receptor agonist (vasoconstrictor) |
| Fludrocortisone | Mineralocorticoid (volume expansion, sodium retention) |
| Aripiprazole (low-dose) | D2/D3 partial agonist (atypical antipsychotic at microdose) |
| Guanfacine | α2A-adrenergic receptor agonist (central sympatholytic) |
| Beta-blockers | β1/β2-adrenergic receptor antagonists; β2 blockade impairs fat mobilization |
| Ivabradine | I_f channel blocker (heart rate reduction) |
