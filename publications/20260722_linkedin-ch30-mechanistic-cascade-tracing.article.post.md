Every ME/CFS patient has a drawer of medications they tried that didn't work — the antidepressant that made things worse, the stimulant that cleared the fog then caused a crash, the beta-blocker that slowed the heart but turned the fatigue into collapse. Each one was stopped and replaced, and every time it was a diagnostic test whose results were never read.
The new chapter of my paper provides a way to read them — as hypothesis-narrowing tools, not as definitive diagnoses. If LDN doesn't help, your bottleneck is probably not TLR4-mediated neuroinflammation or TRPM3 channel dysfunction. If pyridostigmine doesn't improve your HRV, your vagal efferent pathway is likely non-functional — ganglionic, central, or structural. Each null eliminates a class of mechanisms. A positive LDN trial suggests "neuroinflammation, or TRPM3, or endorphin, or orexin." A null suggests "none of these four." More diagnostic information from a failure than from a success.
Midodrine causing severe hypertension at 2.5 mg isn't a failed trial — that's denervation hypersensitivity, pointing strongly toward neuropathic POTS. Fludrocortisone making your orthostatic symptoms worse isn't paradoxical — the volume went to your legs, not your heart: venous pooling, not hypovolemia. The drug that didn't work just pointed toward the one that will.
Aripiprazole giving you akathisia at a microdose — your D2/D3 receptors may be supersensitive, your dopamine deficiency severe enough that even a partial agonist at minimal occupancy is too much. Start at 0.1 mg. Guanfacine improves brain fog but crashes your blood pressure: you may be sympathetically dependent, and removing even a fraction of central sympathetic drive could collapse your hemodynamics. Add midodrine and guanfacine might become accessible.
Midodrine raises your BP but produces no goosebumps — possible cutaneous sympathetic denervation while vascular α1 receptors remain intact, a pattern consistent with small-fiber neuropathy. Antihistamines don't make you drowsy: CNS histamine receptors may be downregulated from chronic mast cell activation. Guanfacine improves brain fog but worsens fatigue — same drug, opposite effects on two symptoms, because they likely involve different mechanisms.
The beta-blocker that wrecked your energy suggests your metabolism depends on β2-AR-mediated fatty acid oxidation — your glucose-to-ATP pathway may be compromised, your body on a metabolic fallback. That's not certainty. But it's a clue. And in a disease with no biomarkers and no validated diagnostics, a drawer full of clues is better than a drawer full of failures.

All medications discussed are mechanistic inference — not clinical recommendations. Any treatment decision requires physician oversight. The chapter's framework carries a default certainty of 0.25–0.40. Nothing here has been validated prospectively in ME/CFS cohorts.

### Acronyms

β2-AR (beta-2 adrenergic receptor), BP (blood pressure), CNS (central nervous system), D2/D3 (dopamine receptor subtypes), HRV (heart rate variability), I_f (funny current), LDN (low-dose naltrexone), P-gp (P-glycoprotein efflux transporter), POTS (postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome), TLR4 (Toll-like receptor 4), TRPM3 (transient receptor potential melastatin 3, non-selective cation channel)

### Medication Classes

| Drug | Class |
|------|-------|
| LDN (low-dose naltrexone) | Opioid receptor antagonist at low dose; TLR4 antagonist; TRPM3 restoration shown in vitro |
| Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) | Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (peripheral cholinergic) |
| Midodrine | α1-adrenergic receptor agonist (vasoconstrictor) |
| Fludrocortisone | Mineralocorticoid (volume expansion, sodium retention) |
| Aripiprazole (low-dose) | D2/D3 partial agonist (atypical antipsychotic at microdose) |
| Guanfacine | α2A-adrenergic receptor agonist (central sympatholytic) |
| Beta-blockers | β1/β2-adrenergic receptor antagonists; β2 blockade impairs fat mobilization |
| Ivabradine | I_f channel blocker (heart rate reduction) |
