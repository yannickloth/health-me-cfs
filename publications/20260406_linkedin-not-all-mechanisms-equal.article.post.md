ME/CFS research lists dozens of mechanisms: immune activation, mitochondrial dysfunction, autonomic dysregulation, viral reactivation, epigenetic changes, oxidative stress, sleep disruption, autoantibodies.

The usual response is something like: the disease is "everywhere," which means it's "nowhere" — too diffuse to treat. But the list only looks that way if you treat all these mechanisms as equivalent, and they're not.

A formal causal analysis distinguishes three tiers. Root causes can alone initiate the full syndrome. Amplifiers perpetuate or deepen it but can't start it. Consequences produce symptoms downstream of the disease process — real and important, but treating them doesn't touch what maintains the disease. The tier a mechanism belongs to determines its therapeutic ceiling.

Four criteria distinguish root causes from amplifiers: whether the mechanism can be triggered by known ME/CFS precipitants (infection, stress, surgery); whether it can alone produce PEM with its specific 12–72h latency pattern; whether it explains chronicity; and whether it accounts for multi-system spread. Under the framework's current mechanistic analysis, three mechanisms satisfy all four criteria and are assessed as candidate root causes — CNS energy crisis, GPCR autoantibody cascade, and epigenetic consolidation. These are framework-level assessments, not confirmed findings. Oxidative stress, viral reactivation, sleep fragmentation, gut dysbiosis are real and important, but assessed as tier 2 or 3.
