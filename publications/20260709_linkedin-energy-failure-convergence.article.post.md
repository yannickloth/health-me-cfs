Mitochondrial dysfunction. Immune activation. Oxygen delivery failure. Endocrine suppression. Gut dysbiosis. Sleep pathology. Connective tissue disorder. Chronic infection. Toxic exposure. Fourteen different mechanisms. One endpoint: the cell cannot make enough ATP.

ME/CFS is not one of these mechanisms. It is what happens when several become simultaneously active and mutually reinforcing. Post-viral immune activation → mitochondrial impairment → sleep disruption → neuroinflammation → autonomic dysfunction → gut toxin buildup → more mitochondrial impairment. The loops close. The disease is the loop.

This is why single-target treatments fail on average but work in subsets. Rituximab helps patients with specific autoantibody profiles. Iron infusions help the low-ferritin subset. Rifaximin helps the SIBO subset. Each drug targets one mechanism. In unselected cohorts, the signal is diluted to noise.

The clinical implication: systematic mechanism inventory for each patient. Test for the treatable mechanisms. Treat each one. Five interventions each reducing the energy deficit by 5-10% collectively create a 25-50% improvement — which in a patient whose functional capacity is measured in single-digit percentages of normal is transformative.

Not a cure. Systematic burden reduction. The tests exist. The treatments exist for many mechanisms. What doesn't yet exist is a clinical system that puts them together for individual patients.

Building that system is the challenge for the next decade of ME/CFS care.
