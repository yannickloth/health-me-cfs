Before the GWAS (genome-wide association study, the largest ME/CFS genetic study to date), giving dopamine drugs to ME/CFS patients was trial and error — try this, hope it helps, no one knew why. The GWAS changed the logic: the genetic risk variants cluster in neurons that are dopamine-responsive. This means dopamine-focused treatments now have a mechanistic rationale. That is a scientific advance. It is not a treatment recommendation.

Methylphenidate (Ritalin) is the most-studied dopamine drug in ME/CFS. The Blockmans randomised controlled trial found it improves cognition and fatigue scores. It does not improve physical functioning. It does not treat the disease — it masks the brain fog produced by the disease. Stimulants suppress the signal that tells you your energy is running out. In a disease defined by impaired energy production and post-exertional malaise (PEM — disproportionate symptom worsening after exertion), that is dangerous. The patient feels capable, exceeds their energy envelope, crashes harder. No stimulant creates energy.

Other dopamine agents have theoretical advantages but zero ME/CFS trials: bupropion (dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor with anti-inflammatory effect), levodopa/carbidopa (the direct precursor used in Parkinson's), rotigotine (a transdermal dopamine receptor agonist patch that would bypass any storage deficit), pramipexole, ropinirole (caveat: 5–15% impulse control disorder risk).

An emerging lead: PET imaging in long COVID shows reduced VMAT2 binding in the striatum — VMAT2 is the protein that packages dopamine for storage and release. The storage mechanism itself appears damaged. If this lesion extends to ME/CFS, drugs that restore packaging or bypass it entirely might be more rational than drugs that squeeze more out of a broken system. Untested.

This isn't a recommendation. It's a statement about what the genetics does to the pharmacological logic: we've moved from guessing to aiming. But aiming at the right circuit doesn't mean any drug that hits it is safe. A stimulant that masks fatigue without fixing the energy deficit is like disconnecting your car's low-fuel warning light — the real problem continues until you run out of gas.
