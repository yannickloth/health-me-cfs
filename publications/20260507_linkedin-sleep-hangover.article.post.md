You know that feeling the morning after too much to drink? The heavy eyes, the thick cognition, the sense that your brain is wrapped in something that prevents it from making contact with the world?

ME/CFS patients wake up like that every single day. Without drinking anything. After eight, ten, twelve hours of sleep.

It's not a metaphor. The brain has an overnight cleaning system — the glymphatic network — that flushes metabolic waste during deep sleep. In ME/CFS, this system faces a triple blockade: alpha-wave intrusion prevents the delta oscillations that drive the flushing, elevated norepinephrine blocks the water channels the fluid flows through, and pre-existing neuroinflammation clogs the plumbing.

The waste stays. Glutamate, lactate, inflammatory cytokines, mitochondrial debris — all sitting in the brain parenchyma come morning. The patient wakes into this chemical environment. It feels exactly like what it is: intoxication by the brain's own uncleaned metabolic waste.

Sleep inertia that lasts 2–4 hours instead of the normal 15–30 minutes. A morning headache that doesn't respond to standard analgesics. And "just sleep more" doesn't help, because adding more hours of broken sleep means more hours of failed clearance.

The article also covers the nocturnal cramps that wake patients at 3 AM — driven by the same energy deficit (the SERCA pump needs ATP to relax muscles; when ATP runs short, muscles can't let go).
