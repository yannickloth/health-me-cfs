If viruses, trauma, and immune activation all trigger ME/CFS, where do they converge? The GWAS just gave us the address.

Epidemiology tells us diverse triggers (Epstein-Barr virus, SARS-CoV-2, human herpesvirus 6, surgery, severe stress) produce the same disease. Clinical convergence requires anatomical convergence — same symptoms, same circuit. The DecodeME GWAS provides the physical address: medium spiny neurons (MSNs) in the striatum.

Why does the striatum make sense as the bottleneck?

Motor control. MSNs gate whether a motor command gets through. When they fail, movement becomes effortful — not because muscles are weak, but because initiating movement is neurologically expensive.

Effort/reward computation. Every action passes through a striatal cost-benefit calculation. In ME/CFS, the cost estimate is pathologically elevated.

Vulnerability to convergence. The striatum has four independently documented vulnerability pathways: neuroinflammation, metabolic failure (highest energy demand in the brain), autoantibody disruption of dopamine receptors, and hypoperfusion. A post-viral patient with immune activation, mitochondrial impairment, and hypoperfusion has three independent insults converging on the same neurons.

The bottleneck reframes treatment strategy. Stop asking "what triggered this patient?" Start asking "what is currently disrupting striatal circuit function?" The shared bottleneck means post-viral, post-traumatic, and gradual-onset ME/CFS may all respond to the same circuit-targeting interventions: dopamine agonists, anti-neuroinflammatory agents, perfusion stabilisation, metabolic support.

This model makes a falsifiable prediction: interventions that improve striatal dopamine signalling should work regardless of trigger type.
