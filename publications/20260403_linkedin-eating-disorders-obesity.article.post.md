When a teenage girl with ME/CFS stops eating and loses weight, a clinician unfamiliar with the disease often reaches for the same differential: female patient, weight loss, food avoidance, fatigue — must be an eating disorder.

That diagnosis is sometimes wrong. And when it is, the consequences are serious — including life-threatening malnutrition while teams pursue psychiatric explanations instead of feeding the patient.

But the story runs in both directions. Other patients with ME/CFS gain weight they can't lose through any conventional means — because the standard advice (diet and exercise) is contraindicated in a disease where exercise triggers crashes.

The real picture is more complex than either story. Eating itself triggers post-exertional malaise in many patients — the gut is hypersensitive, cytokines suppress appetite at the brain level, and chewing and swallowing cost energy some patients don't have. On the other side, weight gain in ME/CFS involves impaired fat oxidation, leptin resistance, and muscle wasting, producing "sarcopenic obesity": more fat, less muscle, even at normal body weight. Standard weight management advice misses all of this, because the fat isn't there because of behavior — it's there because of broken metabolism.

The food problem in ME/CFS is metabolic, immunological, and neurological — wearing the clothes of something more familiar.
