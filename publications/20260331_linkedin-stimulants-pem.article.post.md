Many people with ME/CFS take stimulants — modafinil, Ritalin, Adderall — not because they want to, but because they have to. Because the world doesn't pause for illness.

The stimulants work. The brain fog lifts. You can think, work, function. For a few hours, you feel closer to who you were.

Then the crash comes — twelve to forty-eight hours later, sometimes lasting for days.

That crash isn't a side effect; it's the mechanism.

No stimulant creates energy. They suppress the signal that tells you your energy is running out. In a disease defined by impaired energy production, that is like disconnecting the low-fuel warning light in a car with a nearly empty tank.

The data confirms it. A 2025 PNAS study of 3,925 patients found that stimulants improved brain fog in 77% of users — but had a net negative effect on PEM. The warning light was turned off. The patients drove further. The crash was worse.

And the payback carries interest. Two-day exercise testing shows that overexertion doesn't just cause equivalent fatigue — it degrades the metabolic system itself. The workload gap triples on Day 2.

Not all stimulants are equal: amphetamines carry the highest risk, modafinil has the most nuanced profile, and a newer agent, pitolisant, works through a completely different mechanism and may warrant investigation.

But the real problem is structural, not pharmacological. Stimulants are being used to bridge a gap that should be bridged by workplace accommodations and disability systems that actually work.
