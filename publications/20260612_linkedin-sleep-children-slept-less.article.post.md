The children who later developed ME/CFS weren't sleeping too much. They were sleeping too little — years before anyone noticed anything was wrong.

A birth cohort study of nearly 14,000 children found that those who later developed chronic fatigue had shorter sleep, later bedtimes, and more nighttime awakenings starting 4–7 years before diagnosis.

Each extra hour of sleep at age 9 cut later fatigue odds by 39%.

This demolishes the "oversleeper" stereotype. And it may not just be a marker — chronic partial sleep deprivation progressively erodes the metabolic reserves that eventually prove inadequate when a major stressor arrives.

Sleep isn't optional recovery time. It's when the body repairs its energy infrastructure. Skip it for years and the bill comes due.
