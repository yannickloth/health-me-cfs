749 Belgians with ME/CFS told their government what's broken. The data is brutal.

Belgium's Health Care Knowledge Centre (KCE) just published the most granular needs assessment of ME/CFS patients ever conducted in Europe: 749 patients surveyed, 19 in-depth interviews, cross-referenced against government administrative data.

Quality of life: standardised health questionnaire (EQ-5D-5L) score of 0.36 — worse than multiple sclerosis, stroke, and cancer in prior comparable studies. A 57% decline from pre-illness baseline. The sickest patients couldn't even complete the survey, so the true average is likely lower.

Diagnostic delay: 50% waited >2 years, 39% waited >5 years — and each additional year of waiting independently worsened outcomes (p = 0.010).

Comorbidities: 73.2% have at least one additional condition — fibromyalgia 63%, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) 57%, postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) 17%, mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS) 10.5%. This isn't "complex patient with somatisation." This is multiple diagnosable conditions sharing autonomic, connective tissue, and immune substrates.

Work disability: 8.6 years mean duration of invalidity, from government administrative records — among the longest of any condition studied under the KCE's needs assessment framework.

Graded exercise therapy (GET): a majority harmed — but 26% improved, a still-unexplained minority responder signal that demands investigation, not prescription.

And one finding specific to Belgium but relevant everywhere: French-speaking patients had significantly worse outcomes than Dutch-speaking patients, with the most plausible explanation being structural — the country's single ME/CFS reference centre is in Flanders. Same disease, same country, different access, measurably worse results.

This is a government-funded report addressed to the institutions that control access to care and benefits. 285 pages of data that patients can cite to doctors, disability assessors, and policymakers.
