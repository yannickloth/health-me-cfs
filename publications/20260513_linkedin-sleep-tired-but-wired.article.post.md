Exhausted beyond description. Every fibre says: sleep. Now.

You lie down. Your brain turns on.

"Tired but wired" — physical exhaustion coexisting with mental hyperarousal — is one of the most distressing features of ME/CFS, and it's not insomnia, not poor sleep hygiene. It's an autonomic nervous system that has lost the ability to make its nightly handoff from sympathetic (daytime) to parasympathetic (nighttime) mode.

The transition that should happen: declining cortisol → rising melatonin → increasing vagal tone → decreasing norepinephrine → peripheral vasodilation → core temperature drop → orexin suppression → sleep. In ME/CFS, this sequence fails at multiple points simultaneously. Sympathetic tone persists. Norepinephrine stays elevated — simultaneously keeping the patient awake and blocking the aquaporin-4 channels needed for brain waste clearance. The temperature drop fails because autonomic dysfunction impairs peripheral vasodilation. And inflammatory cytokines create a paradox: they increase sleep pressure (the brain wants to force rest) while fragmenting the sleep architecture that would make rest restorative.

Two systems pulling in opposite directions, and the patient stuck between them.

The article also covers restless legs syndrome — another sleep disruptor linked to dopaminergic dysfunction (the NIH deep phenotyping study found reduced catecholamine synthesis, though this hasn't been independently replicated yet) — and how these mechanisms stack: autonomic failure delays sleep onset, RLS prevents settling, alpha-wave intrusion degrades sleep quality, and periodic limb movements fragment whatever deep sleep survives.
