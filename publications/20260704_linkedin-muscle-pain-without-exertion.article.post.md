Your muscles hurt like you ran a marathon. But you haven't exercised. You haven't walked further than the bathroom. You've barely moved.

If you have ME/CFS, you've lived this. The deep, flu-like ache. The one that feels like your body is paying for something — but you can't identify what you did.

Here is the biochemistry behind it.

In ME/CFS, the muscle is already in crisis at rest. Elevated resting lactate in ~45% of patients. Acidosis. Persistent ATP deficit. Three receptor families — ASIC3, P2X3, and TRPV1 — detect this state as "I just exercised" and fire pain signals without any mechanical trigger.

Two additional mechanisms sustain this: oxidative stress runs a self-amplifying TRPV1-TRPA1 pain loop that operates constitutively, and microvascular dysfunction creates focal ischemia-reperfusion in muscle that varies with autonomic state — not activity level.

Sleep loss amplifies all of it. It shortens the nocturnal melatonin antioxidant window. It prevents glymphatic clearance of inflammatory mediators. It destabilises autonomic control of microvascular perfusion. One bad night creates a morning muscle profile that converges with post-exertion stress.

This is not deconditioning. The pain is real. The mechanism is specific. In ME/CFS, the boundary between "I didn't sleep" and "I ran a marathon" is thinner than for anyone else.
