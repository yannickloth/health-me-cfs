For a healthy person, a 20-minute nap is a reset button. Adenosine clears, alertness returns. The nap works because the machinery works.

For an ME/CFS patient, naps are a trap.

The adenosine clearance is futile — the metabolic deficit (impaired ATP production) regenerates sleep pressure immediately. Alpha-wave intrusion operates during naps too, preventing even brief entries into restorative sleep stages. Long naps trigger amplified sleep inertia: 2–4 hours of cognitive dysfunction instead of the normal 15–30 minutes. Late naps disrupt already-fragile circadian rhythms. And sometimes what feels like "just tiredness" is the early signal of post-exertional malaise — so the nap masks the warning without preventing the crash.

One study (Gotts et al. 2015, n=118) found that afternoon napping in CFS predicted worse objective cognitive performance — though whether napping causes impairment or just reflects greater severity remains unclear.

The thing that strikes me most: every recovery mechanism in ME/CFS still exists. Adenosine clearance works. Sleep spindles can theoretically be generated. Glymphatic clearance activates during delta sleep. Each system is intact in principle and broken in practice, because they all run on an energy substrate that isn't there.

"Tired" is a signal that rest fixes. ME/CFS is a state where the signal fires, rest is attempted, and the machinery that should respond is running on empty. That's a different problem entirely.
