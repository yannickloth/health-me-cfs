Patients treated within their first year or two tend to do better than patients treated five or ten years in. Clinicians and researchers have observed this for decades. The formal model offers a specific mechanism and a quantitative prediction about why.

Epigenetic consolidation. During acute ME/CFS, gene expression changes — metabolic suppression, immune activation — are driven by active signalling. Remove the signals and expression can revert. Over months to years, those changes become written into the chromatin layer through DNA methylation. The cell no longer needs the ongoing signal. It has encoded the disease state.

The consolidation timescale τ_epi is estimated at approximately 18–24 months post-onset. The model's response decay equation predicts treatment response probability falls sigmoidally as methylation consolidates — with the steepest decline around 18–24 months — and that patients treated within this window will show 2–3× higher response rates than patients treated after it. This prediction is falsified if treatment response is independent of illness duration, or if the critical transition occurs at a substantially different timescale.

Two things about the window matter. First, it exists at all — the biology is reversible early in a way it isn't late. Second, it is routinely missed: average ME/CFS diagnostic delay is 5–7 years, which means most patients are past the window before they receive a diagnosis, let alone treatment. Even then, no approved disease-modifying treatment exists.

For patients well past consolidation, the model proposes a patient-accessible pathway: sustained anti-inflammatory therapy combined with metabolic support (CoQ10, NR/NMN) reduces the cytokine drive on methylation-writer enzymes, allowing passive demethylation to gradually erode consolidated methylation over 18–24 months. It is slow, it requires sustained engagement, and it is more effective the earlier it starts. The window isn't closed. It just keeps narrowing.
