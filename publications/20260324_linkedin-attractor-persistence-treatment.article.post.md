Two questions about ME/CFS that look separate but aren't: why doesn't it just get better, and why do severe cases resist treatment?

They have the same answer.

ME/CFS is an attractor problem. The disease state is mathematically stable in the same way the healthy state is, maintained by self-reinforcing loops between immune activation, metabolic suppression, and autonomic dysfunction. Once the system crosses the boundary into the disease basin, it does not spontaneously return for most patients. The trigger opened the door; the locks keep the patient inside. Where persistent viral reservoirs remain active, the attractor and reservoir frameworks are complementary rather than competing.

In milder subtypes, fewer locks are load-bearing, so addressing the dominant mechanism may be sufficient for escape. In severe ME/CFS, no single-parameter restoration achieves that. The separatrix nudging framework predicts escape requires stacking multiple sub-threshold interventions until their combined perturbation crosses the boundary. Across all modeled escape paths, epigenetic consolidation is the bottleneck: other restorations cannot do their full work while the epigenetic program still encodes the disease state, and energy restoration must precede or accompany any epigenetic intervention.

The clinical prediction follows directly: combination therapies stacking mitochondrial support, anti-inflammatory agents, and epigenetic modifiers will show qualitatively different outcomes in severe ME/CFS — not merely additive, but a step-change. A patient-accessible route exists without toxic drugs; sustained anti-inflammatory therapy combined with metabolic support (CoQ10, NR/NMN) over 18–24 months may allow passive demethylation to gradually erode epigenetic consolidation. Current single-target trials are structurally insufficient for this subtype.
