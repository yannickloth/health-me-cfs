Your small intestine is where you absorb nutrients. It's supposed to be relatively sterile. A muscular wave called the migrating motor complex sweeps it clean between meals, pushing bacteria downstream to the colon.

That wave is controlled by the vagus nerve. The vagus nerve is dysfunctional in ME/CFS. When the cleaning cycle slows, bacteria colonise the small intestine and eat your food before you absorb it.

This is SIBO — small intestinal bacterial overgrowth. The bacteria consume your dietary iron and B12. They ferment your carbohydrates, producing gas and stealing calories. They damage the absorptive surface. One variant produces hydrogen sulfide, which inhibits mitochondrial Complex IV at the same binding site as cyanide.

A patient with ME/CFS and untreated SIBO has two energy drains running simultaneously. The SIBO doesn't cause the ME/CFS. But it steals nutrients the compromised mitochondria need, produces a compound that further poisons them, and drives inflammation that costs yet more energy.

The breath test costs under 100 euros. Treatment is a two-week course of rifaximin. In a patient whose energy margin is measured in percentage points, removing a 20-30% additional burden is the difference between housebound and able to leave the house.

Every ME/CFS patient with bloating, post-prandial fatigue, or food intolerances should be tested. The return on a cheap test and a short prescription is potentially the single largest treatable improvement available.
