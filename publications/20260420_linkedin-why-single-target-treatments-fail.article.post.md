Single-target ME/CFS treatments have a consistent record: modest results, incomplete responses, benefits that plateau well short of recovery. This happens across mechanistic categories and research quality levels. The pattern demands an explanation beyond "the wrong target was chosen."

The causal hierarchy framework offers one: ME/CFS is multi-lock by architecture, and single-target designs are structurally incapable of succeeding against a multi-lock system.

The disease is maintained by several self-reinforcing processes simultaneously — epigenetic consolidation, immune exhaustion loops, autoantibody-mediated endothelial dysfunction, metabolic suppression. Each feeds back into the others. When you remove one, the others compensate and hold the disease state in place.

The formal analysis tests this directly. For three of four disease subtypes, there is at least one single-parameter restoration that achieves escape from the disease attractor. For the severe/locked attractor, none does — every single intervention produces improvement but not escape. When pairs are tested, most still fail. The ones that succeed all have the same structure: one energy-producing restoration plus epigenetic methylation reversal. Neither alone escapes; both together do.

The prediction is specific. Daratumumab should work for early-stage autoantibody-positive patients whose epigenetic consolidation hasn't yet deepened their attractor — consistent with the 60% responders in the pilot. The 40% non-responders may be patients whose disease has crossed into the deeper attractor. Secondary lock treatments (antivirals, antioxidants, sleep medication) should produce genuine improvement that plateaus at improvement, not recovery, regardless of dose.

The design implication: the standard single-arm trial testing one agent in unselected ME/CFS is almost structurally guaranteed to underperform. The design the framework predicts would work is combinatorial — stratify by subtype, match combinations to predicted lock structure, measure trajectory rather than snapshot.
