There is a single element that appears in at least three separate ME/CFS failure modes. Not the same mechanism each time — but the same ion, going wrong in different tissues, for different reasons, producing symptoms that all trace back to the same place.

Calcium.

In muscles: after exertion, the sodium-potassium pump fails. Sodium accumulates. The sodium-calcium exchanger runs in reverse. Calcium floods in instead of out. It overwhelms the mitochondria, triggers oxidative stress, causes structural damage. Sodium imaging (a specialized MRI technique) has confirmed the predicted sodium accumulation in ME/CFS patients. This is the most likely biological mechanism behind post-exertional malaise.

In immune cells: natural killer cells kill via calcium. When they recognize a target, calcium floods in through a channel called TRPM3 — triggering the release of their killing payload. In ME/CFS, TRPM3 is broken. A large-scale 2025 study confirmed this across multiple laboratory sites. The cells cannot load calcium. They cannot fire. Pathogens that should be cleared are not cleared.

In the brain during sleep: thalamic neurons need calcium-dependent slow oscillations to produce deep restorative sleep. When thalamic excitability is abnormally elevated, faster oscillations contaminate slow-wave sleep. The brain's waste-clearance system cannot run. Sleep duration looks normal. Sleep quality is not.

Three tissues. Three different calcium mechanisms. One patient.

The question the research hasn't yet answered: are these three independent problems that happen to co-occur? Or is there a single upstream calcium dysregulation — a systemic channelopathy — expressing itself in three places at once?

TRPM3 is expressed not just in immune cells but throughout the central nervous system, including brain neurons. Whether TRPM3 dysfunction in ME/CFS extends to thalamic neurons has never been measured. That experiment would answer whether the sleep problem, the crash problem, and the immune problem share a root.
