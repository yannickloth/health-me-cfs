Radically different triggers — viral infection, severe stress, surgery, vaccination — produce clinically indistinguishable ME/CFS. The same syndrome, whatever the entry point.

The causal framework's proposed answer is that they don't lead to different rooms — they lead to the same final common pathway: CNS energy failure combined with broken restoration machinery. Under this hypothesis, once a patient's physiology reaches that state, the downstream consequences (autonomic failure, immune dysregulation, metabolic suppression, sleep disruption) are determined by the pathway, not the door. This is a framework prediction, not a confirmed finding.

This generates a prediction about disease staging that matters for treatment. In early disease, the entry door is still legible in the biology — post-viral patients show viral immune signatures, post-stress patients show HPA dysregulation. In late disease (the framework estimates 3–5 years, though this has not been established empirically), those signatures converge as amplifiers take over and the disease is increasingly maintained by the pathway itself. Early-stage patients may respond to interventions targeting their specific entry mechanism; late-stage patients cannot usually exit the way they entered, because the door is no longer where the disease lives (patients with confirmed active viral persistence may be an exception).

This asymmetry — going in, the entry point matters; coming out, it mostly doesn't — is one of the more clinically consequential predictions of the framework. It also explains why mixed clinical trials (patients at different stages, different entry points) produce inconsistent results: not evidence that nothing works, but evidence that the trial wasn't stratified.
