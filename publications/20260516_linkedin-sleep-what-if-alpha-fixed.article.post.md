A thought experiment: what if we could fix alpha-wave intrusion in ME/CFS sleep?

Alpha-delta sleep — waking-frequency brain waves contaminating deep sleep — is one of the most documented objective abnormalities in ME/CFS. It prevents the brain from entering the oscillatory state that drives overnight restoration. What would happen if a drug could cleanly eliminate it?

Glymphatic waste clearance would improve (the brain's cleaning system runs on delta oscillations), and so would memory consolidation (sleep spindles need intact slow waves as scaffolding) and pain thresholds (delta sleep actively suppresses nociceptive pathways). The neuroinflammatory vicious cycle would partially break.

But cerebral hypoperfusion, kynurenine pathway neurotoxicity, mast cell-mediated cognitive impairment, mitochondrial dysfunction, and autonomic dysregulation would all remain — none of them depend on sleep architecture. Brain fog has at least three independent mechanistic drivers. Fixing the glymphatic arm addresses one. The other two — quinolinic acid excitotoxicity from immune-driven tryptophan diversion, and histamine-mediated acetylcholine suppression from mast cells — operate regardless of sleep quality.

So: significant but incomplete improvement. Better mornings, less brain fog, reduced pain — but not recovery. The recurring lesson of ME/CFS is that it's a multi-mechanism disease, and no single fix reaches the whole problem.

There is a pharmacological test for this. Ethosuximide (a T-type calcium channel blocker) vs. placebo, one night, one PSG. It would tell us whether fixing alpha-intrusion is even possible with current drugs — a low-cost, mechanistically decisive trial that hasn't been run.
