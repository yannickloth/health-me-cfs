The largest genetic study of ME/CFS ever conducted just ended a 20-year debate.

The DecodeME genome-wide association study (GWAS) analysed ~5,000 ME/CFS cases and found that genetic risk variants cluster overwhelmingly in neurons — not immune cells, not microglia, not astrocytes. The strongest cell-type enrichment: medium spiny neurons of the striatum, the same cell type that computes effort-reward decisions in the basal ganglia.

This changes three things:

1. The "autoimmune vs neurological" framing is obsolete. Immune involvement is real (elevated cytokines, autoantibodies, natural killer cell dysfunction) — but it's secondary. The genetic architecture says the primary locus is the brain.

2. The "it's psychological" argument loses its foundation. A genetic finding expressed in striatal neurons is a neurological finding. No one tells a Parkinson's patient to try graded exercise. The genetics makes the same dismissal harder for ME/CFS.

3. Research priorities shift. Neuroimaging of the striatum, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) metabolomics, and striatal circuit interrogation become the highest-priority non-genetic research avenues. Blood cytokine panels capture the downstream — the upstream is in the brain.

The caveat: 5,000 cases is enough for cell-type enrichment but not for fine-mapping. The field needs a 50,000-person GWAS — the scale that schizophrenia and depression needed to move from "it's genetic" to "we know which pathways are broken."
