"Your sleep study was normal."

Five words that ME/CFS patients hear after spending an uncomfortable night wired to electrodes, knowing — with the certainty of someone who lives in their own body — that their sleep is profoundly broken.

The test says otherwise. The doctor trusts the test. The patient goes home with no diagnosis and the growing suspicion that their medical team thinks this is psychological.

The test has a resolution problem. Standard polysomnography scores 30-second epochs by visual inspection. Alpha-delta intrusion — the defining ME/CFS sleep abnormality — doesn't change the sleep stage classification. An epoch with both delta and alpha activity is still scored as N3 (deep sleep). The contamination that prevents sleep from being restorative is invisible in the clinical report.

Microarousals shorter than 3 seconds aren't counted. Hundreds per night, each triggering a brief sympathetic burst and suppressing glymphatic flow — and the arousal index reads "normal." Sleep stage percentages can look fine even when no sustained slow-wave epoch lasts long enough for restoration. And a single-night study may catch an atypical night.

The tools to detect what's actually broken already exist: spectral analysis (delta/alpha power ratio), extended arousal scoring, multi-night home EEG, concurrent autonomic monitoring. They require computational post-processing, not additional equipment. The cost is willingness to look, not technology.

When a "normal" sleep study conflicts with profoundly non-restorative sleep, the study needs updating — not the patient.
