Medicine is organized around ~10,000 named diseases. But those 10,000 diseases are mostly combinations of ~40 fundamental biological mechanisms.

A physician who deeply understands thalamo-cortical hyperexcitability can recognize it across fibromyalgia, ME/CFS, chronic migraine, PTSD, and treatment-resistant insomnia — without needing a separate protocol for each. A physician trained only by protocol produces nothing when the presentation doesn't fit a known entry.

This asymmetry has real consequences.

A patient gets a polysomnography showing alpha-wave intrusion in deep sleep — waking brain oscillations contaminating slow-wave sleep, preventing restorative function. The mechanism is reasonably well understood: abnormal thalamic excitability keeps oscillation frequency in the alpha range instead of transitioning to delta. Gabapentin targets this mechanism directly. The finding sits in the chart for seven years without triggering that connection, because the neurologist was looking for epilepsy or apnea. Alpha intrusion without a matching diagnosis is a finding with no case to check.

Seven years later, with an ME/CFS diagnosis, the same PSG data tells a completely different story. The data didn't change. The interpretive framework did.

This is a structural failure, not an individual one. The problem has three components: unknown combinations (no entry for multi-mechanism presentations), cross-specialty blindness (findings don't propagate between departments), and guideline lag (current guidelines often reflect 20-year-old understanding).

What would mechanism-first medicine look like? Map active mechanisms, not diagnostic categories. Target accessible ones in sequence. Synthesize across specialties into a unified mechanistic picture.

ME/CFS patients often do this synthesis themselves. That is not how it should work.

The data is there. The mechanisms are known. The tools often exist. What's missing is the architecture that connects them.
