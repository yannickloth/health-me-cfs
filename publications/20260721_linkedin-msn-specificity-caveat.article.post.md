The DecodeME genome-wide association study (GWAS) points to medium spiny neurons (MSNs). So do the GWAS for schizophrenia, depression, and alcohol use disorder. Same cell type. Radically different diseases. Here's why that doesn't weaken the finding — it sharpens the next question.

MSN enrichment is shared across ME/CFS, schizophrenia, depression, and alcohol use disorder. The striatum is a generic brain vulnerability node — high metabolic demand, dense dopamine innervation, multiple convergence pathways. When it's compromised, the output is motivational, motor, or reward-processing deficits regardless of upstream cause.

But the diseases manifest completely differently. A schizophrenia patient hallucinates. A depressed patient experiences anhedonia. An ME/CFS patient crashes after exertion with orthostatic intolerance.

GWAS cell-type enrichment tells you where risk variants are expressed, not what they do. The schizophrenia variants affect synaptic pruning (C4/complement). The depression variants affect stress hormone signalling. For ME/CFS, we don't yet know which genes within MSNs are disrupted — is it D1/D2 dopamine receptor pathway imbalance? Metabolic insufficiency? Receptor desensitisation? Cortico-striatal synapse dysfunction? Same cell type. Four different mechanisms. Four different treatments.

This isn't a weakness. It's a precise research question: what is the molecular signature of MSN dysfunction in ME/CFS that distinguishes it from MSN dysfunction in schizophrenia, depression, and alcohol use disorder (AUD)? Answer that, and you have a drug target — not a shared circuit, but a disease-specific lesion within it.

Shared enrichment does not make ME/CFS a psychiatric disease. Schizophrenia and depression are brain diseases with neuronal genetic architecture. The brain is an organ. Diseases of that organ are neurological. The critical distinction from the GWAS is not "brain vs mind" — it's "neurons vs immune cells."

Every dopamine drug for ME/CFS is currently targeting the right circuit at lower resolution than we need. The next experiment raises the resolution.
