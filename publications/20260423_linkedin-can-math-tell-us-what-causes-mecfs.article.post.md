Biology answers causal questions in the lab — you perturb a system and observe the result. In human disease, most perturbations are neither controllable nor reversible, and the event you most want to observe (onset) is usually unobservable by the time anyone is looking.

Mathematics has a different approach. You build a formal model of the system's dynamics, encode the feedback structure you believe is operating, then ask: can this mechanism alone — starting from a healthy state — drive the system across the boundary into disease? That's answerable analytically.

Ch. 33 of the framework does exactly that with a 67-variable ODE model. Three analyses, all complementary: trigger sufficiency (can this mechanism alone initiate disease?), sensitivity analysis (which parameters most control the disease state?), and lock removal (which mechanisms, when restored, allow the system to escape the disease attractor?).

The trigger sufficiency results: three mechanisms are independently trigger-sufficient — CNS energy crisis (below ~65% Complex I activity, the healthy attractor undergoes a saddle-node bifurcation and disappears), GPCR autoantibody cascade (above ~55–65% normalized titer, endothelial dysfunction triggers the energy–immune bifurcation), and metabolic safe mode activation (a bistable switch with hysteresis that self-sustains through internal feedbacks after the external trigger clears). TRPM3 dysfunction is conditionally trigger-sufficient — it lowers the threshold for other mechanisms rather than independently crossing it.

The global sensitivity analysis (Sobol indices) sums to 1.77 — well above 1 — because of parameter interactions. No single mechanism controls the disease state; the system is multi-lock by architecture. Epigenetic methylation ranks fourth, a moderate but pervasive influence across all output variables.

The lock removal results echo what earlier articles covered: three subtypes have single-parameter necessary locks; the severe attractor has none. Every successful escape from the severe attractor in the analysis requires epigenetic reversal paired with an energy restoration.

What math adds here isn't proof — it's precision. It converts a verbal hypothesis into a falsifiable one, with specific predictions that can be checked against clinical data and specific ways the model can be wrong.
