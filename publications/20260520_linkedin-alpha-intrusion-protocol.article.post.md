Sleeping eight, nine, or ten hours doesn't prevent waking unrefreshed in ME/CFS. Alpha waves intrude into deep sleep stages where they don't belong.

Alpha waves (8-12 Hz) occur during wakefulness. Delta waves (0.5-4 Hz) should dominate deep sleep. In ME/CFS, alpha waves intrude into deep sleep stages, preventing the pure delta oscillations that restore the brain.

The glymphatic waste-clearance system depends on proper deep sleep. During deep sleep, spaces between brain cells open up 60%, letting cerebrospinal fluid wash out metabolic waste, inflammatory proteins, and toxic byproducts. Alpha intrusion prevents this clearance, causing waste accumulation.

The article covers sleep pressure breakdown in ME/CFS (adenosine doesn't build up properly), why standard CBT-I sleep restriction triggers PEM, and a detailed protocol adapted for ME/CFS energy limits.

The approach keeps time in bed 30-45 minutes above actual sleep time rather than restricting below it. Naps under 20 minutes and after 2 PM, with tracking of effects on nighttime sleep. Circadian alignment matters—bright light in the morning, blue light reduction in the evening.

Hyper-arousal reduction options include wind-down routines, progressive muscle relaxation, and worry journaling. Environment matters too—ME/CFS impairs thermoregulation, so temperature control is important. Complete caffeine avoidance for 4-6 weeks (including decaf). Movement helps only if tolerated—recumbent exercise at least 4 hours before bedtime.

Sleep architecture changes typically take 4-6 weeks.

This won't cure ME/CFS, post-exertional malaise, or mitochondrial dysfunction. The approach works around disrupted sleep architecture and improves sleep quality.

Better sleep efficiency reduces morning brain fog. Improved glymphatic clearance reduces neuroinflammation. Strengthened sleep pressure improves deep sleep reliability.

These are improvements, not cures.
