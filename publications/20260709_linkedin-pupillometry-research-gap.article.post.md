Your pupil responds to light in 200 milliseconds. Whether it constricts and dilates properly tells us which branch of your autonomic nervous system is working or failing — specifically at the exact receptors targeted by autoantibodies found in ME/CFS.

Nobody has ever measured this in a modern ME/CFS study.

The last attempt was 1997, with outdated methods, fewer than 20 patients, and no replication since. Meanwhile, pupillometry has become routine in concussion, diabetes, MS, and post-COVID. It takes 30 seconds. You don't have to stand up.

Why does this matter now? Because two things are true at once: (1) GPCR autoantibodies against M3 and alpha-1 adrenergic receptors are found in a substantial subset of ME/CFS patients, and (2) the largest autoantibody screen ever done found nothing — at all GPCR targets, using two different platforms. Is the ELISA detecting real functional antibodies while the other platforms miss them? Or are the antibodies an artifact? Right now, nobody knows. But a patient with a positive autoantibody test faces a real decision: pursue immunomodulatory therapy — expensive, invasive, unproven — or ignore a result that might be telling the truth.

A 30-second pupil measurement could break this deadlock. If the antibodies impair the receptors, the pupil should be abnormal. If they're harmless bystanders, it should be normal.

This study needs three things that already exist: a handheld pupillometer (€3,000, FDA-cleared), an ME/CFS cohort with known autoantibody status (already recruited at multiple centres), and half a day per patient. It is one of the cheapest, fastest questions in ME/CFS biomarker research — and the answer directly tells patients whether a treatment is mechanistically justified.
