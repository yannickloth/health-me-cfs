The English language has one word — "tired" — for three biologically unrelated diseases. This isn't a linguistic curiosity. It's the primary driver of ME/CFS misdiagnosis.

Depression: the reward circuit goes quiet. The body can perform; the brain doesn't generate the impulse to start. Exercise helps. HPA axis runs too hot. Antidepressants work.

Burnout: sustained demand without recovery. Rest resolves it — real rest, weeks to months. Remove the stressor, the person recovers.

ME/CFS: the engine broke. Exercise causes measurable physiological deterioration 24-72 hours later (2-day CPET confirms this objectively). Rest reduces severity but doesn't fix the machinery. HPA axis runs too cold — the opposite of depression. Antidepressants don't touch the core pathology.

The PACE trial prescribed the depression treatment to ME/CFS patients. 74% reported deterioration. The treatment was correct for the wrong diagnosis.

Five questions separate them: Does exercise help or harm? Does rest resolve it? Can you perform when externally compelled? When does exhaustion peak? Was there a clear trigger?

These could be asked at the first visit instead of the fifth. The barrier isn't knowledge. It's one word pretending to describe one thing.
