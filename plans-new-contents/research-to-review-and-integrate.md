 Beyond the Myocarditis Headlines: What the Data and Our Memory Say About COVID-19 Shots for Kids
Kevin Kavanagh
Kevin Kavanagh
Board Chairman (Volunteer) at Health Watch USA
November 10, 2025

Recommendations for COVID-19 vaccine administration are becoming more restrictive. The withdrawal of recommendations are being driven by the fear of vaccine related myocarditis. But this fear is due to both a distortion of scientific data and the forgetting of pandemic history.    We must not develop monocular vision and only focus on myocarditis when determining the risk-benefits of childhood COVID-19 vaccinations. Vaccines have also been shown to reduce the incidence of pediatric cases of long-COVID.  And although post vaccination myocarditis can occur, it is “generally mild with rare cases of left ventricular dysfunction, heart failure and arrhythmias.” 

Let’s also remember that cases of myocarditis were appearing in young male college athletes in the Summer of 2020. Four athletes (15%) were reported to have cardiac magnetic resonance (CMR) findings consistent with myocarditis and two of these had mild symptoms. And in a Sept. 8, 2020 an article in Infection Control Today, we questioned if COVID-19 was not primarily a heart and vascular disease. All of this occurred before any COVID vaccine was available. Researchers from Heidelburg University, Germany, reported that the incidence of myocarditis after COVID-19 infections varies between 2.3% and 5.0%. In a study of U.S. university athletes found the incidence of symptomatic myocarditis after a positive SARS-CoV-2 test was 0.31% (31 per 10,000), when subclinical disease was added, the incidence increased to 2.3% (230 per 10,000).

During a recent Senate hearing it was stated that the incidence of post vaccination myocarditis in children is 6 to 8 per 10,000. I feel many interpreted this statistics as applying to all children. I feel this high rate may only apply to the subset of young post pubescent males, but even in that group the quoted incidence appears to be high. The American College of Cardiology determined the rate of vaccine-associated myocarditis for males between the age of 12 and 24 years to be 1.07 per 10,000.  In a meta-analysis published in JAMA Pediatrics, Watanabe and colleagues reported that, in children aged 5 to 11 years, the rate of post vaccine myocarditis was 1.8 per million (or 0.018 per 10,000). 

Unfortunately, misinformation regarding the rate of vaccination induced myocarditis in relation to infections is rampant. I feel this is due in part to a report from Florida’s Surgeon General, Joseph Ladapo. The Associated Press reported that his final report regarding cardiac-related deaths in young men is reported to have omitted information which “showed that catching COVID-19 could increase the chances of a cardiac-related death much more than getting the vaccine.” The report was also only based on a very small number of deaths, 20, allowing biases to easily alter the results. 

Even in the highest risk vaccination group, young post pubescent males, the data makes a compelling argument that the rate of myocarditis from COVID-19 infections is higher than from vaccination. 

Dr. Vinay Prasad, the current FDA head of the Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research, stated in a manuscript he co-authored, "The incidence of myocarditis found for young men after SARS-CoV-2 infection is larger than what we found for myocarditis following COVID-19 vaccination."  There were, however, a few caveats.  In the paper, Dr. Knudsen and Prasad stated that infection rates for vaccinations were found to be higher in those under the age of 40 after the second dosage of the Moderna COVID-19 Vaccine. It should also be noted that the data used in this analysis is from the Moderna Spikevax vaccine, which has a higher dosage than Moderna’s mNEXSPIKE vaccine, which is available this year for those under the age of 65.

Most studies have found that the greatest risk of post vaccination myocarditis is after the second dose of an mRNA vaccine. Faksova, et al., reported the odds ratio (person-years of follow up) of developing myocarditis after the second dose of an mRNA vaccine is 2.86 for the Pfizer vaccine and 6.10 for the Moderna (Spikevax) vaccine.  The odds ratios are lower for the first, and third doses of the vaccine.  For comparison, Zuin et al. reported a hazard ratio (risk) of patients (mean age of 56.1 years) developing myocarditis after infection of 5.16.   Based on a literature review, Kundsen and Prasad reported the risk of myocarditis in young males aged 12 to 19 years to have a range of 1/2562 to 1/9442.  The authors also discussed the analysis by Patone, et al. which found that when all ages are considered the incidence of myocarditis after infection was at least four times larger than vaccination after a first or second mRNA dose. For those under 40, the second dose of the Moderna (Spikevax) vaccine had a larger incidence of myocarditis than after infection. As stated above, Moderna is currently distributing a new vaccine for younger patients, mNEXSPIKE, which has a lower antigen dosage. 

Dr. Knudsen and Prasad also expressed a concern of bias in studies looking at the incidence of myocarditis after infection, since most studies rely on documented infections or a positive COVID test which may result in an undercount of cases.  Although this may be true, I do not feel this would meaningfully bias Panton, et al’s findings. Boyton and Altmann reported that, “Meta-analyses and studies of large cohorts have shown that around 20–40% of individuals infected with SARS-CoV-2 have asymptomatic disease”. Thus, even if one includes asymptomatic infections; the data still supports that myocarditis after COVID-19 infection has a greater incidence than after COVID-19 vaccination.    As stated by Abumayyaleh, et al. “Myocarditis following SARS-CoV-2 vaccination is rare, more frequently reported in males under 30 years, and is generally associated with a favorable prognosis. Despite this, the benefits of vaccination continue to outweigh the risks.” 

An interesting question is that because the incidence of myocarditis is highest after the second dose of the vaccine, would giving the first and second vaccine doses in early childhood decrease the chances of developing myocarditis in young post-pubescent males?

The overall findings strongly support that COVID-19 vaccinations are safer than contracting an infection. The data which is available make a compelling argument for everyone, not just those over the age of 65 or who have risk factors but for all ages 6 months and older, to obtain this year’s updated COVID-19 booster. This is of utmost importance in children where the first exposure to a virus should be in a vaccine and not an infection.
