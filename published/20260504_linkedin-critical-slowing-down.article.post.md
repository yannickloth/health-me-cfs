ME/CFS crashes seem to come without warning. A patient feels relatively stable, then tips into a relapse lasting days or weeks. By the time the crash is apparent, the trigger is already in the past.

A prediction from the formal dynamical systems model suggests there may be a detectable signal in the weeks before a crash — and that consumer wearables might be sufficient to capture it.

The mechanism is critical slowing down, a generic property of systems near bifurcation points. As a system approaches a tipping point, the dominant eigenvalue of the Jacobian approaches zero: return from perturbations becomes slower, autocorrelation in time series data rises, variance increases. These two signatures precede transitions across the boundary — both worsening and improvement — before the transition occurs.

The model's specific prediction: a sustained increase of more than 2 standard deviations in the 7-day rolling variance of HRV, combined with autocorrelation at lag 1 above 0.7, precedes attractor transitions by 2–4 weeks. HRV is the chosen observable because it integrates autonomic, metabolic, and immune state — the three systems that form the energy–immune feedback loop whose slowest direction governs proximity to the separatrix.

If this holds, the clinical implication is a 2–4 week early-warning window. Long enough to alter behaviour pre-emptively, not merely document the crash after it happens. For research, it would provide a continuous physiological readout of separatrix proximity — not just "is the patient improving" but "how close to the tipping point."

The validation study hasn't been run: continuous HRV monitoring in a cohort of ≥ 50 ME/CFS patients over ≥ 12 months, with ≥ 5 documented transitions for statistical power. The hardware already exists in patients' hands. The bottleneck is the research infrastructure.
