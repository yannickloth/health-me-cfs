The most causally fundamental mechanism in ME/CFS isn't necessarily the best treatment target. That sounds wrong — shouldn't the most upstream cause get the most aggressive intervention? — but the logic breaks down in three distinct ways.

First: the root cause may be gone. Most patients are diagnosed years after onset. The triggering virus was cleared; the stressor ended. The disease persists because the amplifier locks it established became self-sustaining. Treating a cause that is no longer active achieves nothing. The clinically relevant question isn't "what caused your ME/CFS?" but "what is maintaining it now?"

Second: the most upstream cause may be the least accessible. CNS energy metabolism cannot be directly restored with available therapeutics. Meanwhile downstream amplifiers — mast cell activation, NAD⁺ depletion — are addressable now with established safety profiles.

Third: single-mechanism treatments fail by design in a multi-lock disease. Even if a root cause is present and accessible, treating it alone fails because the other mechanisms compensate. Recovery requires releasing enough locks simultaneously that the remaining ones can no longer hold the system.

Treatment priority should be the product of causal importance × therapeutic tractability — not causal importance alone. By that reasoning: the GPCR autoantibody cascade (root cause, moderate-to-high tractability) ranks first. The CNS energy crisis (most causally important, but no adequate targeting tools) ranks seventh. The ranking diverges substantially from the causal hierarchy, and that divergence is the point.

This also explains the near-universal disappointment of single-target trials. A trial testing one highly causal agent against a multi-lock disease is almost structurally guaranteed to produce a modest result — not because the agent doesn't work, but because it's the wrong design.
