Most people who get COVID-19 don't develop ME/CFS. Most people who get EBV don't either. Why do some and not others — from the same infection, in the same community?

The infection cannot be the whole answer. The formal model proposes a multi-hit framework: ME/CFS onset requires reaching a threshold, and in most people a single infection is insufficient because their baseline physiology sits comfortably within the healthy basin. Those who develop ME/CFS have pre-existing sub-threshold vulnerabilities that place them already close to the separatrix — the boundary between healthy and disease states. The infection is the final push, not the whole story.

Three predisposing factors come out of the model: genetic reduction in mitochondrial Complex I efficiency, subclinical GPCR autoantibody formation before any ME/CFS trigger, and TRPM3 channelopathy (a calcium channel involved in NK cell function). None independently triggers ME/CFS. But they interact synergistically — the strongest pairwise synergy is between Complex I reduction and autoantibodies (interaction coefficient γ = 0.55), because both converge on the same bottleneck, oxygen-dependent ATP production.

The Dubbo infection studies documented this pattern: acute illness severity was the strongest single predictor of conversion to post-infectious fatigue syndromes, but prior health state also mattered — pre-existing fatigue, stress history, other vulnerabilities. The multi-hit framework is consistent with both: acute severity determines the size of the perturbation; baseline position determines how much perturbation is needed to cross the threshold.

This also means that sub-threshold infections may incrementally erode the margin — explaining the cumulative multi-hit history many patients report — and that early intervention before consolidation accumulates has a specific biological rationale.
