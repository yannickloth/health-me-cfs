A teenage girl with ME/CFS stops eating. She's losing weight. She avoids food.

A clinician who doesn't know ME/CFS sees: female patient, weight loss, food avoidance, fatigue.

The diagnosis: eating disorder.

It's sometimes wrong. And when it is, the consequences are serious — including life-threatening malnutrition while teams debate psychiatric explanations instead of feeding the patient.

But the story runs in both directions. Other patients with ME/CFS gain weight they can't lose through any conventional means — because the standard advice (diet and exercise) is contraindicated in a disease where exercise triggers crashes.

The real picture is more complex than either story:

→ Eating triggers post-exertional malaise in many patients. The gut is hypersensitive. Cytokines suppress appetite at the brain level. Chewing and swallowing cost energy some patients don't have.

→ Weight gain in ME/CFS involves impaired fat oxidation (the enzymes that burn stored fat are deficient), leptin resistance, and muscle wasting — producing "sarcopenic obesity": more fat, less muscle, even at normal body weight.

→ Standard weight management advice misses all of this. The fat isn't there because of behavior. It's there because of broken metabolism.

The food problem in ME/CFS is metabolic, immunological, and neurological — wearing the clothes of something more familiar.
