Your body recycles 50 kg of ATP every day from a pool of just 50 grams. One molecule of glucose can yield 38 ATP through the full mitochondrial pathway. When that pathway is impaired, the cell falls back on glycolysis — the same glucose, 2 ATP instead of 38. A twentieth of the energy.

In ME/CFS, this isn't theoretical. Fluge and Mella found impaired pyruvate dehydrogenase function — the gateway enzyme between glycolysis and the Krebs cycle. Metabolomics shows a pattern of metabolic inflexibility. Two-day CPET testing shows that patients who exercise to capacity on day 1 perform measurably worse on day 2 — the opposite of what deconditioning predicts.

The relationship between mitochondrial damage and ATP output is nonlinear. The system has buffer capacity. Once damage exceeds that buffer, output drops off a cliff. Small increases in demand that would be invisible in a healthy person push a compromised system past its threshold. That's the crash patients describe — not fragility, but the physics of a system operating at its limit.

This is not tiredness that resolves with rest. It is a damaged engine, not an empty tank. Filling the tank doesn't fix the engine.
