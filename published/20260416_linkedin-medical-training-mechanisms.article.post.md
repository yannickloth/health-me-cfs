ICD-11 contains 17,000 diagnostic categories and 130,000 clinical terms. Medical training is organized around memorizing and matching them.

The number of fundamental biological mechanisms underlying those 17,000 diseases: roughly 30 to 50.

That asymmetry is the root of a massive, systematic failure.

800,000 Americans die or are permanently disabled by diagnostic error every year — the single largest source of serious harm from medical mistakes, larger than surgical errors, larger than medication errors. In emergency departments alone, 7.4 million patients per year are misdiagnosed out of 130 million visits.

But those numbers capture only the acute, measurable failures. They miss an entire category: the patient with a *correct* diagnosis and an *incomplete* mechanistic picture — whose specific dysregulation was documented, whose mechanism was known, whose treatment existed, and who was never connected to it. That harm is invisible in the statistics. It accumulates over years.

Here's what it looks like concretely. A patient has had unrestorative sleep since adolescence. A sleep study, years later, documents alpha-wave intrusion in deep slow-wave sleep. The mechanism is published: abnormal thalamic calcium channel conductance prevents delta oscillations, contaminating restorative sleep with waking-frequency brain activity. The consequence is known: glymphatic clearance fails, metabolic waste accumulates, the brain wakes exhausted regardless of hours slept. A drug targeting this mechanism directly has been in clinical use for decades.

No one prescribes it. Not after the sleep study. Not after the neurology referral. Not after years of follow-up. Because the diagnosis didn't map to the protocol, and the physician was never trained to reason from mechanism to treatment.
