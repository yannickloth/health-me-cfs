Kathi loved travel and the world. Three years ago she developed ME/CFS. Within months she was confined to a single darkened room, unable to tolerate light, sound, or even the noise of her own chewing. Her mother had to purée her food. She was 29.

On March 3rd, Kathi chose assisted suicide. Her friend wrote: "Even though she so desperately wanted to live — she fulfilled her wish to be free again."

Just over a month earlier, Samuel, 22, made the same choice in Austria. His last public message: "ME/CFS kills."

Two young people dead in five weeks. We increasingly understand what drives this disease. What we don't have is a way to save someone who is already severe — and that is the gap that kills, because the funding to close it is a fraction of what the disease burden demands, and shrinking.

Germany is cutting ME/CFS research funding. Austria has no national ME/CFS strategy. France, Belgium, Luxembourg — nothing. Across Europe roughly 2 million patients are living without a single approved treatment, without specialist care pathways, without any institutional acknowledgment that this disease can kill them.

And it's not that we don't know where to look. Immunoadsorption trials show real improvement in autoantibody-mediated subgroups. BC007 — now rovunaptide — cleared phase I. Low-dose Abilify, LDN, mast cell stabilisers: clinicians are seeing symptomatic benefit, the signal is there. None of it moves fast enough without proper funding, and what little there is keeps getting cut.

Every ME/CFS patient who chooses to die because there is no treatment is a policy failure. It is not a personal tragedy in some neutral sense — it is the direct consequence of political decisions about what research gets funded and what patients get abandoned.

If you are a policymaker or an elected representative, the research pipeline exists. The science is not waiting for a miracle; it is waiting for money. Fund it.
